This isn't a single wreck or a dramatic ledge — it's a short, repeatable corridor. The 28th Street Cut is the first‑mile water I check when the bar is marginal or when a short day makes sense. Tide and inlet outflow shove bait off the sand, across a string of sand fingers and abrupt sand‑to‑hard seams. The transitions are shallow — 15–40 ft — and often only a few hundred yards from the cut. That small footprint is what makes the spot practical and pressure‑sensitive.

## Why the cut and gutters matter: the conveyor and the seam

The easiest way to think about it is conveyor physics. Water moving out of the inlet carries bait across flat sand until the current drops off over a shell or hard pocket. Where the sand ends and the harder relief begins the bait quits the sand and piles up. Fluke, sea bass, and the usual small bottoms wait on that seam.

Because everything happens close to the inlet, the window is short and sharp. Unlike deeper ledges that fish through multiple tide states, this water fires quickly when bait and tide align, then cleans up just as fast. That makes positioning and timing more important than fancy lures.

## When the window is open

The reliable window here is the incoming tide. Incoming pushes bait into the downhill edge and turns the flattish sand into a feeding highway. Clean, short‑period swell from the southwest sharpens the bite — the seam stays defined and fish hold shallow. Long northeast swell or raw surf tends to push the fish off the shallow pockets and concentrates them on the first clean edge outside the inlet.

Put it together: a steady incoming tide, a light SW wind, and a trace of bait on sonar are the short‑day combo you want.

## Reading the pocket: what to look for on the water

Look for the seam and the bait that defines it. The clear signs that a pocket is worth working are simple and repeatable.

- Tight, shallow marks on sonar hugging the sand gutter.
- Birds or surface bait patches directly over the seam; they come and go fast, but they're decisive.
- Small, sharp drops and picks on vertical jigs or livebait early in the drift — the soft picks before the main bite tell you the lane is holding fish.

Those signs tell you the conveyor is working. If marks vanish and birds clear, the tide or pressure has shifted the seam and you need to reset your approach.

## How I run the drifts and why angle matters

Positioning beats presentation most days.

Start high in the channel and run slow, controlled drifts across the gutter. The goal is to sweep baits from the sand into the downhill seam — you want to cross the seam, not ride with it. Typical drift angle is 30–45° across the gutter; that angle gives your bait a chance to pass perpendicular through the downhill flow where fish are stacked.

Hold the boat upcurrent of the seam and let the tide carry your presentation down across the hard pockets. Tight, controlled drifts beat freewheeling runs here. If tide is light, idle and minimal scope works; when tide runs hard a small drogue to slow and stabilize the boat makes the presentations readable.

Run the first pass along the upcurrent lip, then sweep successive drifts a boat‑width inside until the seam sours or marks vanish. Repeat lanes — fish often ride the same conveyor, and repeated passes in the same lane usually outproduce random sweeping.

## Tackle and presentations that match the place

This is light, feel‑driven water. Think sensitivity and subtlety over brute force.

- Rods and lines: spinning outfits in the 6–12 lb class for fluke. Braid 10–20 lb main with a light mono or fluoro leader for bite feel and quick hooksets.
- Baits: squid strips, cut bait, and bucktails tipped with small teasers (minnow or Gulp) are the standbys. Where the fish are active, small diamond jigs and 1/2–1 oz bucktails work well.
- Vertical work: light jigs 1/2–2 oz. Keep the rod tip up and feel for the soft picks before committing to a hookset.
- Soft sand handling: long‑shank hooks and light wire help avoid misses on soft sand; when the pick is firm shorten leaders and set quicker.

The point is to match the current. Keep presentations light enough to drift naturally but heavy enough to stay near the bottom through the seam. Overweighting drags you into soft sand and kills bites.

## Pressure, fleet behavior, and quick options

This water is accessible, so it sees pressure and predictable fleet patterns.

On marginal bar days the fleet splits: some boats work the 28th gutters hard; others push to the first clean edge outside the inlet. A quick sonar sweep and a thermometer read usually tells you which choice to make. If 28th goes quiet, boats fan out to neighboring cuts and the outer shoal line; a single bait patch will pull the fleet fast.

Pressure reactions to watch for:
- Pockets tighten and schools go deep when multiple boats stage the same lane. Fish will often migrate a few hundred yards to the nearest unpressured gutter, not miles.
- Repeated quick drifts spook birds but leave marks on sonar. If birds vanish and marks remain, slow the presentation and fish the column or verticals.
- When the inlet is noisy — boats, clammers — fluke retreat to the hardest pockets. Switch to vertical drops near relief rather than wide sweeps.

Quiet strategy: find an unpressured lane, run repeated tight drifts, and work vertical presentations over the hardest scours. Ownership of a fresh lane matters; the first boat to settle it often owns the day.

## Common mistakes I see

- Running long, shallow sweeps parallel to the surf. The fish take bait across the seam; you want to cross it.
- Overweighting presentations and dragging into soft sand. Keep it light enough to ride the current.
- Ignoring tide timing. The pocket runs hard and short; miss the tide window and you're better off moving outside to deeper edges.

## Micro‑observation and what to do next

The fluke bite at the 28th rarely lasts long. When the tide and bait line up the pocket fires quick and shuts just as fast. If you leave, mark the lane and run a contour sweep: the same seam will often repeat along the next gutter to the south or north. The first boat to settle a fresh lane usually owns it for at least a few productive drifts.
