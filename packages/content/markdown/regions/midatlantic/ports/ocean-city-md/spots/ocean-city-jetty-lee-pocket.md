This pocket is the short first‑mile water you use when the bar is working but you don't want—or can't risk—the run out to the shelf. It sits immediately outside the north and south jetties: a sheltered eddy on the down‑current side and a narrow surf‑edge tongue running off the outer shoal. Bottom is mostly sand with patches of shell and occasional hard spots; the tongue commonly shows a 6–15 ft gradient with a thin scour gutter where bait funnels and fish sit up.

Why the lee pocket is the first reliable stop
---------------------------------------------

You run here for concentrated bait and a tidy tidal seam you can actually sight‑fish. The lee compresses bunker and sand eel life against the jetty face on certain tide angles, and the tongue forms a clear uphill edge where stripers will hold and charge. When tide and bait synchronize the place fishes like a different port for a couple of hours—surface alarms, quick boil strikes, and short, violent runs.

Tide angle decides everything
-----------------------------

Start with tide angle; it tells you whether the pocket will be a bait magnet or a messy churn. There are two setups I look for every time.

- Flood push along the jetty: an incoming tide that peels bait toward the lee compresses life against the jetty face. The tongue lights up for sight strikes—clean seams and tight schools.
- Outgoing downhill after a push: when current runs downhill off the shoal, the tongue turns into a conveyor. Bait funnels off the shoal into the scour and fish mill along the edge looking uphill.

A quick tweak: a northerly wind or an upwelling that drags cleaner, cooler water offshore will tuck bait a little deeper on the tongue. Still, birds and a surface bait line are your first, simplest signals.

How I work the pocket — anchor or controlled drift
--------------------------------------------------

This is short‑range work, not a long‑range troll. I use one of two clean approaches depending on how the bait and birds are presenting.

Anchor/spot‑cast: If there’s a concentrated bait ball in the lee and birds are holding, I anchor or slip‑anchor upcurrent in the pocket, present topwaters and plugs into the holding water, and stay ready for fast surface runs. The advantage here is control — you can make repeated short presentations into the same boil without burning the school.

Controlled drift across the tongue: When fish are holding along the scour or the tongue is the obvious seam, I run a tight drift with one motor in. Set up upstream so the drift carries you across the gutter, keep the bow angled into the current, and sweep plugs or eels across the seam. Make short, repeated casts into condensation points rather than long sweeping passes.

Presentations that draw strikes
-------------------------------

Match the presentation to the school and light.

- Early and low light: large topwater plugs, poppers, and long soft‑plastic swimbaits fished close to the boil. Fish are aggressive; you want something that screams and moves a lot.
- Daylight tight schools: 4–6" bucktails and diving plugs worked just inshore of the bait line. Short, accurate casts into the flank of the school.
- Vertical suspension: when birds and sonar show bait hanging in the column, live bait on a short leader over the boil gets tight quickest.

Anchor and drift positions I use
--------------------------------

Safety and placement are the same priorities. Hold where you can present into the flow without standing on the bar.

- Safe anchor: I’ll hold 100–300 yds inside the outer shoal and in the lee of the south jetty when wind is NE–E. That gives shelter and keeps you inside the bait compression without committing to the bar.
- Drift lane: set up upstream of the tongue so the drift takes you across the scour. Keep the bow into the current and control speed with short bursts of throttle.
- Distance from jetties: don’t hug the jetty face. Rip and rocks are real, and shoals shift. Give a few boat‑lengths unless you’re certain of depth and change.

Reading the signs — the fish and the fleet
-----------------------------------------

The fish usually show before the fleet does. Birds that tighten into a single line and hold direction are a higher‑value signal than a scattered flock. Sonar that shows a dense vertical mark under surface activity is confirmation.

Watch the fleet behavior: when the fleet makes short, repeated checks rather than long cruising passes, they’re following a tidy, localized feed. When everyone makes one long pass and moves on, the bait isn’t holding.

Signs the window is building
- Birds tightening into a single line or backing the same seam
- Sonar showing a dense vertical mark under surface activity
- Clear water contrast along the tongue — a clean pocket next to murk
- Bait consistently fleeing toward the shore or into the scour
- The fleet doing short checks instead of long runs

Common pressure and setup mistakes
----------------------------------

There are a few mistakes I see that kill a good window fast.

- Anchoring on the boil instead of upcurrent: you want baits presented into the flow, not downstream of it.
- Sitting when the tide shifts 90°: the bait disperses quickly. Move before repeated casts burn the fish out.
- Assuming birds equal numbers of stripers: birds show bait; stripers will avoid pressure. Look for continuing sonar marks and a tidy current seam before committing.

When the bar is marginal
-----------------------

This is the go‑to spot when the bar report is questionable and a long run isn’t worth it. Close, controllable water with quick escape options.

If the bar report is “no,” don’t try it. Fish tighter inside the lee or on the beach side of the channel rather than risking the entrance. If the bar is sloppy but the lee holds cleaner water, slow your drift, fish vertical presentations, and keep bait live—stripers will work tight in the churned edge even when surface life looks tired.

Final word
----------

This pocket rewards timing and placement more than fancy gear. Read the tide angle, pick your anchor or drift start, and present into the flow. When the pocket works it’s immediate and violent; when it dies, don’t force it—either shift inside to the wreck line or stand down and wait for a better bar window.
