This is a small, high‑frequency piece of water. The jetties and the narrow throat off the inlet act like a first‑mile conveyor for migrating stripers and bluefish—bay outflow gets funneled, bait compresses into sightable seams, and the bite tends to be short, loud, and very tide dependent. It’s a sight‑fishing spot more than a channel‑bottom fishery; respect the bar and the timing, and you’ll get the moments you came for.

## How the jetty and funnel stage bait

The inlet doesn’t produce a reef so much as a hydrodynamic trap. Bay water is forced into a narrow throat, accelerates, then peels off the jetty into the longshore current. That peel becomes the work: a downhill tongue when the current angle is right, a short eddy when it isn’t. Bait gets pinched along the outside face and in the lee pockets; predators sit on the downhill edge and wait.

Bottom is mostly sand with chunks of cobble and shell — nothing dramatic. The attraction is movement and compression, not reef relief. Expect tight bait echoes hugging the jetty flank when the funnel’s doing its job.

## Where the takes usually come from

The sensible strike water is the outer tongue and the lee pocket off the north jetty — not the harbor channel. When the funnel peels, you get boil lines, foamers and bait stacks on the outside face and the immediate lee. Stripers will often take on the downhill side where bunker are being shoved; bluefish will light up on a clean ebb push that carves a seam into the nearshore.

If you’re fishing the inside channel and wondering why nothing’s happening, you’re in the wrong room. Watch the outside edge, the pockets along the jetty, and the first downhill seam off the rocks.

## When the seam fires — tides, seasons, times

This place is a timing game. The windows are short but frequent during migrations.

- Seasons: spring and fall are your highest‑frequency windows for stripers and big bluefish pushes. Early summer can give repeatable late‑day topwater shows if warm surface water and bait stack nearshore.
- Tide angles:
  - Incoming into early slack: prime for stripers staging into the inlet—slow‑moving foamers and visible fish along the outside face.
  - Strong ebb tongues: where bluefish often burn—short, violent pushes along the outside tongue.
  - Full slack: can hold fish, but usually needs a clean wind or active bird work before you commit.
- Time of day: early morning and late afternoon tighten sight opportunities. Midday will still produce when the color is stable and birds are working.

Plan for small windows. The jetty lights off fast.

## Signs the window is building

Watch for a handful of clear signals. When these line up, the job becomes obvious.

- Birds holding a strip instead of circling wide
- Bunker/menhaden balls rolling near the jetty face
- Surface boils that maintain direction rather than scattering
- Sonar showing tight bait echoes hugging the jetty flank

If two or three of those are present, set up for sight casts. If only one shows, keep an eye but don’t commit the whole spread.

## Sight‑fishing and topwater approach

Keep the boat low and slow. Position to present along the foam line, not through the middle of it. A drift or slow motor that keeps your topwater in the strike zone is almost always better than noisy, high‑speed passes.

Cast to the outer edge of a boil — stripers tend to hit the downhill side where bait is being shoved. When birds are corralling bait, tighten the spread: a topwater, a medium jig or bucktail, and a heavier swim bait or live outfit standing by.

Work the lure to match behavior: steady, aggressive cadence when fish are roaming; short sharp pops when they’re looking up but wary. If fish sink under surface pressure, switch quickly to weighted bucktails, heavy soft‑baits, or live‑bait free‑lining in the immediate lee.

## Presentations that convert

Short, tactical choices win here. Match the speed of the push and the size of the bait you see.

- Topwater: 3–4" poppers, 4–6" pencil plugs for sight converts on breaking fish.
- Metals: 1–3 oz spoons and metal slugs for bluefish—fast retrieves into the push.
- Jigs/bucktails: 1/2–1 oz bucktails with a soft tail or teaser for stripers on the outside edge.
- Live bait/fly‑lining: bunker, pogies or eels free‑lined into the seam when you can get a clean presentation.
- Leaders: wire or stout fluorocarbon for blues; lighter leaders for stripers unless the fish are leader shy.

Those are the tools. Use them in small combinations and be ready to tighten or change after the first couple of takes.

## Boat handling, launch and bar notes

The Lynnhaven bar shifts. The jetties make a clear approach in calm conditions, but the bar closes quickly with northeast swell and onshore wind. Don’t treat the inlet like a guarantee.

Practical rules I run by:
- Prefer returning on an outgoing tide when practical, or time your run so you meet a favorable bar and sea state.
- South and southwest winds usually make a clean entrance; extended northeast fetch often means the bar isn’t worth risking.
- Watch local bar reports and get a quick radio check from someone who just came in. Sandbanks move.
- Small skiffs: be conservative. Shallow traps show up fast on a fresh northeaster.

Through the channel keep speed up and tight; slow down early on the outside approach to make accurate sight casts.

## Pressure, common mistakes, and quick calls

Fleet behavior changes the water fast. A few boats in the right spot will often take the visible takes; late arrivals can collapse the window.

Common mistakes I see:
- Committing to the inlet on a marginal bar report and losing the day to a bad exit.
- Fishing the inside channel instead of the outside lee where the seam is.
- Overcrowding the boil—first three boats usually get the action; piling in late often shuts it down.

Pressure reactions:
- Schools scatter and become sonar dots when the fleet arrives from multiple directions.
- Foamers that refuse repeated casts are often pressured and likely to relocate.

Quick conversion rule: when one boat gets a topwater hookup, narrow your presentations and have a live‑bait outfit ready. The first few minutes after a strike often sustain the bite.

## What usually matters more — and less

What usually matters more:
- tide angle and bait shape
- the outside peel off the jetty
- how clean the approach is

What usually matters less:
- running a long spread inside the channel
- exact GPS distance from the buoy (the seam moves)

## Practical takeaway

This seam is a short, tactical piece of water. Fish the tide, watch birds and bait, and respect the entrance. When the right tide angle, clean approach, and tight bait line up, the jetty gives sightable, convertible bait—topwater and pencils make it a fast, worthwhile day. Be ready to move; the window rarely waits.
