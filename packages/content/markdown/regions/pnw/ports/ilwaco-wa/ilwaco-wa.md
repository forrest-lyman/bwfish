# Ilwaco, Washington

## Harbor And Geography
If you run out of Ilwaco you feel the geometry before you see fish: a short motor from moorage drops you into the tidal squeeze of the Columbia jetties, then a few miles more and you're on the outer bar and quickly into true shelf water. That tight funnel—river, jetties, bar and a steep offshore drop—creates both the danger and the payoff. Everything we choose to do starts with that layout.

The practical pieces to watch when deciding where to fish are simple. The twin jetties concentrate bait and make tight seams on incoming and outgoing tides. The bar amplifies swell and tidal sets, and whether it's flat or running determines if you can safely commit offshore. The shelf drops off quickly outside the bar, so productive bottom and hard structure aren't a long haul—ten to thirty miles will put you on mixed bottom depending on your line. Headlands and points give local eddies and wind shadows; those lee spots are often cleaner and hold bait when the exposed shore is blown out. Lastly, the river plume makes the inside water tannic; the first clean, bait-rich water usually shows up where plume meets shelf current as a visible color or temperature break.

Decisions on run length, target and setup come from that map: a flat bar and light offshore wind pushes you out to the shelf and tuna grounds; a running bar pins most boats to the jetties and the close shelf.

## Water And Conditions
Fishing here is about matching three things: a safe bar window, a stable wind/tide combo, and a clean edge where bait stacks. All three together give repeatable bites; miss one and you’re gambling.

In summer the prevailing NW winds favor upwelling and tend to roughen the bar when paired with an outgoing tide. Winter swings SW to W and brings a stormier regime. Upwelling lays down a cold nutrient-rich surface layer and you often get a sharp temperature/color break a few miles off the bar where warmer offshore water meets colder inshore water. Tidal exchange through the jetties produces strong currents and hydraulics—outgoing tide plus offshore swell makes the bar ugly; incoming tide with light offshore wind is usually the run you take.

Expect fog and a marine layer with strong summer upwelling; early runs can be low visibility until the sun burns it off. Clarity moves from tannic, turbid plume water near the jetties to clean blue-green on the shelf, and bait and predators sort along that transition. In practice, the fishable water shows a visible color or temperature change, a current strong enough to hold bait but not shred it, and a bar window that lets you get back in without trouble.

## Fisheries
Ilwaco is a mixed port. The Columbia mouth is the engine—bait funnels there and sets the calendar. Fisheries hinge on how bait behaves around the plume and how the shelf responds to tides and upwelling.

Salmon are driven by bait concentrations. Spring and summer Chinook push into the mouth and hold on the edges of the plume and near the jetties; summer and fall coho show up later and tend to prefer cleaner shelf water and off the points. Halibut sit the sandy flats and transitions just seaward of the bar—where bottom flattens and bait collects. Nearshore groundfish—lingcod, rockfish, Pacific cod—hold the pinnacles, ledges and kelp inside the 10–40-fathom range and bite better when the water clears and current sets over structure. Pelagics like albacore are a summer story: boats read temperature breaks and working birds 20–60 miles offshore rather than fishing fixed marks. The crab and bait fisheries feed the local food web and affect bait availability for everything else.

What turns presence into a day you remember is concentrated bait on a clean sheared edge or thermocline. When birds and porpoise are working and the thermocline is shallow, predators stop looking and start feeding.

## Fleet Behavior
The fleet here makes quick, binary choices: stay close to the river mouth or commit to a long day on the shelf. The first 10–15 miles outside the bar—and whether the bar is passable—drives most movement more than a calendar page.

When the bar looks marginal most boats adopt a short-haul pattern: they hold near the jetties, fish the inside edges of the plume and pick over nearshore structure. Trollers set shallow and fish recognized seams. When the bar is flat and the wind steady, boats go long-haul—20–60 nm runs to halibut, albacore or deeper salmon marks happen all summer.

Search discipline matters here. Local boats prefer methodical grids across temperature and color breaks and along contour changes instead of blind steaming. A fresh bird report, a relay of drift marks or a concentrated live-bait find will pull boats off search tracks and into a spread. Radio culture is practical: bar reports, tide timings and neighbor calls about birds or thermocline marks get immediate attention. A string of hits on a relay line or a sudden offshore color change will pull a portion of the fleet in the same direction fast.

What gets the fleet moving first: a good bar report, tide state and wind direction. Once the bar is passable, bait and birds decide whether boats stay tight to the mouth or run for the shelf.

## Tactical Notes
Out of Ilwaco the work is pattern recognition—current, structure, temperature and bait—and then committing. When you find the signal, slow down and isolate directionality of current over adjacent structure. Most productive bites start where bait is trapped against a hard edge and current pushes into it.

Signals to watch
- Seams at the jetties where plume meets ocean water. Those seams hold smelt and herring and will attract Chinook on incoming and slack tides.
- Temperature/color edges a few miles outside the bar. Fish often sit on the warm side of a cold upwelling tongue or at the line where warm patches shear off.
- Working birds and porpoise. They point to concentrated bait and are reliable for pelagic bites when there’s a shallow thermocline.
- Current over structure. Downhill current over a hard edge concentrates bait on the ledge and triggers ambush predators.

Tactics by target

### Chinook and coho
We work the outer river edges and the first clean shelf water on tidal seams. On the shelf follow the warm-side edges of the plume—that’s where bait stacks. Chinook want deep presentation: deep-diving plugs, flasher/hooked herring on downriggers. Coho respond to shallower gear—smaller spoons and hoochies, faster presentations. Time the approach with incoming and slack tides for condensed bait; early morning fog-breaks often kick off active surface feeding.

### Halibut
Halibut live on the sand transitions and troughs just seaward of the bar, where the bottom flattens and bait gathers. Look for bait lumps and sudden depth changes. Use heavy circle hooks with whole bait or large jigs and keep bait near bottom but not buried—short hops and long drops depending on current. Slow to moderate current with clear bottom conditions gives the most consistent marks.

### Nearshore rockfish and lingcod
Fish the hard edges and pinnacles inside the shelf where contour turns to rock. Vertical jigs and heavy bait rigs work best; fish tight to structure on downhill currents and use heavier leaders because of toothy predators. These species hold better in the clearest water and when current is steady over the structure.

### Albacore
Albacore are a mid-summer, long-run proposition. Commit to temperature breaks and clean blue water beyond the shelf and follow birds and porpoise rather than GPS marks. Fast-trolled lures, short leaders and a readiness for rougher seas pay off. They show up when a warm surface tongue rides over upwelled water and the thermocline is shallow enough to keep fish at the surface.

Common mistakes and simple fixes
- Chasing birds into marginal bar conditions. Fix: confirm a safe return window before you commit to a long haul.
- Fishing the center of the plume. Fix: work the edges where bait is concentrated and water shears.
- Ignoring tide timing. Fix: align approaches with slack or a favorable tide direction for your target.

The local adjustment that matters most: when you find a temperature or color edge, slow the search and test current direction over structure. If bait is trapped against a hard edge with favorable push, you’re in the right neighborhood. Act decisively—these bites are about timing as much as location.
