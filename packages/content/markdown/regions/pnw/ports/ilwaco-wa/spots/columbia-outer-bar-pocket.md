The simple reason this pocket matters is the jetties and the bar act like a funnel. The plume, tide hydraulics, and swell get pushed into a tight corridor and then the bottom drops and eases off into cleaner shelf water. Anything that survives the churn tends to pile in that first quiet seam just seaward of the outer bar. Halibut and the rest of the bottom life treat that trapped energy like a conveyor—bait moves into calmer water, and predators sit where it lands.

## What the pocket looks like on the sounder and on the sea
This is a bar-to-shelf pocket, not a single rock or wreck. You’ll see a shallow-to-moderate sand flat with troughs sitting right outside the most violent bar tongue. The bottom is long and low-relief: inner churn, a band of mixed gravel and sand, then a gentle step toward true shelf depths. Depths change with bar movement and seasons, but the geometry—the seam where turbulence meets smoother flow—stays the same.

Boats know it because it’s the first reliable sand that holds whole-bait lumps outside Ilwaco when the bar is passable. On the sounder the pocket often shows as a loose mid-column cloud ahead of a faint bottom mark—bait first, fish second.

## Why the current makes it work
The pocket is a current hinge. Tidal exchange through the jetties forces the river plume to peel off and meet incoming ocean swell across a tight seam. That seam is the workhorse for holding bait.

Incoming tide and slack smooth the flow over the sand step and consolidate bait into the seam; that’s when the pocket fishes best. A raw ebb widens the churn, scatters bait, and usually kills the halibut bite. When a clear blue shelf tongue shears against plume-stained water the pocket sits on the warm-side edge and becomes a bait trap; fish stack on that warm side, often directly over the sand pocket.

Micro-observation: bait shows up on the leading face of the pocket when current softens; speed it up and the bait disappears first.

## What the bait field looks like and what’s in it
Primary forage here tends to be sand lance and small herring/anchovy pushed from the plume, with scattered crab and shrimp surviving in the scours. Whole baits and small balls show in the calmer backwater behind the outer bar. Birds and porpoise aren’t as common as on the open shelf edge—when they do converge it signals a concentrated ball and often quick pelagic interest further out.

Halibut use the mixed-sand face. They don’t sit on the bar’s top; they orient to incoming current where bait moves off the sand into quieter water and hold just off that face where small relief concentrates food.

## Who fishes it and how the fleet treats the pocket
Ilwaco boats default here on marginal days—it’s close, quick to test, and often fishes when the shelf will not. Expect several boats running controlled, anchorless drifts rather than long searches. If reports show a shelf thermal or birds further out, the fleet will bounce off the pocket fast; this is first‑mile water, not a long‑haul destination.

Experienced boats use it like a test-run: if the bait shows and you get a first string of halibut you stay and work it; if not, you commit to the shelf while the bar remains open.

## When the pocket fishes and when it dies
The pocket fishes when the bar is passable and incoming or slack tide softens current over the sand step; when a visible color or temp edge crosses the pocket; or when bait lumps show on sonar or birds/porpoise work quietly nearby. It dies when a strong ebb rips bait off the bar into open water, when an onshore NW wind muddies the edge, or when the plume’s center pushes out and removes the clean water the pocket needs.

Micro-observation: the pocket will hold bait and fish even when nearby ledges are empty. It’s quieter work than a ledge—less dramatic, but more reliable on marginal days.

## How I run the drift and present bait
I treat this like a conveyor pick-off. Anchorless drifts with a drogue or controlled throttle keep the spread neat; you want baits a few feet off bottom to stay above scours but in the bait zone. Long, slow drifts down the face on an incoming tide are the baseline. When the current picks up and the bottom gets snaggy, switch to short hops so your bait stays in the strike zone without hanging hooks in the sand.

Bait and rigging: large whole baits or whole herring on heavy circle hooks. Keep the hook-to-bait profile short so the presentation lies naturally over the sand. Don’t over‑weight—halibut here prefer a tidy, natural drift more than brute force.

Sounder work: look for loose bait clouds in the mid-column ahead of a faint bottom mark. Bait shows first; halibut often show as a bottom arch behind or below that bait.

## Signs the pocket is holding right now
- a quiet line of cleaner water just outside the bar  
- bait marks on the sounder hugging the mid‑column over the sand  
- birds working low and tight instead of spread out  
- a slow, steady incoming tide with minimal surface slope

These are the practical signals I look for before committing a whole day to the pocket.

## Common mistakes that waste time here
- Chasing the center of the plume. This is a boundary fishery—work the transition, not the dirty core.  
- Burning time on the pocket when the bar window is shrinking. If you don’t get a fish early, you can lose the safe return.  
- Overweighting presentation. Too much hardware drags the bait off the sand face; keep it tidy.

## When to lean on this spot
Make this your go-to when the bar is only fair but passable and you want a realistic halibut shot without a long run. It’s a quick-check ground before committing to the shelf: if the bar’s short but the pocket has a clean temp edge and bait shows, you’ll often take more fish here than on marginal sand farther out.

The fish are behaving simply: they sit where bait collects and current softens. Read the color edge, time the tide, and control the drift. Quiet observation and decisive drift work beat fancy tackle in this pocket.
