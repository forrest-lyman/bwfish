## Why the north jetty makes a focused seam

Go look at the jetty and you can see it: a thin line where tannic plume peels off, meets cleaner shelf water, and the jetty’s hydraulics do the rest. The geometry is the point — not a canyon or a long thermal finger but a compact corridor. The jetty throws a steady lee and a bit of shear where current and plume conspire to compress smelt and small herring into a narrow footprint you can run from the harbor in twenty minutes.

That compressed pocket is repeatable because the gravel bar, jetty shadow, and tidal exchange put the same forces in the same place. When the seam’s in the right spot, bait stacks tight on the clean-side edge and predators sit just outside the turbulence, waiting.

## Reading the tide and bar window

Observation: the seam reacts to tide, swell, and bar state faster than most shelf edges.

- Incoming or slack tides commonly shove cleaner, bait-rich water toward the jetty face and compress bait along the outer edge; those are the windows Chinook prefer. Outgoing tides either peel a tidy tongue of plume seaward or rip the seam into a smear depending on swell and wind.
- The lee pocket on the down-current side of the jetty is variable. Sometimes it’s a smooth slick holding bait; other times it’s a boil of birds and porpoise. The difference usually comes down to small changes in current angle and wave state.
- Bar state determines whether you can fish hard. Light offshore wind and an incoming or slack tide make the seam productive and safe. If the bar is running outgoing swell, the seam still exists but fishing it costs drift control and increases risk.

Read the water before committing. If the seam is tight and reachable with a safe return, it’s often the best first check off Ilwaco.

## Where the bait lives and what follows it

You’ll find smelt and small schooling herring as the primary forage, with the occasional anchovy when the plume brings warmer water. On calm days those schools show up as tight, dark patches; birds tend to collapse onto a single line instead of spreading out across the mouth. That’s your visual cue that bait is concentrated in the seam, not scattered.

Predators stack predictably around that bait. Early-season Chinook stage just off the seam and use the jetty face as an ambush line. Halibut don’t sit on the jetty face — they tend to hold on the sand pockets and gutters a few hundred yards seaward where sand meets rock. Rockfish and lingcod tuck into nearshore relief around the breakwater where steady tidal flow pins bait into the structure.

## Signs the seam is worth stopping for

You don’t need every sign; you need a pattern.

- A visible color or temperature change within a boat length of the jetty.
- Birds and porpoise concentrated in a narrow line along the jetty face.
- Bait snapping at the surface or a slick where tannic water peels into blue.
- Compact bait marks holding on sonar when you drift the lee pocket, not streaking off.

If you get two of those, run a proper lap. The bite here comes quickly and in short concentrated runs — birds, bait, boats, then a lull.

## How the seam fishes when it’s on

Expect focused, short runs rather than a wide-area search. Chinook show best on incoming or slack; you’ll get quick bites and then the stretch will tighten up. Halibut take over when the current softens and your sounder shows the bottom on nearby sand transitions. Rockfish and lingcod respond where tidal flow pushes bait into micro-edges around the jetty.

Key pattern: bait stacks on the clean edge and fish stage just outside the turbulence. Don’t fish the middle of the plume — the clean-side edge and the lee pocket hold the life.

## How the fleet runs the corridor

Locals treat the north jetty seam as an early-season first check and a short-haul play.

- First run: a morning lap to see where the plume edge sits relative to the jetties before heading to the shelf.
- Tight lanes: boats run parallel drifts along the lee side, then hop across the seam to isolate where bait is trapped.
- Information spreads fast. One experienced boat on a good mark will empty the harbor; pressure here collapses the bite quicker than most places.

Experienced skippers separate themselves by how they approach the lee pocket — some stop tight on a firm edge and work small lanes, others push a couple hundred yards seaward to the sand transition for halibut. Less-experienced boats tend to crowd the face and fish the center of the plume, which usually fails.

## Drift, presentation, and sonar work

Drift control matters here more than most spots inside the mouth. Small changes in engine and heading move your baits in and out of the corridor.

- Position on the clean side of the seam or inside the lee pocket. For salmon, run a spread that reaches the warm-side edge; for halibut, set long baits and control drift over the sand transition.
- Watch sonar for compact marks hugging the edge. Marks that streak rapidly toward the jetty are bait being pushed; stop and isolate where they settle, not where they came from.
- Space boats. This seam is tight; multiple boats on top of one another accelerates bait scatter and shuts the bite.

A practical lap: find the clean-side edge, feel the drift, and decide whether to isolate salmon on the edge or push seaward for halibut on the sand pockets.

## Common mistakes and simple fixes

You’ll see the same errors here because the seam is small and tempting.

- Mistake: fishing the middle of the plume. Fix: work the clean-side edge and lee pocket. Fish prefer the transition, not the plume center.
- Mistake: committing when the bar window is marginal. Fix: confirm a safe return — the seam isn’t worth getting stuck outside.
- Mistake: running the same line as the whole fleet when marks tighten. Fix: pause, re-run a perpendicular check, or try the sand pockets a few hundred yards seaward.

What usually matters most: tide timing, where the plume actually peels, and a small patch of cleaner water. What usually matters less: how many boats are on the jetty (until bait collapses) or chasing every single bird report without a visible edge.

## Small observations that change decisions

The seam often announces itself before the fish — birds first, sonar marks second, bites last. Treat those as steps, not guarantees. When the seam is compact and the birds are tight, you can expect a quick, predictable run. When the birds scatter or the plume covers the jetty fully, the clean-side edge matters far more than the center.

If you check only one place from Ilwaco when the bar looks fair, make it a proper lap around the north jetty seam: confirm the edge, feel the drift, and choose the target — salmon on the edge, halibut on the sand transition, or the shelf if the seam never materializes.
