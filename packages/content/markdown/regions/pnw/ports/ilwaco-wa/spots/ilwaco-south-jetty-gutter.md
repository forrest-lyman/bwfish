What sets this place apart is geometry first: a hard jetty face, a shallow outside gutter, and the Columbia’s tannic plume shearing into clean shelf water a few hundred yards to a few miles off. The south jetty makes a predictable seam where river outflow meets tidal exchange. When tide and wind cooperate, that seam compresses bait into a tight belt and the bite shows up fast. You can fish meaningful water inside the 10–30 fathom band without committing to a full shelf run — that’s why boats park here when the bar is marginal.

## Why the jetty creates a tidy seam

Observation: the plume peels off the river and the jetty channels that shear into a short, downhill seam. On an incoming tide the gutter traps smelt and anchovy against the jetty edge; the plume peels off a shallow pocket and the color/temperature change becomes a conveyor. Birds will sit on the warm side, bait stacks tight in a narrow band, and predators stage just outside the tannic water.

Explanation: the hard face and shallow gutter keep water movement organized where most open coast edges are messy. The plume provides a lateral boundary; the jetty gives a vertical relief. Together they concentrate forage in a way that favors short, intense windows rather than long, diffuse patches.

## The three things this spot needs to work

This place lives or dies on a short checklist you should test as you cross the bar:

- a visible color/temperature line a boat-length to a few hundred yards off the jetty,
- a tide that compresses (incoming or compressing slack) rather than rips,
- and a bar state that lets you run outside to the edge cleanly.

If any of those three aren't present you're usually fishing skinny bait and scattered marks. The water reads first — read it before you set the spread.

## Current patterns and when it bites best

Observation first: the strongest, most reliable bites happen on the incoming tide as the plume peels and the jetty produces a downhill seam into cleaner water. Incoming squeezes bait shallow and tight; outgoing tends to smear the plume, pull it broader, and scatter bait.

Notes on modifiers:
- A light offshore breeze clears the face and sharpens the seam. Onshore wind pushes the plume close and blurs the edge.
- Swell or a rough bar will change the hydraulics at the mouth; heavy chop will push bait off the face and shut the seam down.
- When the bar is marginal this becomes the fallback — short runs, quick checks, and tight water.

Signs the edge is useable:
- a crisp line of color a boat-length to a few hundred yards off the jetty
- birds working the warm-side ribbon and holding direction
- tight bait marks on sonar hugging the shallow side of the seam
- porpoise or small foamers indicating a concentrated bait ball

## Bait — what sits in the gutter and how it looks

The system is powered by small, dense forage. Spring smelt and adult anchovy or packed small herring show up in the gutter and along the peel of the plume. When schools are tight the seam behaves like a conveyor: bait rides the line and predators stage just outside the tannic water.

What you'll see:
- Typical bait: smelt, anchovy, small herring (seasonal)
- Surface signs: birds picking along a thin warm ribbon, bait balls that roll for short bursts
- Subsurface: narrow, shallow marks on sonar in the 10–40 ft range when the tide pushes correctly

Observation: the bait sits shallow in the gutter; the salmon hold a lane just outside it. Keep baits upstream of the band rather than directly over the pile.

## Target species and how they use the seam

Chinook
- Stage on the warm side of the plume and use the jetty seam as a highway into the river.
- Best in spring and early summer on incoming and compressing slack.
- They can be suspicious and short-biting — presentation and angle matter.

Coho
- Show later and prefer cleaner water just off the jetty or a bit seaward.
- Respond to smaller spoons and hoochies and cleaner, faster presentations.

Nearshore rockfish / lingcod
- The scattered rock and kelp in the gutter hold rockfish when visibility clears and current sets tidy.
- They're a close-range bonus and respond to vertical work tight to the relief.

## Fishing rhythm — how the window opens and how it dies

When the window builds it happens fast. Birds drop, bait compresses into a shallow ribbon, and sonar traces go from diffuse marks to tight bands. The bites often arrive concentrated in short bursts.

Pressure sensitivity: the fish get wary. A steady stream of boats pushes bait to the surface, the seam softens into a wash, and the bite dials down. The best work is usually early — before the fleet turns up — or late in the tide when compressing action reappears and boats thin out.

Micro-observation: when birds hold direction instead of scattering and sonar shows a narrow ribbon hugging the shallow side, the window is building. If those signals vanish when you cross the line, the edge is probably too blurred to work.

## How the fleet treats this spot — copy what works

Local boats treat the south jetty as the first realistic stop when the bar is questionable. On marginal bar days effort compresses here: short hauls, quick checks, and a lot of radio calls. On good bar days the fleet splits — some commit to the shelf, others grind the jetty seam until birds or sonar force a move.

Experienced-boat behavior to copy:
- Grid the seam from the jetty out a few miles rather than anchoring on one GPS point.
- Use neighbor sonar marks as one more data point, not gospel.
- Leave early and quiet to avoid spooking shallow bait.

## Positioning and rigs that make sense here

Positioning: run the jetty face and work the warm side of the plume. Hold a drift line that keeps baits upstream of the bait band so fish see the presentation as it moves through their lane.

Gear notes:
- Chinook: downrigger-set flashers with whole herring or large hoochies; deep-diving plugs on falling/incoming tides when fish show deeper targets.
- Coho: shallower spoons and smaller-profile hoochies; cleaner, faster presentations to trigger a chase.
- Rockfish/vertical work: short vertical jigs and bait rigs tight to gutter rock; heavier leaders and hookup tactics for toothy fish.

Tactical rule: if sonar shows fish shallow in the 10–40 ft range, shorten the rig and speed up presentation. If fish are deeper, go downrigger or deeper plugs.

## Quick signals the window is building (scan for these)

- birds lower and hold a direction instead of scattering
- sonar shows tight bait marks hugging the shallow side
- a clean color line that doesn't diffuse when you cross it
- traffic thins briefly as boats commit or leave

## Common mistakes and corrections

- Mistake: fishing the stained center. Correction: work the clean side where bait concentrates, not the middle of the plume.
- Mistake: anchoring on one patch. Correction: grid the seam and move after two or three fruitless stops — this tight water changes quickly.
- Mistake: committing long with no bar window. Correction: treat the jetty as the fallback; confirm a safe return before burning fuel chasing birds farther out.

## When to treat it as a short‑day play

This spot is a practical first-stop and a reliable short-day option when the bar won't let you reach the shelf. Fish here reward careful drift angles, quiet work, and decisive movement more than noisy, desperate casts. If the seam shows and bait is tight, grind it while it's honest — if not, move on before pressure and time collapse the window.

Watch the water. Read the bait. Move decisively. The south jetty gutter fishes like a small system — tight, fast, and very pressure-sensitive.
