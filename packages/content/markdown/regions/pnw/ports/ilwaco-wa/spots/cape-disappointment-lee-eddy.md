Cape Disappointment is a small, reliable organizer in a messy mouth. It doesn’t make a canyon or a big bank — it peels the Columbia plume, sheds a lee pocket and spits out a short, clean seam. When tides and wind line up that seam is a narrow ribbon of cleaner, warmer water packed with bait and ready for staging salmon or tight rockfish work. When they don’t, it’s a green mess that gives nothing away.

Anytime you run from Ilwaco and want a quick shot without committing to a long shelf day, this is the first useful piece of water you’ll hit. Treat it like a staging lane: short-lived, pressure‑sensitive, and all about reading the ribbon and the birds.

## Why the point peels the plume and makes a seam

Observation: plume water wraps around the headland and shears against the shelf flow. You’ll see a color line and often a temperature difference. The point forces the currents to split and a clockwise lee eddy forms downstream.

Explanation: that peel concentrates cleaner, slightly warmer water into a narrow lane. Bait prefers the warm flank and the eddy hangs schools against nearby rock and sand fingers. Small downhill pulses over relief turn that bait into predictable ambush zones for rockfish and give salmon simple transit lines to intercept.

Read it as geography plus tidal momentum: the headland sets the geometry; tides and wind decide whether the geometry produces a tidy ribbon or a fraying mess.

## Reading the lee ribbon on any tide

Observation first: the seam looks like a blue ribbon tucked against greener or tannic water. Birds line up on it. Sonar shows narrow bait streaks. When birds work it, the place flips on.

How tides and wind change the ribbon:
- Incoming tide with light offshore wind usually gives the cleanest, narrow seam. The lee will hold warmer water and concentrate bait.
- Outgoing tide often shears the seam offshore quickly or turns the lee into turbulence if swell stacks with current.
- Strong upwelling or persistent NW wind pushes cold green water into the mouth; the headland then becomes a contrast point where occasional warm pockets ride over colder tongues — those pockets are the spots worth hunting.

Practical read: if the color/temperature contrast is distinct and the birds are purposeful, assume there’s a short window of tidy water along the warm flank. If the whole area is uniformly green or tannic and birds are scattered, move on.

## Bait, birds and what you’ll see on sonar

This is bait-driven water. Small schooling forage collects on the warm side of the seam and on downhill seams off the point.

Typical observations:
- Surface forage like smelt and young herring sits near the surface on the warm flank; anchovy and juvenile sand lance tuck into cleaner fingers closer to the lee.
- Birds will sit tight on the ribbon and make quick, repeated dives when the bait is active.
- Porpoise will often run single-file along the lane; foamers and darting surface signs are a clear trigger.
- Sonar shows narrow marks hugging the slope, usually 10–30 ft when the thermocline is shallow.

If you see a ribbon with birds working and sonar marks streaking up, the bite is building. If the marks are smeared or bait is scattered, the seam is either breaking or under pressure.

## When the lane favors salmon versus rockfish

Salmon: Chinook stage on the outer edges of the plume and will hold on the seam into the lee; coho show later and like cleaner, tidier seams. Early runs favor downriggers and bigger plugs on the seam; when birds show and the surface turns on, switch to flasher/herring.

Rockfish and nearshore predators: small kelp and rock fingers and short ledges off the lee hold rockfish when current pushes downhill into cleaner water. Vertical work and short drifts over the downhill patches produce the best tight-structure marks.

Timing note: the lee fishes best early in the window — before the fleet stamps on the seam or the tide shreds it.

## How the fleet typically works the seam

This is a short-haul play from Ilwaco. Boats usually check the bar, run to the mouth and either make a short pass across the lee seam or spread east–west along the clean ribbon.

Typical approaches you’ll see:
- Short stop and test: run to the seam, slow troll a couple passes with smaller spoons, hoochies or small flasher setups. If birds and sonar agree, go to downriggers or larger plugs for Chinook.
- Isolation and vertical work: when bait is tight and birds are working, boats will hold a tight grid along the seam and fish vertically over downhill patches.
- Split strategy under pressure: because the seam collapses fast with fleet pressure, good boats split off to less obvious downhill fingers or wait for slack to rebuild the seam.

Fleet behavior matters. If you roll into a crowded seam the fish often sink or push deeper. Split up, slow the drift, or find the adjacent downhill fingers instead of stacking on the same line.

## Tactical positioning and gear choices

Observation first: fish hang on the warm flank and sprint across into the lee to grab bait. Your position and gear should match that transit.

Positioning:
- Set up just outside the clean edge on the warm side of the seam. You want fish to run into your baits as they chase bait into the lee.
- Keep an exit lane and confirm the bar window before committing the fleet — the return can change fast.

Gear and presentation:
- Chinook: start with downriggers and big plugs on the seam early. Move to flasher/herring when birds are actively surface-feeding.
- Coho and slick surface fish: use smaller spoons, hoochies or fast, shallow trolling when birds are tight and fish are breaking.
- Rockfish: anchor short drifts over downhill rock fingers on the lee side; vertical jigs and bait close to the face on a mild flood work best.

Boat handling:
- Slow controlled passes beat frantic running. The seam rewards patience and accurate placement.
- If the seam slides offshore on an outgoing tide, don’t chase it past reasonable range — grid-search adjacent downhill fingers instead.

## Signs the window is opening, holding or closing

Watch these signals; they tell you whether to commit or move on.

Signs the window is building:
- Birds tighten to a narrow ribbon and make repeated dives.
- Sonar shows bait hugging the seam and marks streak upward.
- Current over downhill patches holds direction instead of swirling.
- The color line is distinct and persistent for 15–30 minutes.

Signs it’s fading or under pressure:
- Schools sink below sonar range when too many boats stack the seam.
- Foamers break and run off the seam east or west — the bait has moved, follow bait, not noise.
- The seam turns into a wide, tannic mess with scattered birds.
- Heavy upwelling or a strong outgoing tide tears the seam offshore.

Common pressure reactions to expect: fish drop deeper, foamers scatter, or bait gets pushed off downhill fingers. If that happens, slow down the spread, split the fleet, or give it time to reset.

## When to leave and use the proximity

Cape Disappointment’s value is its proximity to Ilwaco. Use short, disciplined checks: a quick diagnostic pass will tell you if the seam is working. If the bar report or weather shortens your return window, or the seam is a mile‑wide mess with no purposeful birds, don’t get stuck.

The headland gives tidy bites when pieces line up: a visible ribbon, purposeful birds, bait tight to structure and a favorable tide. Read the ribbon, read the birds, respect the tide — then commit or move on.
