The trough is the quiet half‑mile lane that lives between the chaos of the Columbia bar and the first real shelf drop outside Ilwaco. It isn’t a single canyon on the chart but a working seam: scalloped sand flats, shallow gutters, short downhill lips and the odd cobble finger where bait piles when the river plume thins. When the bar allows a calm transit this line is the first place the moving bait settles—and where halibut habitually hang on the edges.

## Where bait compresses: sand pockets, gutters, and lips

You’ll see bait first in the shallow pockets and low spots. The pattern is consistent: bait rides off the bar, rides over the trough, and then piles where the flat dips into a gutter or where harder bottom forces a funnel. The halibut hold on the lip more often than on the flat—watch the transition from sand to drop, not the middle of the flat.

If you picture the trough as a ribbon, the productive bits are the scallops and the holes cut into that ribbon, not the flat expanse between them.

## Tidal exchange and the useful window

Tidal flow through the jetties drives the whole show. Strong ebb will churn the pockets apart and scatter bait; incoming into slack is the window. Two practical rules you learn quick:

- Incoming to slack compresses bait. The trough calms and the sand pockets hold.
- Outgoing with swell through the bar breaks the pattern. Marks lift, bait spreads, and boats fight to keep rigs off the sand.

A clean, calm transit across the bar is the gatekeeper. If the bar is noisy, the trough will be noisy and the fish won’t hold where everything’s stirred.

## Water shear and visible boundaries

The river plume often peels across the trough. When clearer offshore water rides over stained plume water you get a shear—a visible color break. Bait piles on the cleaner side of that shear. On sonar that often looks like a tight green band over tannic water; on the surface it can show as a thin line or stacked color.

Those shears are more reliable than single sonar specks. If you can see a color/temperature change across the trough, start working the edge of it.

## What bait and birds tell you

Typical forage here is small herring and smelt with the occasional anchovy or sand‑lance. They collect in low spots and around any small hard patch. Birds are useful but intermittent: scattered gulls aren’t a guarantee, but concentrated diving—terns or loons punching down—usually means a tight bait ball worth checking.

Don’t treat the trough like a pelagic road. It’s a mixed‑bottom, bottom‑oriented holding area. You won’t often see albacore or surface tuna here; the fishery is halibut first, occasional lingcod and rockfish second where cobble fingers exist.

## How the fleet runs it

When the bar is marginal the trough becomes the compromise. It’s close enough to be safe and deep enough to hold halibut without the 20–60 nm run to the shelf. Fleet behavior you’ll recognize:

- Short‑run play: boats set timed, contour drifts along the trough rather than long anchor‑style drifts.
- Frequent corrections: short hops and motor‑backs to keep baits just off bottom.
- Pressure handling: when the trough is tight with boats, good skippers scatter, slow down and isolate pockets. Parking on the same line pushes fish deeper or spooks surface bait.

If you’re in a crowded trough, don’t try to out‑anchor everyone—move to the next pocket and work its lips.

## Drift setup and presentation for halibut

Positioning is simple in principle: find the pocket‑to‑gutter transition and drift the lip. In practice that means running parallel to the trough, making short deliberate hops across the drop and keeping the bait just off the bottom.

Recommended presentation from this spot (what experienced skippers use):

- Bait: whole herring or large squid strips.
- Distance off bottom: 6–10 feet—halibut sit on the lip and charge up.
- Sinker: 6–10 oz depending on current. Short scope on anything faster.
- Drift control: on light incoming you can let the boat slide. On anything with push shorten scope and motor‑trim often; better to hop than let rigs bury.

Electronics matter: look for dense near‑bottom arches and slow them down. A clean, tight sonar band over tannic water is often where bait compresses and where halibut sit low.

Timing-wise, aim for late incoming into slack or the first slack after a morning fog lift. That’s when bait tightens and you’ll see the most consistent takes.

## When it’s working and when it’s dead

Working signs:
- Bait tightens into discrete lumps on sonar hugging the bottom.
- Water shows a clear color/temperature shear across the trough.
- Birds concentrate and dive rather than loafing.
- Bar report allows a calm transit.

Dead signs:
- Outgoing tide with swell through the bar—bait scattered and marks lift.
- Sonar specks that are wide and transient rather than compact and near‑bottom.
- Surface chop that makes keeping a controlled drift impossible.

Micro‑observation: halibut almost always live on the lip. If your bait is sitting flat and buried you’ll see a lot of misses.

## Signs the window is building (quick reference)

- Sonar: bait compresses into a few dense clumps near bottom.
- Surface: distinct color shear in the trough.
- Birds: concentrated diving activity.
- Logistics: a safe, calm bar window.

Those four are better indicators than a single sonar arc or a lone diving gull.

## Common mistakes and what to do instead

- Mistake: Parking on the middle of a sand flat. Do this instead: move off the center and work lips and gutters—fish rarely take deeply buried baits.
- Mistake: Excess scope on a fast tide. Do this instead: shorten scope, motor‑trim, and make frequent short hops so rigs don’t bury and tangle.
- Mistake: Chasing a bird ball into rough bar conditions. Do this instead: confirm a safe bar window first. One bird ball isn’t worth a dangerous run.

Experienced crews prioritize drift control and preserving the window over getting greedy with bait piles.

## What usually matters more than exact spots

- Tide timing over an exact GPS point.
- Bait compression over isolated sonar specks.
- A clean transit window over a tempting long‑haul report.

The bar‑to‑shelf trough is a predictable first‑mile halibut strategy from Ilwaco: not glamorous, not huge, but steady when the tide lines up and the bar gives a safe window. Keep the drift tight, watch the lips, and don’t fish the flat. Respect the bar.
