## Where the pinch sits and why you run there

You don't find a long reef out here. The pinch is a scatter — low hummocks, short ledges, and sand gutters where the broad Westport shelf narrows and the contour drops. You'll run through sand and kelp patches, then hit a tighter band of mixed hard bottom: knobs and little ridges separated by sand ramps. The pattern repeats for a few miles, close enough to work from Westport without burning fuel on a long run.

I treat it like readable ground. The hummocks are small, but they organize moving water. That organization is what stacks bait and makes fishing predictable when the conditions line up.

## How moving water uses those hummocks

Observation first: when the pinch is doing something you can see a line of foam, debris, or a color change where the water peels around the knobs. On tidally active days small eddies and squeezes show up more clearly than any long steady current.

Why it matters: the hummocks force bait to the downhill faces. Tidal pushes, eddies, and any northward drift of Columbia plume material will peel around the knobs and create downhill seams. Bait compresses there, predators orient, and a bite window opens. If a temperature edge stalls on the pinch — even a 1°–2°F step — it’ll often lock birds and bait on the hummocks and turn incidental bottom marks into a working bite.

Note on clarity: color changes fast here. Inside the bar it's green and murky; a few miles seaward you can hit cleaner blue water. That jump in color usually brings different strike behavior: slower, selective takes in blue water; louder, faster strikes in green.

## Bait patterns and the life they draw

What you’ll see first is bait in the lee of knobs — sand lance and small schooling forage stacking in pockets between hummocks. Sonar shows tight bait marks hugging the downhill sides; birds and porpoises will work small slicks and porpoise when bait’s being chased.

How predators use it: rockfish and lingcod stack under the bait, halibut sit on adjacent sand ramps and take big, slow baits. Tuna and salmon only show up when a warm filament or thermal fringe rides the contour and concentrates surface bait.

Watch for these signals — they tell you the patch is readable:
- Tight bait marks compressing against a downhill face.
- Birds working isolated slicks near the hummocks.
- Marks that streak upward off the bottom instead of a flat clutter line.

## Signs the window is building (read these like a checklist)

Start with sonar and the birds. Bait growing taller and tighter against a hummock, circling birds, and a consistent color break running parallel to the contour are the early signs. Also watch how marks behave — if they lift off the bottom in streaks rather than sit as a flat clutter, be ready.

Short list to scan quickly:
- Growing, tight bait marks on sonar.
- Birds holding a hummock or slick for 20+ minutes.
- A temp or color break aligned with the contour.
- Repeating vertical marks on subsequent passes.

When those align, the fleet tightens up and the hummocks fish like a small ledge.

## How the spot behaves when it’s actually working

Observation: the hump tells you before the fish do. Bait compresses into vertical marks along the downhill face; birds start circling; what was a mottled bottom clutter becomes lift and streaks. Boats narrow their arcs and hold station on the hummocks.

If it dies, it dies fast. Bait disperses, birds leave first, sonar shows smaller scattered marks, and the positive, noisy strikes turn into quiet, buried fish. Pressure accelerates the shut-down here; a packed fleet will push fish deeper and make what was a steady bite intermittent.

## How I fish the downhill seam — positioning and presentations

Position matters more than the exact jig. The goal is to get your bait or lure to fall through the seam where the bait and predators are stacking.

Read the downhill first. Anchor or short‑drag drift on the downhill side of a hummock so your presentation falls down the lee flank. If the tide is strong, shorten drifts and add weight; if slack, use lighter jigs and hold the lure in the zone.

Vertical jigging: heavy, blunt‑profile jigs that fall fast, then held in the strike zone with short, sharp lifts, work for lingcod and rockfish. Change cadence — fast snaps to trigger reaction strikes, then slower falls into holes to catch follows.

Halibut and big rockfish: use live or chunk bait on stout leaders and present low in the column near the sand/ledge transition. Halibut often sit on the sand a boat‑length off the reef; when you find halibut marks slow everything down — big slow baits, controlled bottom contact. Quick pops and heavy drag tend to lose the bite.

Repeat the pass. First bites usually mean fish will hold a hummock while bait remains. Circle back and tighten your pattern rather than moving off after one hookup.

Use sonar as a guide. Differentiate bait from structure by watching movement — bait compresses and lifts, fixed rock returns steady echoes. When you see temporary streaks off the face, be ready to pitch or drop into that lift.

## When to stay and work the pinch — and when to move off

Stay and grind when bait holds tight against several hummocks, birds work the same slick for 20+ minutes, and sonar shows repeating vertical marks. Halibut flags or light keening marks off the nearby sand ramp are another good reason to sit and work the seam.

Move off when the main signal is a crisp temperature break running seaward of the pinch, or when birds and bait start working a moving front beyond the hummocks. If sonar shifts to large thin bait layers away from the knobs, widen your search. In short: follow the bait and the edge, not the GPS point on a hummock.

Quick pointers:
- Stay: stacked bait, persistent birds, repeatable vertical marks.
- Move: clear edge running past the pinch, birds working beyond the hummocks, bait layers offshore of the ledges.

## How pressure changes the picture

The first boats on a hump usually light it up. By mid‑morning the same hummock often tightens and only small bait remains. Foamers or fast surface activity that appears after repeated passes often shuts down quickly under pressure.

When a temperature edge appears, the dynamic changes — boats will run wide arcs instead of stacking on a single knob. Experienced guys follow the edge, not the guy tied to a hump. In short: pressure makes bites shorter and pushes productive water onto adjacent seams or the outer edge.

## Fleet behavior I watch for

Locals treat the pinch as first‑stop water on a blue‑water day and fallback water on marginal days. Early you’ll see methodical arcs along the contour; when something holds, the fleet tightens on any persistent bird or bait activity.

Use the noise as a signal, not a directive. If the pack stacks on a hummock and the bait still holds, fine — join the pattern. If they tie up and the edge is moving, run the edge. The boats that keep moving with the water usually catch the day’s transition from structure work to blue‑water fishing.

## Micro observations that pay off

Small things matter: the hummocks that hold bait at slack tide will often still hold fish a couple hours after the tide turns if the bait hangs. A single isolated sonar mark with no birds is rarely worth the commitment; a temperature filament or repeating bait lift is.

Final thought: fish here are organized by moving water more than by the rock itself. Run parallel to the contour, watch how bait stacks and moves, and set your drifts to let your presentation fall into the seam. If bait moves off the knobs and a temperature edge rides by, widen the search and follow that edge — that’s where the day usually goes from local structure work to blue water.
