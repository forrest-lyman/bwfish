What makes this water repeatable is geometry and timing. A broad sand‑and‑mud shelf peels off the harbor throat into short flats cut by scour channels and a narrow tidal neck. Those parts—sand fingers, the neck, and scattered rock hummocks—set the pattern. When the pieces line up the flats hold halibut and put rockfish on tight pockets; when they don’t, the same ground fishes dead.

## Why the flats are a predictable fallback

Start with the map, then watch the tide. The inner flats are the first workable ground inside the bar: shallow sand and mud with deeper cuts and a constricted tidal neck. That layout forces bait and current into predictable lanes. The system is small and responsive — a clean incoming tide stacks bait along the gutters and the lee of sand tongues; a hard ebb clears it quickly. Because the geometry is simple, you can see the signal (bait, color, sweep) and act on it faster than you can on the outer shelf.

Observation first: when the tide angle is steady and a cleaner tongue pushes into the harbor, the flats will produce for hours. Explanation second: the neck concentrates forage into the cuts and the sand tongues create sheltered feeding edges where halibut and rockfish can sit with minimal effort.

## How the tide‑neck moves bait and makes the bite

The neck behaves like a conveyor on the incoming and a vacuum on the outgoing.

On the flood the mouth peels bait off the launch gut, piles it in the lee of sand fingers, then funnels it into the deeper cuts. That piling is what creates the holding lanes for halibut and gives rockfish concentrated food near hummocks. On the ebb the same throat strips bait seaward and can turn a tidy drift into a blank in short order.

Practical note: the bite often begins when the tide softens just enough for bait to stack in the lee of a tongue. That stacked bait stays longer than you’d expect and is the easiest place to find consistent hooks.

## Color line — the real gatekeeper

The change between stained harbor water and cleaner coastal water matters more than depth here. A cleaner tongue pushing into the harbor compresses forage along the edge of the neck and down the gutters. That color pinch — even a slight temp uptick and a visible surface line — turns bait presence into fish.

You’ll see the difference on the sounder and at the surface. If the cleaner band is pushing in during a steady incoming tide, the flats will light up; if the harbor stain thickens and the tongue retreats, the flats usually go quiet long before the shelf reads anything useful.

## Bait, birds, and structure life

Patterns observed here differ from the shelf.

- Forage: small schooling bait rides through the neck, sand‑anchovy and sandflea patches concentrate in gutters, and occasional shrimp wracks show up. Enough food to keep halibut on the flats and rockfish tight to rock pockets.
- Birds: less dramatic than the shelf. You’ll see terns or surface birds mark concentrated surface bait during strong tide pulses, but bird work is not a consistent locator here.
- Structure: rockfish live on hummocks and any exposed cobble near the cuts. They respond to vertical presentations and will show clearly on the sounder when the drift is tidy.

Read the sounder: stacked marks near the channel mouth during an incoming tide are the clearest sign the system is working.

## How the fleet uses the flats

This is the “first option” when the bar is iffy.

Boats stage at slack, slip out the launch gut and run 5–20 minutes to the nearest sand fingers. When the shelf is closed, pressure concentrates on the neck and local knowledge wins — experienced skippers pick different gutters and keep moving until a drift hooks up. Short windows get shortened more by pressure than by tide; expect quick radio calls like “neck clean, bait stacked on the 8‑fathom cut” and be ready to reposition.

The advantage here is repeatability and shelter. You can work multiple known cuts in an hour and still be close to the launch if the bar or weather turns.

## Setting the drift — what actually works

This is tide fishing more than gear fishing. Read the drift before you commit to a presentation.

### Drift setup
Run upcurrent and set so the boat sweeps down the face of a sand finger into the deeper cut. Favor longer, cleaner drifts that let baits pass across the mouth of the channel rather than short noisy turns. Use modest slip so line angle stays natural; halibut on these flats prefer a slow, natural bait rather than jerky motion.

### Where to place the boat
On the incoming tide stage downhill of the hummock so your drift crosses the face into the cut. On the ebb invert that: start just inside the gutter and work outward as the sweep drags bait past the face. If you see stacked bait on the sounder ahead, make one clean uphill pass into it instead of running multiple short passes that spook the feed.

### Presentations and reading response
Slow, heavy baits on the bottom near the channel mouth are baseline for halibut. For rockfish, vertical jigs and slowfalls over the hump get consistent reactions — they’ll hit on the drop and when you hold the jig in the column. Change presentation immediately when water color or drift angle shifts; what worked in pea‑green harbor water rarely holds once a clean tongue slides into the neck.

## Signs the window is building and when it dies

Signs the window is building:
- a clean tongue or lighter band pushing into the harbor
- marks on the sounder stacked near the channel mouth
- bait showing on the surface inside the neck during incoming tide
- a steady sweep rather than a roiled, broken current

When it dies:
- a hard ebb that strips the neck clean
- a fast, wind‑chopped inside with the bar closed
- the harbor stain deepening and the cleaner tongue retreating

The flats can look good but be dead if bait was shoved seaward overnight. Watch the color and the sweep before you commit a full day.

## Common mistakes and quick fixes

Common mistakes are largely avoidable if you read the pattern.

- Fishing across the seam instead of along it. Fish orient to the channel mouth and face; move your presentation along that line.
- Staying on one gut after the bite collapses. The neck is short — run the next nearby finger rather than double‑back.
- Ignoring inside color. If the harbor stain deepens, pack up before you waste daylight.

Quick fixes: when the drift softens, change moving speed, switch from slow fall to vertical presentation over hummocks, or move to the next finger. If pressure kills a drift, don’t sit; reposition and run another known cut.

## A practical short‑day plan

If you leave Westport with one inside plan, make it this: watch the launch gut, pick a tidy incoming tide, run the nearest sand finger, and read the color. Start downhill of a hummock, set a clean sweep into the cut, and look for stacked marks. If the clean tongue is pushing in and bait is stacking, settle into longer drifts and present slow, heavy baits on the bottom. If the tongue retreats or the ebb pulls hard, pull the anchor and run another finger or head for shelter.

This water rewards attention to small signals — color, stack, and sweep. Treat the neck like a gating mechanism: when it feeds, fish show; when it doesn’t, move.
