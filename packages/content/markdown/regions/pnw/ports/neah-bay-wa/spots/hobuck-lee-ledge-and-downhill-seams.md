Hobuck is a small, tactical piece of water. It sits on the lee side of the western approach into Neah Bay — a modest point, scattered rock and kelp, then a quick step into sand pockets and short downhill ledges. It’s not a canyon or a big bank; it’s a place where the current peels around structure and the tide angle lines up the whole food chain. When it’s working you get two fisheries in one drift: halibut on the sand margins and vertical rockfish/lingcod on the face.

Why Hobuck organizes bait the way it does

The bottom is a patchwork: ribs of rock and kelp fingers that drop into sand gutters and small troughs. Current moving across those ribs peels bait off the rocks and pins it on the downhill sand. That peel is the organizing action — not a single seam the size of a bank, but a bunch of predictable downhill seams where bait collects and predators follow.

How tide angle runs the show

Tide angle is the primary signal here. I watch how the tide rounds the point more than absolute speed.

- Incoming that rounds the point and sweeps across the ledge tends to compress bait on the downhill margins. That’s the classic halibut setup.
- Outgoing will pull bait into gutters and pockets; on ebb you often find fish sitting in the tails of sand pockets rather than out on the open downslope.
- Around larger rocks the current will form short, firm eddies — micro‑hangs. Those little hang spots are where rockfish and lingcod settle when the seam is present.

If the wind and swell align with the tide the seam cleans up and the bite is obvious. Cross‑shelf wind or a sloppy swell scatters bait and pushes predators tight to the rock where only vertical work will pay.

What you see on the sounder and at the surface

When bait is present the sounder shows a layered column: tight marks hugging the reef, a faint smear over the sand, and occasional surface birds if the bait gets shoved shallow. Expect sardine‑sized schools and thinner forage first along the face; they get pushed out into the sand fingers as the tide peels them off.

The most useful signs are how concentrated the bait is and how steady the push remains for 30–60 minutes. Birds working in short, tight knots are a better signal here than scattered flocks.

Where the fish actually stack

Halibut: They hold on the sand margins and in the downslope hollows where rock gives way to sand. The edge is their highway — fish will sit at the transition and feed into the seam.

Rockfish and lingcod: These are tight to rock and kelp necks. They live on the vertical and respond to accurate drops. When the seam is on, they’ll wait in the micro‑hangs and ambush the bait as it peels past.

Occasional small salmon will pass through if a bait lane lines up with the headland seam, but Hobuck isn’t a primary salmon staging lane. Treat salmon as a secondary chance, not the target.

Signs the window is building

- Consistent bait marks stacked along the downhill seam.  
- Birds working in short, concentrated knots.  
- A steady tide push that holds direction for 30–60 minutes.  
- Clear separation on the sounder between rock marks and sand marks.

When the window arrives and when it collapses

The spot turns when tide, bait, and sea state line up. Best runs are moderate tides with light wind: enough push to pin bait without blowing it around. When that happens the seam reads clean on the sounder and a controlled drift keeps baits in the feeding lane.

It dies quickly with pressure and rough water. A crowd of boats will shear the bait; the first boat on the seam usually gets the best shots. Strong crosswinds or a heavy swell will collapse sight feeding and push predators tight to the rock. Fog hides surface cues — then it’s sounder work and steady transects.

Common mistakes I see

- Anchoring on the face with a hard tide running. You’ll drag and miss the downhill hang. Hobuck rewards drifts and controlled pass‑work.  
- Running wide, aimless searches instead of parallel transects. The seam is narrow; methodical passes find it.  
- Treating a single bait mark as a guarantee. Bait piles can vanish inside a tide change—confirm direction and persistence before committing.

Tactics that actually work here

Halibut: Run a controlled drift that keeps baits just off the downhill edge. Shorten scope or add a light kellet in firmer current so the bait sits rather than flies. Fish the turn and the first half of the push — that’s when bait is most compressed.

Rockfish and lingcod: Put the boat on the mark and make precise vertical drops. Move between marks fast; a handful of accurate drops trumps a long, sloppy search. When birds are working and the water’s clean, shorten the spread and keep lures higher for reaction strikes but be ready to switch to bait and deeper presentations quickly.

Practical run plan

- Confirm tide angle before you burn fuel — a clean push across the point beats a stronger but chaotic tide.  
- Run parallel transects across the ledge to map bait density before committing.  
- Commit to a drift setup once bait and birds confirm the seam; expect short windows and be prepared to leave when the bait breaks up.  
- Don’t anchor unless you’re set up for vertical work on the rocks and the tidal run allows it.

Micro‑observation

Halibut sit where rock drops into sand. Rockfish sit on the face. The tide decides which group shows up first. Watch the peel, follow the bait, and run the seam until it quits.
