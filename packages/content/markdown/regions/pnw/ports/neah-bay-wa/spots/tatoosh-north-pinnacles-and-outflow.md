Why the north flank matters
Tatoosh’s north side is one of those short, intense pieces of water you hit leaving Neah Bay toward Cape Flattery. You’ve got a string of shallow-to-mid pinnacles, kelp fingers and sand gutters that drop quick from the island into mixed rock-and-sand flats. That geometry plus the headland hydraulics creates tight seams and an outflow tongue off the island that routinely compresses bait into small, targetable patches. When tide and bait line up, everything gets condensed: vertical structure for rockfish and lingcod, short downhill sand pockets for halibut, and a narrow staging lane for Chinook and Coho.

Reading the tide and outflow
Observation first: the fish show up with the current pattern, not the other way around. The north pinnacles live on the downstream side of little eddies, peel zones and flow seams.

- On a turning tide you’ll see an outflow tongue peel bait into the lee of the pinnacles. That tongue is the line you want.
- A steady flood sharpens the seam on the upstream faces; ebb moves bait back into the gutters and sand holes.
- Wind or swell cross to the shelf smears the edge — on clean, tide‑aligned days everything compresses; on wind‑against‑tide days the marks go dirty and quiet.

Watch the sounder and the birds. The mark you want is a tight cloud pushed up against a distinct face with a clean sand gutter beside it. In fog the birds and your sounder do the sighting for you.

How bait and predators organize
You’ll find sand lance, small herring and other juvenile forage stacked in the lee pockets and downstream sides of the pinnacles. Birds feather low, porpoise quarter tight, and often the birds arrive before the boat marks look textbook.

What changes when it’s working:
- bait condensed on the upstream face or in the outflow tongue
- birds tracking the same line and holding direction
- marks holding on the faces instead of just milling in open water

How each fishery plays here
The same piece of rock will give you three different fisheries through a tide turn. Think of the spot as a short-run, tide-sensitive neighbourhood — position and timing matter more than brute effort.

Rockfish and lingcod
Observation: rockfish and lingcod live tight to the ledges and edges. They want short, accurate presentations.
Tactics: short, precise vertical drops along the face. Move fast between marks; several well-placed drops beat a long, unfocused search. Fish are most active on the tide that produces current along the face — that seam pushes bait past ambush points.

Salmon (staging and short troll lanes)
Observation: salmon stage where the outflow tongue peels into a drop. They show as a midwater band when bait funnels along the edge.
Tactics: when birds and sonar line up, shorten your troll spreads, keep lures shallow, and be ready to switch to bait. In strong rips go deeper with smaller baits and hold the current column on the downstream side of the seam.

Halibut and sand pockets
Observation: halibut sit on the downhill sand next to rock. They favour the lip where the rock drops to sand.
Tactics: controlled drifts or short anchors that keep baits on the bottom along the downhill lip. Bites tend to favour tide changes — the first sweep of new current or the turning tide will wake them up.

Signs the window is building
You don’t need every signal to commit. A couple of these in sequence is usually enough:
- a narrow band of birds holding direction instead of just circling
- sonar showing a tight bait cloud on a face with a clear sand gutter beside it
- steady current across the pinnacles that’s fishable but not violent
- porpoise quartering the same line as the birds

Fleet behavior and pressure
Locals treat Tatoosh like a first‑mile checkpoint. On the good tides a couple of boats will sit a single pinnacle for long stretches. That pressure collapses the easy bites fast.

Common pressure reactions to watch for:
- schools sinking deeper and refusing surface work
- foamers burning out after the first couple of casts
- fish sliding to the far side of the pinnacle out of range of most vertical presentations
- halibut tightening to the smaller sand pockets and moving off the obvious drift lanes

If you see that, don’t grind the same hole — move a mile and find the same seam pattern. Fish tend to slide along the contour rather than vanish.

Boat handling and practical tactics
Pick the tide first. If you can’t make the headland run and fish the turning tide through Cape Flattery, don’t commit — the first mile out often decides the day.

Set up to the current. Anchor or drift so your spread and drops live in the current column or along the downhill lip. On rock use short, accurate vertical work; on salmon shorten spreads and keep lures shallow when bait funnels. In strong rips shorten leads, go deeper and keep your gear tight to the column.

A few realistic cautions
The pinnacles are shallow in places and wrapped in kelp. Current flips hard around headlands — what looks like a slow seam can become a nasty race with little warning. Plan exits, be conservative on headland runs, and expect your drift to change faster than it did an hour ago.

Micro-observations that pay off
The outflow tongue usually shows before the bite. Watch the birds and the sounder — if the bait cloud collapses, the bite usually shuts off before the marks disappear. Small timing wins here: a short window of clean, compressed bait will produce; everything else is background.

What matters most on this spot
- current orientation and bait compression
- tide timing at the headland
- whether you can fish the first mile without getting pushed off the water

Tatoosh’s north pinnacles aren’t flashy. They’re close, changeable, and decisive when tide and bait line up. Fish the current, follow the bait, and let the seam tell you where to put the next drop.
