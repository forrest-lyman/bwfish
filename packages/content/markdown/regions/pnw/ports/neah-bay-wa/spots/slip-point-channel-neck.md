## Why Slip Point organizes migrating fish

Slip Point is a small piece of coastline that often does a lot of organizing. Where the channel narrows and current peels against a short headland face you get a tidy, short seam—bait shears off the point, compresses on an upstream face, and creates a conveyor for fish on the move. When that seam is clean the water presents bait in a narrow column; migrating Chinook and Coho, and predators that follow them, will stage there. It’s not a broad shelf gate—this is a tight, high-value lane that reads and dies quickly with changing tide and wind.

## The channel, bottom, and sightlines

The bottom is mixed: kelp and boulder fingers close to the point step quickly to ledges and sand gutters a few hundred yards seaward. You’re fishing a short neck of accelerating current—think a sharp hydraulic change, not a long, gentle edge. On clean days you can see birds and porpoise line up the seam; in fog the sounder becomes the primary tool.

This place generally supports one to three boats on most tides. That small footprint is what makes it decisive: a correct read puts fish on board quickly, a wrong read means wasting time.

## How the seam behaves

The seam is tide-driven and reactive. It builds fast on steady, tide‑aligned flow and collapses fast when wind or swell interferes.

Observations:
- Flood often peels bait into the headland face and creates a tidy upstream side; ebb can pull that same bait out into the channel depending on cross‑shelf flow.
- Direction of the dominant current (inbound vs outbound), whether wind aligns with the tide, and whether the upstream face stays tidy are the primary controls.
- Cross-wind or a northwest swell will shred the seam; bait disperses and the lane effectively collapses.

Micro-observation: a steady flood will assemble the bait band in minutes; a sudden cross-wind or building swell will unravel it just as quickly.

## Bait, marks, and immediate signals

This is a conveyor more than a holding pond—anchored herring, sand lance and small juvenile forage peel off the headland and stack on the upstream side or get pushed into the gutter. Watch the sounder closely; the bait tells you whether the seam is working.

Reliable signs the window is building:
- A soft, continuous bait band hugging the headland face on sonar.
- Birds working tight lines down-current toward the channel.
- Bait that holds direction instead of milling.
- Porpoise surfing the seam—short, immediate confirmation.

If birds and a consistent bait band line up, expect action within minutes. If the marks are messy or scattered, the fish usually won’t commit.

## How I fish it

Read the flow first, then set up to present in the compressed column. My choices change with target species and water clarity, but the principle is the same: put bait in the bite zone where the seam concentrates forage.

### Salmon (Chinook / Coho)
When the seam is tidy I favor a slow, directionally correct troll or mooching into the column where the bait compresses. Set your spread so lures or bait run down-current into that bait band rather than across it.

- Clean water: flashers plus bait or hootchies, sized to hang in the column.
- Dirty, tidal rips: shorten leaders, run smaller-profile baits and deeper rigs so baits sit in the strike zone without being blown off depth.
Why: salmon key on direction and presentation. If your spread isn’t running into the compressed bait, you’re giving the fish a poor intercept.

### Rockfish / vertical work
The boulder and kelp fingers close to the face respond to a shifting tide. I make precise vertical drops tight to the rock—short moves between marks rather than long searches. If bait compresses at the edge of kelp expect short, aggressive bites early and late in the tide.

Why: the relief is close; fish stack tight and bites are brief. Cover structure deliberately.

### Halibut notes
Slip Point isn’t a primary halibut lane, but sand pockets off the ledges can hold fish when the tide softens. Fish the downhill and holes adjacent to the seam on slack to incoming; anchor or set a drift after the current loses its edge.

## Spot behavior under pressure and weather

Productive when the seam pins a dense bait band against the point and upstream water stays clean. Dead when wind or swell smears the current and scatters bait—fish will still be present but won’t commit.

Pressure reaction is fast: one boat often converts the first few fish, the rest of the fleet stacks up and the window collapses. Expect schools to slide deeper and tighter to structure under pressure; foamers and bird activity will be briefer.

## What the local fleet does

Locals treat Slip Point as a tide-driven checkpoint. Typical behavior:
- Short search passes at varying speeds to locate the bait band on sonar.
- If bait and birds appear, boats set a localized trolling drift oriented to the current; when marks are tight and the current light, some will stop and mooch.
- Experienced operators avoid committing early in a cross-wind or on a rapidly building swell; they stage off and return on the next tidal pinch.

That pattern tells you a lot—if boats are passing quickly and none commit, the seam is likely marginal.

## Setting up the boat — practical steps

Read the current first. Then present bait relative to where the seam is compressing.

- Position so baits hang just upstream of the compressed layer for intercepts, or on the downhill edge when fishing structure targets.
- Salmon trolling: run a spread that lets you shorten and settle baits quickly when birds or marks tighten.
- Rockfish: make a few precise vertical drops next to identified structure; don’t waste time on long drifts.
- Halibut: wait for slack to incoming to anchor or set a drift; that’s when holes hold best.

These are decision points more than drills. If the sounder and surface activity confirm a tight bait band, commit and work it aggressively. If they don’t, move.

## When to skip it

Don’t waste time when the downstream current is dirty and the upstream side is thrashed by wind. If the seam is blown apart and bait is scattered, move off the point—sweep the outer ledges, pull east or west to the shelf gate, or find a quieter seam elsewhere. Slip Point only pays when the seam is tidy enough to compress bait visibly.

## Practical takeaway

Slip Point is a short, tide‑sensitive lane: small footprint, high payoff when it runs. Watch the bait band and the upstream face. Present baits into the compressed column, shorten and settle quickly when marks tighten, and be ready to move the moment the seam frays. When it lines up, work it hard; when it’s messy, there are better uses of time and fuel.
