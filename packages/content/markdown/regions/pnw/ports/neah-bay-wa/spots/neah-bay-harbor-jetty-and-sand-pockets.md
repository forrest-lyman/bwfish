This is first‑mile water. The ground is tight—jetty lee pockets, shallow approach channels, short downhill edges where rock fingers meet sand. When the bar and headland give you a clean tide window those small pieces of relief do the work: bait compresses into seams, halibut sit in sand holes beside rock, and rockfish/lingcod hug the fingers and kelp lines. It’s short runs, quick setups, and timing more than distance.

## Why the jetty lee and sand gutters actually hold fish
Observation first: you’ll see bait stacked on the seam, fish tight to the face, and halibut parked off the downhill shadow of rock. The lee of the jetty and the sand gutters are small, predictable places where prey and predator come together.

Explanation second: the jetty turns flow into a seam; gutters get scoured and force bait into depressions. Rock fingers interrupt that flow and create short downhill gradients—perfect spots for halibut to sit out of the main current while still being in range of passing food. Rockfish and lingcod use the fingers and kelp as cover while feeding on the compressed bait.

## Tide and headland hydraulics that run this strip
You’re fishing hydraulics, not open shelf. Narrow channels, jetties and headland curvature turn tidal flow into seams, eddies and short, strong pushes that flip with flood and ebb. Two practical points to watch:

- A steady push (clean flood or defined ebb) gives the best lee pockets: bait peels into the lee on a flood; a clear ebb will scour gutters and concentrate prey at downhill edges.
- Wind against the tide muddies and destroys the pockets fast. Calm or tide‑aligned wind keeps the seams clean and predictable on the sounder.

Those are the mechanics. Read the tide push and the headland rip first—if the hydraulics aren’t right, nothing else matters.

## Signs the bite will build
I watch a short list of signals and act only when more than one lines up:

- A tight band of bait on the sounder hugging the jetty or gutter.
- Birds loafing over the same patch and repeatedly returning.
- Current beginning to pin a direction instead of just milling.
- Bar report and run timing showing at least one clean in/out window.

If you get the bait and the current is pinning, you’ve got a real shot; isolated marks without those signals rarely hold.

## When the window opens and when to leave
This fishes on a narrow clock. The best moments are the tide turn and the first hour or two of an incoming when bait is being pushed into the lee and gutters are actively scoured. Slack by itself rarely produces—it's the change (slack into a steady push) that wakes these pieces up.

Conversely, wind or swell across the headland shuts it down. When the capes are up the first mile becomes noisy; the seams smear and the fish spread out or stop cooperating. If the bar timing doesn’t give you a clean in/out, don’t commit.

## Sounder signatures to trust
What you see on the fishfinder tells the story if you learn the shapes:

- Rockfish: tight marks hugging the ledge and kelp; short, sharp arches that track with the face.
- Halibut: slow, diffuse arches sitting off the face in sand holes or in the downhill shadow.
- Bait band on the seam: the green light. A concentrated bait line with current gives conversion; scattered single marks without bait seldom hold.

Trust the bait seam under current more than an isolated arch or a single bird.

## How I set the boat and present baits
Set the drift to the current and use the hydraulics, not force them. Practical calls that pay off:

- If bait and birds are concentrated, run a controlled drift oriented so baits pass the downhill edge and sand gutter cleanly—no dragging into structure.
- Halibut: position on the downhill side of the rock, keep baits on bottom, and fish the slack‑into‑incoming window. They tend to sit just off the rock in sand holes, not on top.
- Rockfish/lingcod: vertical drops tight to the face with quick relocations between marks. Those pockets go hot early and again around tide swings.
- Anchoring: short anchors or stern‑anchored drifts work if the current allows. Too much rode in a strong headland flow is a liability—your boat becomes a big passive attractor that pushes bait and fish off the seam.

These are positional decisions—where you put the boat changes which micro‑current and which edge your baits ride through.

## How pressure changes the behavior
This strip compresses fast. One boat can pull baits through a pocket and diffuse the bait layer; multiple boats usually push fish deeper or laterally into the next gutter rather than make them vanish. Watch your own marks and where your fish move; following the crowd often means chasing a ghost.

If you plan to work a tight patch, time your arrival to the push, set up clean, and avoid overfishing a single pocket. Move sooner than you think when the marks change.

## Practical reminders and safety items
- Read the bar and plan your in/out window before committing. This is first‑mile water only if you can get back across the approach safely.
- In fog, birds disappear but the hydraulics don’t. Trust your sonar and the current angle—bait on the sounder under a seam is the same valuable signal whether you can see birds or not.
- One bird strike isn’t a guarantee. Look for sustained bait and consistent current direction before you set everything up.

## Micro‑observation: how halibut use the relief
The bait holds in the lee. Halibut don’t sit on top of rock; they sit on the sand beside it, in the downhill shadow where a small scour keeps food passing. That’s why you want baits passing the downhill edge, not landing on the rock.

Why boats keep checking this strip
It’s accessible, repeatable, and time‑sensitive. On the right window you can make halibut drifts and vertical rockfish work without a long run. When the pieces line—clean tide push, bait stacked on the jetty seam, and the bar giving a safe in/out—the first mile outside Neah Bay is worth the trouble. When the capes are up, it isn’t.
