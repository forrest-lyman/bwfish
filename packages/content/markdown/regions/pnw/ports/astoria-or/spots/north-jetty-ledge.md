The North Jetty Ledge is a small, obvious patch of harder bottom at the mouth of the Columbia where river torque meets shore contours. It’s not a long reef or canyon—think a handful of pinnacles, a rubble slope and an adjacent sand flat. Fishermen keep coming back because the tide and current compress bait into predictable lines there.

## How the ledge looks on the water and chart
You’ll pick it out in two ways: chart detail and what the water tells you. On the plot it’s a faint texture change; on the water you’ll see seams and tide rips around the jetty and, when wind allows, a cleaner outside edge to the river plume. The jetty makes local eddies and shear; the ledge takes that moving water and concentrates bait where halibut and rockfish can feed.

Approach with the plan to drift the edge rather than plant the anchor on the obvious rock. The angle of tide matters—set up so your drift cuts across the working side of the rubble, not straight onto the pinnacles.

## Tide windows control the bite
This place is short and tidal. A steady flood or the first reliable push on a moderate tide is usually when halibut work the flats and rockfish move off the rubble to feed. On strong, chaotic bar-outflow days the whole zone gets muddied; the ledge still holds life, but bites are tough until the plume thins or a slack window appears.

Small changes in tide speed—half a knot—matter here. When the push softens into a steady run the bait settles along the slope and the marks go from smeared to tight. When that push dies, the bite often dies with it.

## Current patterns and where bait collects
The jetty bends the flow. Expect reversed seams, uphill faces on the lee side of pinnacles, and back-eddy pockets where bait shelves up. The river plume usually hugs the shoals; when the plume is thin or sits north you get a clear color and temperature break on the outside edge of the ledge. That short color break is where fish compress.

If a NW wind pushes the plume south, the ledge can sit inside the stain. Fish remain, but presentations and color choices become more important—your bait needs to read like the local forage in stained water.

## Bait and what feeds the system
This is a bottom-and-near-bottom place. Sand lance and smelt live near the flats, small juvenile hake and shrimp on the rubble. You won’t usually see massive surface blitzes; look for tight bait bands on the sounder or small slicks where the tide channels over the ledge. The food chain often begins as a thin band along the contour and expands outward; predators compress into that corridor.

Birds are secondary here. They show sometimes, but more reliable signs are what the sounder shows on the slope.

## How it fishes: where halibut and rockfish live
Halibut and rockfish use different parts of the feature at different tide phases.

Halibut
The halibut hang on the sand/gravel fringe adjacent to the rubble. The best action is where the sand funnels into harder bottom—the base of the slope. Fish that line will often bite as a steady flood pushes sand and bait across the flats toward the jetty. Treat the drift as the catch: run the same trough repeatedly until the mark goes quiet.

Rockfish
Rockfish sit on the pinnacles and rubble. When current cleans up across the pinnacles you’ll see marks rise off bottom on the sounder. That’s when short vertical presentations—jigs or bait dropped through the column—work best. Suspended marks mean precise drops; fish often live just off the structure on the working side.

This spot rewards repeated passes over the same line. Sonar will show the bait band; run that drift until it stops producing, then change angle or depth.

## Signs the window is building
Watch the sounder and the tide. The window is coming together when you see:
- tight bait marks hugging the slope;
- marks rising off the ledge as current cleans;
- repeat short taps on the same drift line;
- the tide move from chaotic run to a steady, usable push.

If those pieces line up, the next couple drifts matter more than any single anchor spot.

## Fleet behavior and pressure reactions
Local boats treat this as a short-day, reliable mark. On a good tidal window the fleet stacks quick. Experienced crews adjust depth, tighten a drift, or move a few hundred yards instead of sitting on the obvious waypoint. Word travels fast—verify the mark on your sounder before committing.

Pressure pushes fish down and tight to the bottom. Boats that anchor on top of the pinnacles often make suspended fish disappear; if your first two drifts bring only small fish, change depth or angle rather than hammering the same presentation.

Lighter presentations often trigger better quality rockfish; heavy leaders and oversized jigs tend to spook suspended fish here. When the fleet stacks, broaden your search along the edge—the fish either go deeper or slide to the edge of the rubble.

## Practical tactics — halibut
Treat the drift as the primary tool. Aim to pass the same trough or edge repeatedly with bait near bottom but not lodged in rock. The best tide is a modest flood or the first usable push after slack; too strong and your gear drags, too weak and bait sits still.

Use a three-way or drop-shot style with whole herring or squid. Weight enough to keep bottom contact on the slope, but not so much you bounce off the rubble into rock. Micro-observation: halibut here most often bite near the base of the slope where sand meets rubble, not on top of the pinnacles.

## Practical tactics — rockfish
When marks are suspended, use short vertical presentations over the working side of pinnacles. Drop through the column with slow, precise motions. Vary jig weight and descent speed—marks can shift on slight changes in current angle. If you see small fish high in the water, push deeper or change drift angle; the better fish usually sit lower, often just outside the obvious numbers.

## Small details that matter
What usually matters more: exact drift line and tide phase, small changes in bottom type (sand to gravel to rock), and which side of the ledge is working. What matters less: surface birds (helpful but not reliable) and a single GPS waypoint—the fish live on moving water, not a dot.

Common mistakes to avoid:
- anchoring on top of the pinnacles and expecting suspended fish to behave;
- ignoring a 0.5–1.0 kt change in tide speed—those shifts move where bait lands on the slope;
- treating this like big-water open-ocean; it’s a tactical, tide-driven spot best exploited during short windows.

Micro-observation: the better halibut days line up with a short flood push over the flats; when that push stops, the bite usually stops with it.

The North Jetty Ledge is honest and local. It’s close enough to the bar to matter for access and small enough to demand attention to tide and drift. Cross the bar, find the color edge, and hold the correct drift through the ledge—you’ll be fishing the exact piece of water the fish expect.
