## Where the plume peels offshore and why the corner matters

The "corner" isn’t a pin on a chart. It’s where the Columbia’s stained lens peels off the mouth, meets cleaner shelf water, and the two waters stack into a tight band. You see a sudden color flip from brown‑green to blue, a quick 2–4°F surface jump, and a lane where birds and bait compress. That seam usually sits just inside the deeper contours of Astoria Canyon and behaves like a corridor—steady forage supply on one side, cleaner blue on the other. When that band holds, salmon and migratory pelagics use it the same way trucks use a highway: predictable, repeatable traffic.

Observation first, why second: the plume brings juvenile forage; the physical contrast—color, temp, current—creates a consistent uphill/downhill relationship fish exploit. Get the bar window, the edge stability, and visible bait and the bite concentrates quickly.

## How currents, tide and wind set the edge

Watch wind direction more than raw speed. Light offshore breeze or steady NW upwelling flow tends to push the plume south and let a defined edge set against the blue shelf. On the tide side, ebb tightens the corner as plume water accelerates offshore; flood can shove the stain back and blur the seam.

Practical cues:
- A light offshore breeze keeps the edge coherent and the bar manageable.
- Onshore or strong NW wind often smears the line and pushes the plume farther down the coast.
- Tidal exchange creates alternating seams—expect the corner to tighten on ebb and flatten on a strong flood.

Micro‑observation: the edge tightened at first light. By ten the birds had a lane and the meter built under the stern. That pattern repeats—early tightening, birds commit, bait shows shallow—if the wind and tide cooperate.

## How bait arranges itself and what to read on sonar

Bait here is variable—anchovy, smelt, juvenile hake—but the pattern is the same: the plume funnels small forage against cleaner water and compresses it into a narrow band. Sonar usually shows bait shallow to mid‑column on the warm (stained) side and scattered deeper marks along contour breaks.

Read the bait, not the foam:
- When bait streaks are consistent in depth along the edge, the corridor is live.
- Isolated foamers without bait beneath are often misleading; the fish may be transient or already pressured.
- Bait under the blue side of the edge is a more reliable trigger for both salmon and albacore than bait on the stained side alone.

## How Chinook work the uphill side

Chinook treat the seam like a highway. They hold on the uphill side of current seams and on the shallow flanks of canyon cuts where bait funnels by. Depth stacking commonly falls in a 30–80 ft band inside the edge, varying with tide and season.

Tactics that reflect fish behavior:
- Troll across seams so lures climb into the holding side—fish often eat on the uphill pass.
- When birds are tight and bait is shallow, tighten rigger spreads and slow down enough to keep hoochies and plugs in the zone.
- If sonar shows deeper, slower marks, move to downrigger or lead‑core presentations to match depth.

Operational cues:
- Repeat passes matter more than flashy gear. Hold speed steady and make measured adjustments.
- If the first few stops don’t show bait or birds, you either move along the edge or test farther out—the bar decision usually dictates that choice.

## When albacore show and where to run them

Albacore aren’t constant but when warm surface pockets ride onto the shelf the plume corner becomes a corridor into albacore range. They usually relate to warm pockets and bait on the blue side—just outside the stained lens. Surface schools are mobile; one good stop can turn into a run down the edge.

What to look for:
- A stable temperature band that rides the corner.
- Birds slightly offshore of the color break and flying low.
- Light chop—some motion helps the pattern; dead calm can make stops fragile and hide action.

Simple albacore checklist:
- Test the edge before committing: a 10–20 nm run toward the shelf to confirm temp and birds.
- Run a mixed spread—cedars and small skirted lures—on the blue side; keep a few plugs higher for quick hookups.
- If a stop produces a prototype bite, stop cleanly and get bait in fast—the school either builds or vaporizes.

## How fleet pressure reshapes the window

The corner concentrates boats. When it’s working, you’ll see a stack of boats right on the obvious color/temp line; when marginal, boats scatter and test the slope. Repeated passes change fish behavior quickly: schools sink, foamers shut down, bite lines shift a mile or two after heavy traffic.

Common pressure reactions:
- Fish sink or scatter after repeated runs.
- Foamers refuse repeated casts if boats circle too long.
- Productive lines can migrate 1–2 miles after sustained pressure.

Practical response: if you’re running heavy pressure, compress your work—shorter runs, more focused passes—or move a mile and test a fresh section. Repeatedly circling the same foamer is often counterproductive.

## Short moves and tactical rules for the corner

Work along the edge rather than charging across it. The fish live in the relationship between stain and blue; your presentation should respect that relationship.

Tactical notes:
- Read the plume before you mix speed and spread—confirm where the holding side is.
- Time initial passes with tide changes—ebb tends to tighten seams and concentrate bait.
- In stained water, use flasher/bait; on the blue side switch to smaller, flashier plugs and cedar patterns.
- Sonar: prioritize bait under the blue side of the edge. Don’t chase isolated surface foamers without bait showing below.
- Bar planning: if the bar forecast tightens mid‑day, shorten the run and concentrate effort near the mouth where you can head home fast.

## What actually matters here

Trust what the water shows, not yesterday’s GPS line. The corner pays when three pieces line up: bar window, a clear temperature/color edge that holds over several miles, and bait compressed against that edge.

What usually matters more:
- where bait is stacked relative to the edge
- edge stability over a stretch of water
- tide‑driven seam direction

What usually matters less:
- the exact GPS line where the edge was yesterday

When the pieces line up, the work becomes simple: find the seam, pick the holding side, and repeat passes until the stop tells you to move. When they don’t, the corridor looks right and fishes empty. Follow the water.
