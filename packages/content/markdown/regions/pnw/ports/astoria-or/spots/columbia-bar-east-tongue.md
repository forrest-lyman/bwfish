The Tongue isn’t a single rock or canyon—it's a working seam. Off the Columbia mouth you get a band where stained plume water peels against cleaner shelf water and a shallow-to-deeper contour corrals bait. From the bar it looks like a color and current line a few miles seaward. Boats find it by color, thermometer, and bird travel.

When that plume thins and a firm temperature change sits on the color line, the East Tongue becomes a short, reliable migration lane for Chinook moving along the mouth rather than pushing inside. It’s primarily troll water. Secondarily you can drift nearby flats for halibut when bait compresses off the edge. The lane can light up fast and go quiet just as fast.

## How the East Tongue forms and why fish use it

Observation first: you’ll see a darker shelf peel away from the plume and bait stacked on that border. Birds trail it or drop into tight balls. Fish sit tight to that visual edge.

Why that matters: the plume compresses anchovy and small forage along the change in current and depth. Migrating Chinook will either hold on the uphill side of the seam or ride the border where prey is concentrated. The inside shelf and the river’s tidal exchange create predictable uphill/downhill zones fish use to stage before moving on.

Short version: the Tongue is a bait conveyor and a highway seam. Fish use the edge where prey is easiest to eat.

## When the Tongue lights up — the real signals

Start with the bar window. If you can’t cross the bar safely, you’re not fishing this water the way it fishes. Beyond safety, these are the things that matter in order:

- A firm temperature break (roughly a 3–5°F surface shift) across a narrow distance. You’ll see it on the thermometer or chartplotter and it usually lines up with the color.
- Visible bait on the sounder hugging the edge and birds working the seam.
- A moving tide that creates a defined seam at the mouth; salmon key to the seam direction and the uphill side of current.

If those three line up you have a short, repeatable troll lane. If one goes away—especially the temperature edge—the Tongue often dies quickly.

Signs worth running for:
- a clean color line you can see from the bow
- bait marks along the edge on the sounder
- birds traveling purposefully along the seam
- a firm temp break on the plotter

## How I search it and decide to commit

Observation, not hope. Make a measured run off the bar to confirm color and temp before you commit. Don’t commit to a full spread until you see bait or a stable edge.

Search routine I use:
- Run off the bar, check thermometer, confirm the color line and bait on the sounder.
- If birds are traveling and the temp edge holds, set a repeatable lane that cuts the seam slightly uphill.
- If the edge is thin or inconsistent, back off and keep searching—there’s no value in wide, uncontrolled drifts across this seam.

Boats that stop to read birds and thermosigns usually leave with fish. Boats that chase every boil often leave with time.

## Running lanes and depth control

The Tongue fishes best when your gear matches the bait layer and the current, not an arbitrary depth number.

Tactics I rely on:
- Run lanes that cross and climb into the seam—the uphill side is where salmon sit. Make turns that present the offering into the current; many strikes come on the climb into the line.
- Repeat lanes. When bait and birds lock on a patch, circle back and re-run the same lane; the first twenty minutes are the most revealing.
- Depth control matters more than surface temp alone. Use downriggers and planers to mirror the bait layer. As pressure increases, fish compress deeper on crowded seams.
- Keep the spread narrow when pressure is high. Crowded edges favor subtle presentations; big, noisy spreads can push fish down or out.

## Gear choices by clarity and current

Pick the presentation to match the edge and the clarity. No need to reinvent:

- Stained edge: larger flashers with hoochies or flasher/bait combos—flash creates profile and visibility.
- Cleaner blue shelf: smaller, more natural plugs and spoons that read like bait.
- Speed: adjust to keep your bait in its natural swim relative to current and tide. Too fast and you’re presenting an unnatural offering; too slow and the bait stalls.

Best initial Chinook setup I run:
- Narrow rigger spreads: two lines inside, two slightly outside.
- Hoochie or flasher/bait on the long rigger.
- Depth set to the bait layer, speed matched to current.

## Reading birds and the fleet

Birds and boats are extra sensors. Watch them, but verify.

- Birds that travel the seam or drop into small concentrated balls are the quickest sign something is holding.
- A string of boats parked in a narrow stretch usually means the edge is working—verify depth and bait before reacting. Don’t assume everyone’s seeing the whole picture.
- When boats pile on, fish often compress deeper and sometimes refuse repeated presentations on the surface.

The fleet is helpful—use it—but don’t let it force you into a blind commitment without checking thermosigns and sounder marks.

## Common mistakes and pressure reactions

Watch what other boats do wrong so you don’t repeat it.

Common mistakes:
- Crossing the edge without setting lanes. The seam is lateral; plowing across it wastes a run.
- Anchoring or heavy drifting in the stained plume. Visibility and bait are poor inside the plume; fish use the edge.
- Ignoring tide. A lane that works on an ebb can die on a strong flood—presentation changes with current direction and depth.
- Committing to the run without a bar plan. If the bar changes, the smallest delay can put you inside mid-effort.

Pressure reactions to expect:
- Fish compress deeper on crowded seams.
- Foamers sometimes refuse repeated casts.
- The productive lane can shift a few hundred yards along the edge in an hour.

## Micro-observations that matter

A few small reads are disproportionately useful:

- The bite often starts when the darker shelf water peels away from the plume and birds stop drifting and begin traveling.
- Boats that stop, read birds and thermosigns, then run a tight lane usually find fish. Boats that chase every scattered boilline burn time and fuel.
- The Tongue is short water; small shifts in bait or temp move the productive patch quickly.

## Practical close

The East Tongue rewards patient, methodical work. It’s short water—edge reading beats brute distance. If the bar window, the temperature edge, and the bait all line up you’ll have a focused, efficient day. If any of those variables fails, the Tongue feels like an empty corridor. Run measured passes, match depth to bait, and treat the birds and fleet as sensors—not gospel. That way you fish the seam, not the noise.
