This feature stands out because relief is rare in the lower mouth—mostly sand. The Lower‑Mouth Basin Pinnacle is a single hard spike rising out of shallow flats inside the plume's reach. It’s not a broad reef; it’s a vertical piece of rock surrounded by soft bottom. That contrast is the whole point: when tide and river push compress bait against the face, better rockfish and the occasional larger lingcod concentrate on the relief while the surrounding flats sit quiet.

## Why the pinnacle concentrates fish

You’ll notice the fish show when two flows meet: the plume shearing against cleaner shelf water on certain tides. That intersection creates a small staging post where bait gets crammed along the uphill face. Current clips the rock, an eddy forms on the lee, and the relief creates a predictable ambush lane.

Observe first: bait and marks form a tight band, not a scattered mess. Then the explanation: the rock interrupts flow, concentrates bait, and gives predators a short, efficient strike lane. It’s a small, high-return target when the water is moving right.

## When the window opens and closes

The spot is driven by movement, not by the river gauge.

- The best bites line up with tidal push—moderate flood or ebb that creates a steady uphill/downhill difference across the rock.
- You’ll get two useful hours more often than a long all-day bite. The bite builds with the tide and dies as the plume surges past or slack arrives.
- If the plume slips by too fast the bite shuts off. Likewise, two boats on top will scatter bait and collapse the window quickly.

Read the tide first; treat this like a timing game. If the current’s right, plan for one or two good drifts rather than a long sit.

## What bait and life tell you

Look for bait compression and you’ve found your trigger.

- Anchovy, sand lance and small smelt collect along the face. On calm days you’ll see tight bait marks on the sounder or small silvery flashes at the base.
- Rockfish stack early on a rising current. Lingcod tend to show later in the tide or when bait squirts into crevices.
- Surface birds are uncommon compared with shelf edges, but birds over nearby cleaner water can point to a bigger bait corridor close by.

In short: bait on the face is your signal. Where the bait sits—depth and tightness—dictates presentation.

## Where the fish actually sit

The better marks aren’t usually glued to the top of the pinnacle.

- Fish often suspend 10–30 ft above the rock rather than dead on the bottom.
- A small eddy on the down‑current side becomes a holding area; main flow clips the face and fish tuck in the lee or sit in the uphill column.
- When you see a curtain of bait over the pinnacle and marks rising off the bottom, expect concentrated fish in a tight depth band.

Don't fish the top because that’s where the rock is; fish the water that’s doing the work.

## How the fleet treats this spot

Local boats use it as an early‑tide ticket.

- Small skiffs run in on the first push, work a drift or two, then move on when the window fades.
- Good skippers don’t park on top. They run repeated passes along the uphill side and downstream lee to intercept the same column of productive water.
- If word gets out you’ll see quick traffic on good tides. Pressure scatters bait fast; quieter tides mean better returns.

Watch what other boats do—not to copy, but to see where the productive water is. If everyone’s anchored on the rock, they’re probably not catching the suspended stuff.

## How I work it: approach and presentations

Start above, work down, repeat.

- Approach from up‑current so your jigs and baits move down across the relief instead of sweeping parallel to it.
- Vertical first: drop a heavy, slow‑fall jig down the face to locate suspended fish. If fish are tight, slow‑pitch jigs or baited vertical presentations get the best results.
- If the first pass finds fish, re‑set an offset drift to target the same uphill lane—fish often sit in the exact same column.
- Watch the sounder for bait compression 10–30 ft off the bottom; that’s your primary trigger. Don’t fish blind just because the boat is over the rock.
- Match presentation to water color: flasher/bait in stained plume water; smaller, more natural jigs in cleaner pockets.
- When the tide is strong, shorten leaders and use heavier jigs to keep contact. If slack arrives, pull off and reposition for the next push.

Those are practical sequences—approach, vertical check, repeat the edge—rather than tricks. The water tells you what to do.

## Common mistakes I see

- Parking on the pinnacle and expecting the same bite all tide. The productive water usually sits off the face.
- Casting long and wide instead of working vertical. When fish are under the boat, long casts waste time.
- Ignoring nearby flats for halibut when current starts pushing bait off the rock. Sometimes the halibut show up as the pinnacle goes quiet.

Avoid those and you’ll keep more of the good hours.

## Signs it’s working (field observations)

- The bite usually arrives with current and bait compression, not with birds.
- Marks around the base and off the face beat marks on the very top.
- The better lingcod tend to hit late in the tide or when the current softens and bait squirts into holes.

If you see a curtain of bait on the screen and distinct arches rising off the bottom, set up for vertical work and expect a focused couple of hours.

## When to mark it on the trip list

Make this an option when conditions line up, not a must-do.

- Moderate sea state with a steady tidal push and a visible temperature or color break nearby.
- Clean enough to see bait on the sounder, or stained water with clear bait layering and working marks.
- Early in the tide cycle—commit for one or two productive drifts, then move if the bite doesn’t build.

Treat it like a drift target: start above, work the uphill lane, and leave before pressure or slack kills the bite.

This is a small, operationally useful pinnacle. Not a wide reef, but a reliable ambush spot when tide and bait line up. Trust what the sounder and the current tell you, work vertical, repeat the edge, and don’t overstay the window.
