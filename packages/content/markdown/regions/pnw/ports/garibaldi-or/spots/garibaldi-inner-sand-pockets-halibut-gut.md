The Gut is a small collection of sand pockets and narrow scour gutters sitting just outside Garibaldi’s mouth. On the chart they show as sand flats interrupted by scattered rock and low ledges — the kind of inside-shelf relief that turns a tide push into a tight, repeatable halibut drift.

Seen from the boat it’s straightforward: sand flat, small channel or gutter, then a fringe of rock or boulder clusters. The whole fishery is the contrast between that clean sand and the nearby relief. Rock pins bait on the edge; the tide peels the bait into the gutter and the halibut follow.

## The fish that matter

Primary target: Pacific halibut. Big, slow movers that will edge onto the flats to feed when bait is available and the current runs tidy.

Associated life: sand lance and small forage, juvenile rockfish, crabs and the occasional lingcod along the rocky fringe. Their presence — a tight, predictable source of prey — is what makes the halibut commit.

If the current softens the fish tend to slide deeper. That’s one of the clearest behavioral cues here.

## Why the sand-and-rock edge concentrates halibut

Observation first: the rock sheds prey into a narrow flow as the tide pushes. Sand lance and crabs get peeled off the edge and collect in the gutter. Halibut park on the downhill side of that relief to intercept whatever drifts by.

Explanation second: on long, featureless flats the bait spreads and the halibut don’t concentrate. At the Gut, every projecting rock or small ledge creates a short, high-value drift because it corrals bait into a defined strike zone.

## When it works

The trigger is a tide push that actually peels bait off the rock and carries it into the gutter. Building flood or ebb that produces a steady downhill streak into the channel is what turns this from a sand flat into halibut water.

You’ll also want a passable bar and a manageable sea state — calm or easing swell through the bar keeps your drifts tidy. The water inside can be stained and still produce; clearer inside water simply makes the marks easier to read.

## Signs the window is building

Watch for these signals; they tell you when to commit.

- Small bait marks tight on the rock edge that start streaking into the gutter as current builds.
- Birds working the first mile with deliberate drops or tight corks rather than a scattered flock.
- The sounder showing bait streaks sliding downhill in consistent lines, not random blobs.
- Other boats slowing their drift or switching to hooded rigs instead of anchoring.

Those signals usually arrive quickly and the good part of the push often gives you a 30–60 minute window.

## How adjacent rock drives the bite

Observation: halibut place themselves downhill of projecting rocks and ledges — not in the open sand. The rock is the engine that pins and sheds prey; gutters act as receiving bowls.

Explanation: pockets where rock projects into sand make short, dense drifts. Long, featureless flats don’t concentrate bait and won’t hold the same numbers or sizes. If you can identify where the rock throws flow into a gutter, you’ve found the strike zone.

## Practical setup and boat work

Start with the approach — run a controlled, across-the-edge sweep so your baits travel from rock into the gutter. Presenting baits slightly across or parallel to current puts them into the downhill flow where halibut expect prey.

Boat control: keep engine input minimal during the bite. Use small corrective bursts to maintain drift angle. Continuous throttle ruins the natural presentation; you want your bait carried through the strike zone by the current, not by your prop wash.

The fish here respond to contact and feel. If the push is strong, use enough weight to hold bottom; if you’re too light you’ll lose contact and miss bites.

## Gear and presentations that work

Run setups that keep the bait on or just above bottom and present it naturally into the gutter.

- Hooded-sinker rigs (heavier than you’d use on slack-water flats): they hold in the push while letting the bait flop free enough to look natural.
- Big baits: whole herring, squid, or large hook baits that sit true against the sinker.
- Stout leaders and large single or circle hooks to deal with big halibut.
- Short flasher-and-squid setups when fish are working slightly off bottom or aggressive beneath a thermocline.

Set line length so you can feel a bite and give a halibut time to pick up the bait, but keep enough control to detect slack before the fish sucks the bait. I run the drag relatively loose until I know where the fish are sitting under the boat.

## What to run and why

- Heavy hooded sinker + large bait: holds bottom in the push, presents the bait into the gutter.
- Short flasher and squid: for fish that are rising a bit or when you want added attraction without losing bottom contact.
- Single-hook big-bait combos: converts cautious halibut that ignore flashy offerings.

These choices are about controlling presentation and keeping the bait where the halibut expect it.

## When to arrive

Be early. Get in 30–60 minutes before the predicted peak so you can feel the current build and position yourself for the first clean drifts. The Gut tightens quickly and can die just as fast once the tide peels past or the bait disperses. If the first run is sloppy, move on — it often clears and sharpens on the next pass.

## How electronics read here

Useful marks are thin lines hugging the edge with streaks sliding off into the gutter. A clean bottom return with discrete bait marks above it is the best signal. Wide, amorphous marks across the flat rarely pay; floating clutter or silt on the graph gives lots of false positives.

If you see consistent downhill movement of dense bait streaks, you’re in the right place. If the sounder is a mess, don’t force it.

## Common pressure mistakes

- Anchoring on the edge and waiting for the drift to sort itself — the Gut rewards a controlled sweep, not staying in one hole.
- Using light weight in a building push — you’ll lose contact and miss the bites.
- Arriving after the push — bait and fish slide off fast; late arrivals find only leftovers.

If there’s crowding, be ready to move rather than fight for a small lane.

## Why the Gut is a good small-boat option

Short runs when the bar is cooperating and tight bite windows make this an efficient in-and-out fishery for small boats. On marginal days when the fleet won’t commit to the shelf edge, the Gut often gives the only practical halibut water inside the bar.

## Fleet behavior here

Locals treat the Gut as first-call inside work when the bar looks marginal. The first few drifts often set the tone — a single good hookup or a short radio update compresses effort fast. Expect commercial gear and crab pots to influence lanes; experienced captains will shift rather than fight a crowded line.

## Micro-observations

- Fish bite hardest on the downhill side of a projecting rock.
- A single tight bait mark converts more often than a broad, messy return.
- The first 45 minutes of a push usually tells the day.

## Bottom line

The Inner Sand Pockets are a timing play. Read the rock, arrive ahead of the push, and run hooded-sinker presentations that hold bottom while still looking natural. When the tide lines up and the bait peels into the gutter, the Gut fishes like a short, repeatable halibut ground. When it doesn’t, it looks like any other sand flat.
