What makes this spot distinct
This is the first meaningful piece of mixed hardbottom outside Tillamook Bay: a short, irregular ledge that runs into a neck of kelp and then drops off into shallow sand pockets. It's not a long reef—more a stitched line of rock, boulders and kelp that translates tidal exchange from the inlet into tidy downhill seams. Boats from Garibaldi run here when the bar allows a short, controlled day; it fishes often enough that most local skippers keep it on a short list when the swell is marginal.

The spot organizes life because the ledge forces bait off the shallow bank into a channelized flow along the kelp neck. When the tide has a clear push the bait piles on the downhill side and predators stack tight to that seam.

Why the ledge punches above its size
This ledge is small, but it concentrates fish the way a headland concentrates birds. Observation first: when we get a firm push the sonar shows a narrow band of bait directly off the outside face. The bass of the line—rock, boulder, kelp—funnels the water so the current runs focused and predictable. Fish don't need a long reef here; they just need a seam where bait gets tied up.

The practical consequence is simple: you can cover the productive water quickly. If you can read the seam and present into the downhill flow, you'll see tight marks and quick hits. If you can't, the kelp hides everything and you end up fishing blind.

How the tide and inlet surge run this spot
Tide and inlet surge dominate what happens here. Two patterns keep showing up in the log and on the boat:

- A firm downhill push on ebb or flood pins bait along the outside face and creates a repeatable drift line.
- When the current softens, the bait thins and the marks collapse into the kelp; fish tuck into the kelp or off the face.

Micro-observation: on a recent run the fish sat tight on that first downhill swing. Once the surge eased the graph went quiet and the bait sank into the kelp pockets.

Read that and plan: if you arrive before the push and the tide builds, you get a clear bite window. If you arrive late, the bite's already moved into the kelp and becomes a grind.

What lives on the neck — bait and predators
Common forage here is small sand lance, juvenile herring and the crab life that lives around kelp and rock seams. Birds show up in short, angry bursts—useful as a confirmation, not gospel. The sequence I watch for is the tide building, bait compressing on the downhill face, sonar flashes, then short bird work and foamers. Lingcod and rockfish sit on the rocky fingers and seams; halibut patrol the sand gutters just off the kelp for anything pushed out.

When the spot actually fishes
This is a tide-driven bite, not a calendar one. Best windows are tied to current and water clarity:

- Arrive ahead of a clear tide swing; fish usually activate as the current comes up or begins its downhill run.
- After a light offshore wind or a slack bar that leaves the inside water cleaner—bait cohesion and visibility help.
- If the fleet is running wide to the shelf, this place often stays quiet. It's nearshore work for short-day conditions.

Signs the window is building
Watch for these signals. They're quick to see on the sounder and from the boat:

- Tight, consistent marks on the downhill side of the ledge.
- Bait hugging the kelp neck instead of scattering across the flat.
- Short, focused bird work over a single seam rather than spread-out birds.
- A clean current line on the surface or a slight color change over the face.

Drift vs anchor — how I choose
There are two ways to fish it. Pick based on current strength and how tidy the seam looks.

Drift — preferred when current is moderate
Observation: when the current is moving but not violent, drifting lets you sweep the downhill face repeatedly and find the tightest marks.

How I run it:
1. Run parallel to the shelf until I see bait on the graph.
2. Turn across the edge and set a controlled cross-current sweep so baits come into the downhill flow.
3. Keep leaders short, feel for the bite, and be ready to pick the boat up quickly as the drift angle changes with tide.

This suits vertical jigs and short drop rigs that present directly above the marks. Drifting covers more of the seam and exposes the same water repeatedly.

Anchor / controlled set — when it makes sense
Anchor when the tide is steady and you can hold position in the lee of rock or kelp so your baits sit in the downhill seam. This is better when the push is predictable and you want long presentations—halibut rigs with heavy hoods or big flasher setups for the adjacent sand gutters.

Notes on anchoring:
- Anchor in the lee of a rock finger or kelp pocket, not on the steepest edge where you smother the face.
- Watch scope and swing; if the boat smears your line across the edge, pick up and reset into a drift.
- If boats stack on an anchor, the bite collapses faster.

Tactical callouts
A few habits that save time:

- Don’t anchor right on the steep edge if a controlled drift will sweep the downhill face. Drifting generally gets more bites for less fuss.
- If the tide is a short, sharp surge, time the run to arrive 30–60 minutes before the push peaks.
- Clean sonar marks under kelp are often a better indicator than surface activity; kelp hides fish and surface signs can be misleading.

What pressure looks like and how fish react
This is a small, pressured piece of water. Repeated anchoring and heavy presentation change fish behavior quickly. From experience:

- Schools sink and refuse surface offerings after repeated boat traffic.
- Foamers will pop for a minute and then the birds peel away.
- Halibut on the sand move off after heavy anchoring in the same spots.

If you see the marks get deeper and the birds fade, the best play is to either move to a fresh sweep or lay off for a while.

Useful comparisons — what matters here
When you boil it down, these matter most:

- Current strength and timing.
- Bait stacked on the downhill side.
- Your ability to run a controlled cross-current drift.

These matter more than long troll spreads or wide search patterns. This is short-day, tight-water work; cover the seam properly.

How I decide to run it
Start with the current. If there's a clear downhill seam and bait piled on the outside face, run a controlled drift and work the marks. If the tide is steady and you can hold a clean position in the lee, set up and fish long presentations into the seam or the sand gutters for halibut. When the tide softens, expect the marks to collapse into the kelp and adjust to smaller, slower presentations or move on.

This place rewards reading the flow first, then matching presentation. Fish the current and the bait will tell you where to put the boat.
