## Why the East Tongue Decides Your Day

The east tongue at the Tillamook Bar isn’t a fixed rock you aim at — it’s a moving sand/current feature that organizes the first mile outside Garibaldi. Tide-swept channels, sand guts, scattered rocks and a short, steep drop into the 10–30 fathom band all get rearranged by swell and tides. For most boats it’s the day’s first binary decision: punch the bar and commit to short halibut drifts and early salmon staging, or stay inside and work kelp and nearshore rock. If the bar is closed, nothing past the entrance matters; if it’s passable, the tongue still controls where the bait stacks and which seams fish first.

## Entrance Behavior — Currents, Guts, and a Changing Bottom

The inlet is compact and violent. Floods will peel off the tongue and create quick seaward currents; ebbs run hard down shallow guts. Immediately outside the mouth the bottom is a mixed sand field with occasional gravel shoulders and pockets that cut into 10–30 fathoms. Those gutters and sand pockets are where halibut push and where bait stacks form.

NW swell and short-period wind waves can rewrite the tongue in a day. A calm shore-to-shore day can still hide a long-period swell that makes the tongue unreliable. What the tongue actually does is simple and practical: it steers the first plume of bay outflow and the first flow of bait onto the shelf. That steered water then sets the early seams where salmon and halibut will stage.

## Recognizing a Real Window

You get little signs before the entrance will work. Watch for a combination rather than a single tick:

- A green bar report and swell spacing longer than roughly 10–12 seconds. Short, steep waves make landing unpredictable.
- Overnight offshore wind or a slack night — the entrance cleans quickly after an offshore breeze.
- Arriving outside ahead of a flood peak or an ebb slack, depending on the drift you want. Timing the tide is often what separates a good run from a wasted one.
- Birds working just outside the mouth or on the tongue at first light. Birds usually show the lane before sonar does.

The birds tell you the tongue is organizing bait. The bar report tells you whether you can get there safely.

## How Bait, Birds and Fish Stack on the Tongue

The tongue is where bay water meets shelf water; it concentrates surfperch, small smelt and fleeing bait into narrow lanes. That concentration is what draws predators and defines the first bite.

Halibut: bait gets pushed into sand pockets and along gravel shoulders. Good halibut hang on the sand adjacent to the first rocky relief and wait for the chute current to bring food. They sit tight until a tide change increases the flow off the tongue.

Salmon: early-season Chinook and Coho stack on the uphill side of the shallow tongue when warm surface fingers ride out. They’ll hold in the first tidy temperature seam if it’s steady; a single stable warm finger will produce far better than a broken patch.

Birds are a practical early-warning system. They usually arrive before sonar marks do. Working birds over the tongue are a signal to follow, slow down and set a pattern — not a guarantee, but a reason to commit and probe.

## Who Fishes the Tongue and How the Fleet Reacts

The tongue suits a few profiles: small boats that run conservative bars and want short halibut drifts; boats that plan to scout the first 10–30 fathoms before deciding to run farther; and occasional early-season salmon boats looking for a quick uphill troll. Fleet behavior is conservative — when the first boats find bait off the tongue, the fleet fans out along the 10–30 fathom contour instead of bunching up on the immediate entrance. If several boats chase the first bait line, fish often sink or slide to the next seam; the early-arrival boats take the best bites.

## Tactical Notes — Field-Used Checklist and Work Patterns

Short Bar Checklist
- Bar report: green preferred; yellow means be conservative.
- Swell period: 10–12 seconds minimum for most small boats; longer periods are worse for landings.
- Fuel and run-time margin: expect extra time waiting for clean windows at the entrance.
- Tide plan: decide if you want to arrive before flood peak or before ebb slack.

Halibut work — first-mile, short runs
Fish the sand pockets adjacent to isolated rock or gravel shoulders. Heavy, slow baits on hooded sinkers or large jigs are the ticket; you need weight to hold bottom and time to feel soft takes. Drift control matters: work the downhill side of a small ledge or sweep across a sand gutter on a steady drift rather than anchoring on top of the relief. Best bites show when the tide peels off the tongue or during the immediate change and peak.

Salmon staging — early-season short troll
Troll uphill along the inside flank of the tongue where warm surface fingers ride the edge. Run a two-depth spread with shallow and mid plugs; the uphill track through concentrated bait often outproduces speed changes. When birds and small bait show, commit — the window closes fast as the tide shifts.

Practical drill: arrive, read birds and current, set a controlled cross-current sweep, and be ready to fan out along the 10–30 fathom band if the first lane gets pressured.

## Common Mistakes and When to Commit Offshore

Common mistakes are straightforward and avoidable: running the bar because swell height looks small while ignoring period and direction; anchoring on the top of the ledge instead of fishing the sand run; or committing to a long offshore run before tasting the first mile — the tongue will tell you whether to go long.

Leave the tongue and commit offshore when you see:
- Clean water patches and a firm temperature break outside the 10–30 fathom band.
- A clear bird line and repeated sonar bait marks on the edge.
- A weather window long enough to run and return safely across the bar.

If the bar is closed, work the harbor and near kelp. The tongue only matters if you can get past it.

## Quick Observations from the Edge

The outside seam often fishes before the inside ledges warm up — you can pick halibut off sand before the rockfish bite starts. Pressure compresses the edge quickly; early arrival matters. Read the bar, watch the birds, time your tide, and fish the sand runs before the rocks. Let the entrance tell you whether it’s a short halibut day or the start of something farther out.
