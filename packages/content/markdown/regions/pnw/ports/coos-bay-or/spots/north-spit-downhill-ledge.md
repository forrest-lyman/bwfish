The North Spit downhill ledge is the first clear contour you hit after the bar on the bay’s north side. It isn’t a single bump — it’s a sequence: a kelp-and-rock fringe, a defined downhill face, then a sand pocket and a gutter that runs off into the flatter shelf. Locals use it as the short‑run option when the bar is passable but weather, time, or taste for a shorter day keep you nearshore.

What makes it worth the run is simple observation: tide-driven current pushes bait off the rock and into the pocket, compressing food into a narrow drift line. On the right tide that compressed line gives repeated halibut hookups and cleaner rockfish/lingcod drops than you get in busier headland water. This place rewards pattern reading more than brute distance.

## Why the downhill face funnels fish

Start with the relief. The kelp-and-rock fringe breaks the current, bait tumbles off the structure, and the downhill face funnels that bait into the sand pocket and gutter. Halibut slide up from the slope to feed where the bait compresses; rockfish and lingcod sit in the fringe and hit as the drift brings food past holes and kelp fingers.

Two practical points you can see on any pass:
- When bait stacks on the face you get a narrow, repeatable drift line. That's where halibut concentrate.
- If bait is spread across the flat there’s nothing to hold fish — the pocket goes quiet fast.

I pay attention to the pocket first, then the immediate downhill shoulder. When one stops working the other often gives a second shot.

## Reading the working lane (current, signs, and timing)

This is a tide-window fishery. Flood and early ebb that shove bait downslope into the pocket are the useful windows. Slack destroys the compression; too much push and you can’t hold a clean drift.

Watch the surface and your sonar. Useful signs:
- a seam where green kelp water meets cleaner shelf water
- foam lines or kelp fingers running parallel to the ledge
- bait marks tight to the downhill edge on the screen
- a color change in calm conditions from shallow to deep

The bite usually starts within the first hour of a steady push. When the tide softens the marks thin and the pocket often dies for the day. If you miss the build, you’ll rarely see a rebuild.

## Bait, life, and what actually holds

Bait here is patchy and dense when present — crab bits, sand lance and small forage tuck against the face before the pocket. Those short, dense pockets over the sand gutter are what put halibut on the bite. Lingcod live glued to the rock fringe and trigger on a drift that snakes food into holes.

I watch for bait compressing against the downhill face and for small high‑density clouds that sit over the pocket early in the tide. They disappear quickly when boats push through or when the current shifts.

## Which species show and where they sit

- Pacific halibut: hold in the pocket and neighboring sand gutters on the right push. They move up off the slope to intercept compressed bait.
- Rockfish & lingcod: occupy the kelp/rock fringe and the immediate face. Vertical drops and jigs pick them out; bait rigs do well on the cleaner areas.
- Occasional salmon and pelagic activity: not a pelagic road, but birds and passing forage will bring trollers to try a quick stop.

Think of the area as three lanes: kelp fringe for lingcod/rockfish, the downhill face for stacked bait and vertical hits, and the pocket/gutter for halibut.

## Fleet behavior and how boats work it

Locals treat North Spit like a short‑day fallback. Expect tighter spacing and short, repeated drifts when something shows. The fleet pattern is conservative: work the same lane until it produces, then move. When offshore reports are hot, this ledge turns into a staging point or is skipped completely.

Common approaches you’ll see:
- single-boat drifts along the downhill edge, repeating the same lane until bites repeat
- two-boat spacing when halibut show — one on the pocket, one probing the shoulder
- quick vertical jig checks in the kelp fringe if bait rigs don’t produce

Pressure matters. Small pockets compress quickly under traffic; fish will move to deeper corners or the next pocket when boats bunch up.

## How experienced boats run it — tactical checklist

This is the practical bit. Keep it tight and decisive.

- Start with the tide: be over the face as the push builds. Late arrivals usually find the pocket gone.
- Drift control: keep bait near bottom on the downhill angle. Too much boat speed lifts presentations off the zone.
- Gear choices: heavy bait rigs and circle hooks for halibut; drop jigs and softbaits for lingcod and suspended rockfish. Shorten leaders when current is strong.
- Sonar: look for bait stacked on or just above the face. Marks 10–30 ft above bottom often equal a clean drift band.
- Move decisively: if a couple controlled drifts don’t produce, widen the search along the contour or run to the next gutter.

The things that usually matter most here are drift angle and speed, whether bait is stacked on the face, and getting the bar window that lets you start the tide in the right place. Exact GPS waypoints matter less than reading the lane.

## Common mistakes and when to move on

Mistakes I still see: arriving after the tide peaked and expecting a rebuild, running big spreads and losing drift control, and treating this like open shelf water instead of a ledge‑and‑pocket fishery. When boats chase too many options they scatter the bite.

If bait and birds vanish after a short probe, don’t circle aimlessly. Either fish the kelp fringe for lingcod/rockfish or run a few miles offshore where the shelf gives longer, more productive halibut drifts. The North Spit is a short‑window option — use it when conditions fit, and leave when they don’t.

Micro‑observation to bank: when the pocket is working, halibut bites repeat on the same depth band. When they stop, they usually stop for good.
