What the place looks like

Small islands and steep points stitch the inner end of Passage Canal. Between them the water breaks into tight kelp necks, short lee pockets and tide‑cut corridors — not big banks, but neat pieces of relief that organize bait and predators close to Whittier. Each line is short: a kelp tail, a rock knob, a narrow pocket behind a point. They either light up quickly or do nothing at all. The upside is reach: you can hit the good seams in minutes and run several checks before the tunnel window or weather closes.

Why kelp fingers matter

Observation first: bait compresses on those fingers. Sand lance, shrimp and small schooling fish ride the seams that peel along kelp and pile into the lee of a point. Rockfish and lingcod live on the vertical edges — the kelp necks frame drop‑offs and boulder fingers. Salmon moving into the inner Sound will stage in narrow lanes when a clean finger of water peels off a glacial run or river mouth.

The simple pattern is this: fish prefer narrow, clean lines. Broad, milky tidewater rarely holds the same concentration.

Reading the kelp tail

Start by looking at water color and shape, then confirm with birds and sonar.

A kelp tail you want looks like a clean lane of water running along the kelp instead of an all‑over milky wash. Birds working tight to a point or along a line of kelp are worth a check — often they’ve found bait before a sonar mark builds. On sonar bait will show pinned to the inside edge of the kelp; rockfish and lingcod marks hug the bottom relief immediately beneath the kelp.

Use these simple definitions when you talk to your crew:
- the tail: the downstream edge of a kelp mat where cleaner water peels off and bait compresses.
- the neck: a narrow kelp squeeze that forces current into a small eddy — the first place for vertical drop work.
- the lee pocket: quiet water behind a point where salmon will pause on incoming pushes.

When the fingers fish

They fish in tidy, narrow windows. The current has to soften enough for boats to control drift but still move scent along the kelp tail. Color contrast matters more than absolute clarity — a greenish finger peeling off a glacial run is usually better than a wide, milky expanse.

Watch the birds and the foamers: when birds drop and hold line, or foamers run and stay instead of milling, that’s often the opening.

Signs the window is building

You’ll see a few consistent cues:
- birds concentrate on the lee side of a point rather than scattered across the tide
- sonar shows bait as a thin stripe hugging the kelp instead of a broad blob
- boats fishing the line get short, intense bites and then go quiet as pressure builds

Tactics that actually work

If the useful water is narrow, your presentations need to be tight and fast.

Controlled down‑current drifts across the kelp tail are the bread-and-butter. Anchor only when a lee pocket is tight and clean; otherwise you waste time and spook fish. For lingcod and rockfish you want vertical presentations over the neck — short, sharp drops that keep the bait in the strike zone near the bottom relief. For salmon, a single bait free‑lined or fished on light leader through the staging lane works when birds and color line up; keep drags light and let the current carry the bait naturally.

Move quickly between fingers. The useful water is narrow and pressure‑sensitive — check two or three and commit to the cleaner one.

Fleet behavior and pressure

These lines compress fast. Charters and private boats split the inner islands on calm days. Experienced, faster boats will check several fingers quickly and move when the water softens or the color degrades. Less experienced groups tend to pile on a single pocket and expect it to keep producing; it rarely does.

When boats stack on a kelp neck the foamers will often refuse casts and rockfish tighten to the immediate relief. Good boats back off tight water, look for the next tail or the headland‑side clean pocket, and conserve position for the tidy tide that usually follows.

Common pressure reactions
- foamers will cut off altogether after repeated casts
- rockfish will sink tight to the bottom and refuse vertical presentations
- salmon will slip deeper, down a lane instead of staging in the shallow lee

What matters more than a GPS coordinate is drift speed and scent line, and finding a single clean finger of water. What matters less is staying glued to a boat that has already bored the line out.

Sonar and bird cues — how to read them together

Sonar will often show bait before the birds commit, and birds working tight to a tail can be the first sign the bait line has reformed. Trust both, but always read them against the water color. A narrow green lane running along kelp beats a wide, milky expanse where nothing holds.

Quick checks before you commit

Before you set up, run these simple checks:
- Is there a visible clean finger along the kelp or point?
- Are birds focused on one line and staying put?
- Is the drift speed manageable for a short run of vertical work or a free‑lined bait?
- Can you leave and get to another finger within the tunnel window or before weather closes?

Pressure‑management playbook (short)

- Check two fingers and commit to the cleaner one.
- Run minimal anchor or short controlled drifts; reset quickly.
- Avoid stacking on obvious foamers; follow boats that left for a reason.
- Use quick vertical work — short windows reward fast presentations.

Micro‑observation and decisions

The useful water is small. If the kelp tail looks right and the tide is forgiving, drop quickly and hold the line. If it’s crowded, move — the next tail often fishes better because it’s quieter. When the outer banks are closing, these kelp fingers and lee points will still give a good day if you read the seams and time the drift.
