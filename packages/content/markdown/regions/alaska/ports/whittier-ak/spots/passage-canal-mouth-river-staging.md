## Why the mouth matters

The mouth itself isn't some obvious shelf or reef — it's a seam. Freshwater fingers from the canal and nearby creeks meet deep salt, and the tide peels that stained water into a narrow band. Bait gets squeezed along that band and fish use it like a transit lane.

From Whittier this is a short, sensible run. That matters more than people give credit for: you can make two or three quick checks across the mouth and pick the best window before the fleet stacks up. When the color line is coherent and the tide angle pins it to a point, kings and coho show up predictably. Halibut read the same scent and bait differently — some will sit shallow on sand fingers, others will shift to saddles when the plume muddies.

## How the plume behaves

Watch the color and the peel — that tells you where bait will concentrate.

Freshwater tongues change the game. Heavy runoff gives you wide, milky lanes; light runoff creates narrow, tannin‑colored fingers. The tide doesn’t just move the water, it peels the plume into a tight band along points and into the outer edge of the mouth during flood and slack transitions. Clean, salty lanes tend to run inside points and behind small headlands; the dirty water peels off and folds into eddies and pockets.

A thin, tannin line with clean water on either side usually beats a wide, milky mess for salmon. The narrow line concentrates bait and keeps fish feedable.

## Reading the salmon window

Salmon don’t feed in the middle of a milky plume — they ride the color break where bait is condensed. Treat the lane like a highway: you want to find where fish are staging, not where the water is most dramatic.

Signs a window is building:
- birds working a narrow color break
- tight bait balls against the stained edge on sonar
- consistent, parallel marks under the color line on good units
- a steady tide that pins the lane to a headland

Behavior notes that matter at the helm: early-season kings often show as deeper, slower marks before they move up to eat; strong spring tides bring more of them and they push into the canal along the lane. Coho and pinks are later and quicker — they’ll light up a thinner break, especially on overcast days. And when a fleet stacks, fish either sink or move to the deeper flank; give the lane room or graft to adjacent lee pockets instead of piling on top of it.

## Where halibut sit and how the plume changes drifts

Halibut read scent and drift speed. The plume changes both, so your approach changes.

When you have a clean, clear lane beside a tannin edge, halibut will often hold shallower on the sand fingers right adjacent to the color line. Those drifts are shorter and shallower. With a wide milky plume they tend to push off to the edges or out onto saddles where the scent reconcentrates. If runoff brings white silt, sonar goes noisy; fish usually hold deeper and the bite moves to longer, deeper drifts.

Tactical conversions to test quickly:
- thin, colored plume: shorten leaders, lighten presentations, work sand fingers and shallow saddles tight to the edge
- wide, milky plume: move off the immediate mouth to down‑current saddles or deeper contours and lengthen drifts
- noisy sonar from silt: trust bird work and surface bait more than depth numbers — the unit will lie to you

Don’t overcomplicate it. Change leader and presentation first, then move ground if the feedback doesn’t come.

## Approach from Whittier — run, check, decide

The short run is your advantage. Use the tunnel timing and get on the water early to test the first slack or tide change.

A quick routine that saves time:
- circle the outer edge of the plume once to note where color peels into clean lanes and where birds are working
- pick a likely sand finger or inside point and put a controlled slow drift over it for halibut, or set up a tight troll/cast across the color break for salmon
- manage drift speed: too fast and halibut won’t track; too slow and they follow without committing — break the drift into short runs and resets over suspected seams

Start light on the leader and presentation when the lane is thin; if it’s milky, lengthen and deepen the presentation or slide off to the flank.

## Pressure signals and when to back off

Close run equals lots of boats when the window shows. The first boats often get the cleanest water; the rest push the seam until it dies. Watch how the lane reacts to traffic.

Common pressure reactions:
- foamers refusing repeated casts
- sonar marks collapsing into a broader column as boats arrive
- fish sinking to deeper saddles or outside edges

If the seam goes quiet quickly, don’t sit and grind it out. Move to adjacent lee pockets, small points, or inner flats — the next good fish is often one calculated move away from the obvious line.

Tunnel timing matters — don’t commit to a long search if your exit window or fuel margins are tight.

## Short reads that decide the day

These are small checks that should change your next move.

- A single bird working a tight strip usually matters more than a dozen scattered birds. Follow the focused bird.
- If bait is thick under the stained edge but fish aren’t committing, the tide is probably wrong — reset or move to the deeper flank.
- The better current is often inside the point on the flood; on the ebb the lane peels wider and fish can pull deeper.
- Water color trumps memory: if the plume is a focused tan/tea color and the tide is right, stay. Wide white silt lanes rarely hold feeding kings — shift to edges or deeper saddles.

When the seam widens into milky water, birds pull away, or you can’t control the drift through the narrow channel current, it’s time to move on.
