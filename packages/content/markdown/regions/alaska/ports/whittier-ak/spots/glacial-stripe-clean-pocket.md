What makes this place obvious on the water isn’t a rock or a buoy — it’s a moving color line. A milky sheet of glacial runoff peels out of the fjords, tide and headland shear tear it into fingers, and where one of those fingers peels away a cleaner, saltier lane rides against the stain. That clean finger is short-lived but recurring enough in Passage Canal that you can fish it deliberately if you pay attention to color, birds, and current.

## How the stripe forms and peels

You see the milky water first. That’s runoff from shallow shelves and fjord mouths after melt or heavy rain. The stripe is a surface phenomenon; tidal shear and the flows around headlands and points tear the milky sheet into narrow seams. On the outside edge of those seams cleaner pockets form where saltier water can hug the shoreline or tuck behind a point.

Observations to keep in mind: these pockets can be a few hundred yards across or just a tight finger you can run end-to-end in five minutes. They form fast and they disappear fast. Wind and tide move them; a neat lane at 0600 can be a soup at 0900.

## Where the clean pockets usually show

You don’t need a waypoint to find this — you need the right water behavior.

- Outside narrow headlands and island lee-lines where stained water peels seaward.
- Along tidal seams in Passage Canal where faster throughflow meets slower back‑eddy water.
- Behind small islands and points that shelter a strip of clearer water from the main milky flow.
- Just outside river mouths when the runoff stays shallow and an incoming tidal tongue pushes clearer water aside.

The practical test: look for a sharper edge than the surrounding silt — a color change, a thin line of foam or debris, or birds that suddenly tighten into a lane. Sonar usually lags; birds and color tell you first.

## What the water tells you (and why it matters)

When you see the clean line, two things are in play: a current break that concentrates bait, and a scent/salinity contrast that holds predators nearby. The clean pocket is the bait stage; the fish hang just off that stage where forage gets pinched or funneled.

Sonar behavior in these conditions is important to note. Glacial silt ruins resolution. Expect:

- false mid‑column clutter — the milky curtain creates mushy returns that mask small bait layers,
- lost vertical contrast — tight bait schools smear into the background.

Because of that, fish the color and the birds first. If you must choose, trust the edge you can see and the birds working it over an otherwise promising but fuzzy sonar mark.

## How salmon and halibut use the seam

Start with observation, then set your drift.

Salmon (kings and coho)
- They ride the cleaner edge. You’ll see them stage on the outside of the stain and feed where sand lance and small forage are being pinched.
- Early morning and tidy incoming tides tend to be the most reliable windows. Fish the seam, not the stain.

Halibut
- They use the scent line the differential water creates. When your drift holds a bait along the edge over a adjacent saddle or sand tongue, halibut will sit just off the pocket on the bottom.
- The best work is tight, controlled drifts that keep the bait moving along that edge. If the drift blows, the halibut will either pull deeper or move off the shallow ridge.

Secondary species (rockfish, lingcod) will tighten to any kelp fingers or ledges intersecting the pocket — useful when the pocket slips over structure.

## Reading the window: when to commit and when to bail

The window is brief and pressure-sensitive. Signs the window is building:
- a distinct color line where milky water peels into clearer water,
- birds tightening and working small patches instead of wide churns,
- bait rising near the edge in short, concentrated streaks on sonar (when the screen clears),
- a predictable drift angle that will carry baits along a downhill saddle or sand tongue.

Things that kill the window fast:
- a sudden runoff pulse that widens the milky stripe and buries the pocket,
- a tidal reversal that flips the clean water away from the edge you planned to fish,
- crowding: small pockets die quickly under multiple boats and repeated chum.

If the stain widens or the current flips, have a contingency — kelp necks or sheltered flats inside the pocket usually fish when the seam goes soft.

## Running the seam: a typical pattern

Treat the pocket like a short lane, not a spot.

- Run the visible edge looking for birds and the clean‑line visual.
- Set an upstream drift that will carry baits along the edge for a tidy pass over the adjacent flat or saddle.
- Keep boat spacing; one or two boats working alternating passes holds the pocket longer than five boats stacked across it.
- If the edge moves, run parallel passes rather than crowding one spot; you’ll keep the bait pressured less and the fish will stay willing longer.

Decisions explained: you keep spacing because salmon foamers go quiet and sink when boats stack the edge; halibut pull deeper or move when current spikes or the drift corridor gets crowded.

## Common mistakes and pressure reactions

You already know some of these, but it’s worth pointing out what kills a day.

Common mistakes
- chasing the center of the milky stripe — bait is nearly always on the clean side,
- parking on the first foamer — pockets are narrow and boats stack them out,
- over‑relying on sonar in heavy silt — it will often lie.

Pressure reactions
- salmon foamers go quiet and sink when boats push the edge,
- halibut move off the shallow ridge or pull deeper with spikes in current or congestion,
- the pocket can “jump” a few hundred yards if tide or wind shoves the stain — don’t assume the line is static.

## Quick operational rules

Short, observed rules to keep on the dash:
- The bait hugs the clean side. The fish follow.
- Birds and color tell you faster than your sonar will.
- A tidy drift over a narrow saddle next to the pocket beats wide, aimless searching.

If you remember one operational takeaway: fish the seam, not the stain. The clean finger is the stage; the fish use the stage when the drift and scent line up.
