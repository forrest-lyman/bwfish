## How the throat moves water

Right outside Whittier the Passage Canal squeezes tidal exchange through a narrow gap. That constriction focuses flow into fast, narrow jets on both ebb and flood, and it spits off cleaner, saltier fingers that hug walls, points and lee shelves. The ground isn’t complex in depth or structure—the thing that matters is moving water and the geometry that lets clean lanes peel away from the main boil.

You watch the lane, not the chart. The peel shifts with stage and wind; when it sets correctly it’s where bait, birds and salmon stack. When it’s wrong you’re in milky silt and fishing becomes a guessing game.

## Tide runs it — slack is the short window

Tide stage controls everything here. The main jet lives in the center of the throat; short, sharp slaps of current run past the heads and the cleaner fingers run along the edges. Slack is brief—enough time for a tidy halibut drift or a salmon stack, but not long. The best bites come at slack or a short time after the peak when the jet softens and you can hold a controlled drift.

Two operational notes from experience:
- Current strength can swing quickly with stage and wind; a calm seat‑of‑the‑pants run can turn into a punchy transit inside an hour.
- If the jet picks up while you’re on a halibut drift, you either drift past the saddle or move into a lee. Fighting the jet usually costs the drift.

## Where salmon stage and how to read the sign

Salmon won’t sit in the milky silt; they concentrate in the cleaner fingers that peel off the main flow. That’s especially true on the flood when cleaner lanes push into Passage Canal and compress bait against the edges.

Watch for these practical signs that a staging lane is forming:
- birds patching inside the channel instead of a wide scatter
- a visible peel of clearer water hugging shore or an island
- bait as a narrow band on sonar rather than a wide cloud
- boats parked and drifting aligned with the peel rather than crossing it

These staging windows are short. When the fleet stacks the same finger the fish often go quiet; moving ten to twenty yards into the lee or finding the next peel is frequently the difference between a hookup and nothing.

## Halibut want position more than noise

The throat acts like a scent conveyor. Halibut here are position‑sensitive: they intercept baits that ride in the clean lane with a predictable drift. The best drifts look tidy—boat nearly parallel to the peel, keeping 20–50 yards of clean water between silt and your spread so the scent trail isn’t smeared into the dirty line. The real window is short; control the boat during slack instead of trying to overpower the current.

When a halibut refuses the bait, common conversions are useful and economical:
- switch to vertical jigging on adjacent rock edges where current lightens
- use short, timed chum slicks—light, practiced slicks tend to work better than sloppy, long chums in this tight water

## Reading bait on the echo

The throat often shows up first on sonar. Bait compresses into thin vertical bands or streaks in the cleaner fingers; the pattern on the screen tells you what to expect at the surface and what to expect below.

Practical interpretations:
- long, tight vertical marks near a clean finger = active bait and a good chance of surface activity or salmon reaction
- thin bait streaks hugging a wall = uphill or downhill seams; good spots for tight halibut intercepts if your drift lands you there
- a broad, milky blob under the jet = glacial runoff and generally poor surface action; look for the cleaner fringe instead

Let the echo guide where you place the boat. Don’t drop into the milky band hoping to find fish—work the cleaner edge.

## Fleet behavior and pressure patterns

The throat fishes like a short, local game. Experienced boats get there early to pick a clean finger and move off quickly when it dies. If the lane fills, small adjustments—moving into the lee, shifting a few yards up or down—usually reopen the bite.

Common pressure reactions I watch for:
- when boats pile in, surface schools flatten and refuse casts after a couple of passes
- salmon slip into deeper saddles farther out in Passage Canal if the throat is over‑fished
- halibut pull deeper or scatter when wind comes against the tide and current spikes

Boats that respect the seam—backing off small water instead of crashing the same kelp edge—tend to outlast boats that try to bully a spot.

## Tunnel timing, transit and safety

The throat sits near the Anton Anderson tunnel and that logistics clock matters. Tunnel windows set the road schedule—trailers and shore support don’t wait. Don’t stretch an extra pass through the throat if you need to be back for a tunnel slot; misjudging returns and tides is where good days end badly.

A few safety points from running the gap:
- transit the throat on conservative headings; expect a punch from the jet if you cross it at the wrong angle
- brief eddies behind points can be deceptive—pick your lee carefully and be ready for the jet to snap back
- loaded boats or boats towing trailers need extra margin; the jet can make close approaches tricky and you’ll want a plan B in the Sound if the tunnel or weather shifts

## What usually matters — and what doesn’t

What usually moves the needle:
- small changes in drift angle relative to the peel
- where the clean peel sits in relation to the edge
- being on the right half‑hour of slack

What usually wastes time:
- worshipping exact GPS pins (the peel moves)
- lingering on a single foam line once pressure builds
- chasing a wide milky front instead of the thin clean fingers

## Tactical takeaways

Scout quickly to find the peel; a fast pass to locate cleaner water beats dropping into crowded, milky water. Time your halibut drifts to the short slack window and focus on boat control rather than overpowering the current. Respect tunnel windows and have a conservative plan for the run home. And when pressure builds, move—often a short reposition will reopen the day.

Final note: the throat gives short, decisive windows. Get in front of the clean finger, run a tidy drift, and be ready to leave early when the tide or tunnel demands it.
