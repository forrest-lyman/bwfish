## Where the ridge fingers sit and why they hold fish

This isn’t one big bank. It’s a broken line of downhill ribs, short saddles and scattered rock heads that peels off the shelf between Montague and Hinchinbrook. The relief itself is the piece — shallow saddles give halibut something to park on, the downhill faces compress bait, and isolated ribs hold rockfish and lingcod.

What separates these fingers from the inner Sound is water exchange. It’s where cleaner Gulf water first meets the fjord outflow. When that outside push lines up you get narrow, well-defined color breaks and downhill seams. When it doesn’t, the whole area looks and fishes like the milky inner Sound.

## How the outside exchange controls the bite

Exchange matters more than cloud cover here. The bite comes when the outside push peels a clean lane along the ridge flanks. Those cleaner fingers usually run parallel to the ridge rather than straight across it. The mechanical result is simple: a narrow saltier tongue pushes bait and scent into reduced cross-section, compression follows, and predators come to the edges.

Tidal behavior matters too. The outer approaches make local eddies and short-lived current necks around islands. A hard, raw sweep strips bait and smears marks. An easing tide or slight slack turns a wide, harsh sweep into a manageable, sweepable drift the halibut like.

## What bait, birds and sonar actually look like

When bait piles up the signs are consistent.

- You’ll see sand lance, capelin and small herring-like bait stacking on the outside faces and in the lee of saddles.
- Birds tighten up and work a narrow corridor, not a wide scatter.
- Sonar shows compact, mobile arches pinned to the downhill face with isolated bottom marks beneath.

A common sequence: bait compresses on the downhill face, birds and foamers work the clean lane, then bait disappears off the face as halibut move up to feed. When forage concentrates you can get halibut on the flats adjacent to rock marks with rockfish and cod mixed in — opportunistic, not picky.

## Which fisheries define the place

Pacific halibut are the main draw. The saddles and flats between the fingers give steady, sweepable drifts halibut prefer when the tide is right. Rockfish and lingcod sit the rockier ribs and isolated heads — usually converted with vertical work or short down-current drifts. Mixed bottomfish like cod and Pacific tomcod show up in deeper ribs and scoured pockets, especially when current softens and baits hang in the strike zone.

## When to pick these ridge fingers over inside options

Pick the ridge fingers when the outside shows a clean seam that hugs the ridges, you have a stable weather window for the run and return, and inside kelp points or river mouths are fouled with milky runoff or pressure. You’ll usually be fishing for bigger halibut and denser mixed-bottom marks than the inner shallow flats will produce.

Move inside when the exchange is raw and messy, when wind is against tide on the outside, or when your fuel or tunnel margins are tight. A marginal outside run can cost you speed home and safety; better to take a reliable inside mark than to push a cold return.

## Fuel, tunnel windows and operational margins

From Whittier this is a longer-commitment run. Plan for transit out, a sustained search, holding time on a mark, and a potentially slower return. Head seas and currents will slow you down.

Practical rules of thumb:
- Add a 25–30% fuel reserve to your outbound estimate for holding and searching.
- Factor tunnel/window timing into your plan — missing a scheduled vehicle window can cost you an extra hour or more.
- If the tide through Hinchinbrook is against you on the return, increase your fuel margin or pick an inside alternative.

If fuel or timing are tight, the smart call is inside kelp points and river mouths. You’ll drop the outside saddle opportunity, but gain reliability and a safer exit.

## How the spot behaves when it fishes — and when it doesn’t

When it fishes: a clean outside push peels a narrow lane over the ridge, bait stacks on downhill faces, birds tighten, sonar shows compact dense marks, and drifts are sweepable. Halibut bite predictably when the bait and current line up.

When it doesn’t: a strong exchange or big wind creates a raw shear that strips bait off the faces, smears marks, and scatters halibut. Thick glacial milky water that buries the ridge kills consistency — fish can still be present but inactive.

Pressure notes: this sector tolerates pressure better than tiny kelp points, but a crowded saddle still pushes foamers deeper and shortens the window. Good boats move to adjacent fingers or to a deeper flank rather than fighting for the same pass.

## Signs the window is building — and common mistakes that kill it

Look for these practical signs that the window is coming together:
- a clean tongue of saltier water hugging a ridge flank
- birds working a narrow tight corridor
- sonar showing bait stacked on the outside face with isolated bottom marks beneath
- current easing from a hard sweep into a steady sweep or slight slack

Common mistakes:
- Committing to one mark and ignoring adjacent flanks. These ridges run in fingers; fish move laterally.
- Running a hard-exchange tide expecting a normal drift. Too much current gives shallow, flat bites.
- Forgetting tunnel and fuel windows. Outer runs that turn marginal become expensive and unsafe fast.

## How the fleet treats these fingers

Experienced boats treat this as a line of targets, not a single GPS point. If one finger lights up they test neighboring saddles instead of stacking on top of each other. When inside water is quiet and the outside shows clean seams the fleet pushes out quickly — and visible foamers can collapse fast if boats pile in.

Information spreads fast; scouts and comms will move boats to either flank. If you arrive late your options are either to get on a flank early or plan to move when traffic compresses the bite.

## Search patterns and drift control from Whittier

Search until you find a clean seam, then work slow angled drifts across the saddles. Run the outside edge to pick up the saltier lane, then convert to short vertical drops over ribs if the marks show rockfish. Drift control is the piece that makes or breaks a day — keep the bait scent line in the strike zone for 30–60 seconds per pass when you can.

Presentations that win:
- Live or large-cut bait for halibut on the flats and saddles.
- Vertical jigs and heavier baits for rockfish on the ribs.
Be ready to shift from a slow sweep to tight vertical work quickly; these fingers can change target species in short order.

## Safety and tunnel timing reminders

Respect the weather window. The outer Sound builds fast; the return is where most boats get burned if they push a marginal forecast. Watch tides through the narrow passages — currents can be stronger and more confused than charts suggest, which affects fuel burn and drift control. Plan your tunnel timing into the exit and don’t bet on sprint speed home.

## Micro-observation — the simple test that tells you to stay or go

When the outside peel pins a narrow clean lane along the ridge fingers you’ll see the same set of signs everywhere: birds tighten, bait stacks, and halibut move from the flats for a predictable bite. If that clean lane vanishes, don’t hang on hoping it comes back — move inside and fish what the water gives you.
