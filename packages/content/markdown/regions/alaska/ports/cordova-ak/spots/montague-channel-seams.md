## The channel’s shape and what it makes the water do
Montague Channel isn’t a static patch of bottom you mark and forget. It’s a working corridor: a narrow throat between Montague Island’s north flank and the inner Sound’s islands and shoals. Current gets funneled, spills into basins, and interacts with steep faces, saddles and downhill gullies. That geometry is the engine — it compresses bait into seams and creates predictable drift angles when the tide is clean.

What you feel running it tells you more than the chart number. There are downhill pockets that hold fish and shallow saddles that stop baits. When the channel lines up with the Copper River plume edge, the staging lanes change fast and hard.

## Tide stages that actually matter here
Tide is the organizing factor. The channel behaves in three practical stages:

- Flood: the inside edges and lee pockets soften. Bait pushes into those spots and kings will sit the seams, ready to bolt through the corridor.
- Peak exchange: water races and rips form around points and headlands. You get whirl lines and back-eddies; birds tighten but working boats need respect — the water is unforgiving.
- Ebb and near‑slack: the compass for halibut. The current finger thins, drift speed lets you control presentation, and fish hang over saddles and downhill gutters.

Slack is short here; near‑slack is the only time you can set a reliable halibut drift. If the peak exchange is running, the channel will look active on sonar but be useless to hooks.

## How bait arranges itself and why fish follow
Herring are the big organizer. They compress into tight balls in lee-water and along steep island faces, and when they move the whole picture changes — birds follow, sonar lights up, and salmon move into the cleaner lanes. Sand‑lance and mixed forage push into shallow gutters and are the reason salmon stage on inside lanes.

Sonar and birds tell the story first. Foamers and tight bait marks usually mean there's a working lane; isolated big balls can be transient. When the plume muds into the channel, bait shifts — either into cleaner lanes or right along the plume edge, and that will change where kings and cohos look for food.

Common forage patterns to remember:
- Herring: tight in lee-water, hold near rock edges during tide changes.
- Sand lance/forage mix: shallow gutters and saddles — the staging reasons for salmon.
- Birds/foamers: immediate, but transient indicators of a working lane.

## When different fisheries use the corridor
This channel is a short, high-value run when the window opens. Seasonal timing is straightforward:

- Kings (Chinook): May through early July. They stage and transit with the Copper River run — mostly on flood and near‑peak flows when seams form.
- Sockeye & Coho: June through August. Sockeye and then coho use the shallower edges and cleaner lanes as the plume thins.
- Pacific halibut: May–September. Best on near‑slack tides where you can hold a slow controlled drift over saddles and sand gutters.
- Rockfish/lingcod: hang the nearshore rock heads and steep faces on calm days; good fallback if the main channel is blown or dirty.

## Reading the window — signs it’s building and when to commit
You learn to watch small things. A window builds quickly if current geometry, bait compression and a slack or manageable drift align. Watch for:

- birds dropping and working tight along a single seam
- sonar showing thin strip marks and bait sheets holding direction
- foamers holding steady direction instead of milling
- the plume edge running parallel to the channel rather than cutting it

When you see those, a clean bait road will attract boats fast. If one of those signals is missing, the channel tends to look tidy but fish slow.

## Fleet behavior and the pressure patterns that kill a bite
Locals stage inside early for kings, then spread for halibut as the tide softens. Visiting boats often hang on a single mark too long. The channel rewards movement — find the cleaner lane and commit, or you’ll watch it die.

Typical pressure reactions you’ll see:
- repeat drifts crowding a saddle until the bait scatters
- schools sinking deeper when boats anchor or hang
- foamers refusing casts after repeated passes

If you get in on a clean bait finger, expect the fleet to show up in ten minutes. If you don’t move when the lane shifts to the plume edge, you’ll miss the next good bite.

## Drift setup — what to run and how to present it
Setups here are simple but sensitive to angle and speed.

Halibut:
- near‑slack drifts only. Hold the boat off the downhill edge so your baits sweep across the flat; too much current and the presentation rides above the fish.

Salmon:
- fish flood and near‑peak. Stage inside lee pockets and work plugs or drift lines along the seam. When the plume edge sits against the island, expect fast, surface‑aware kings.

Sonar and angle:
- prefer long, thin bait roads over single big balls; those roads hold longer.
- small changes in drift angle matter — swing your drop slightly into the cleaner side of the seam when bait is stacked on the lee.

Quick tactical checklist:
- check where the plume sits against the channel
- pick your tide window and plan halibut around near‑slack
- run the lee side of points for salmon on flood
- be ready to move quickly to the plume edge or inside bays if the channel dirties

## Safety and access notes that matter
Runs from Orca Inlet are short but the channel concentrates flow. When wind works against tide you get confused seas and short steep chop. Peak exchange brings rips and eddies around Montague points — small boats should avoid tight lines during that period.

Practical safety points:
- avoid peak exchange in small boats — water is fast and unpredictable
- watch for tide‑against‑wind chop near island shoulders
- fog hides birds and boats; keep radar/plotter attention on crossing traffic and ferries
- have an inside plan to move to protected bays quickly if Gulf builds from the south

## Micro‑observations — the small things that change the day
The bait shows the fish before the pit does. When herring move into a lee, kings follow on the next tide. What usually matters most is not the same GPS number you fished last year but:

- tide timing and your ability to control the drift
- where the Copper River plume sits relative to the channel
- bait compression on the lee/downhill side

What matters less:
- running long exposed legs when a clean bait finger sits nearby
- hanging on a mark when the cleaner lane has moved

Why you keep checking this spot
Montague Channel isn’t about long runs — it’s about timing and finding the seam. When current, bait and a slack window line up you get focused salmon shots and manageable halibut drifts within a short run from Cordova. When they don’t, the channel looks neat and the boats drift quietly inside. That pattern repeats enough times to make it worth standing watch.
