Where the reef and kelp sit

Blackstone is a compact string of small reef heads, scattered boulder patches and kelp corridors just inside Orca Inlet. The relief is sharp for an inside spot: isolated rock pushing up through otherwise soft bottom, short downhill faces and little saddles where tide races past. You get pockets of shallow sand and gutters tucked between the kelp lines and the rock—small, repeatable pieces of structure rather than one big bank.

Why we run Blackstone

It's close, sheltered and predictable when the outer Sound is blown. Fish concentrate here because the relief is tight: bait pins to kelp edges and the downhill faces, rockfish suspend off the edge, lingcod sit tight in saddles and overhangs. It's also reliable fallback water for halibut drifts when slack or a moderate current lets you control the boat.

When the spot matters

Blackstone isn't a go-anytime place. It turns into a go-to when:

- the outer passes are closed and you need structure near Cordova,
- tide is moderate to falling and gives a clean, angled drift along the kelp/rock transition,
- the Copper River plume isn't washing mud into the immediate entrance—clearer water lets bait hug the edge.

Seasonally, rockfish are present year-round, but most pressure—guided and private—clusters from late May through August when weather opens up. Lingcod show best on early- to mid-summer drops when bait piles heavier in the kelp lanes.

How the life arranges itself

Observation first: bait—small herring and sand lance—tends to hug the kelp edge and stack on the downhill faces. Rockfish suspend over the edge or sit off the drop; lingcod bunker in boulder saddles, overhangs and the kelp fringe and ambush from nearby cover.

Why that pattern works: the kelp and abrupt relief make vertical targets and calm lee pockets. Current breaks off the heads create small calm zones where bait stacks; fish locate to those concentration points and to the adjacent flow seams that bring food past their noses.

Reading the window (signs it's working)

Watch bait on sonar and birds on the surface. The window builds when:

- you see a thin, persistent bait layer hugging the kelp edge on the screen,
- birds work tight and shallow over the kelp lanes,
- current rips past a head with a calm pocket behind it.

Those are the signals that bait is pinned and the edges are holding. If the exchange tide gets too strong or the plume clouds the inlet, the layer breaks and the spot quiets.

Drift angle and presentations that win

The place fishes on a drift. The best approach is to start up-current of the kelp edge and work an angled drift so hooks and jigs cross the downhill face rather than running broadside across heads.

Practical presentations:

- Rockfish: slow, bumped jigs or natural bait worked across the edge where fish will suspend—keep the presentation getting across the drop, not away from it.
- Lingcod: short, vertical presentations over saddles and near overhangs—slow lifts and falls with precise stops. They want something coming through the hole, not a wide cross-current pass.

Tactical sequence to try

1. Get set up up-current of the kelp/rock line.
2. Establish an angled drift so you cross the downhill face cleanly.
3. Watch sonar for bait hugging the edge and tighten the drift once you see it.
4. Use short, precise stops over boulder saddles for ling; longer, shallower crosses for suspended rockfish.

Boat control and the things that actually matter

What shifts the day here is drift angle and boat control, then where the bait sits relative to the kelp edge, then the tide stage. Too much exchange ruins a clean drift. What matters less is punching the same GPS from past years without reading bait and current—Blackstone changes with the day.

Fleet behavior and common mistakes

Locals work it in short windows and move out when the Sound opens. Common mistakes:

- Grinding a single head during heavy exchange—fish tuck deeper or shift to untouched pockets.
- Letting your drift go broadside across heads instead of angling across the drop.

A better approach is to work several nearby heads, adjust your drift angle, and treat each head like its own little spot.

Safety and access notes

The run is short but can be awkward with southerly wind through the inlet. Some heads are shallow and chart detail around isolated rocks can be misleading. Kelp hides marks that look fishy but are tight on snags, and current can pin you against kelp.

Practical safety pointers:

- use conservative approach speeds and local charts in the narrow channels,
- avoid running the inner mouth on a strong flood against wind—it can pile tide into awkward angles,
- remember protected doesn't mean easy in fog or sudden southerlies.

Micro-observation

On a morning when birds landed tight on the kelp line, the first stop pulled a lingcod. The bait was stacked, the drift crossed the downhill face—simple geometry, quick result.

How I leave it

Blackstone is useful, close water: high-return when tide and bait sharpen the edge, quiet and tight when the plume mucks the inlet or the exchange tide blows hard. Read the bait, control the drift, and treat the heads like a string of small, independent pieces of structure instead of one big spot.
