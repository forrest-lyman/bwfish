Eyak is the closest working mouth to Cordova. It isn’t dramatic — a shallow delta, a narrow scoured lane and a nearshore transition where fresh water peels into Orca Inlet — but that modest geometry does a lot of organizing. When the pieces line up the lane becomes a short, predictable holding and feeding strip; when they don’t it’s blank water and a lot of boats looking.

## Why the Eyak lane consistently stages fish
You can see it in the geometry: a fresh-water flush, tidal exchange from Orca Inlet and just enough nearshore relief. The result is not a big open-ocean edge but a narrow seam — cleaner finger against muddier water — where bait compresses and salmon slow down to feed or sit and wait.

Observed patterns:
- Salmon moving up the Copper system and coastal migrants frequently slow at that fresher water on the incoming tide.
- The nearshore bottom (sand/gravel rolling into small rock heads and kelp) gives the lane enough structure to hold rockfish and feed salmon without requiring a run out to the outer sound.
In short: small-scale features, predictable behavior. The lane’s simplicity is what makes it fishable.

## How tide and the freshwater peel control the window
This place is tide-driven. Flood tide pulls fish toward the mouth and pins bait on that fresher-water line. Slack and the early part of the flood are your useful windows for most salmon work.

Watch the peel and local eddies more than color alone. A thin, clean finger can hold a dense ball of bait and trigger feeding. A broad muddy plume tends to push active salmon deeper or downstream. On days with strong exchange the flow becomes confused, birds scatter and the whole seam blows out; on manageable exchange the fish stage predictably along the peel and the bite is easier to read.

Current reversals matter: when the exchange flips hard the lane fills with chaotic flow. When the exchange is moderate, the peel holds and you get a short, sharp window.

## Reading the window as it builds
The signs pile up in a predictable order — watch the surface and the fleet first, sonar second.

Key signals you’ll see before the bite:
- Birds tighten over a narrow, slightly cleaner strip.
- Trollers shorten lines or switch to shallower diver settings; they’ll also add flat lines when the seam narrows.
- Sonar shows broken marks and bait just inside the peel.
- Boats ease their drift angle to ride current toward the mouth.

Those signals tell you the seam is compressing bait and the fish are stacking. When they line up, the window often opens quickly and closes just as quickly.

## Bait and which species use which part of the lane
Bait is small schooling forage and plankton pushed out of the inlet and delta. It’s compressed along the seam rather than spread across the shelf.

Observed timing and species behavior:
- Kings: often first on the early-season floods, taking advantage of that fresher water.
- Sockeye: peak mid-June through July, staging tight on the seam when it forms.
- Coho: show up later into summer, using the same lane but often on different tide timing or as the bite moves deeper.
- Rockfish and lingcod: sit on the nearshore relief and kelp, taking baits during slack or when boats move inside protected structure.

Think of the lane as a conveyor: bait gets squashed against the peel and the species rotate through predictable timing windows.

## How boats actually fish the lane
The fleet behavior mirrors the seam. First boats see the signs and the rest follow — which makes spacing and timing part of the tactic.

Typical approaches:
- Early-morning runs: most boats stage close, find the color seam and birds, then set controlled trolls or light-drift bait presentations.
- Trolling: short lines and shallower diver settings when the lane tightens. The first boats often get the cleaner bite; pressure changes everything.
- Drift/live-bait: anchorless drifts holding direction into the mouth on a rising tide. Keep drift speed slow enough for fish to pick up baits without burning them off.
- Bottom work: short hops on small heads and kelp edges during slack or when the mouth is blown out to the south.

If you arrive late and the seam is already tight, consider working off the peel rather than fighting for the shallowest line.

## Common mistakes that kill the bite
Experience shows the same errors over and over. They’re avoidable if you read the water.

Frequent errors:
- Committing to the mouth on a heavy exchange tide — flow gets messy and fish scatter.
- Fishing a broad, muddy plume with the same tactics you use on a thin clean finger.
- Holding too close to shore when the current peels offshore a few boat-lengths — fish ride the seam, not the shoreline.

Correcting those usually means moving off when the peel widens, or changing to deeper presentations when the plume smears.

## When the lane is live and when it shuts down
Live: a clear finger of fresher water pushing into Orca Inlet on a rising tide; birds concentrated; bait compressed at the peel; manageable wind and short fetch from Cordova. Those conditions give you a short, intense window.

Dead: broad muddy plume, sudden south swell, tide running hard against wind, or thick fog hiding birds and fleet cues. On those days the lane either disperses bait or the fish move deeper/down the coast.

## Fleet pressure and how experienced boats behave
Local boats drop in early for kings and then compress the lane as the flood starts. When a clean seam forms the fleet converges — the bite can be very brief.

Experienced crews:
- Give the lane space and reposition off the peel rather than crowding the shallows.
- Read the first boats’ setups and adapt quickly; sometimes backing off by a few boat-lengths produces better, longer bites.

Pressure is as much a factor as current here.

## Safety, access, and simple boat handling
Run from Cordova is short when wind and fetch are light, but Orca Inlet will build on a Gulf wind and close options fast. Nearshore bars and shifting sand around the mouth are real — charts help, but local pilots run sounders and use visual cues.

If the tide is running hard against wind, don’t force the mouth; move inside to protected bays. Depth sounders, a conservative approach to the sandbars, and reading the fetch are the simple safety measures that keep you fishing longer.

## Quick field notes
- Birds tighten before sonar reliably shows bait.
- When the peel narrows the bite window often collapses to about an hour.

Tactical takeaway
Read the peel, time the flood, and expect short, pressure-driven windows. Eyak is short water that fishes predictably when the seam is clear — get there early or pick another play.
