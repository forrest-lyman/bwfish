This is small water inside the Narrows, visible from Ketchikan. Think of it as a handful of seams rather than a single mark: the lee side of finger points, the down‑current edge of kelp beds, the sheltered strip below ferry routes and dock piles. Current peels, bait stacks, and scent hangs there. The patches reappear in the same places every tide if the pieces line up.

## Where the lee pockets form
The features repeat in a pattern. The main flow of the Narrows runs fast and narrow; the pocket is the place that flow changes angle and sheds energy.

- behind finger points where the main flow peels off
- the down‑current edges of kelp fingers and isolated paddies
- small rock ledges and boulder fingers that sit under kelp
- shallow sand gutters and flats tucked between kelp and steep shore
- the sheltered side of docks and timber where wake and current meet

It’s not a single GPS book mark. You run the contours, find the tidy eddy or bait ribbon, and work it until the seams move.

## What actually holds fish
Fish follow bait and scent. The lee gives both.

Kelp fingers and paddies make narrow channels where bait compresses. Small ledges and boulders give vertical relief to hide behind once a boat shows up. Sand pockets and shallow gutters are the halibut spots—those fish sit on the edge where the flat meets structure. Dock pilings and shallow flats give easy refuge and quick concentrations after a ferry pass.

I watch where the bait lies on the sounder more than an exact depth number—the shape of the baitline tells you where a fish will sit relative to the kelp or rock.

## How tide, current and ferries shape the bite
Tongass Narrows is a working hydraulic. Tides carve the seams; ferries and cruise wakes add friction and move bait.

- Strong exchange (full flood or full ebb): bait compresses and moves along the channel. Salmon will stage and push through these seams but halibut drifts are harder to control.
- Easing tide / short slack: the pocket tightens. Halibut hold better on the flats and gutters; bottom presentations come together. Rockfish will move off the immediate face and respond to short verticals.
- Ferry movement: a ferry will disturb bait, then leave a denser band in the lee. That refill window is short but often productive.
- Repeated boat traffic: after a few passes fish tighten to the immediate relief or move deeper; surface and near‑surface presentations can shut off.

A small but reliable micro‑sign: gulls often sit along the ferry lee before the sounder shows a distinct bait ribbon. Birds give you the first read more often than anything else.

## Primary targets and behavior
- Pacific halibut: small to medium fish on sand pockets and gutters near kelp. Best on the easing side into slack and the short period after slack when you can hold a drift. They stage on the outer edge of the lee where bait and scent funnel across the flat.
- Salmon (Chinook, later coho): show up when bait is being swept along the channel during stronger exchange. Short, intense windows—fish move fast in tight runs and stop behavior.
- Rockfish: hold tight to vertical relief and kelp roots. They come to short vertical presentations when the tide is moderate; too much scour and they lock tight to the face.

## Signs a window is building
Watch the bait, birds, and ferry schedule. The pocket gives clear, quick signals.

- birds holding a steady line rather than being scattered
- bait stacked tight against a kelp edge on the sounder
- marks rising near the ledge instead of a flat, uniform layer
- a ferry has just passed and the lee is holding a tidy ribbon

When two or three of those line up, the next 20–40 minutes are usually worth working hard.

## Typical tide windows
Match the presentation to the tide.

- Halibut: final easing into slack and the first half‑hour after slack—drift control is manageable and fish spread out onto the flats.
- Salmon: stronger flood or ebb—bait is moving and the corridor fishes on the move.
- Rockfish: steady, moderate flow—enough water to bring fish off the face but not so much that everything clamps down.

## How fish respond to pressure
The Narrows is close water and fish change quickly under pressure.

- Halibut will slip deeper or off the flat after repeated drifts.
- Salmon push harder and faster if multiple boats bunch on the same running line.
- Rockfish pull tight to the immediate face and stop taking surface or long presentations.

If you work the pocket and the marks tighten or move off, the next logical move is depth or distance—get deeper, or get out of the beaten running lines.

## Practical approach and run plan
Treat this like a short, diagnostic check more than a marathon.

Start with a quick run on a tidy tide to see where the bait is stacked. If the lee holds bait after a ferry pass, you have a predictable 20–40 minute window—work it with targeted drifts and short presentations. If bait scatters or boats pile in, move before the fish redistribute.

Keep runs short and checks frequent. Use the pocket as an early read of the day’s bait distribution before committing fuel to outer runs. If the pocket fishes well and the ferry schedule cooperates, you can put together a tidy, close‑in bite; if not, use what you learned and move.

## Safety and local realities
This water is inside busy lanes. Respect the main channel and the wakes.

- Give ferry lanes wide berth; ferries and cruise vessels produce wakes that can upend small boats or fouled gear.
- Narrow‑channel currents near points can be deceptively strong—plan anchor and drift angles with current in mind.
- Fog and rain hide visual cues (birds, other boats). Rely on AIS, VHF, and sounder references tucked into the lee.
- Kelp and floating debris collect after storms. Lines and props foul quickly; be ready to clear gear.

The pocket is small water with a specific rhythm. Read the birds, watch the ferry schedule, manage drift, and pick the slack you want. When the pieces line up it’s a tidy, close‑in bite; when they don’t, it’s a good place for a fast check and to save fuel for the outer banks.
