## What the lane actually is

This spot isn’t a single rock or a named flat — it’s the seam where Behm Canal peels into the wider channel outside Ketchikan. Think kelp fingers, small lee pockets off points, shallow sand gutters that step quickly into a 60–120‑ft downhill scarp, and a narrow current seam that compresses bait. The flow funnels fish into a tight corridor; when the peel is tidy you get a predictable staging lane for salmon and a short, sharp halibut intercept.

## How the water behaves — reading the peel

The bite here is driven by how the canal’s outflow meets the channel. That meeting either peels cleanly as a single seam or shears into scattered eddies. The clean peel pins bait against kelp and points and creates a shallow-to-deep corridor boats can drift across.

- Floods that shove fresh bait into the mouth give sharp, short salmon windows.
- Strong exchange will push the seam tight against kelp necks and points — that’s prime for halibut and bigger rockfish sitting on the edge.
- Slack and easing tides matter more here than on open shelves; halibut need a predictable, slow drift angle to stay in the strike zone while salmon stage on a tidally compressed bait line.

Watch the seam move laterally with the tide. If it shifts off your GPS mark, move the boat — the fish follow the peel, not the waypoint.

## What actually holds the bait and fish

Structure is simple and repeated:

- kelp necks and fingers around small islets that trap and concentrate forage;
- sand/mud gutters and shallow flats just inside the mouth that step into a steep downhill scarp;
- narrow current seams where the Behm outflow meets the channel and compresses forage into a tight strip.

Fish use the seam differently. Salmon stage in the compressed bait against kelp and points; halibut sit on the down‑current edge of gutters and the downhill step, waiting for the drift to soften.

## Where to run from Ketchikan and when to go

- Typical run time: 30–60 minutes from Ketchikan, depending on tide and how far up the mouth you run.
- Faster on a planing boat with the tide; allow extra time in fog or when ferries are running.

Season timing (what we see most years):
- Early season (May–June): Chinook staging on strong tidal exchange.
- Mid to late summer: coho windows and tidy halibut intercepts when slack or easing tides hold surface bait.

Pick days with settled weather and planned ferry timing. If the forecast’s marginal, this place turns into a safety and pressure headache instead of an opportunity.

## Signs a window is building

You’ll know it before the fish hook up if you watch bait and birds:

- birds holding a single narrow line instead of scattered picks;
- bait balls compressed against a kelp neck or point;
- sounder marks streaking up from the deeper edge toward the kelp;
- a visible seam tightening into a single current line on the surface.

When birds lock to a clean seam, the fleet stacks up quickly. Expect the bite to be fast and localized.

## How to position the boat and run the drift

Run a controlled drift across the seam. Salmon want a consistent bait flow; halibut want the downhill edge as the drift eases.

- On a tidy exchange, angle your drift so the presentation crosses the seam and slides past kelp necks and point leeward pockets.
- On strong exchange, concentrate on the lee side of points and the down‑current edge of kelp necks — fish sit in the calmer water there.
- Anchor or slow‑drift only when the tide backs off into a workable slack. If you anchor too early in heavy flow you’re either out of the strike zone or sitting in chaotic water.
- If ferries are due, position outside their wake and be ready to pause the drift; ferry wash will spook foamers and rearrange bait.

Drive for small adjustments. The seam moves; frequent short repositions beat following a stale GPS mark.

## Salmon vs. halibut — where they sit

- Salmon (Chinook early, then coho): they stage in the shallow seam where bait is compacted against kelp and points. Look for moving marks just off the kelp or birds working a narrow strip.
- Pacific halibut: best intercepts are on adjacent sand gutters and the downhill edge once the drift softens — prime time is easing tide or near slack when scent and presentation linger in the strike zone.

Don’t expect long, broad halibut drifts here. The intercept is short and tied to tidal timing.

## Fleet behavior and pressure

This channel sees ferries, tugs, and occasional cruise traffic. Ferries use predictable lanes that sometimes overlap the seam. Boat noise and repeated passes either compress or scatter foamers quickly.

The usual reactions:
- When gulls lock on a clean seam, the fleet stacks up and the windows are brief.
- Repeated anchoring and high boat density push halibut deeper and off the flat.
- Foamers often show for a few passes, then scatter along the channel when boats cast repeatedly.

Plan for quick windows and expect other boats to chase the same line.

## Common pressure reactions

- Salmon windows compress to the immediate kelp edge and close after a few passes.
- Halibut move deeper and off the flat under heavy pressure.
- Foamers show briefly, then streak longitudinally when boats keep casting.

When pressure’s high, change your approach quickly — move off the direct edge, work the next pocket, or wait for the tide to reset.

## Hazards and practical safety

- Fog is routine; the run back through Narrows needs attention in low visibility. Ferries and tugs are loud but not always visible.
- Current can switch quickly — plan your drift and exit with the tidal table in hand.
- Give ferries wide berth; their wash displaces bait and can make the drift unusable.
- Floating timber and debris collect in seams after storms. Watch speed and prop clearance.

Respect the traffic and the tide. This spot rewards patience and attention more than horsepower.

## What usually matters more — and less

What usually matters more:
- current angle and seam tightness;
- clean bait pinned against kelp or points;
- choosing slack or early easing tide for halibut work.

What usually matters less:
- lingering on a single GPS mark when the seam has moved;
- chasing a scattered foamer without a sustained bait line.

## Micro observation

When the birds hold a hard, single line into the mouth, the best salmon bites come fast and in a narrow strip. The halibut work waits for the tide to soften — don’t try to force a long halibut drift on a tight exchange. Watch the bait and the birds; they’ll tell you when to move and when to sit tight.
