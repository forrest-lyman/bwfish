## How the saddle and points organize bait

This spot isn't one big feature — it's a string of small decisions the water forces on bait. Points, kelp fingers, short saddles and sand gutters line up with the channel exchange and create tight seams. You see it in the flow: clean exchange down the main, quick eddies tucked behind points, and bait pinned just down-current of kelp necks.

Those micro-features act predictably. Flow accelerates across shallow ridges, slows into adjacent gutters, and bait compresses where speed changes. Halibut like the slower gutters below a saddle; salmon and surface feeders show on the faster peel-off lines.

Read the tide first, then the features. The saddle tells you where the bait will be a long time before the fish commit.

## Where to run from Ketchikan

This is a close, portable spot:

- 15–45 minutes from the marina depending on whether you fish inner points or push into the wider saddles.
- Inner kelp/point checks inside Tongass Narrows are quick — 15–25 minutes.
- Larger saddles and mouths of side fjords take 30–45+ minutes.

Because it's close, skippers can cycle the area during a single tide window without burning a lot of fuel. That matters when pressure and timing shrink the bite.

## Structure you'll actually be fishing

Most of the useful structure is subtle:

- kelp fingers and small kelp patches off point noses
- rocky ledges with short downhill faces into sand gutters
- shallow saddles between deeper channel cuts — sand pockets and rock heads
- tight lee pockets and eddies immediately down-current of points

These are short, repeatable features. Learn the necks and gutters and you’ll know where to park the drift.

## Tide, current and traffic — the clock that runs the spot

Tide does the work here. The channel exchange makes the bite appear on a predictable clock:

- Points create tight seams and lee pockets on flood or ebb; bait piles in the lee and along downhill faces.
- Saddles funnel bait — the flow peels across the ridge and the adjacent gutters hold the softer water where halibut sit.
- The better current usually clings to the channel edge. When that flow is clean, bait compresses between it and the shoreline; when wind or fog messes the exchange the marks scatter.

Traffic matters. Ferries and cruise lanes run set tracks through the narrows. Their wakes will collapse surface bait and spook fish briefly, but once the water settles that same action can help group bait. Local boats watch ferry schedules and plan crossings with margin.

## Bait, birds and the rest of the life

If you watch the bait and birds the rest follows:

- Typical forage: small herring and sand lance in summer; shrimp and mysids in cleaner, warmer pockets; migrating smolt and juvenile herring during salmon windows.
- Birds tighten to the seams — gulls and terns often point to the bait line before your sounder does.
- Rockfish and lingcod hang on the vertical relief. Halibut sit off sand/rock transitions and in lee gutters.
- Salmon stage along the faster exchange on the channel face — spring kings earlier, coho later.

The bait signature changes with tide and season; learn the look of compressed bait in the lee and the thin, scattered marks when the exchange is off.

## How the spot fishes — patterns to trust

When it's working the surface often looks ordinary but everything underneath lines up: bait pinned to a point, birds holding a deliberate line, sonar marks stacked off the edge. When it's dead the water still looks tidy — no birds, bait scattered, marks thin. That usually means the current is off-angle or the tide window isn't right.

Pressure shortens windows. The first passes after a seam builds are usually best; repeated runs push foamers deeper. If the seam goes soft, move early and find the next lee pocket.

Practical drift notes: controlled halibut drifts over saddles produce the most consistent legal fish. The biggest halibut come from deeper channel cuts, but the saddles will give steady action if you can control drift speed.

## Fleet behavior and how pressure changes the bite

Local skippers run a simple hierarchy: quick point checks and kelp necks early, then set up controlled halibut drifts on a preferred saddle as the tide eases. Private boats tend to stick the inner points while charters stage the larger saddles farther out.

Information travels fast here. A single good seam will draw a line of boats and narrow the window. Experienced boats time their entries around ferry movements and try to approach the seam in a way that won’t push the foamers before the drift is set.

## Signals a window is building

Watch for these practical signs — they come fast and give you a short decision window:

- birds holding a steady line instead of scattered feeding
- bait compressed into the lee of a point or against a kelp neck
- sonar marks lining up along a shallow ridge or gutter
- current settling into a steady drift speed (not a runaway)

If two or three of those line up, get set. The best bites are often in the first hour after the seam forms.

## Typical tide windows by species

Halibut
- Best when the tide softens: the hour before slack into the first hour after slack on a lee side. Too much flow makes a controlled drift impossible.

Rockfish and lingcod
- Fishable through more of the cycle but tighter on a moderate flood or ebb that moves bait past the face.

Salmon
- Favor stronger exchange: steeper flood or ebb through the channel that peels bait off the bank and stages it along the seam.

Match your approach to the species and the clock. If the tide is running hard and you want halibut, find a lee pocket or wait the slack.

## Common mistakes I see

- Trying to fish a saddle during peak exchange without a plan to control drift speed.
- Ignoring ferry and cruise schedules — wakes change drift angles and move bait.
- Repeating the same pass until the foamers refuse — better to move and reset on a fresh lee pocket.
- Overweighting halibut rigs on the saddle; lighter setups and attention to angle keep hooks in fish.

## Safety in the narrows

This place has narrow lanes and predictable traffic. Keep a working radar/commo plan and watch ferry timetables. Visibility can change fast — favor inner points or a conservative return route if the forecast hints at fog or rain. Avoid runs home with wind against tide; a short passage can become uncomfortable or unsafe quickly. After storms, expect floating debris and extra kelp in seams — props and gear can foul.

## Quick tactical checklist

- Run the points first to find the bait line.
- When birds and bait line up, set a single, controlled halibut drift off the saddle — lighten weight, watch drift angle.
- Use kelp necks for vertical work on rockfish and quick lingcod moves when the tide is steady.
- Move early if bait scatters; don’t burn a tide beating a dead seam.
- Time entries around ferry schedules to avoid busting the foamers.

## Why we keep checking this spot

It’s close, readable and repeatable. The features are small but they organize bait reliably. You don’t need to go far to buy good odds — read the tide, watch the birds, and the channel will tell you where to put the drift. This spot rewards attention more than horsepower.
