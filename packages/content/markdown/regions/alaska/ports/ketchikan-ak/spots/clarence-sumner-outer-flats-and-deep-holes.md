The place is a pattern more than a point: wide sand-and-mud flats that funnel bait into scoured 60–150 ft holes and steep trough mouths. Rocks and short ledge faces interrupt the flats and create downhill seams where halibut and stacked rockfish hang. When tide-driven bait rides the current, the whole area fishes like a single unit.

## The pattern: flats that feed holes and ribs

What you see first is flat—sand and mud that shoal up into pockets. Then there are the holes: scoured, steep-sided, 60–150 ft deep depending on the tide and how far out you run. Between them are rubble ribs and short ledge faces where the flat drops into the main channel. Kelp and isolated rock show up near the edges.

Observation: halibut use the flats and the downhill mouths. Bigger fish and stacked rockfish sit deeper in the holes and troughs. Explanation: the current pushes bait off the flat and concentrates scent and forage at the hole entrances and along the ledge faces.

## Getting there and running the water

From Ketchikan this is the outer end of the local road—plan 30–90 minutes depending on tide, weather, and how far up the straits you go. If you're working the near flats you can make short runs; expect to burn more fuel and daylight when chasing the deep holes or when the fleet spreads out.

Practical point: pick your run based on the tide window you want to fish. If you need the slack or the first easing ebb, get there early and don't waste time drifting the inside water when the outer holes are about to come on.

## Tide-driven exchange controls everything

Observation: the area is ruled by tidal exchange from Clarence and Sumner channels. The flow sets where the bait goes and where the fish stack.

- Strong exchange compresses fish to downhill edges and squeezes bait into troughs. In heavy flow a free drift across the flat becomes useless.
- Easing or slack tides clean up the drift and are when halibut take off the flats.
- The angle of the tidal sweep matters more than the raw height—a current that runs along the flat into a hole is far better than the same speed running crosswise.
- Ferries and commercial traffic use the same lanes; their wash will scatter birds and bait and alter your drift.

Why it matters: think of the holes as places where the current concentrates bait and scent. If the flow is right you can deliver the bait into the hole mouth on a steady drift; if it's wrong you chase marks that won't hold.

## Where the bait shows and what it tells you

Observation: sand eels, shrimp and occasional small schools sweep the flats on tide change. Cleaner water or river plumes can compress small schooling forage against ledges.

- Birds are subtle here. A tidy line of gulls or terns working a seam usually means bait funneled along a current and fish beneath. If birds line a channel seam, check the adjacent hole mouth.
- Rockfish and lingcod will be on the faces and ribs; halibut will be out on the flat or tucked in the downhill mouths.

Why it matters: bait location gives you the vector. If bait is sliding into a hole, set a drift to meet it there. If bait is stacking on a face, slow vertical presentations win.

## Typical windows and how to time them

Observation from local runs: halibut usually show best on the last hour of flood into slack and the first hour of ebb easing. Salmon stage earlier on firm exchange lines (spring kings) and coho arrive later on cleaner, shallower bait seams.

How to use it: aim to have your drift settle into the hole mouth as the flow eases. If the exchange is ripping, move to a lee pocket or a deeper trough where you can make a controlled pass. For rockfish, fish when current crosses the face—vertical relief becomes active when bait sits on it.

## Signs that a window is building

Watch for these signals on approach; they're simple and repeatable:

- Small schools of bait riding toward a hole mouth
- Sonar marks lifting off the flat and rising along a face
- Birds holding a neat line instead of scattered working birds
- Boats that were trolling hauling up and switching to drifts

Those signs mean bait and scent are compressing into a predictable seam. When you see two or more, you should be set up and ready.

## Fleet behavior and pressure

Observation: charters and locals stage this water by tide and risk. On marginal days they work short halibut drifts on the inside flats; on good tides they run to the deeper holes. Pressure shows quickly—popular holes get pounded and fish slide to deeper edges.

Use the fleet: a quiet approach pays. If a hole is hammered, listen to where boats move next and look for the adjacent trough. Watch ferry schedules—ferry tracks will wreck a drift and scatter birds for a while.

## Drift control and presentations

Observation: drift control beats cover. The successful boats set drifts so bait and scent arrive at the hole mouth in sync.

Tactics I use and why they work:
- Set a drift that times the bait into the hole mouth—angle matters more than being on a single GPS mark.
- If the flow is ripping, fish the downhill edge or the channel margin with short, controlled drifts rather than long free drifts.
- When the sounder shows bait or fish stacking on a face, slow presentations win: small chunks, natural baits, short-leader live setups.
- If birds are working a narrow seam, run a parallel drift and be ready to make quick passes into holes where bait funnels.

The principle: match presentation speed to how the bait is moving. Too fast and the halibut won’t commit; too slow on a ripping day and the bait disappears.

## What usually matters more—and less

Do more of:
- getting the drift angle right into the hole mouth
- finding small patches of cleaner water over the flat
- timing slack or easing tide for a controlled presentation

Worry less about:
- staying glued to a famous hole during a hard-exchange day
- long trolling runs when the flats are holding bait and the current favors drifting

## Safety and run planning

Routine hazards here are practical: fog and rain, ferries and commercial traffic, and floating debris after storms.

- Give extra fuel and time for returns in poor visibility.
- Keep a conservative station when ferries approach and avoid stopping in obvious ferry tracks.
- Strong exchange and eddy lines make anchoring or slow drifts unpredictable—carry tide tables and plan slack returns.
- Watch for floating logs and debris when holding near kelp or shallow ribs.

The outer holes are forgiving in calm conditions and problematic if wind stacks against the tide. Plan runs so you get the window and a safe ride back.

## Micro-observations, common mistakes, and when to walk away

Micro-observation: the bait rides the exchange. When the flat cleans and bait funnels into a hole, halibut follow—and often come in under the birds rather than ahead of them.

Common mistakes:
- sticking to a famous hole during a ripping exchange
- running big, fast presentations over tight, shallow flats during slack
- ignoring ferry schedules and the turbulence they bring to a drift

When to walk: if the current is three knots plus and there's no lee, the halibut work collapses. Move to a sheltered flat, a lee pocket, or shift to ledges where vertical presentations still make sense.

This water rewards simple reads: watch the bait, pick the right slack, match your drift to the seam, and treat holes as part of a system rather than a single mark.
