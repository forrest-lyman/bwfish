## Why these north saddles matter

The north saddles are not one big feature; they're a string of shallow saddles and narrow shelf fingers that break the basin into predictable drifts. What matters here is movement, not contours on a chart. When the tide fingers across a saddle you get a clean sweep of current that lines bait up against the shallow side and creates short, repeatable windows. When the tide is wrong the same ridge looks dead on sonar. Fish follow the bait—and the saddle funnels the bait.

## How the current usually behaves

Observe first: when the tide is moderate and steady you see a neat flow over the saddle and then a peel down the outer flank of a finger. Sonar shows tight bait bands and a clear drift line. If the current is blown and chaotic your gear drags; if slack, bait scatters and nothing concentrates.

Direction is important. A flood that cuts across the saddle tends to stage salmon on the shallow side; an ebb that peels outward sets halibut tight to the down‑current base. So you’re not just watching speed—you’re watching angle.

## Signs a short window is building

The simplest, most useful signals are observational:

- A steady bait band hugging the shallow side on sonar.
- Birds tightening up and forming lines along the fingers instead of milling.
- Boats holding the same drift angle rather than fanning wide.
- Mid‑column marks rising toward the surface as the tide starts moving.

These are the signals you act on. When they show, the bite usually follows quickly and doesn't last long.

## Timing: when the windows appear

You get windows on schedule, not randomly.

- Late May–July: early kings will stage on a tidal push over the saddles. Best on the first manageable tide of the day.
- June–August: coho and pinks move through in pulses tied to upriver timing—short, sharp windows inside the fingers.
- May–September: halibut are interceptable on drifts along the down‑current face; the bigger fish often sit on quieter saddles away from the tightest pressure lines.

Put another boat on the water during those months and you’ll see these rhythms repeat.

## How salmon and halibut use the saddle

Salmon ride the bait conveyor. They stage on the shallow, up‑tide side and in the lee of the fingers where bait is concentrated and travel is feedable. Halibut sit where the current drops—saddle bases and adjacent sand pockets—using scent and the slowed water to ambush prey. They want the bait near bottom without a constant drag; that’s when they feed.

## Working the spot — what to do on the water

Start inside and confirm bait. Local boats often do a quick check of the inside mouths and commit only when birds or sonar show a steady band. Your drift should cross the shallow side and sweep down into the finger.

For salmon, present trolled plugs or flasher rigs across the bait band so the fish have to move through your offering. For halibut, slow, controlled drifts with bait or a vertical jig that stays near bottom are the ticket. The objective is simple: keep the bait in the strike band.

Follow the bait, not the GPS waypoint. Mark the bait band and the clean line into the finger on your sonar, then run that line again.

## Tactical checklist (short and usable)

- Drift angle: aim to cross the shallow side and sweep down the face.
- Drift control: adjust bow and boat speed to keep gear in the strike band; change motor or gearing if the line lifts.
- Sonar work: mark the bait band and the cleaner line into the finger—let the bait dictate placement.
- Boat placement: staggered drifts work better under pressure; don’t sit in the obvious path once the fleet compresses.

These are practical fixes you can use without guessing.

## Fleet pressure and common reactions

The fleet compresses the shallow side fast. After a few boats the fish space out or sink deeper. Foamers will push the bait and then often refuse repeated pass-throughs; that can flip an action quickly.

If pressure builds, your two best replies are: move to a quieter saddle a mile or two along the ridge, or change presentation depth and drift angle. Often a small change in angle makes a dead line live again.

## Access, launch windows, and safety notes

Petersburg’s harbor makes this a reasonable first‑mile run, but there are navigation and timing considerations. Narrow channels and commercial traffic matter—time your departure and return with the tide. A building flood into the sound can tighten anchorages and complicate a return, especially in smaller boats.

Watch for log booms, seine gear, and tugs near the approaches. Fog erases bird work; when visibility drops rely on sonar, radar and local waypoints rather than following birds.

Carry fuel margin and plan conservative offshore limits. The north saddles are close, but weather and traffic can turn a short run into a long one.

## Small observations that pay off

On the best halibut days a clean bait band on the shallow side starts the day and a measured drift down the finger finishes it. If the bait disappears but marks stay on the drop, the halibut likely slid deeper—change angle or slow the drift and you’ll often pick them off.

## When to walk away

If the bait is scattered, birds have left, and multiple boats have run identical drifts without result, move. The next saddle or a quieter finger will often hold what the pressured water just gave up. Don’t bury yourself on one mark hoping for a miracle when the current and bait have moved on.

## What usually decides a trip here

What matters most: the exact drift angle through the saddle, bait concentration and depth, and timing your run with a usable tide.

What matters less: hugging the same GPS mark after pressure builds, or banking on one lucky cast when the current has already changed.

## Bottom line

These north saddles give you a tidy mechanical fishery when tide and bait line up: short, predictable windows for salmon and repeatable halibut drifts when the angle’s right. Read the bait band, control the drift, and respect the tide and local traffic—those are the details that decide whether you leave with fish or just a good line on the sound.
