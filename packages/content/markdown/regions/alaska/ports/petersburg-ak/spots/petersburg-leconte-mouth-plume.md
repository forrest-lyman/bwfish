LeConte’s mouth behaves like a short conveyor belt. Meltwater, tannin and silt peel out of the fjord, form a tidy stained tongue against clearer sound water, and carry bait along a narrow line. Fish and birds read that color break like a road sign: salmon tend to stack just outside the stain, and rockfish and lingcod hang the kelp/reef margins where the dirty water meets structure.

## Why the plume makes a tight staging lane
You can see it before you catch anything — a distinct ribbon of color that sits against cleaner water. That ribbon runs close to kelp edges, small reef fingers and point lee pockets instead of spreading across a broad flat. The result is a condensed transit where bait, birds and fish line up in a short, focused lane.

Observation first: bait rides the edge, birds follow the same direction along the stain, and boats cluster where the color breaks. Explanation second: the fjord outflow compresses forage into a narrow strip and the adjacent structure gives predators a place to work that compressed bait.

## When the window opens (and when it closes)
The best windows are short and tidal. I watch tide movement first, color and bait second.

Seasonal timing you’ll recognize:
- Late May–July: kings will stage on fjord mouths and can be taken on tide changes.
- June–August: coho and pinks show up in short, productive bursts.
- May–September: rockfish and lingcod are reliable where kelp and rock meet the stain.

Daily timing: the sweet 45–90 minute stretches usually form as the tide begins to move — early flood or early ebb depending on which way the plume peels that day. Long daylight and calm seas make the tongue visible and extend the window; fog or chop collapses it fast.

## How the water moves here
The plume peels off the fjord as a ribbon. On a modest flood the stained tongue will press against outside kelp lines; on ebb it thins and retreats. Wind changes the geometry: southeast wind tends to shove the plume inward and muddle the lee, northwest wind sharpens the stain and tightens the staging lane.

Practical point: tidal timing matters more than calendar time. If the tide is moving and the plume makes a clear edge, plan for short intercepts rather than long hangs.

## Bait, birds, and fish behavior
What you see on the surface tells most of the story.

Observation: small herring, juvenile forage and shrimp will sit along the color break. Birds concentrate where bait is visible. Salmon position on the clean side and sail through or hold waiting for a clean crossing; rockfish and lingcod quarter the kelp and reef margins where bait is compressed against structure.

Signs a window is building:
- birds lowering and running the same direction along the plume
- bait streaks on the surface or a thin ribbon on sonar
- boats converging into a tight transit line instead of spreading out

If those signals line up, the lane fishes hard but briefly.

## How the spot behaves when it fishes
When it’s on: bait lines are tidy, birds run the lane, salmon will commit to single passes and give short, repeatable intercepts. Rockfish respond when the tide gives a steady crossing over kelp-to-rock interfaces.

When it’s dead: the stain looks flat and uniform, birds mill, sonar shows scattered marks with no upward motion. Small boats often stage inside and wait for a change rather than grind long drifts.

Pressure reactions are practical to expect: repeated trolling or casting in the exact same pass often scatters salmon; rockfish will tuck deeper into corners or shut down on pressured, bright afternoons.

## Fleet behavior and how to use it
Local boats run short tides from Petersburg and work the lane in bursts. The first boats in usually get the cleanest intercepts. Word spreads fast; one good transit draws the fleet into a tight corridor. When the bite shuts, boats either widen the search or run offshore to saddles.

Don’t follow blindly. Experienced skippers change presentation or drift angle rather than parking on a single GPS point. Watch what the bait and birds are doing, not just where boats are sitting.

## Approach and tactics — what to actually do
Start with observation and position on the clean side of the stain. The mechanics are simple: get in position, make short, purposeful passes, and vary depth/angle rather than repeating the same run.

Key practical steps:
- Leave on a conservative slack-to-early-flood or slack-to-early-ebb run depending on plume orientation. Plan fuel and return timing — narrow channels and tender traffic matter.
- For salmon: position just outside the stain where bait is entering cleaner water. Short trolling passes or timed cast-and-retrieve beats long drifts here.
- For rockfish/lingcod: work kelp necks and small reef fingers with tight, short drifts. Keep vertical presentations slow and accurate.
- Gear: lighter profiles and precise depth control for salmon; slow vertical jigs and small baits for the kelp/rock margins.
- Safety: expect log booms and tender traffic near the approach. In fog, the plume and birds disappear — rely on electronics and conservative margins for the run back to Petersburg.

Tactical checklist
- Tide window first; color and bait second.
- Start outside the stain and work into the edge.
- Keep drifts short; change depth or angle between passes.
- Watch fleet movement but follow bait and birds.

## Micro-observation that matters
The plume does most of the work. If you can see bait where the stain peels, the lane will fish. If you can’t, the mouth is scenery and you’ve lost the short game.

## Why run this from Petersburg
It’s a short, practical run that gives a quick answer before committing to a longer shelf trip. With the right tide and a tidy plume you can get fast salmon intercepts and reliable vertical work on kelp edges without a long open-water run — as long as you respect narrow-channel timing, local traffic and sudden weather changes.
