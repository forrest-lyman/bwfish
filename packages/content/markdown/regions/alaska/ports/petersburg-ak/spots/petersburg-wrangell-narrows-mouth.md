The mouth of Wrangell Narrows behaves like a filter. Where the channel tightens and the sound opens up, current energy shears against points and kelp collars and rearranges bait into narrow bands. For a Petersburg boat it's a first‑mile check: short runs, quick reads, and very sharp windows when tide and bait line up.

## Why the mouth breaks into kelp fingers and seams
Watch the water for a few minutes and the pattern shows up. Fast current through the narrows finds the path of least resistance and peels off at every piece of structure—points, small rock fingers, and kelp necks. The result is a patchwork of jets and lee pockets:

- jets through tight cuts that push bait along predictable lines
- clean pockets behind kelp where bait pools and predators can ambush
- downhill seams off small rock points where current shears and creates lift

Those seams are short and intense. Predators prefer the edge of the organized flow rather than the open, messy water.

## When the mouth actually fishes
This place is about timing, not distance. The good bites are brief and tied to how the tide is behaving where current from the narrows meets the broader sound.

Seasonal timing:
- Late May–July: early king salmon stage on the stronger current fingers. Best on tide changes and early morning pushes.
- June–August: pinks and coho run inside the narrows; they form short, pressure‑sensitive staging lanes along kelp edges.
- May–September: rockfish and lingcod are consistent on the close structure when current softens—kelp collars and boulder fingers are primary holding spots.

Signals that a window is building:
- bait streaming along a kelp leash or being pushed into a lee pocket
- consistent shallow marks on the electronics that track with the seam
- birds lowering and holding direction instead of milling
- a line of boats all moving the same way on transit

Those are the things you act on. If you see two or three of them together, you have time for a 30–60 minute sweep that will usually tell you if you should stay or run.

## How I set up across a kelp neck
Start upstream of the neck and work a controlled drift that lets gear sweep the kelp edge and the point seam. Don’t sit in the main jet—your job is to present across the edge, not to wrestle with the current.

Key points I watch and adjust:
- Drift angle: a small change in angle turns a dead pass into a feeding pass. In a small boat I move the stern a half‑boat width and watch how the bait tracks.
- Positioning: let the gear cross the seam on a predictable path; if the stern is riding the jet you’ll either overrun the seam or bury your gear.
- Depth reading: salmon often ride the current fingers higher in the column. Rockfish and lingcod stay tight to the bottom under kelp; vertical work (jigs, slow drops) wins there.
- Boat spacing: when the obvious kelp finger is crowded, back off to the lee pocket a boat or two lengths away—fish that have been pressured will slip into quieter water.

A tidy 30–60 minute sweep is your diagnostic. If bait and marks load up, set up and stack the area. If nothing shows, move to the next obvious saddle.

## Reading pressure and fleet behavior
This mouth is close water—local boats know the same seams. Pressure changes how the fish behave, and experienced crews adjust rather than grind the same hole.

Common patterns under pressure:
- Salmon pushed off the immediate mouth will re‑stage on a nearby seam or deeper on the kelp edge.
- Rockfish retreat to deeper pockets on the lee side of the kelp when the top edge is hammered.
- Foamers (surface feeders) will shut down on repeat casts once a zone has been worked hard.

What good boats do:
- change drift angle instead of repeating the same pass
- move to quieter lee pockets where fish slip after being pressured
- take a quick inside read and then run a measured drift to the nearby saddle

Boat spacing matters. Crowding compresses the water and drives fish tighter. Give the seam some room and you'll often find better topwater and mid‑column responses.

## Safety, launch timing and local traffic
This is narrow, tidal water with a lot of local traffic. Plan for margins.

Practical points:
- Narrow channels and tender/commercial traffic demand tidy navigation. Expect to queue for fuel and dock space on return.
- Launch around the afternoon slack when possible; returning on a tightening flood makes small‑boat docking harder.
- Logs, seine gear and small tugs are common hazards—don’t assume a straight transit.
- Fog drops birds and shoreline cues first; radar and dependable electronics are practical necessities here.

Leave space for the unexpected. The narrows rearranges traffic as surely as it rearranges bait.

## Micro‑observations I trust
The kelp finger that looks perfect at first light often dies by mid‑morning once the fleet compresses the water. A quiet lee pocket three boat‑lengths off the main seam will hold better bottom fish after the obvious edge has been worked. Those are small reads, but they win more days than fancy rigs.

What usually matters more:
- correct drift angle across the seam
- watching where the bait is actually moving
- timing the run to a softening tide

What usually matters less:
- re‑trying the same kelp neck after two boats have worked it without changing presentation

Wrangell Narrows’ mouth is close, practical water: short runs from Petersburg that reward careful timing, clean positioning, and the willingness to fish the quieter lee pockets when the obvious edge is hot and pressured. Fish the patterns, not the map.
