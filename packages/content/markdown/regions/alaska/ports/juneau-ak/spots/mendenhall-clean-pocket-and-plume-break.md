## Why this color edge matters

Look at the water before you look at your GPS. The Mendenhall clean‑pocket isn’t a rock or a canyon—it’s a visible lane of darker, clearer water sitting against the milky stain of the glacier plume. When the glacier is running hard, that cleaner ribbon is often the only workable water for miles. Bait piles there because the current and color shear organize it; fish follow the bait more predictably than any set of coordinates.

The practical result is simple: where the stain peels and the cleaner lane forms, you’ll find concentrated bait and a short, tidy staging route for salmon and a sensible halibut drift when the pocket pins. Treat the color line like structure.

## Reading the color line

The edge is visual first and instrument second.

You’ll see a hard contrast—a darker band next to gray, sometimes a surface sheen or foam. Width varies: a thin knife at times, a broader pocket others. Expect 50–200 yards most commonly. The pocket shifts with tide and wind: flood pushes the stain off the point, ebb pulls it back and tightens the edge. Wind against tide will shred the plume into tongues that re‑form unpredictably.

Find the darker lane, park, and watch. Birds and bait sort themselves fast; they’ll tell you if it’s worth staying. The fish follow the bait, not the mark—if the bait moves, be ready to follow.

## How bait changes with melt and runoff

Baseline is glacial melt. Melt intensity drives how distinct the pocket is and where the bait stacks.

- Strong melt: plume thickens, sonar gets flat in stained water, and the clean pocket becomes the concentration point. Bait piles tight near the surface early and late.
- Moderate melt: the edge fans; bait spreads across a wider band and halibut will pick adjacent sand gutters.
- Low melt: color goes spotty; bait breaks into smaller fingers and the pocket’s window shortens.

Watch for surface slicks and working birds on the darker lane—those are live signals. If birds are diving and sonar is blank, you’re often looking at shallow, tight bait under a feeding metaline; shift shallow. When sonar clears inside the pocket, marks snap from smeared to dense; that’s when you set up confidently.

Micro‑observation: bait behavior dictates tactics. Follow the bait column before you rig up a dozen patterns.

## Salmon staging and halibut lanes

This pocket acts like a short transit lane.

Salmon stage along the seam where river plume meets channel current. Early kings will use the edge on tidally favorable pushes; silvers prefer the cleaner lanes later in the season. For salmon that means short trolled runs or anchorless drift passes across the seam—keep presentations in the strike zone as the fish move along the ribbon.

Halibut use the cleaner water for better scent and bait presentation. Expect shorter halibut drifts that start as the tide softens; the fish here are often inside, smaller but consistent, and respond when you can hold contact without running three knots of flow. When the plume thickens, salmon tighten into the cleanest fingers and halibut may drop off into muddier, deeper water and turn sonar‑shy.

## Drift and boat control through the pocket

Tide and current are the engines. The difference between a smart drift and a mess is modest boat handling.

Best drifts begin as tide softens—enough flow to carry scent and bait past fish, not so much that you drag gear or snag bottom. Run parallel to the color edge instead of cutting straight across; that keeps your presentations in the zone longer. Use a light drogue or drift‑sock when the tide spikes unexpectedly to slow the boat without ruining the angle.

A practical setup is two rods shallow for salmon and one baited drop for halibut on the same pass. If bird work is patchy, slow circling passes will keep bait in front of staging fish and let you read changes without moving off the seam.

## Electronics and silt caution

Sonar is useful—right up until the plume lies to you.

Stained water flattens returns and smears marks. Don’t trust a clean echo in heavy stain as a feeding school; test the water. Drop a baited jig to confirm targets before committing the spread. When you get clear sonar inside the pocket, marks tighten into arches and you can fish anchorless drifts with confidence. Until then, expect deception from the returns and let visual cues and birds guide you more than screen numbers.

## Fleet behavior and pressure effects

An obvious color break near Juneau fishes fast and attracts boats fast.

A single good day brings charters and locals; pressure will collapse the visible window, often by mid‑morning. The experienced boats micro‑move along the edge instead of parking on a mark—they chase bait, not numbers. When pressure ramps up, schools tend to sink deeper, split into thin fingers, or refuse repeated casts. Foamers will turn off after a few boats; don’t be the one who overstays the first patch.

Common pressure reactions:
- schools sinking below sonar range
- bait scattering as boats arrive
- salmon moving to the outer seam away from the visible edge
- halibut holding lower and refusing surface presentations

## Quick signs the window is building

These are the short, readable signals worth acting on:

- Birds tightening and working the darker lane.
- Bait lifting in the water column inside the pocket.
- Sonar marks compressing into tight arches when you sit in clear water.
- Current softening from a strong push to a steady, manageable drift.

If you see two or more, set up and keep moving along the edge as the bait dictates.

## Common mistakes

You’ll see the same mistakes every season. Avoid them.

- Committing to a single GPS mark—this is a moving water feature.
- Trying to fish deep in a thick plume—sonar and presentation fail.
- Overstaying a pressured patch—windows close faster than they open.
- Ignoring wake and traffic—Gastineau Channel and cruise‑tender wakes will wreck delicate drifts.

## Tactical callouts

Short, actionable moves that work here:

- Fish early morning and the last hour of flood/ebb transitions—the pocket tightens and bait compacts then.
- Check wind vs. tide before committing; easterlies can blow the plume offshore or shred it depending on tidal state.
- When the pocket is thin, go light: small presentations and light line for salmon. If the pocket broadens, use heavier spread‑trolling to cover more water.
- If bird work is spotty, make slow passes and micro‑move along the seam rather than sitting on a single mark.

Micro‑observation: a clean color break next to matted silt is often the only place you’ll see fish on a heavy melt day—treat it like premium structure.

## Why skippers check it fast and have a plan B

It’s an easy run from Juneau and can give high‑value, short windows for kings, sockeye and reliable halibut drifts without a long haul to Icy Strait. That convenience makes it a go‑to when weather or time narrows options. It also makes the spot pressure‑sensitive and ephemeral—the plume shifts, the pocket vanishes, and the fish move.

Always leave with a plan B: follow nearby points and lee seams where cleaner pockets often re‑form, and be ready to trade a fixed mark for following the bait and the birds.
