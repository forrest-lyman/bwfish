## The saddle and the downhill fingers — how the place organizes bait

What you actually notice from the boat is simple geometry. A shallow, broad shoulder runs out and spills into a few narrower downhill fingers. On the chart it looks like a low ridge; on the water it shows up in the drift angle and the way bait piles or strips along seams. The saddle is not a single hook-up spot—it's a small system: a shoulder, feeder fingers, a couple of rock knobs and clean-water pockets tucked behind points and kelp. Fish show up where that structure organizes current and bait.

You can run this system in 10–30 minutes from Juneau. That short run is part of the calculus—traffic, tide and tight windows decide whether you fish or wait.

## Tide and drift — the operational core

Tide controls this place. The saddle fishes best on the edges of flood and ebb when drift speed drops into a controllable range. If the flow is too strong your rigs ride; if it's too slack bait piles and gives no scent. The practical trick is to line up a drift that lets your rig touch without dragging, using the seams where the saddle meets the fingers to hold contact.

Fish the seam from inside the saddle and let the boat slide across the finger instead of trying to crash the contour. Orient the drift so the rode runs roughly parallel to the face of the finger—presentation stays natural and you reduce hangups. When the tide is running hard, shorten leaders, add a little weight and move to the lee of a finger where the flow softens.

## How the window builds — what to watch for

The early signs are behavioral and visual rather than a number on a tide table. Look for bait and predators arranging themselves.

- Bait rocking on the surface upstream of the saddle.
- Birds tightening over a narrow seam that points down-finger.
- Sonar marks that hold in the column instead of streaking away.
- Halibut follows or light bumping as the drift first eases.

Those cues tell you a controllable, interceptable lane is forming. They also tell you where to set the first drift.

## Color, bait and clean pockets — where the fish actually sit

Mendenhall silt and other runoff make color patchy here. The fingers act as separators—one gutter can be pea-soup while the next is a clear lane with stacked sand eels or herring. Fish follow color and bait more reliably than GPS waypoints.

Pay attention to narrow color breaks along points or kelp: fish typically sit on the clear side. Bait tends to hug the drop-off of a finger rather than the top; halibut mirror that. Birds ahead of the color break—small rafts and terns—are better signals than a scattered bunch of gulls.

## How boats approach it — common patterns and fleet effects

Experienced boats run several short drifts across the fingers instead of one long messy sweep. Typical pattern: start inside the saddle, work a promising finger, then move to the next if nothing holds. If kings show, rigs tighten and the drift becomes more of a short troll through the seam.

Fleet behavior changes the place. One good boat will draw a crowd and the next high-pressure tide can push fish deeper or off the face entirely. When a single finger gets hammered, the smart move is usually to back off and work a quieter finger or wait for the tide rotation—fishing the quiet finger often beats fighting the fleet on a pressured one.

Common pressure reactions you’ll see:
- Surface foamers and breakers dive and reappear deeper on the next pass.
- Halibut stack lower and stop taking live bait after repeated passes.
- Schools that once broke become grey marks on the sounder and indifferent to lures.

## Rockfish, lingcod and tight vertical work

The fingers have rock knobs, gutters and kelp leeways. Those reliefs will hold rockfish and lingcod on the faces and in the gutters when current sweeps across them. Short, focused vertical stops behind a point or anchored short-drift usually outfish random slow drifts. Keep your vertical presentations tight to structure and be ready to change drop speed with the current.

## Sonar, drops and confirming a mark

Near glacial inputs the sounder can lie. Use baited drops to confirm marks. If the sounder shows a slow-moving column of targets that holds relative to the bottom, it’s worth a drop. Marks that streak or vanish with the sweep are often bait or turbulence and rarely hold fish long enough to be productive.

## Decision rules — when to stay, move, or wait

Start on a single finger and test the fall. If halibut respond, vary leader length, move a rod position or adjust bait depth and stay in the area to work variations. If two passes produce nothing and the sonar remains quiet, move. The saddle rewards deliberate search over stubborn repetition.

A short checklist for workability:
- Tide speed: under your comfortable drift threshold.
- Color: a clear lane or narrow color break near the finger.
- Birds/bait: tight, active groupings rather than a wide scatter.
- Traffic: manageable boats on other fingers; remember that one-boat sites cool quickly.

Micro-observations: the better drifts often begin with bait showing inside the saddle and the boat presenting across the finger without fighting steerage. A clean-grey finger will outfish a crowded, pretty surface every time.

## Safety and access notes

Runs from Juneau are short but the channel traffic is constant. Cruise tenders and commercial traffic put wakes through delicate drifts; stage out of the main lane where practical. Tide flips change drift angles quickly—watch your set, keep a manageable rode and have an exit plan if the run tightens.

## What usually matters, and what doesn’t

What usually matters:
- Tide stage and the drift angle across the finger.
- Finding a clean-water pocket near a point.
- Presenting bait with steady contact down the face.

What usually matters less:
- Long, straight-line runs to reach a single GPS waypoint.
- Repeating the same drift after boats have thinned the water.

## Bottom line

Stephens Passage saddle and its downhill fingers are a high-value, short-run piece of water for Juneau boats. Time the tide, pick the clean pocket, present bait with steady bottom contact and manage traffic. Read the color, trust the bait, and don’t get married to one finger—this system rewards movement, careful drift control and reading the small signs.
