Why this little line matters
The north kelp necks off Douglas Island are short, rocky projections and isolated boulder fingers that face into Gastineau Channel. From Juneau docks or Auke Bay you’re minutes away — five to twenty on most boats — so this strip gets checked a lot. On the surface it reads as kelp paddies and low rock. Underneath it’s a compact, broken relief: small drops, gutters and pockets that funnel bait and create vertical edges. That mix is exactly what rockfish, lingcod and halibut use when the tide and color line up.

How the current sculpts the bite
Tide controls everything here. Gastineau Channel narrows around Douglas and the kelp necks break the flow into short usable seams. Watch the tide first — the line rarely fishes the same way for an entire tide.

- Flood through the channel tends to push cleaner water into the lee of the necks; bait funnels along the up-current faces and you’ll see life stacked there.
- Ebb tightens the flow, squeezes bait and forces fish deeper into holes and caves.
- The Mendenhall silt line will wrap around points; often the only productive pocket for a stretch is the first clean water behind a neck.
- Small, short-lived eddies form on the down-current side of boulders. Those eddies move the strike zone from the face into the pocket.

The practical rule: fish the tide when it softens enough to hold contact without dragging the bait off bottom. If it’s blasting with no slack window, the bottom gets unreadable and you’re mostly fishing for tackle.

Where the bait and fish stack
The forage is local—herring and juvenile schooling bait pushed off shallow flats, with sand lance and juvenile pollock showing in summer. Bait stacks on windward faces and in gutters between rocks; the useful pockets are the places bait congregates.

How the species use the structure
Rockfish: stack on vertical faces and under kelp shade. They hold tight when current is moderate; you’ll see compact sonar marks hugging bottom.

Lingcod: live in the larger boulder pits and under overhangs. They sit in the dark and move onto the edges to ambush passing bait.

Halibut: sit on adjacent sand gutters and small troughs. They usually park off the rock line and feed when scent from a pocket drifts into their zone.

Signs the pick is working
Look for these, they matter more than a chart coordinate:
- birds working tight to the north shore or hovering over bait slicks
- sonar marks hugging bottom along the kelp line rather than scattered in the column
- a narrow strip of cleaner color behind a neck — the classic lee pocket
- bait rising in the column near the rocks as the tide softens

How I fish it — short drifts first, anchor later
This water is small and controllable. Short drifts win more often than long tows or random passes.

Short-drift approach
Run a drift roughly parallel to the necks so your presentation sweeps the rock-to-sand transition. Watch your drift angle: you want the bait to sweep across that edge, not blow straight over it. Use light, adjustable weight — enough to maintain contact but light enough to feel a halibut and avoid constant fouling in kelp.

Anchor and vertical work
When you find a clean lee pocket, set a short anchor or a stern tie to hold the boat into the drift without spinning over kelp. Vertical jigs, big single plastics or a live minnow on a short leader over the rocks pull rockfish and lingcod better than long tows. If current is steady and predictable, switch to vertical work; if it stutters, go back to a short drift.

Positioning for lee pockets and silt
Scout color from a few hundred yards. The productive pockets are clear while surrounding water may be gray from glacial runoff. Work the first clean pocket you find — if the line is swamped with silt, fish will hang in that clear strip. Set up so your bait scent drifts out of the pocket toward the gutter; halibut often sit on the edge where silt meets clean sand.

Reading sonar and structure
Sonar is interpretive here, not definitive. Learn the signatures:

- Good boulders show as tight, high-contrast bottom returns with a shadowed lee downstream.
- Rockfish marks are compact, close to bottom.
- Lingcod often appear as longer, denser returns hugging structure.
- Halibut are low, flat marks off the edge in the sand — don’t mistake scatter for a sitting halibut.

Always verify with a baited drop. Near kelp and silt the screen lies to you sometimes.

Pressure, fleet behavior and common calls
This line fishes quick and pressure follows. Locals treat the necks like homework: quick checks in the morning, then a move across the channel if picks get pushed or silted.

Expectations:
- A single boat can blow out a shallow neck; fish go deeper or move to the next pocket.
- Repeated hits push fish into deeper holes. If that happens, either commit to deeper vertical work or move.
- Don’t waste the useful tide sitting on a crowded shallow pick. A neighboring deeper neck often holds the same bait on a different depth band.

Common mistakes that waste time
- Anchoring directly on kelp banks — fouls and stuck boats follow.
- Drifting perpendicular to the necks so baits never sweep the sand edge.
- Staying on one neck after the tide flips and the drift angle goes wrong.
- Ignoring a visible color break — fish follow bait, not coordinates.

When to walk away
Move before you grind the tide. Walk away when:
- the whole line is mud-grey from runoff and there are no clear pockets within range,
- current is blasting with no slack window expected,
- you’ve made repeated stops with no positive sonar confirmation or bites — chances are pressure pushed the fish deeper.

Short practical checklist
- Start with a quick run along the north-facing necks looking for clean lee pockets and bait.
- If you find a pocket: set a short drift to sweep the rock-to-sand edge; lighten weight if you need feel.
- If marks look tight and vertical: try a short anchor and vertical presentations.
- If color is grey or the pick is crowded: move before the tide eats the window.

What you’re actually fishing for
Close, controllable structure—good for quick halibut checks and hands-on rockfish/lingcod work. It’s effective when tide and color cooperate. Look for the clean lee pockets, set your drift to sweep the edge, and be ready to convert to vertical work when the current bites. If silt or fleet pressure wins, move on and find the next pocket.
