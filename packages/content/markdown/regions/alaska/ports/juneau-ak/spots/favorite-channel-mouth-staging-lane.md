Why this mouth fishes like a sluice

The mouth of Favorite Channel consistently organizes bait into a narrow tongue. Tide and local freshwater pulses strip glacial silt and creek water off the flats, creating a stained plume that peels along the throat. Sand lance, herring and other forage compress into that seam — not randomly, but predictably. Salmon use the seam as a staging road on runs; halibut and bottom predators sit just off the mouth to intercept whatever drifts out.

This is close water work. You don’t win the day by running farther out; you win it by watching color, bait and current and presenting inside the lane while it exists. The window is narrow and the signals are specific.

How the current and plume create the lane

Observation first: the strongest activity comes when the tide is changing. Early flood into Favorite and the first half of ebb out of it are the moments the tongue organizes most clearly.

Why that happens: moving water peels the stained freshwater off the shore and rides it out as a concentrated band. Where that band meets cleaner channel water you get sharp color contrast and a shear line — an obvious seam for bait to hold on. Small eddies and lee pockets form down-current of points and flats; those pockets act like traps where bait stacks and where you can hold a drift or set a short anchor.

Micro-observation: the fish follow the bait, not the GPS line. If the bait band slides north or south of the charted mouth, the salmon slide with it. Follow the tongue.

Reading bait, birds, and staging cues

You’ll usually see salmon before you hook one because the staging behavior shows up on the surface first.

Common, reliable cues:
- Birds tightening over a narrow dark line or small foamers working the plume edge.
- Bait schools holding tight to the plume edge instead of scattered across the channel.
- Birds sitting low or making repeated dips over one seam — that often equals active bait, not a random floater.
- A dark, clean strip beside stained water; that contrast almost always signals concentrated forage.

Signs the window is building
Watch for these in combination: a steady line of bait along the plume tongue, birds that hold position instead of cruising, elongated sonar marks moving with current rather than vertical blobs, and foamers that travel directionally instead of milling. Any one sign is useful; together they tell you the lane is worth committing to.

When the lane fishes (timing and runs)

- Early run (late spring): incoming kings stage on tidy flood tides and the fresher plume edges.
- Mid-summer: sockeye and coho stage on river pulses tied to upriver movement and tidal staging.
- Halibut: best short intercepts when the tide softens — current still organizes bait but you have enough control to keep bottom contact.

Tactical choices: drift angle and presentation

Observation: presenting baits through the narrow seam wins more bites than crossing it.

Tactical calls:
- Drift angle wins. Run parallel to the plume tongue and keep baits traveling with the seam. Crossing it perpendicularly often produces poor presentations and missed fish.
- Slack isn’t always better. When the tide goes completely slack the bait disperses. The productive window is usually as the tide softens, not at full slack.
- Color matters more than depth here. Halibut and other predators key on the stained/clean boundary. If the plume smothers the bottom with silt, fish will move off the seam.
- Sonar can mislead in stained water. Use a baited drop to confirm marks; birds and bait lines are the primary selectors, sonar is confirmation.

Short halibut intercepts off the mouth

Where to look: the cleaner side of the plume where it crosses a bottom contour change — down-current edges of flats and saddles. Halibut sit on those edges and pick off what drifts by.

How to fish it: run short, controlled drifts with enough weight to maintain contact. Too much weight drags; too little and you lose bottom feel when the current grabs the line. A one-boat discovery often becomes a crowded lane quickly; if the seam fills with boats, either push deeper off the mouth or find the quiet lee pocket behind the next point.

How the fleet changes the lane

The lane behaves like a magnet. One solid bite draws the fleet fast and the seam compresses or fishes differently after repeated passes.

Typical pressure reactions:
- Fish sink below easy sonar range.
- Bait tightens into a narrower band away from boats.
- Foamers refuse repeated casts.
- The seam fractures into multiple thin bait fingers.

Experienced boats prefer to hold a clean drift line and leave the noisy surface-side to fast trollers and quick-check boats. Commercial and tender traffic can wipe a window; plan to punch wakes or wait for a tide change when staging is crowded.

Quick practical checklist before you run

- Check recent river pulse timing and local tide charts.
- Scan for a visible tongue or color edge from the launch.
- Plan drifts that run with the plume, not across it.
- Put someone on the bow to watch birds and bait while you monitor drift and sonar.
- Have a plan to move off the mouth quickly if the seam collapses under pressure.

Failure modes and what they look like

- Plume covers the bottom with silt: sonar goes noisy, bait disappears, halibut move off the seam.
- Current too strong: drift becomes unreadable and hooks fail to hold bottom.
- Heavy traffic: the seam becomes disrupted and short-lived.

What usually matters most — and least

Most important: exact plume placement and color contrast, drift angle through the tongue, and timing with river pulses and tide change.

Less important: running a fixed distance off the charted mouth or trusting a single sonar pass that shows isolated marks without bait or birds. Follow the bait and the birds.

Bottom line: read the color, follow the bait, move fast

Favorite Channel’s mouth is a short, tactical fishery. When plume, tide and bait line up it gives concentrated salmon staging and quick halibut intercepts close to Juneau. The work is watching color, following bait, and presenting along the tongue before pressure or current turns the job over. If the visible signals aren’t there, move—this stretch rewards quick decisions more than long hope.
