## Where the corridor changes the game
The approach from Juneau into Icy Strait isn’t a single point on a chart — it’s a corridor where inside-channel flats fall away into steeper ribs, saddles and downhill seams. The bottom goes from mixed sand and mud to sharper drop-offs; those short seams focus bait and create narrow “fingers” of cleaner, often bluer water inside a larger, muddier matrix.

Those fingers act like gutters. Bait funnels into them, birds tighten along the line, and predators—halibut and migrating salmon—use them as highways. When a clean finger lines up on a slope or saddle the corridor fishes very differently than the shallow inside flats closer to Juneau.

## When you leave the protected runs
This is a weather- and fuel-driven decision, not a default stop. Go outside for two reasons: to chase larger halibut that skirt the flats and stack on steeper faces, or to intercept broad, directional salmon lanes when a warm or clean finger links river plumes to the outer exchange. If you don’t have bait, fuel margin or weather room, the inside heads and flats will usually produce more consistent daylight.

## Reading the fingers: color, bait, birds
Color change and bait are everything here. A narrow blue or green finger running through stained water is worth more than a tidy GPS line.

- Birds usually find the lane before your chart does. They compress quickly along the feed line.
- Sonar will show scattered marks on the face of the drop for halibut; confirm with a baited drop before committing the whole day.
- Current fingers can be narrow—often you’re in clean-looking water 50 yards from slurry that shows nothing.

Look for:
- a thin band of cleaner water along the slope,
- bait tightening or visible inside the finger,
- birds concentrated in a line rather than scattered,
- marks on the drop that hold position instead of streaking through.

Those are signs a window is building.

## Setting halibut drifts on the downhill seams
The better halibut work here is driven by how the boat moves relative to the seam, not how neat your GPS line is.

- Set up to drift downhill across the contour so the boat follows the seam rather than cutting it perpendicularly.
- Keep the drift slow and steady; you want honest bottom contact as the depth changes. Too much speed and the face strips contact.
- Read the tide—best work often starts as current softens. Full flood or ebb can wash marks and make contact unreliable.
- Drop to the face and stay honest on depth. Larger halibut will hang deeper on the upper edge of a seam and move up as the day warms or pressure changes.

Use heavier, honest rigs when the face steepens; light gear gets blown off bottom when the seam tightens.

## Fishing salmon lanes through the exchange
When a warm or clearer finger links a river plume to the approach you get wide, directional salmon lanes.

- Troll across and along that feed line; let birds and surface bait tell you the direction, not the charted contour.
- Pressure kills surface windows fast. A fast-moving lane can go from frantic to empty after repeated casts.
- Expect the lane to be broader than the halibut seam, but its useful part still sits on the cleaner water and the connecting troughs.

If you see birds compressing and surface bait moving, assume it’s a feed line until shown otherwise.

## Fleet behavior and pressure
Experienced boats stage on the clean fingers early and shift as tide and color change. One good bite line draws the fleet in quickly.

When pressure mounts:
- schools sink deeper,
- foamers and surface fish scatter or move off the visible line,
- the best fish retreat to narrower seams and steeper faces.

Plan for the first boats to take the easy marks; expect harder work afterward.

## Mistakes I see—don’t make them
Most problems here are predictable. The common errors are:

- committing to the outer contour without fuel or an alternate plan if the forecast turns or fog rolls in,
- fishing a perceived GPS line instead of following color and bait,
- running deep halibut drifts during peak exchange—too much flow blurs the face and strips contact.

Don’t treat a clean line on a display as a permission slip to push limits. Water, birds and bait tell you when to commit.

## Fuel, weather and contingency plans
Have a plan you can execute if conditions flip.

- Fuel to get there, fish, and run inside if wind or fog comes up. Leave a healthy margin beyond planned transit.
- Pick at least two inside alternates that work on shorter windows and get you home quick—Favorite Channel saddles, Douglas Island kelp and boulder patches, or the Auke Bay heads.
- Keep the ferry and cruise ship schedules in mind; their wakes and traffic compress delicate windows.
- If visibility drops, move to protected heads and kelp edges—sonar and bait are less trustworthy near glacial silt.

If you can’t run the plan safely, don’t.

## Sonar, rigs and confirming marks
Marks here look different from the inside flats—deeper, more scattered, often single targets on the face rather than thick clouds.

- Treat those marks as tentative until you confirm with a baited drop. Current fingers shear targets in the water column; sonar alone can mislead.
- Use heavier leaders and weight to maintain contact when the face steepens. Light rigs are fine on placid flats but get blown off bottom here.
- Pay attention to how marks behave—targets that hold position on the face are useful; marks that streak mean you’re in shear and contact will be intermittent.

## Safety notes and visibility
The corridor is exposed to outside fetch. A tidy inside morning can close the outer approach by afternoon as swell and wind wrap into the channels. Fog hides birds, boats and shoreline references—don’t take a fog window as an invitation to commit far offshore.

Tide against wind will make the corridor unpleasant quickly. Plan runs around softer tides when possible.

## Micro-observations: how the big ones show
From years on the water you learn small patterns:

- The biggest halibut tend to show where a cleaner finger narrows against a steep face.
- Sometimes the better current stays tucked inside a saddle while the noisy fleet chases the wrong green patch.
- The useful lane often appears as a tight ribbon of color with bait and birds pinned to it—read that ribbon and act fast.

## When to commit
Commit only when you have four things: bait, a narrow clean finger, fuel to get back, and weather room. Without those you’re gambling on a short window that can close faster than you think. When all four line up the corridor rewards timing and water-reading more than distance.

This approach is where Juneau boats go when they want larger halibut and real salmon lanes—provided you read the color, respect the current fingers, and carry the contingency to get home.
