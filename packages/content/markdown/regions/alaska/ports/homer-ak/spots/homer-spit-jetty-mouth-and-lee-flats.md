How the bottom lays out and where the fish sit

This first mile outside the Spit is a patchwork, not a flat: sand gutters and mud pockets stitched with scalloped rock fingers, isolated pinnacles and the odd kelp collar. It’s shallow compared with the shelf, but the relief is real enough to hold lingcod and big rockfish, and to let you run quick, controllable halibut drifts without burning a full day. In practice the halibut park on the sand edges where the rock drops away; ling and rockfish tuck on the faces, in pinnacles and around kelp collars.

Why this short run gets checked so often

You can launch, make two drifts and be back with fuel to spare. That convenience is the obvious draw, but the place fishes because of two moving parts: tide and bait concentration. The Spit and jetty create predictable seams on tide swings; bait gets funneled and pinned along the tongue and into gutters for short bursts. When the flush lines up you get heavy turnover water—short windows that will pay off before the wind builds or the fleet shows up.

Getting there and reading the wind

The ramp on the Spit makes this a classic short-run play. That’s useful but also the hazard: boats stack up quickly.

Take the channel markers seriously; the entrance is straightforward until the southwest fetch builds. On calm days the Spit gives a tidy lee—easy runs, clean drifts. When the southwest wind comes up the lee collapses and short, steep chop wraps into the jetty mouth. What felt like a ten‑minute run at first light can be unpleasant by noon.

If visibility or fuel is marginal, keep the run short. Fog eats birds and you’ll lose a key sign; don’t fish the run blind on sight alone.

When the window opens

This spot is tide-driven more than calm-sea dependent. The best work usually comes on an easing tide or around slack when drift speed lets bait lay across the sand/rock transitions naturally. Early-morning cleanup days are common here: the bay clears before the outer harbor, birds reappear, and the drift lines set themselves up fast.

Short, tidy flood or ebb pulses can light up the pinnacles when bait pins against the rock. Expect the mix to change mid-drift — a halibut flat will flip to rockfish/lingcod as you cross broken relief or the current tightens.

Setting a drift that consistently works

Positioning beats fancy rigs. The dependable pattern is an across-drift that crosses the gutters and rock fingers rather than running parallel to them.

Start up-tide of the sandy gutter or rock edge you want to fish and let the boat settle. Read that first pass before you drop a second bait. Aim for a line that cuts both sand and mixed rock—halibut use the sand next to structure; ling and rockfish live on the faces. If the current picks up, shorten leaders and keep presentation tight; when you overdrift you lose bottom contact and you’ll miss the bite.

Vertical work for rockfish and lingcod is straightforward: when your sounder shows tight marks on structure, go deep and vertical with a lively bait or slow jig and keep the rod tip moving. Small pinnacles and kelp collars can hold fish even when the flats go quiet, so don’t ignore isolated structure.

Electronics and surface signs I trust

Look for clean, low targets over sand with a denser mark where the rock rises—that’s classic halibut parking. Dense, stationary returns hugging a pinnacle are your lingcod and big rockfish. When marks begin to lift off the bottom as the tide eases, fish are activating; that’s a small window to work fast.

Surface signs that matter: birds sitting tight on a seam instead of chasing, bait edges running down the Spit tongue, and foamers holding a track rather than milling. Those are higher-confidence signals than a loose flock moving through.

How pressure and fleet behavior changes the bite

Because it’s a short-run, nearshore game, local skiffs and charters check this water early or as a quick stop. Small boats use it as a halibut reset between shelf runs; charters will spend a half-hour here before committing further out. That convenience turns into pressure fast, and pressure changes behavior predictably.

Common pressure reactions I watch for:
- Foamers stop committing to casts after a couple of boats have worked the same seam.
- Fish that showed high and clean in the morning go deeper and slower by midday.
- Crowds push fish down-current or into tighter structure where only vertical work will reach them.

Common mistakes

- Running the flats at peak of a strong tide. If you can’t control angle the bait won’t present.
- Chasing the nearest foamer while a cleaner bait seam sits 200 yards away.
- Assuming the lee will protect you when the southwest fetch builds—the jetty mouth fills with short, confused chop quickly.

Quick tactical priorities

What usually wins here: tide timing, drift control, bait concentration and quiet positioning. What usually loses time: multiple short casts in noisy, pressured water and waiting for glassy seas to push you outside.

Micro-observations that change a day

The bay cleans up before the rest of the harbor; the first two drifts usually tell the story. A quiet boat sitting mid-lee often gets the last useful run while the stacked boats fight the edges.

How I run it

I treat the first mile like a tight two-drift spot. Get up with the tide window, start just up‑tide of the edge you want, run an across drift that crosses both sand and rock, and read the sonar and birds on the first pass. If marks lift and birds sit, work fast. If the fleet stacks or the southwest wind builds, move off—this place pays in short, sharp windows, not by grinding until noon.
