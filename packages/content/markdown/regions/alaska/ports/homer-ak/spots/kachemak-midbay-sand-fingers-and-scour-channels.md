What reads different here is texture more than depth. Wide mud and sand flats are cut with narrow scour gutters and scalloped rock fingers that poke up through the flat. On a chart it looks subtle; on your sounder it becomes obvious — short downhill breaks, shallow pockets and tidy channels that steer current and compress bait. That geometry is the reason halibut and migrating salmon show up: they don’t need dramatic structure, they need water that concentrates food and makes the presentation for them.

## How the fingers and scour channels concentrate bait

Observe the current closely. It snakes around the fingers and funnels through the gutters. Where that flow tightens you’ll get bait seams and marks; where the tide softens the bait piles up and fish move in. The features are short and abrupt — a small cut with cleaner sand on one side, a little headland that sheds bait into a seam — and the fish use those edges.

- Halibut often sit on the face of the cuts and move up into cleaner gutters as drift speed eases.
- Salmon stage along channel edges and at the heads of small prominences when bait piles on a flank of the tide.
- Rockfish and lingcod stay tight to scattered stones and pinnacles and key off any cross‑current that sends bait past their faces.

Those are simple, repeatable patterns. You read the tide and the bait first; everything else follows.

## Recognizing the tidal window

The bite here isn't on the peak so much as when the tide goes from hard to manageable. Watch for the build and the easing — the fish usually show as current softens, not when the flow is strongest.

Common signals that the window is building:
- Narrow bait seams lining a channel instead of bait scattered across the flat.
- Sonar marks that start to climb off bottom into mid‑water as drift speed drops.
- Birds sitting quietly along a headland rather than chasing foam.
- Foamers that hold a direction instead of milling — short, sharp surface windows.

Those signs are quick to appear and quick to disappear. Treat them as a short timer.

## Drift setup and boat work that actually produces

Approach from the up‑current side and set a line that takes the bait along the gutter and across a sand finger. If you can, cross the relief at a slight angle instead of running dead down the channel — you present more water to whatever’s sitting on the face.

Few practical points I pay attention to:
- Control drift speed before you pitch bait. Too much tide and the bait drags; too little and the scent cloud never reaches the fish.
- Run baits tight enough to feel changes when the bottom changes. The edges are short and sudden; you want contact when you pass them.
- Put yourself on the lee side of small prominences using chart and GPS. Cleaner water behind a headland is where fish queue.
- Keep the drifts short and tidy. A single, quiet pass that hits the seam often beats a scatter of attempts across the whole flat.

Repeatability is valuable here. Watch your boat track on the GPS and try to hit the same line when the window resets.

## Reading the sonar and the small signals

Sonar tells you what the current is doing with bait and where fish hang.

What I look for:
- A clean, defined gutter on sonar with bait stacked on one side — worth staying on.
- Small, consistent arches over sand followed by little or no targets over rock — often halibut on the face.
- Marks that scatter or go flat when the fleet shows up — those are pressure reactions; shift to a quieter parallel line.

Micro‑reading matters: a bait seam stacked on the down‑current side of a finger tells a different story than bait smeared across the flat. Adjust drift angle and bait presentation to match.

## Fleet pressure and where to go when it’s busy

The fleet tends to bunch on the obvious gutters on tidy tides and that pressure usually pushes fish deeper or down‑current. A quieter boat that picks parallel sand fingers or an adjacent seam often gets the last productive pass. Information spreads fast here; if you’re the one that polishes a seam into nothing, you learned the lesson quickly.

When the fleet stacks you’ll see:
- Marks flattening as more boats pass.
- Fish moving off the obvious line into quieter water.
- Birds moving from tight stacks to other pockets of bait.

If pressure shows up, look for the next parallel finger, or a slightly different angle across the same gutter.

## Half‑day vs full‑day decisioning

This mid‑bay pays a half‑day more often than not. The runs are short and productive windows come and go on a single tide change. Most skippers make money inside the bay on a softening tide.

- Choose a half‑day when you can hit the usable tide window and work repeatable drifts.
- Commit to a full day only if you’re chasing bigger halibut on the outer shelf or the mid‑bay windows are narrow and you can’t get a usable drift early.
- Morning cleanup matters. The bay often cleans up before winds build; if there’s a forecasted midday southwest fetch, plan conservatively.

In practice: read the tide first, position second. If the window’s short, don’t burn daylight on an outer run.

## Common mistakes that waste time here

The main errors I see are predictable:
- Overdrifting with too much tide and expecting a natural presentation.
- Waiting for glassy seas before committing — useful windows here can be marginal and short.
- Chasing lone foamers without checking the larger bait seams and nearby gutters.
- Burning daylight to make an outer‑shelf run when the mid‑bay tide window is closing.

Avoid grinding a single over‑fished pass; move to a parallel line or another finger and try the same angle again.

## Tactical priorities — what to focus on

These are the things that usually matter more and less here.

Usually matters more:
- Drift angle across the gutter.
- Bait shape and concentration in the seam.
- A slackening tide that lets fish move up the face.

Usually matters less:
- Exact depth on an otherwise featureless flat.
- Lingering on one over‑worked pass.
- Waiting for perfect surface conditions.

Micro‑observation to finish on: fish often arrive as the tide softens — not at the maximum flow but as it eases. A clean, short pass on that easing tide inside the bay will often beat a longer, riskier day chasing edge fish elsewhere. Read the tide, find the bait seams, and present the bait into the cleaner water behind the fingers.
