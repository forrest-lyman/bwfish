## Where the midwall actually runs and why it matters

The "midwall" isn't a single hole—it's a line. It sits along the steep fjord face and the short saddles between Fox Island, Caines Head and the central walls of Resurrection Bay. What matters is how the wall shapes the water: tight jets, lee pockets, small saddles and the flats just outside them. Those features rearrange bait and create predictable lanes that migrating kings and coho will use.

You won't recognize the midwall by depth on a chart; you'll recognize it by behavior. The water moves differently along that face and the fish follow the differences.

## How the water organizes bait and fish

Start with seams. When flood or ebb peels around a headland or a creek mouth it makes a tight, often narrow seam of cleaner water. That seam stacks bait and gives salmon somewhere to hold. Where a glacial silt or river plume meets cleaner water the bait compresses on the clean edge—fish habitually sit on the outside of the stain, not inside it.

Current strength changes the whole picture. A light, steady push holds bait tight and makes long, consistent drifts useful. A ripping jet strips bait off the face and scatters fish; the same shelf and flat that produced a steady halibut bite yesterday can be useless when the midwall jet tightens. Behind small points, lee pockets form when the flow softens; those pockets are short, repeatable halibut drifts when you can get the gear to stay in the zone.

Observe first, then explain: the wall makes the seam, the seam stacks bait, stacked bait creates lanes.

## Recognizing the staging lanes

Salmon treat those seams like highways. They're moving but they stop when food and current line up right.

Signs I look for before committing:
- a distinct bait band holding one depth for several minutes
- sonar marks that streak and then ride parallel to the wall
- boats slowing and repeating similar tracks around the same band (not proof on its own, but meaningful when the bait shows)

Operational habits to watch for: salmon will often ride a bait band parallel to the wall rather than crossing it. Spring kings will show earlier and deeper in the column; summer coho ride higher and react quicker to shallow baits. Read the bait first—match depth and angle to it, not to where the waypoint says you fished last month.

## How current strength alters halibut work

Halibut live on the flats just off the downhill side of the midwall and on the small saddles between relief. They use the flow that brings scent and food across sand and gravel—so the current controls whether they feed or tuck.

- Light, steady current: best for long, controlled drifts across saddles. Bait stays near bottom and halibut feed.
- Strong jets: the gear is hard to keep in the zone; halibut either tuck or move off the flat. You get short windows as the current eases.
- Very little movement: bait scatters and halibut quit feeding. In those times a slow, scent-rich drift matters more than perfectly parked boat position.

In practice you adjust leader length and weight to keep contact: shorten leaders when the jet tightens and focus on maintaining bottom contact—long drifts mean nothing if your rig is riding off the bottom.

## Sonar and surface signs that actually matter

I treat the fishfinder as an early warning system, not gospel. On the midwall it tells you where the band is and whether it’s holding.

Useful sonar signs:
- a tight bait layer at a single depth
- marks streaking along the seam and then holding
- targets rising and staying near structure

Warning signs:
- bait spread as a broad curtain with no tight band (that’s bait washing through)
- marks moving erratically with tide changes
- a loud, uniform bottom echo under a lee pocket—often little bait there

Birds help when they're visible, but steep walls and fog hide a lot. If the screen and the birds disagree, trust the screen.

## Positioning, angles, and drift setup

My approach is methodical: start wide, find the clean side of the seam, then work in.

- Approach the seam from the clean side. Make controlled passes across the bait band rather than drifting blindly along the wall.
- Angle the drift so your gear crosses the seam down-current instead of running along it. Salmon staging on a seam often take during cross-current passes.
- For halibut, pick the saddle and set a drift that keeps the bait near bottom as you cross the downhill gradient. If the current tightens, shorten leaders and concentrate on bottom contact.
- When the main jet is too strong, use small lee pockets and the protected side of points. Those pockets often hold rockfish and an accessible halibut bite when the main seam is blown out.

These are decisions, not rituals. Adjust angle, weight and leader length as the tide shifts.

## When the window builds and what to commit to

You’ll know a real window when several pieces lock together: bait compresses into one band, sonar marks ride the same track, and the clean-water seam peels off a creek plume or headland predictably. Boats slowing and running consistent tracks can be a confirming sign when the bait band is real.

Signs the seam is worth committing to:
- a bait band that holds a single depth for several minutes
- marks rising and riding the same track
- birds working low over the seam (when visible)

What usually matters more:
- drift angle and bait depth
- tidal timing (the first steady push after slack)
- the clean-water edge relative to the stain

What usually matters less:
- the exact waypoint you ran yesterday
- big numbers on the screen without a tight bait band

## Timing through the season

Timing here follows melt and migration.

- May–June: early king staging. Expect wider depth ranges and windows that are short and tide-driven because of heavy meltwater and stain.
- June–August: the best mixed opportunity. Cleaner mid-bay water and steadier weather give repeatable staging lanes and more reliable halibut drifts.
- Late summer: coho and pinks push through and ride seams higher. The halibut bite is still around but more variable.

Fish change how they use the seam through the season—read the bait each trip.

## Common mistakes and how pressure changes fish behavior

Mistakes I see often:
- fishing the obvious face when the bait has already slipped outside the stain. The fish are almost always on the clean edge.
- using the same drift tactics on every tide. Midwall runs demand different angles and drift lengths as jets strengthen or soften.
- pushing into the main jet on marginal weather. Once the water goes ugly the seam vanishes and so does a predictable bait band.

When pressure shows up the school response is clear: marks tighten to the bottom, fish sink, foamers refuse repeat casts. Boats clustering tends to make fish tuck lower or move into smaller pockets. If the fleet stacks, look for the clean edge and smaller lee pockets instead of fighting the center.

## Micro observations and how I think about the midwall

The seam often shows on the screen before the birds ever find it. A tidy mid-bay push will flip a dead wall into a short, sharp salmon gate—and the halibut will follow if you can keep the bait on bottom.

Think of the midwall as a water-reading problem: locate the clean edge, match depth to the bait band, set an angle that crosses the seam, and pick a drift appropriate to tide strength. When the current lines up the lane looks simple. When it doesn't, the best move is usually to fish the lee pockets and wait for the next predictable window.
