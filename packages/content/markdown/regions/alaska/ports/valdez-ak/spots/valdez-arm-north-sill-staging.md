Why the north sill matters

The north sill is where Valdez Arm stops being a long, flat fjord and starts dumping into a wider basin. The depth shoals quickly, a short steep ramp forms, and the headland forces a flow turn. That geometry turns a broad exit into a narrow exchange lane — bait gets pushed up against the shoal, water peels off in thin tongues, and the current either strips clean water off the sill or slams silt back into the arm. When the pieces line up the sill does two consistent things: it stages incoming salmon on a tidy peel, and it creates a down‑current intercept where halibut sit to pick off spilled bait. Neither is automatic; both live or die on tide speed, water color, and whether a clean pocket forms on the lee side.

Why the sill concentrates bait

Observation first: incoming pushes shove denser basin water over the sill and then the flow peels back. That shear compresses forage against the ramp and often lifts bait into the mid‑column and surface. Salmon will stage on that peel — especially early Chinook pulses — riding the cleaner edge before committing upriver. Where the fast water softens on the down‑current flank a lee pocket or eddy forms; bait spills off the sill into that seam and halibut line up in the spill‑off. Color matters: glacial runoff can smear the seam and kill the pattern, but a small clean tongue will compress life into a tight lane.

How the water organizes salmon and halibut

- Incoming tide and salmon staging
  The peel on the first half of an incoming tide is the conveyor. Salmon ride the seam outside the mouth and hold on the cleaner, denser band until they decide to run. When the peel stays steady you’ll see short, repeated surface breaks and salmon lanes that tighten into a working line. The fleet treats that line like a gate — run it steady, present in the depth band where bait stacks, and fish it while the peel holds.

- Down‑current halibut intercept
  When current crosses the ramp and softens on the lee you get a repeatable intercept lane. Bait compressed against the sill drifts off the edge into the spill‑off and halibut position to feed on that flow. The bites show up as repeatable strikes on one drift lane more than sporadic hits across the flat. Best window is the soft phase of the push and through slack, not the ripping peak.

- Color and silt control the machine
  If runoff peels a broad muddy stain off the arm the sill is usually dead. If a narrow clean pocket peels into the basin — even a small tongue — life compresses and the spot wakes. Birds often pick that out before sonar, unless the seam is choked with silt.

Signs a window is building

Watch for combinations, not single cues. The useful combinations:

- bait pinned tight against the sill instead of sweeping wide
- birds making repeated, local drops over the same patch
- sonar arches narrowing and rising into the mid‑column
- salmon lanes tightening at the surface on an incoming push (short, repeated breaks)
- a clean pocket forming on the down‑current flank (small and defined, not a wide stain)

Birds without a bait pin, or sonar without an appropriate tide, rarely hold. When multiple signals line up, you have a window you can work.

Fishing the seam — where to run and when

Salmon (how I fish the peel)
Fish the seam on the first half of the incoming while the peel remains consistent. Present gear in the depth band where the bait is stacked — if bait climbs, shorten spreads and run shallower lines. Keep speed and depth steady through the peel; if fish tighten to the face make controlled, tight turns through the working line rather than broad passes that strip the school. The goal is repeatable presentation where fish are staging, not wide coverage.

Halibut (setting the intercept)
Halibut want the spill‑off when bait drifts across the ramp slowly. Set up a drift that crosses the down‑current edge rather than running along it: start up‑current in the cleaner pocket, cross the seam at a shallow angle, and let baits pass the lee into the spill‑off. You’ll see repeatable bites on one line if the drift is right. If the current is ripping, pull off and wait for the softening; the intercept rarely works in full steam.

Drift control and repeatability
The useful drift is one you can repeat. If the tide picks up and drift speed goes wild, reposition into the cleaner pocket or slip off until the exchange softens. Holding a narrow lane and repeating passes is more productive than trying to cover the whole sill while the flow reshapes under you.

Tactical breakouts — when to stay and when to move

Stay when:
- birds hold tight to a narrow tongue and keep coming back
- sonar arches compress against the sill and bait depth stays consistent
- you can make repeated passes through the same lane

Move when:
- the water is broad and murky with no clean pocket
- birds are scattered and making one‑off drops
- the current shifts from manageable to ripping in minutes (the sill can reshuffle fast)

Pressure and common mistakes

Common mistakes I see: treating the sill like a permanent spot — run the same line all day and you’ll be chasing dead water; committing to the obvious outside edge when the better halibut drift is tucked in the lee inside the arm; or trying to grind fish out when the clean pocket collapses under the fleet. Small clean pockets die fast under pressure. When it gets crowded, look for the same exchange on the far side of the sill or deepen the drift where others won’t hold.

Micro‑observations worth remembering

- The clean pocket is small and it’s the only thing that matters when the run is working.
- Birds often show the window before sonar, unless silt has smothered the seam.
- When salmon stage heavy the halibut intercept can be right under the school — timing your drift so baits pass the spill‑off, not the foam line, makes the difference.

Read current and color first, bait and birds second. The north sill is a simple machine: tide delivers bait, the sill edits it, predators use the seams. Favor the cleaner pocket over obvious foam, time your drift to the soft phase of the exchange, and be ready to move when the seam peels or the fleet collapses the window.
