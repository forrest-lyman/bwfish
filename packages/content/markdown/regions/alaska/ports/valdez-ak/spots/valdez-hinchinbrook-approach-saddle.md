The Exchange Saddle is where Gulf water squeezes into Prince William Sound. The bottom drops, then shoals, the mouth narrows and the flow accelerates. That sequence—sill, sudden channel, then a flaring basin—makes tidy hydraulics: a fast ferrying jet between calmer lee pockets, hard shear lines, standing seams and quick eddies. Those features organize bait into lanes. When the pieces line up you get tight, short windows for migrating salmon and concentrated picks for halibut and reef fish.

## How the saddle organizes bait and fish

You can read this spot like a door that opens and shuts. Water is forced through a narrow throat, it speeds up, then peels off into quieter water on either side. The lee pockets hold the bulk of the bait; the peel line and the jet between pockets are where schools, birds and foaming fish tend to arrange.

The physical sequence is why: the sill compresses the flow, the channel channels it, the basin gives the flow someplace to shed. That compression drives bait depth and concentration more than surface foam alone. Where bait compresses against a lee-pocket edge, predators show up.

## When the window opens and when it dies

This fishery is a tide‑window fishery. Slack and the first, manageable push—flood or ebb depending on day's setup—are the useful times. Expect windows measured in minutes to a couple of hours, not all day.

What kills the window quickly:
- wind standing up against the exchange. A tidy seam at dawn becomes a confused mess under opposing wind.
- heavy glacial runoff or turbidity. Muddy tongues show on sonar but rarely hold real feeding pressure once the fleet arrives.

Where the window tends to form:
- clean pockets beside stained runoff. Those clear edges concentrate bait; the muddy tongue looks promising but usually fizzles.

## Signs the window is building

Watch for behavior first; instruments second. The reliable signals I watch for are compact and repeatable:

- birds repeatedly dropping over the same line
- sonar marks rising toward the surface along the peel/jet
- bait compressed tight against the lee-pocket edge instead of being scattered
- salmon tightening up at the fjord mouth on an incoming push

When you see two or three of those together, you should be moving into position. One alone is noise.

## Running lines for salmon staging

The seam is the organizer; you fish it with shape and control, not blind speed.

- Run controlled trolling passes through the seam rather than cutting straight across. Turn shape and lure depth change how you intercept a compressed school.
- Target shallow foamers on the outside flank of the exchange—migrating kings often school there before they peel into the arms.
- Expect to move; the window can shift laterally as bait peels. Stay with the bait, don’t expect the seam to hold in one spot for long.

I treat the pass like a timed event: get in position early, make repeated controlled passes, and be ready to exit when the marks thin.

## Where to work halibut and bottom picks

Halibut and reef fish want the calmer shoulders and lee faces once the tide softens.

- Work lee pockets and the down‑current shoulders of the sill when the current drops. Those are scent lanes and holding water.
- Set short, repeatable drifts that cross the scent lanes. They beat long, wandering runs because they keep you in consistent contact with the fish-holding edges.
- Keep presentations low and line angles tidy when the current changes; a good drift will disappear when the tide stands up or reverses.

If the boat traffic piles in, shift to smaller, cleaner pockets off the main lane—those often hold better fish later in the window.

## Rockfish and lingcod on the cleaner side

The cleaner side of the exchange gives you closer-to-structure picks.

- Fish the deeper edges and nearby pinnacles on the clean side of the exchange rather than trying to force the jet.
- Vertical presentations on the lee face are more reliable than casting into the turbulent flow. You want the bait to fall straight through the holding zone.
- Pressure from other boats will move the better fish to less obvious corners; check the edge away from the busiest boats if marks thin.

## How the fleet uses the saddle

Locals treat this place like a switch: one boat finds a clean lane and the mark fills, then dies quickly as pressure arrives. That behavior changes where the good water is.

Patterns to note:
- small, clean pockets usually outperform obvious foamers once the fleet shows up
- big boats commonly stage offshore and time a run for slack; sprint-and-hold often beats a slow, late arrival
- experienced skippers prefer to sit upcurrent of a pocket and time their drift through it rather than trying to anchor in the jet

Read the fleet as another signal. If everybody’s chasing the obvious foamer, look for the cleaner water beside it.

## Approach from Valdez and safety around the entrance

You can work the saddle from Valdez in reasonable weather, but timing and respect for the water are mandatory.

- Don’t run the approach when wind is opposing the exchange; seas steepen fast and the entrance becomes a confused, dangerous run.
- Plan to arrive before the peak of the current if you want a slack window. Coming in late usually means running into the worst of the flow.
- Glacial runoff and whitewater hide birds and make sonar noisy; prefer cleaner lanes even if they look less obvious on first pass.
- Always have an inside alternative—the harbor and nearby arms give useful fallback water when the entrance is blown out.

Treat the transit like part of the fishery: your arrival timing and safety margin determine whether you get a window or just a weather beat.

## Quick checklist before committing from Valdez

- fuel and daylight margins for a possible wait—windows are short
- wind direction vs predicted tidal flow through Hinchinbrook
- visible clean pockets beside any stained water
- VHF contact—ask who’s on the saddle and where the cleaner lanes are

A fast, well-timed push to a clean pocket is usually worth more than arriving late to the obvious foamer.

## Micro‑observations that matter

A few small things I watch that change a day:

- the saddle fishes like a door: it opens short and closes fast
- the cleaner pocket beside the main peel usually wins; the muddy tongue rarely holds once the fleet arrives
- what usually matters most: tide rate and where you sit relative to the peel, bait compression and depth, and getting on before the pressure wave arrives
- what usually matters less: running the exact same line as everyone else, or chasing small isolated foamers with no bait under them

The Exchange Saddle is honest water. When tide, bait and visibility line up it concentrates fish in a way that rewards timing and positioning. When they don’t, it can look busy and fish dead. Plan the run, pick the cleaner lane, and treat the window like something you have to arrive at—not something that will wait for you.
