## Why these reef fingers matter

There aren’t big banks here—just short, broken reef fingers and rock knobs that sit off the fjord mouths around Valdez Arm. What they do is simple: they turn a messy, silted fjord into a string of tight, predictable pockets. Tide-driven water peels along the headlands, pushes bait against the face, and leaves a cleaner strip on the lee side where rockfish and lingcod stack. That concentration is what makes a tiny finger fish more like a bank for an hour than a scatter of rocks.

Think of the fingers as bait sorters. The structure is compact; the tide is the sorter. When the clean band forms and bait is compressed into it, the fingers light up in a way the open basins rarely do here.

## Reading the tide peel and the clean band

Start with the current. The useful movement is a peel—either incoming or outgoing—that runs down the headland and drops a ribbon of bait into the lee of each finger. You’ll see three things in sequence:

- a tideline where dirty fjord water meets cleaner water,
- a narrow, cleaner strip immediately inside that tideline,
- a repeatable vertical mark on the forward face when the tide begins to move.

Those are the signals you want. If the water is milky from glacial runoff the pocket disappears; if you can find a clean band a few boat lengths inside the plume you've probably found fishable water. The difference between a working finger and a dead one is often just that narrow lane of cleaner water.

## How fish stack on the face and in the lee

Bait compresses into a thin band between the dirty plume and the lee. Rockfish tend to use the vertical relief—marks that climb the face, hang over ledges, and repeat on multiple passes are your classic rockfish fingerprints. Lingcod sit lower and can be out of obvious marks until you put gear into their ambush zone; they’ll take heavy jigs or a well-presented live bait when your drift angle puts the bait into cracks and holes.

The productive water isn’t the highest top of the reef; it’s the cross-current lane—the transition from face to lee where bait moves past holes and ledges. That transition is the ambush line.

Occasional halibut show up near larger fingers where sand pockets descend from the reef, but they’re intercepts, not the main target here.

## What the sounder shows and what to trust

Sonar finds the pocket before birds do. A clean, defined arch on the forward face that repeats is worth more than scattered returns in silted water. Sonar can lie where silt mixes—so prioritize tight arches and a defined band of bait over a mushy, widespread return.

If marks climb off the face into the water column, that’s your cue to change tactics: shorten the drift and get a vertical presentation into the zone.

## Presentations that actually work

Vertical work wins in these pockets. Two presentations cover most of the day: slow vertical jigs and live bait.

- Vertical jigs: slow, controlled falls into the face, then work them with a cadence that keeps the metal through the strike zone as you clear the edge. When marks rise, shorten the fall and cadence toward contact.
- Live bait: small herring or sand lance run tight over holes. Keep the bait near structure but avoid tangles; a short tip-down or tight drift over the hole is usually enough.

Quick chum pulses or a tip-down will restart a quiet pocket sometimes, but the effect fades fast with pressure.

## How to run the drift

Short, controlled drifts beat long bombs here. Run high and stop close—drift across the face, not parallel to it. You want the bait to move past the ledge into the lee, not sweep past the ambush points at speed. Tight drift control, braking, or a quick anchor stop are often the difference between hits and nothing.

Work both presentations on the same drift: jig the face on the way past, then drop a live bait into the hole as you slow or stop. If the marks climb off the face, switch immediately to a vertical jig and shorten the drift.

## Signs the window is building

Look for these signals in order—when several line up, the pocket is likely to produce:

- a narrow clean band inside a silted fjord mouth,
- bait arches stacking on the forward face,
- marks that climb from the ledge on successive passes,
- birds or surface splashes concentrated over the lee (when visible).

The first two are the most reliable. Birds are helpful but often absent in these confined pockets.

## What kills the bite: common mistakes

A few repeatable errors will wipe these pockets fast:

- fishing the high point of the finger instead of the face-to-lee transition,
- running long, fast drifts that sweep gear past ambush points,
- parking on a foamer when there’s no bait holding below,
- staying on a finger after the clean band has disappeared with the tide.

If you can’t find a clean strip, move. These fingers are small-value, short-window stops—don’t wait for the bite to come back.

## How the fleet treats these fingers

Local boats treat them like short-value hits: tight work, fast moves, and first-boat advantage. The first boat into a clean pocket normally takes the best marks; after repeated passes fish go deep or tuck into corners. Small, clean pockets often outfish obvious foamers because the fish are stacked and accessible for a short time.

Pressure matters. If you expect to hold a pocket, keep the operation tight and move off before the pocket collapses.

## Operational cautions and quick field notes

- Glacial silt and wind flip a pocket in minutes. If the water looks milky, hunt for the narrow clean strip rather than fishing blind.
- Be ready to change depth and presentation quickly; the pocket will shift as the tide accelerates through the sill.
- Clean water three boat lengths inside a silted fjord mouth is worth the extra anchor or slow drift.
- Shorten the drift and switch to a vertical jig when marks climb off the face.

These fingers aren’t dramatic—they reward boat control, quick vertical work, and a nose for clean water amid the silt. When the tide peels bait across the right face and a narrow lee holds, the day compresses into short, repeatable windows. Work them tight and move off before the pocket dies.
