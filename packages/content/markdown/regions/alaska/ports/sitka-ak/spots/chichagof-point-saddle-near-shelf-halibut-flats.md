## How the saddle reads and why halibut sit it

The saddle is the broad, shallow band between the broken rocky point and the first outer bank. On sonar it shows as a low, wide high-relief stripe over clean sand — occasional cobble ribs and bits of broken rock buried in the flat. From the surface it often looks cleaner when the tide peels around the point.

Halibut treat it like a first-shelf lane. It isn't a steep drop that corrals fish; it’s more of a contour the bait moves across. When bait stacks and the boat can sweep steadily across that contour, the fish line up shallow and take. When current is wrong, the same bottom is empty.

Observe first: find the clean band of bottom and the ribs on your screen, then look for bait hanging over or just off those ribs. The structure makes sense when the bait lingers along it.

## When the current fits

Current angle and speed are the deciding factors here.

We see productive windows when the boat can execute a steady, sweepable drift across the saddle — typically around slack into an incoming or mid‑flood where the lee softens the flow. In practice that means keeping the boat moving at about 0.5–1.2 kt across the feature. At 0.8–1.2 kt the shelf often holds; at 2 kt the face clears and the fish leave.

Short squeezes around the point create quick windows. You can get a couple good hours on a favorable tide flip, then the water peels and the bite fades. The micro-observation you want to bookmark: as the tide softens, fish slide shallow. When the drift eases the bites come.

## Bait, marks, and what to watch on sonar

This saddle fishes when bait shows and stays in the column.

Primary forage is usually sand lance and small schooling herring pushed by the current around the point. Birds help, but they aren’t the only story — on big bait days the birds sit wide while the halibut work under the surface.

On sonar the productive picture is a clean band of scattering bait over the saddle or discrete bait clouds hugging downhill ribs. Marks that elongate with the drift or streak upward toward the surface often mean a bite window is building.

Signs the window is building:
- bait lying in the column directly over the saddle
- marks elongating with the drift instead of lifting and fleeing
- boats holding direction instead of constantly re‑positioning
- current easing, especially inside the lee of the point

Those are the things you can act on before the first rod slams.

## How we run the saddle — presentation and drift control

We fish this more like controlled sweeps than long down-current runs. The point is to let the bait hang in the scent trail as the boat crosses the contour.

Keep presentations near the bottom but off the pad. Fish here often take in the top 10–20 ft off the bottom; too much weight buries the bait, too little lets it ride out of the scent zone. Whole herring, taped baits, or big live sand lance work — whatever keeps scent and profile consistent in the flow.

Drift setup steps:
1. Motor into the wind to slow the boat and set a steady angle.
2. Use a small drogue or modest sea anchor when the tide is stronger to control swing.
3. Start the drift high, let the boat sweep across the saddle, keep short leaders, and maintain contact.

Halibut here can bite softly and move off if you give slack, so keep the line tight and rebait cleanly but not repeatedly in the same small patch. If the tide peels hard, break off and work lee pockets inside the point; those will hold when the main saddle is blown out.

## Fleet behavior and pressure patterns

Sitka boats use Chichagof Point as a predictable first-step: a quick check from the harbor or a fallback when outer runs don’t pencil. Commercial effort during slow salmon windows concentrates pressure on the flats.

Experienced skippers tend to:
- check the saddle early on a tide change, then move fast if bait isn’t stacking
- commit to controlled sweeps and a defined drift line instead of aimless circling
- watch the inside lee for cleaner water; better current often stays inside the point when the outside peel is messy

Pressure reactions are predictable. Multiple boats often push deeper-holding fish to the edges or a touch deeper. Repeated frantic rebaiting scatters the bait and kills the window faster than a single, well-executed drift. Quieter, steady presentations usually out-fish heavy drops when boats pile in.

## Common mistakes and what fails fast

The saddle dies quickly under a few predictable conditions: strong southeast winds against the tide, a ripping current, or fog that hides bird signals and makes positioning across a broad saddle tricky.

Common errors I see:
- Overrunning the drift with too much engine correction — that converts a good sweep into a scattered bait patch.
- Chasing another boat's track instead of reading your own marks.
- Anchoring or trying to grind the same small patch when the feature rewards movement and sweepable presentation.

When visibility is poor, trust your sonar and the ribs on the bottom. When the tide peels hard, move to lee pockets or run deeper if you can.

## Decision rules on the water

Make simple calls:
- If bait stacks over the saddle and the drift holds 0.5–1.2 kt: stay and grind controlled sweeps.
- If current rips or the bait peels off the bottom: move inside to lee pockets or run to a deeper saddle if fuel and weather allow.
- If pressure is high and marks are shy: fish the edges and ribs with quieter presentations instead of repeat heavy drops.

Short version: Chichagof Point Saddle is a practical, weather‑sensitive halibut piece. When the tide softens and bait stacks along the sand-and-gravel contour, controlled sweeps produce. When the current rips or the water cleans out, it’s a spot to move from, not grind forever.
