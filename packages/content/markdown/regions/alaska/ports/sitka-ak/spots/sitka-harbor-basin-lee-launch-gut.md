Sitka’s first‑mile fishing lives in the geometry of the basin. The harbor squeezes boats toward a narrow launch gut on the way out; off the piers the bottom breaks into shallow sand fingers, kelp necks and scattered cobble and boulder before the water opens into Sitka Sound. That near shelf—mostly 10–60 ft—sets every early drift and tells you where the halibut and structure fish will sit when the tide behaves.

How the gut sets the drift
The launch gut is the place that decides whether you get a short, tidy drift or a washed‑out mess. At tide change the gut amplifies the exchange—short, sharp jets push bait and water through, then peel off into calmer lee pockets. If you learn the gut’s timing you can pick the exact seam where the drift will begin before you leave the dock.

Small features matter here: points and breakwaters throw tidy lee pockets; kelp beds form narrow necks and downstream seams; sand tongues run out from the flats and mark where halibut will park. The same GPS number fishes differently depending on how the gut peels into those features.

Current and timing
The basin is governed by brief, violent changes rather than long gentle shifts. The gut runs hard for about 20–30 minutes on tide flips—those are the cleaning pulses that move bait fast and reset the basin. After the jet eases, bait piles into lees and predators move in; that easing window is where the bite concentrates.

Wind against tide in the gut makes a confused chop and a surface sheen that hides bird work. In that situation rely on sonar and watching kelp tails instead of looking for birds on the surface.

Where the bait hides
Herring and sand lance are the currency here. They stage along kelp edges and the outer face of the flats. When a clean pocket forms behind a point you’ll see small birds working low and short sonar arches tightening under the kelp. Those moments convert a lee into a dense, short‑range predator zone: rockfish and lingcod around kelp and boulder, halibut hanging on the sand seams just outside the kelp edge.

Signs the window is building
When the basin goes from quiet to hot you’ll notice the sequence more than any single signal:

- bait hugging the lee side of kelp and compressing in the water column
- sonar arches rising off the bottom along the sand/rock transition
- birds moving from free‑flying to working low over a pocket
- boats slowing instead of blasting the gut

The micro‑observation: that 20–30 minute hard run on flips is the trigger. If you can hold bait in the lee as the jet drops, bites often follow inside that window.

Where each species sits
- Halibut: short, controlled drifts over sand fingers and shallow saddles just outside the launch gut. Drift speed and timing beat exact GPS spots—get the drift right and the fish come to the bait.
- Rockfish & lingcod: kelp necks, boulder outcrops and shallow ledge lines inside the basin. Vertical presentations and slow drifts work best once the current softens.

How I run the drift
Run out with the gut timing in your head. Arrive too early and the jet will wash your drift; too late and the bait is already scattered. My setup is simple and deliberate: position up‑current, let the boat sweep the kelp neck into the sand tongue, then control the drift so the bait lingers on the contour edge and saddles.

Tactical calls:
- Hold the lee until the flow eases. Fish move up and shallow when the current drops; moving off too early means you miss that slide.
- Watch your drift angle more than a GPS number. A ten‑degree change in angle can move your bait off the fish.
- Small lateral boat moves along the kelp edge find the bait tongue—halibut sit on the down‑current side of those fingers.
- Expect seiners and commercial pressure during salmon tides; the better small‑boat halibut bites often happen before the commercial fleet shows.

Fleet behavior and pressure
Locals treat the gut like a micro‑farm: frequent checks at tide flips and quick movement between pockets. When one pocket lights up the basin crowds fast; experienced skippers back off to adjacent kelp necks where drift control is cleaner. Pressure tends to flatten a bite—quiet, controlled drifts on the edge of the main pocket usually out‑produce a fight for the center.

Safety and practical notes
The gut looks sheltered until a southeast blow or an opposing tide builds short, steep swell on that short run to the outer sound. Fuel and daylight matter—this area rewards short, high‑frequency checks, but committing to deeper banks means leaving the gut behind. Fog kills bird cues; in low visibility favor tight kelp‑edge checks and disciplined use of sonar.

What usually matters more here
- timing the gut and the tide flip
- holding a clean, slow drift across the sand/rock seam
- reading bait in kelp tails and sonar

What usually matters less
- fighting for the center of a pressured pocket
- sticking to a single GPS number when the bait has migrated along the edge

If the harbor looks dead, run the gut again on the next easing. The basin shows life in short windows—when the gut peels into a clean lee and the bait queues up on the kelp tails, you’ll know it. Fish here answer the tide and the bait, not the map.
