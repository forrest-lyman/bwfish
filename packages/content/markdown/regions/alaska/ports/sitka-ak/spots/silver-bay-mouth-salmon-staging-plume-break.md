This place is simple and repeatable: a small river plume peels off Silver Bay, meets cleaner Sitka Sound water, and sets a short seam against a shallow drop and a lee pocket behind a low headland. When the plume lines up with tide the birds and the fleet turn up fast. Watch the stain and you’ll know the story before the sonar tells you.

Why the plume/clean seam matters
The seam concentrates bait and creates a holding edge that salmon use as a staging lane during an incoming tide. The stained water pushes bait to the downcurrent edge; salmon ride the cleaner flank and pick off compressed bait where the current shear slows it. It’s close to port, tight on tidy days, and honest when the tide cooperates.

Tide and current — the window that actually works
The lane is made by an incoming tide interacting with a small freshwater outflow and the headland’s lee. The useful break is a narrow tongue of stained water against cleaner flow; the fish hang the flank when bait stacks there.

Best window and failure modes:
- Flood beginning through mid‑flood is the productive window. That’s when the plume peels out and the stain is a narrow, punchy edge.
- If the tidal speed gets too high the seam widens and fish stop holding — bait disperses and the lane blows apart.
- At slack or on a weak tide the bait flattens; the edge dissolves into diffuse marks and the staging lane disappears.

Bait behavior and early signs
Herring and sand lance collect on the downcurrent edge of the stain. The bait peels off the point before the salmon arrive — that’s a reliable sequence. Birds are often the first live indicator; they’ll sit on the seam when the bait is tight, giving you a visual heads‑up before sonar shows stacked marks.

Signs the lane is live
- birds riding the stain/clean seam
- small, stacked marks on sonar along the color edge
- boats holding parallel drifts just outside the stain
- quick surface boils or foamers on the clean flank

How the fish use the seam
Chinook stage on the flanks and will take a presentation nailed to the cleaner side when tide and bait align. Coho and chum follow the same seams but tend to be shallower and take faster. Halibut work short intercepts on the adjacent sand and shell flats when your drift speed lets the bait linger near bottom.

Behavior pattern to expect: when the seam is tight, bait compresses and salmon feed directionally — they move upstream along the cleaner side and hold with the stain at their back. When the tide ramps up the seam disperses and the same area goes quiet; boats that chase visible boils often find nothing once the structure washes out.

Fleet behavior and pressure
This lane pulls boats fast during salmon windows. The experienced boats park slightly outside the stain and run measured parallel drifts; less experienced crews chase the boil points and can push fish below sonar or spook them. Seiners and charters will show up during peak runs — pressure can shut a short lane quickly.

How locals work it
1. Approach: pick your tide window and make a tidy, fuel‑conscious run from harbor. Get there early; the lane builds fast.
2. Search: run the plume/clean edge slowly with sonar. In fog, birds come first.
3. Presentations: for kings use flasher/mooching setups; for faster coho/chum switch to fly‑lining or light sinker live‑bait on the clean flank. Keep everything moving slowly through the column.
4. Halibut intercepts: drift the nearby sand fingers on slack to moderate flood with short baits on bottom and tight weight control.
5. Drift control: only anchor or pole‑hold if the tide allows a natural presentation — too much anchor pressure kills the setup.

Common mistakes that kill the window
- running straight into the boil points and spooking fish
- fishing the middle of the stain instead of the cleaner flank
- committing to the lane after the tide has already peaked and the seam is dispersing

Electronics — what to look for on the screen
Sonar will show bait stacked tight on the downcurrent edge; marks that streak toward the surface mean the window is building. If the marks flatten to a uniform band and the birds leave, the seam has usually softened and the holding behavior is gone.

When to move
This lane is honest and short‑lived. If the seam softens and the fleet folds, don’t waste time. Check the adjacent lee pockets, kelp fingers, and inside the bay — the bait moves fast and the work often slides shallow or to the next point. Learn the tide rhythm, watch the bait, and treat the stain/clean break as the working edge, not a magic GPS dot.
