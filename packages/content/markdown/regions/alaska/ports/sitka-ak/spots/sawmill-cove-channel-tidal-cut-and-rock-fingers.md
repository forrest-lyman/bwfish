The spot announces itself by shape: a narrow tidal cut flanked by low islands and bedrock fingers, with small kelp pockets tucked in the lee. It’s a first‑mile decision out of the harbor — short transit, quick checks, and the option to either grind a short window or hole up in protected water if the outside gets ugly.

## How the cut organizes the water

This is short‑water, tide‑driven fishing. The throat accelerates the flow mid‑tide and the channel peels water off the headlands into cleaner seams behind rocks. On an incoming the push peels along the flanks and stains seams; on the outgoing that same geometry forces bait into the lee as the current backs. Those tidal compressions are the piece you watch — when bait stacks predictably in a lee, predators move in fast.

Slack here is brief. The useful windows live on the tail of the push: too early and nothing has squeezed, too late and the pocket blows out. Wind that’s opposite the tide will shear the lee seams; steady, tide‑aligned wind preserves the pockets and keeps bait tight.

## Reading the lee pockets and rock fingers

The fingers and kelp form the strike geometry. A clean pocket behind the first major rock is the common trigger — a subtle color change or a faint stain across the cut is often all you get before things light up. Those pockets hold cleaner water and concentrate sand lance and small herring against the kelp necks.

Watch sonar here. Bait will often show on the screen before the birds commit, especially on tight tides. Birds do arrive, but they’re frequently a lagging indicator in this tight water. When the bait sits tucked against a finger you'll see marks climb a few meters in the column and compress against the face instead of spreading out.

## Bait signatures and target fish behavior

Sand lance and small herring are the main show. They ride the clean seams and tuck into the kelp and broken rock. Salmon — Chinook and coho — stage just outside the mouth on incoming tides and pick the flanks as the tide peels. When bait pins into the cut they peel in to feed; when it’s loose they work the outside and ignore the neck.

Rockfish and lingcod hold the vertical structure — short, violent strikes when the current softens and bait hangs on the edge. Halibut are occasional: the nearby sand pockets will produce on slack or a gentle outgoing drift, but this isn’t the primary halibut patch.

## How boats work the cut

Most crews treat the cut like an early check or a fallback stop. Run in, sweep the lee pockets with sonar, and decide: linger and work a bite or move on. The common approaches are controlled drifts tight to the kelp necks and fingers, pitching jigs or live bait into known ledges. Anchor drops are uncommon; vertical work is usually done while drifting and pitching into the structure.

When salmon refuse to hold inside the throat, short uphill troll runs outside the mouth will intercept those fish staging on the push. Keep drift speed low enough to let bait linger off the face — too fast and fish slide off the bite zone.

## Signals the window is building

You’ll hear this from the boat: simple, sharp signs that matter.

- A narrow band of bait hugging the lee edge on sonar.
- Birds lowering into the pockets instead of circling wide.
- Marks compressing upward a few meters rather than smeared across the cut.
- Salmon making short, directional pushes along the flank instead of milling.

These signals usually show up in sequence: sonar bait first, birds next, then fish commit. If you get that chain, position quickly; these windows fill and empty fast.

## Pressure effects and common mistakes

The better pockets go dead fast under multiple boats. Once a seine or a couple of boats work a pocket it either disperses or moves to an undisturbed seam. Two things I see cost days: staying on the first foamer after it cools, and dragging presentation speed when current spikes. Fish will sink or leave the face at peak flow; the rock that held them at mid‑tide will be empty at peak.

Repeated casts to the same foamers rarely pay; fresh presentations and small positional moves (10–20 yards) toward cleaner lee water are what convert quiet marks into hooks.

## Small adjustments that change the day

This water rewards detail. Time your drift for the tail of the tide push. Run sonar actively — marks show before birds and before the bite. Move up or down the flank in small increments; a little upstream into cleaner lee often turns a mark into a hookup. Respect fleet pressure: give a packed pocket a pass and hunt the quieter seam.

What separates a good day from a bad here is predictable: clean, stacked bait in the lee; a manageable drift speed that holds your presentation in the strike zone; and the patience to find a quieter pocket when the harbor crowds the first one. When the cut lines up it fishes like a much larger piece of water — tight, efficient, and instantly rewarding. When it doesn’t, it looks dead fast. Read the bait, position early, and punch the narrow tide slice before everyone else figures it out.
