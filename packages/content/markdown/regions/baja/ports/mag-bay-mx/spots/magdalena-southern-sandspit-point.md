The southern sandspit reads simple on a chart: a long, low tongue of sand that shelters a broad shallow flat on the bay side and empties through a narrow throat into a short, mixed sand-and-rock edge outside the mouth. In practice that geometry creates three working parts — a wide staging flat, a confined evacuation throat, and a short hard edge where predators wait. The spot fishes when those three parts line up; when they don't, it looks quiet.

## Why this spit matters

On many tides the flats are the first place bait reaches a confined exit. When the channel pulls the flats down the bait funnels predictable and tight. That predictability is the whole point: predators can pick a lane and hold it.

Two outcomes I watch for every trip: a falling tide that concentrates roosters and big jacks on the lee, and isolated rock or shell ridges near the point that hold snapper and reef fish. And a practical note: when the bar is rough the spit becomes the safe play — fish the lee and flats instead of risking a run to the shelf.

## The tidal conveyor: how the lee edge forms

On the ebb the flats empty through the throat beside the spit and the current peels a thin outbound finger along the outside edge. Bait — mullet, sardina, small baitballs — compress into that finger. Predators either sit just outside the lee on the downhill edge or stack on the uphill side inside the throat, waiting for the flow to push food past them.

Typical pattern through an ebb:
- Early ebb: bait begins to move, sierra and smaller pelagics stack in the throat. Sonar shows marks even if the surface is flat.
- Mid ebb: the finger tightens, birds and surface activity often show up, roosters and jacks patrol the outside edge.
- Late ebb into slack: bait thins quickly; fish slide shallow or disperse into the flats and the window closes fast.

Observe the pace of evacuation. Fast, clean exits light the outside seam; slow, smeared drains leave the predators absent or very tentative.

## Micro-observation: what changes the mood

On a dozen trips the pattern was the same: when the flats emptied fast the roosters hit solid. When the evacuation was drawn out the lane rarely produced. So watch the rate at which bait leaves the flats, not just the presence of birds. A visible seam, clean water moving along the outside edge, or a concentrated line of foam are better commitment signals than scattered surface activity.

Two things I wait for before committing: bait visibly evacuating the flats, and current clean enough to form a distinct seam across the throat. If outside water is stained or the bar is rolly, the setup feels wrong and fish will be cautious or absent.

## Signs worth watching

These are quick reads that tell you the lane is working:
- Bait lines streaming from mangrove cuts toward the throat.
- Birds sitting just outside the point instead of scattered over the flats.
- Tight, narrow sonar marks moving through the shallow channel.
- A clear outbound seam hugging the outside of the point.

Any single sign is useful; two or more together is the call to commit.

## Presentations that actually work

Roosters and big jacks care about pace, angle, and a bait that looks trapped. Keep presentations tight to the hard edge and move them with the current — fighting the seam almost never helps.

Simple, practical options:
- Live bait on a running leader presented from the up-current side into the seam. Let the bait ride with the flow and swing past the outside edge.
- Large, slow poppers or plugs on the outside face when fish are visible. Use a slow cadence with pauses; roosters often hit when the bait looks like it’s escaping the edge.
- Long casts to the outside of working birds and let the bait surf back toward the point on the current.

Don't overwork the same patch. A hot fish will close after a few casts; heavy repeating and circling the boat pushes them off the lane.

## Bottom shots and the hard patches

There are a few short, scattered hard spots and shell ridges just beyond the sand. Snapper and reef fish sit tight to those features and will take bottom presentations when the current pushes bait across the face.

Tactics that win here:
- Short drifts across the patch with slow, bottom-contact presentations — small cut bait or natural baits that hang in the texture.
- Anchor or drift upstream and drop baits to the downhill face; keep the boat off the mark so the line doesn't bury under current.
- When snapper are picky, let the bait sit longer and watch for light, tentative takes — patience and small adjustments in presentation matter.

## Boat handling and access

The bar and swell control your options. If the mouth is rough the spit is your fall-back; you can still catch fish on the lee and flats on the right ebb.

Practical handling notes:
- Approach from the bay side on a falling tide and set up to present baits on the up-current side of the seam.
- Holding speed is often slower than it feels — let the current move the bait.
- Keep the boat out of the throat when casting to roosters; shadow and excess wake kill the bite quickly.

## Common mistakes

A few habits kill days here:
- Waiting for the perfect surface show. The bite is frequently present before birds or boils; bait evacuation and a clean seam are enough.
- Too much drift speed. Presentations that move with the current produce cleaner eats.
- Circling the same hole after a couple of fish. Fresh angles and moving lanes keep the pressure down.

## Pressure and timing

This spit is pressure-sensitive. A handful of boats can collapse a visible surface bite quickly; the best windows are short and usually early on the ebb. The repeatable plan: make an early run, test the throat on the first decent ebb, and be ready to move to the next cut when the window narrows.

## Final line

Treat the spit like a tidal gate. The flats supply bait, the throat makes the lane, and the outside edge does the catching. Time the ebb, keep baits tight to the edge and moving with the flow, and respect the bar. When those pieces line up you get a short, sharp bite; when they don't, it looks quiet for good reason.
