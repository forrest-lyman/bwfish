The “finger” isn’t a single fixed object. It’s a transient corridor — strings of weed, little kelp paddies and bits of wood pushed by the ebb and shaped by the bar and prevailing wind. Some mornings the lane sits well inside the mouth, running from the throat toward the drop. Other days the fingers peel off the bar and lie half in the lagoon and half in cleaner blue water. When that debris lines up with a warm surface filament and tight bait schools, it becomes the quickest way to mahi and smaller tuna out of Mag Bay.

## What makes the finger matter

The feature matters because it simplifies where the act happens. Floating life collects in one visible seam and bait stacks under the weed, which gives predators a single place to hunt. That’s why a short stretch of debris can look quiet one pass and explosive the next: the system is short‑lived and pressure‑sensitive. A good patch can be worked out or shredded in a few passes.

Two simple points to keep in mind: the finger concentrates surface life where shallow inside water meets the shelf, and the predator activity is usually tied to a warm tongue touching the debris. Read the tide and wind at the mouth — that’s where the useful fingers form and die.

## When it fishes

The best versions show up on a moderate ebb or a steady offshore wind with light swell. Those conditions push floating material into a narrow lane and let a warm surface tongue sit against the debris. The usual sequence we see is predictable:

- a weed/wood line forms on the ebb or with a steady NW breeze  
- sierra and small pelagics stack under the debris first  
- birds and porpoise arrive and start working the seam  
- mahi or small tuna show on the warm side of the paddy or in the lee of a larger piece of debris

Micro observation: the warm patch often sits on the bay side of the paddy, and fish tend to show on the up‑current side more than the down‑current side. When the paddy drifts into cleaner water the warm filament will frequently be on the blue side of the debris — read the color and temp grabs to know which edge to work.

## Reading the finger at the mouth

If you’re coming in, treat the finger like a checkpoint — quick visual and electronic scans before you commit. On your first look you’re trying to answer three things: are there bait under the weed, is there a warm filament touching it, and are birds or porpoise committing?

First pass: cross the finger at an angle, slow down and watch for birds, porpoise and color change. Keep electronics on and look for marks under or just downcurrent of the debris. If you see life, slow up for a careful second pass. If it’s quiet, move on — the finger can be a time suck.

## Signs the paddy is holding fish

These are the things that mean it’s worth committing a few passes:

- birds repeatedly working the same patch  
- baitfish boiling or “sizzling” under the weed visually  
- sonar marks directly under or just downcurrent of the debris  
- porpoise circling the line rather than merely passing through  
- a temperature grab that shows a quarter‑degree or more difference across a short distance

If you get two or more of those signals, you’ve got a live patch. One alone — for example, a neat long line of weed with no birds — usually isn’t enough.

## How the water moves it

Tide across the bar defines where the fingers live. Strong ebb tightens the lane and pushes it farther seaward; slack or flood scatters the material back into the flats and mangroves. Wind stacks and tears the line — a light, steady offshore breeze makes tidy fingers; a gusty onshore blow turns the surface into scattered patches you’ll chase all day.

Operational rule: if the finger sits just outside the mouth in cleaner water, it’s worth a few cautious passes. If it’s matted inside and the bar is sloppy, you’re usually better off fishing the outside edges of channel mouths.

## How to work it

When a patch is hot you don’t need to invent anything fancy — you need controlled presentations in the right place. Work in stages and let the fish tell you where to commit.

Search pattern & commitment
1. First pass: cross at an angle, watch and scan.  
2. Second pass: slow troll or drift along the warm side and make long casts with live bait to the outside edge.  
3. Third: if birds or sonar marks persist, run a short grid across the seam — don’t stick to a single line until the action is sustained.

Tactics that pay off
- slow down and watch; many bites are short, bright affairs that need a calm presentation.  
- long casts to the outside or downwind edge with live bait — present just off the kelp/wood shadow rather than into the mat.  
- when fish show aggressively, kite or chunk off the stern to keep baits spread and visible.  
- small topwaters and medium poppers work when the school is tight and feeding on the surface.  
- if sonar shows fish under the material but no surface commotion, drop a live bait or lightly weighted offering straight down and let it work back toward the seam.

### Live‑bait and long‑cast details

Pick the cleanest, most lively baits you can find — small sardina or mackerel‑sized bait are ideal. Use a long leader and drop the bait outside the paddy, then let it back toward the weed on a slow retrieve. Present out and away from the mat to avoid fouls and to trigger chasing predators. When birds are committing, switch to a spread of live baits and add a small teaser pattern to hold attention.

## Pressure reactions and common mistakes

Boats change fish behavior here quickly. Too many boats working a neat patch will push fish to the downcurrent side or force them under the mat. Repeated short casts into the same small patch scatter the school — sometimes the right move is to let a paddy rest and run a pass on the other side. If the wind freshens the finger will shred and the productive zone will move fast; don’t chase every strip of weed unless the indicators line up.

What usually matters more:
- tide across the bar  
- a narrow warm filament touching the debris  
- bait density under the weed

What usually matters less:
- a neat long line of weed without birds  
- an isolated piece of wood with no bait beneath it

## Fleet behavior and practical choices

Locals treat the finger as a fast check. If birds and porpoise are committed, one or two boats will slow and work it; if it’s quiet, most boats back off and search the first kelp line outside. Experienced skippers prefer a few methodical passes and long casts rather than noisy, repeated short casts that drive fish off. If you’re in a busy patch, work the edges and the downcurrent side — that’s where pressured fish tend to hide.

## Safety and practical choices

The bar controls access — if the paddy forces you to work just outside a shallow, shifting bar, keep a hard eye on sea state and maintain an escape plan. Often the smartest move is to fish the channel edges inside rather than risk the bar for a marginal paddy.

## Quick operational observations

- The fingers form best on a steady ebb or a steady offshore wind.  
- Warm filaments will usually sit against one side of the debris; read your surface color and temp grabs to know which edge to work.  
- Mahi can arrive in minutes and be gone in the same measure.

If the weeds are alive with birds and porpoise, set up a slow, spread presentation and keep baits away from the mat’s shadow. If the only sign is a tidy line of weed with no life, run a few passes across the seam and accept that most days it’s just driftwood.
