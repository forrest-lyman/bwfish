Scale and squeeze — that’s the first thing you notice here. The inner mangrove cuts are a tight web of channels that run off the main lagoon into flats and scours. Most of the bottom is sand and shell, but the cuts carve persistent pockets: throat holes, ledged drops and sand lips a foot to several meters deeper than the surrounding flat. Those pockets are fixed pieces of structure fish learn to use with every tide.

This is a system, not a single mark. There are main trunk channels that take the brunt of the tidal exchange, smaller side cuts that dump bait on the ebb, and a handful of deeper scours where predators stage out of sun and current. You can pick them out by how the water runs and by how quickly bait reappears in the same hole after a push.

How the tide reassigns value

Tide is the engine here. The cuts change their meaning every hour.

## Flood versus ebb: who sits where
- Flood: the bait moves into the mangroves and across the flats. Predators hold on outside edges and at mouths waiting to be pushed back out.
- Early ebb / falling tide: the hot window. Flats evacuate, bait funnels through throat holes and over sand lips; roosters and jacks stage on the uphill edge and feed.
- High and slack: work often dies. Bait scatters and the fish stop staging on obvious edges.

The strongest ambush lanes are where a narrow channel runs into a slightly deeper hole and the outgoing flow compresses bait against a lip or mangrove root line. Those seams look ordinary until the tide begins to run; then they light up.

Where the bait lives and how it moves

Inside the bay the forage is compact and local — small schooling bait, finger mullet and sardina-sized fish. They pile into cuts on the flood or hunker in gullies as the flats empty. Birds aren’t a reliable cue in here like they are outside; the water and bait behavior tell the story.

Watch for:
- curtains of bait moving along the channel throat,
- bait holding just inside a deeper hole as the flat empties,
- and small tight schools that re-form on the downstream side of a lip.

Those three behaviors match the geometry predators like: a narrow approach, a confined lane and a quick ambush. Roosters and big jacks use the same setup. Snapper and other reefy fish sit deeper on hard edges and inside scours, taking baits as current funnels food past them.

Reading the window — signs the cut is about to fish

You don’t need perfection. You need the right pieces to line up.

Signs the window is building:
- a clear outbound sweep across the channel throat,
- bait visible in the current instead of scattered over the flat,
- fish backs or wakes tucked just inside a deeper hole,
- single boats getting repeated follows or strikes.

If those things are showing, the cut will usually produce a short, intense run until slack. If the water goes meek or bait thins, the lane closes quickly.

How to set up and present (why position matters more than outfit)

Positioning beats gear in these cuts. The idea is simple: let the tide carry your bait into the ambush zone.

- Set up upstream of the throat. Presentations from slightly above the cut let natural flow put the bait into the hole.
- Fish the down-current side of the lip and the deeper throat — not the flat. If the bait is holding a couple feet down, your live bait needs to ride there, not on the surface.
- Live bait on a running leader is the default. Roosters and jacks prefer a tight, natural run; slow plugs and poppers have a place when fish are committing high.
- Keep baits tight to structure. Cast or drift so the bait fishes the uphill edge and drifts past the hole. Don’t present from the side that lets the bait blow away from the ambush.
- Boat handling is the control knob. Small changes in drift angle or speed expose your bait to a different part of the hole and can flip a strike on or off.
- When the tide softens, stop. Repeated passes will push fish deeper and out of cast range.

Why you win and why you lose is almost always about where the bait enters the seam. If your bait tracks into the ambush, you’ll see action; if you force the bait away from the edge, the fish simply sit it out.

What pressure looks like

The cuts are sensitive to pressure and to sloppy presentation.

You’ll notice:
- fish sinking deeper into the throat and refusing surface baits,
- short, tentative follows instead of solid commits,
- and cleaner bites early or during the last usable hour of the tide.

What usually matters more and less

Most boats waste time fretting about the wrong things. In these cuts:

Usually matters more:
- tidal velocity through the cut,
- where bait is stacking in the throat,
- drift angle into the lip.

Usually matters less:
- how many boats were there yesterday,
- the exact color of the water on the flats,
- long troll runs away from the system when the tide is running.

Boat patterns and fleet habits

Local pangas and skiffs run this the same way most captains learn: quick checks of a trunk cut, then commit to a favored throat if the tide is right. The experienced boats stay mobile within the system. Newer or visiting boats tend to park and throw repeatedly in one spot — the fastest way to see the fish withdraw.

Radio calls are blunt and immediate: “throat running,” “bait in the hole,” “two roosters in the mouth.” Those three phrases decide movement more than anything else.

When the cut goes quiet

The cuts die when the tide slacks or when outside conditions change the local exchange. A strong onshore wind will stain the water and push bait into random pockets instead of a controlled funnel. Repeated pressure and bad angles teach fish to sit tight on the bottom.

If the bite is gone, stop and move. The fish wait on the uphill edge; if you can present there with the tide doing the work, you’ll pull them. Trying to make the tide work for your cast generally trains the cut to hide.

Quick checklist for a usable day
- Tide: falling to early ebb through the cut.
- Water: reasonably clear in the throat.
- Bait: visible or consistently stacking in the hole.
- Boat: upstream set, drift controlled, baits at throat depth.

These inner cuts aren’t flashy. They reward timing, quiet boat work, and letting the current do the heavy lifting. When the pieces line up a single cut will outproduce a morning on an outside kelp line. When they don’t, the cuts just look like mud.
