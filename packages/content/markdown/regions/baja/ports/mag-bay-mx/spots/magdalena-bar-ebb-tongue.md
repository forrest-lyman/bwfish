The bar at Magdalena reads like a timer. On the ebb it peels off a narrow, faster tongue across the shoal and down the first mile of shelf. Bait held on the crest or trapped in the throat funnels into that lane. Predators sit on the downhill edge, pick off panicked bait and then ride the current back into the bay.

This is short‑window water. Half the time it looks ordinary; the other half it’s a frantic conveyor. Reading the tongue, the bar’s state and the tide together is the whole trick.

## Why the ebb tongue shows up

Observation: the channel throat constrains flow. When the tide goes out the water accelerates through that squeeze, shearing across the sand and forming a narrow outbound seam. Bait that was sitting on the crest or in shallow pockets gets swept into the lane and stacks where the fast water meets slower pockets.

Explanation: predators use that seam like a road. They won’t sit in the fastest water — they sit on the downhill edge where bait piles and the current does the work, or just inside the throat where evacuation funnels bait past a predictable hold.

Wind and swell change the pattern. Light swell and an unbroken tongue keep bait tight and predictable. Choppy, onshore conditions break the seam and scatter the bait; the same bar that’s firing on a calm day will turn into a confused mess with the wrong swell.

Micro‑observation: bait almost always shows on the crest first. The first porpoise or jack that pins it down usually makes the rest obvious — you don’t find the line by guessing, you find it by watching who finds the bait.

## Picking the run — when to commit and when to back off

The window closes faster than you expect. Read these conditions before you run the bar.

- Commit when you get a moderate, steady ebb, light swell and clean water. The tongue will be coherent and fishable.
- Back off if the ebb is strong and the bar is sloppy. Yes, the bite can be good, but exit and entry get dangerous quick.
- Don’t bother on slack or incoming tide. The tongue plugs, bait drifts into cuts and mangroves, and predators disperse.

Signs the window is building:
- A tight line of bait streaming from the throat.
- Surface foam or a slick that paints a dark strip across the bar.
- Birds and sierra or jacks working the same narrow band.
- Single boats that check and stop inside the tongue instead of blasting past.

If you can see the throat doing the work, the rest follows.

## Where to fish: inside the mouth versus the immediate outside

The tongue sets up two distinct zones. Fish behave differently on each; so should your presentations.

### Inside — the throat and uphill edge
Observation: predators hug the uphill edge and the deeper pockets just inside the bar on early ebb. Bait is being pushed out and they wait for it.

Tactics:
- Live bait tight on a running leader works here. Presentations that run slightly slower than the tongue often trigger follows.
- Big, slow plugs at the edge of the boil and bold surface casts for roosters when they’re showing.
- Keep the boat on the up‑current side of the mouth. If you charge through you scatter the bait; if you sit upcurrent you get natural drift into your baits.

### Immediate outside — across the bar and down the first mile
Observation: the downhill side of the tongue where fast water slams into slower pockets is where blitzes and jack pushes happen. Fish can stack shallow on the crest and then hang a few boat lengths deeper beyond the break.

Tactics:
- Short troll runs across the seam, then drop back and let the line settle down the downhill line.
- If kelp or floating wood shows up, long live‑bait casts to the outside flank often pay sooner than you expect.
- Watch depth closely — a few boat lengths can move fish from a foot of water to a few fathoms.

Remember: predators position on the downhill edge, not in the fastest water. Fish the seams and edges, not the core of the current.

## Boat handling and bar safety — practical rules

The bar moves. Treat it with respect.

- Get a recent local report. Buoys and markers lie on shifting sand.
- Time your run so you enter at a predictable tide stage and have fuel and daylight to get out. Ebb windows close fast.
- Approach slow and controlled. If the tongue is firing, stop a few boat lengths up‑current and present. Charging across the seam often scatters the bait and ruins the bite.
- Anchoring is a last resort and only in calm conditions. Anchoring on a shifting bar is how you end up repairing gear and waiting for a tow.

If you doubt the bar’s state, fish inside where exits are predictable.

## Common mistakes that kill a good window

- Trusting a single buoy or marker for depth. The sand shifts; the depth you expected can vanish.
- Racing the bar with onshore swell because the surface looks good. A firing tongue doesn’t make a bad exit safe.
- Fishing the wrong side of the seam — predators sit on the downhill edge, not in the fastest water.

## Tactical checklist before you commit
- Bar state: recent local report or eyes on the water.
- Tide stage: early ebb or falling tide preferred.
- Swell and wind: light wind and short swell keep the tongue coherent.
- Visual signs: bait line, birds, and sierra/jack activity.

Run this checklist out loud before you go and don’t skip the bar state.

## The last word

Fish the tongue when it’s tight and leave it when it goes sloppy. The bar tells you whether the day is an inside ambush or a quick outside check — watch the throat first; the rest follows.
