## Where the alley actually is

Punta Viento’s weedline alley isn’t a single mark on the GPS — it’s a travel lane. Wind, tide and the irregular shelf outside Punta Viento collect sargassum and floating debris into a narrow corridor that runs roughly parallel to the near shelf. On days with an east‑southeast swell or a steady afternoon thermal the band tightens and looks like a tidy seam on the surface. When northerlies or a hard norte get involved the mat blows apart and the alley diffuses into patches.

Observe the band first. When it’s tight it traps tiny bait, insects and small schooling fish. Dorado show up first; as the baitball thickens the corridor brings in wahoo and then yellowfin on the cleaner, faster seams that cut the weed.

## Getting there and sea‑state choices

This is a short run from East Cape when the swell is from the southeast or the wind is light. If a northwest swell or a strong norte builds, the alley goes marginal quick and the inside ledges get dirty. Best days are light mornings or steady east/southeast breezes that pile the mat into one tidy band. On northwesterlies expect the line to broaden and fish to scatter — you’ll be chasing patches or running further off.

Practical decision: if the wind’s east, sit on the line and work it. If the wind’s north, don’t waste time trolling the whole shelf; switch to spot checks on isolated clumps or push offshore for cleaner seams.

## Seasonal windows and what usually shows

Summer (peak): dorado dominate. Warm blue water and steady tide push long, multi‑boil feed windows — this is the primary money window.

Late summer–fall: wahoo and yellowfin come on the cleaner seams that intersect the weed corridor. Those bites are shorter and explosive.

Spring/shoulder: you’ll get scattered mats and occasional small dorado, but the line usually firms up later in the season as surface temps stabilize.

What precedes a proper bite: birds lowering onto the line, a tight dark color of weed, and surface fish shadowing debris. Sonar commonly shows bait stacked under the line before the birds make noise.

## How boats run the alley

Observation first, adjustment second. Most crews begin with a straight run down the line at trolling speed to locate where bait and birds are concentrated. From there you commit to the presentation the fish want.

Typical sequence I use:
- First pass: run the spread and map the seam — skirts on the corners, ballyhoo center, a small iron or cedar shallower to tempt following wahoo and yellowfin.
- Stop and chunk: when birds or foamers appear, stop the boat and chum small chunks or free‑line a livie off the stern into the working area. Keep the bow upwind of the seam so your chum drifts through the mat.
- Short casts: dorado often hit on the paddy edges — make short casts to the clean side instead of blind long trolling.
- Speed control: slow, steady speeds keep skirts in the zone for dorado. Quick bursts, flat‑falls or a fast iron work where wahoo are streaking the edge.

Run the line like you’re feeling for a pulse — if the bait is stacked and birds are sitting, tighten the presentations and slow the spread. If the line is patchy, switch to spot checks and chunking.

## Typical spread (what to set)

Use this as a starting point, then match what the birds and fish tell you.

- outrigger left: medium skirted lure, shallow
- outrigger right: cedar plug or medium skirt, mid depth
- center planer: ballyhoo or small skirt
- short cast: live skipbait or popper on the clean side

Adjust leader length and lure profile if fish push deeper or pressure gets high.

## Tide, wind and the working lanes

Tide push into the shelf and wind direction control where the alley sits and how tight it is. A flood or a strong southeast push usually aligns the line and tightens bait into uphill troll lanes — those are the lanes you want for dorado and yellowfin intercepts. East/southeast wind piles the mat; thermal afternoons can form the seam later in the day and light up a bite quickly.

When tide and wind oppose each other the weed breaks into patches and the fish shift from long corridors to scattered clusters. At that point you stop running a continuous troll and start prospecting clumps and isolated mats.

## Sonar and bird signals that matter

Use sonar to confirm bait density under the weedline. A narrow, high‑density bait band under the mat is the commit signal. Often the band sits inside slightly warmer surface water.

Bird behavior is more telling than numbers: birds sitting on one side of the line are better than birds everywhere. Boats that pick the clean side of the weed usually get first boils. A clear color contrast between blue water and the weed fringe is the best predictor for yellowfin; dorado are less picky and will work grayer water longer.

## How pressure changes the bite

Pressure makes fish move and change presentation preferences. Expect these patterns:
- Dorado: with more boats they tend to ride deeper or stick to the trailing edge of mats — shorter leaders and more natural offerings help.
- Wahoo: they’ll ghost after a few strikes; keeping speed varied, changing plugs and throwing a flat fall iron or fast skirted plug will buy you more chances.
- When the corridor collapses under pressure, fish retreat to isolated mats or the thermocline edge.

Read the fleet: if the line has several boats and only scattered wakes, start prospecting mats rather than slugging it out on the main seam.

## Key things that usually determine success

- Whether the weed is a tight, continuous line or a cloud of patches.
- Which side of the mat holds cleaner blue water.
- The timing of the tidal push relative to the morning thermal build.

Those three usually matter more than any single lure choice.

## Micro‑observation that wins days

The alley fishes best when bait sits under a clean line of weed and the birds are quietly working instead of in a frenzy. Quiet, focused bird work with a taut, dark seam is when you shorten leaders, slow down the spread and make short casts to the edges.

## Why locals check it first

Punta Viento is close enough for a short‑day gamble and consistent enough in summer that most East Cape boats run it first for dorado, then push across the shelf if the line dies. The routine is simple: check the weed, if it’s tight follow the seam, if it dies move to the adjacent thermocline. Boats that read the seam before the fleet usually finish with a few fish — because the line shows itself early if conditions are right.
