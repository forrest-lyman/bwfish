## Where the corridor sits and how it moves
The alley isn’t a fixed mark on a chart. Most days it runs 8–25 nm west to southwest of Cabo and lines up with the prevailing wind and current that pushes floating material off points and down the slope. You’ll see long seams of sargassum and kelp with dense paddies spaced along them rather than one big mat. That pattern — strings and seams punctuated by denser paddies — is the working geography. The corridor shows up where clean blue water pinches against greener upwelled water or eddy seams; that color break is what actually pulls in bait and predators.

## Why the line attracts dorado and wahoo
Observation first: most of the time the fish show up on the color contrast, not the weed itself. The sargassum is habitat and a visual focal point; the real magnet is the clean blue tongue meeting greener water. Where that contrast exists, bait stacks along the weed edges and predators stage to intercept. Dorado will hold tight to the debris and come off in bursts; wahoo prefer downhill and spooked-bait situations where fleeing bait creates speed strikes. In short: weed creates the stage, color and current set the script.

## When the alley fishes best
The corridor has a handful of repeating patterns:
- Best days: warm surface temps, light to moderate northwest wind that organizes weed into tidy lines, and a steady tide — either a push or a soft ebb. Summer into early fall produces the most persistent dorado pushes.
- Wahoo windows: when the weedline is sliding downhill on a current or when bait is spooked by a passing boat. Those are fast, short windows that reward speed and short leaders.
- Dead or marginal: homogeneous water with no color contrast, strong onshore wind that mixes the weed into a mess, or heavy pressure that flattens a paddy. A paddy’s life is often measured in hours; once it’s been hammered it cools quickly.

## Reading the line — what actually matters
Look for the color break first. If you have a clear blue tongue on one side and greener water on the other, slow down and consider the first 200–500 yards of that transition your primary zone. Signs that the corridor is worth committing to:
- Birds sitting and working low over a linear slick.
- Small baitfish tight against sargassum edges.
- Trolling marks that show uphill or inside the weedline.
- Pieces of debris holding small dorado or occasional foamers.

Quick observational checklist to confirm: water color contrast within 200–500 yards, a persistent bird patch over a seam, long seams with occasional dense paddies (these fish better than isolated mats), and tide direction — downhill favors wahoo, a steady push favors dorado.

## How the fleet treats it
This is a short-run, fast-check fishery. Boats will run the alley and hit a paddy for 5–10 minutes, then move if nothing commits. On good days boats string out along the line to avoid crushing one mat. Pressure matters: a paddy fished repeatedly goes quiet. Experienced skippers will mark a good mat and either return later or set a light spread upstream to intercept whatever’s moving through. And information travels fast — one radio call collapses the search pattern, so early sightings are valuable.

## Fishing the alley — setups and decisions
Fish both edges. The outside blue edge and the inside weedline need different presentations and you should cover both.

- Dorado setups: work persistent mats with chunking and short live-bait. Dorado will often come in waves off a mat; teasers can pull them into exposed strikes away from the debris. Fish aggressively for a short window — they usually either show in numbers or they don’t.
- Wahoo setups: prioritize downhill presentations and speed. If the line is sliding down-current, run a couple fast plugs just outside the weedline and use short, stiff leaders. The window is brief and abrupt.
- Spread and positioning: set a spread that covers the blue edge and the inside line — fast lures and teasers outside, smaller skirted lures and live options tight to the mat. If bait is flowing cleanly through the mat, present upstream into the flow rather than circling and spooking life.
- Use float structure as a trap, not a chore. A paddy with consistent life is worth anchoring a pattern on; random fluff that’s been wind-blown is not.

Don’t overcommit to one paddy. Fish it hard for a short period, then pull off and watch. If dorado are present they’ll usually return in bursts; if they’re just curious, life will leave once boats get messy.

## Micro-observations that matter
The weedline behaves like a staged highway. Fish often arrive with the blue water and pass through with it. Long seams with intermittent dense paddies consistently out-fish scattered single mats. A single persistent patch of birds is more reliable than a scattering of birds across a broad area.

## Common mistakes
We tend to make a few predictable errors:
- Chasing every tiny mat. Ephemeral bits rarely hold more than a single fish unless color and birds back them up.
- Fishing slow over downhill weed when the current is ripping — that’s a wahoo day, not a trolling-in-place day.
- Leaving a paddy too long once boats have saturated it. If you’ve pressured a mat and it goes flat, move. The next good seam is usually a few hundred yards down-current.

## When to stay close to port
If northwest swell is building and the entrance looks sloppy, the alley becomes a practical choice. It’s close, quick to check, and spends less time in exposed water than outer banks. Short runs to the nearest line beat long runs to cleaner banks when the bar is marginal.

## When the window ends
The alley produces explosive, short-lived windows. When the color contrast fades, the birds scatter, or the mat has been fished flat, don’t mourn it — the next productive line is often just down-current. Learn the feel of a productive seam, fish it fast, and move on when the signals die. Sargassum Alley isn’t glamorous. It’s efficient: find the line, read the current and color, cover the edges, and be ready to run the next seam.
