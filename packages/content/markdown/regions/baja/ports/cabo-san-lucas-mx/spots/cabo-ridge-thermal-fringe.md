## Where the ridge sits and why it matters
The Cabo Ridge is a long, shallow contour break that runs roughly west–southwest from the harbor. It’s not a single pinnacle but a continuous shoaling and falling of the slope. That subtle change in relief is enough to snag current fingers and, when warm blue water meets cooler upwelling, it produces a tight thermal fringe that corrals bait and concentrates predators.

Most working pieces sit 8–25 nm out, so it’s a short run by Baja standards and a good option when the bar and entrance sea allow an early commit. The ridge’s value comes down to one thing: how clean and firm the color/temperature edge is.

## How the water arranges bait and fish
Observation first: the fish line up where blue water kisses green water.

Warm blue water frequently rides over or past the ridge and meets cooler, greener pockets pushed off the slope. That meeting line is the thermal fringe. Current fingers will peel off the ridge flanks; bait often pins on the uphill lee and predators position on the downhill or in the seam depending on how the bait behaves.

Winds and swell change the geometry. Northwest swell tends to compress the fringe and push bait deeper; southeast winds flatten the approach and can move the productive band closer to shore. When the edge is firm you’ll usually see a narrow transition — roughly 200–500 yards — where everything stacks.

## Reading the signs: color, birds, dolphins, and marks
If you can see the divide, fish are probably nearby. The micro‑signals are reliable and quick to read if you know what to trust.

- Clear signs the fringe is holding: tight streaming bird lines right on a color break, dolphins working a narrow uphill band, short shallow streaks from uphill trolling, and isolated foamers or surface boils.
- How life stacks physically: bait pins to the uphill lee or in the seam; mahi and wahoo blitz around floating debris; yellowfin will either sit on the deeper flank or push bait to the surface when feeding aggressively.

Those small observations tell you where to run your spread and when to switch to live bait or chunking.

## What shows up and how they behave
The spot produces three quick, distinct games depending on the edge and debris present.

- Yellowfin tuna: They show when coherent bait is pinned uphill and there are steady uphill trolling marks. They can sit deep on the flank or come to the surface; once marks are visible, live bait and chunking convert best.
- Mahi (dorado): Appear fast when warm water and weedlines intersect the fringe. Small paddies or slim weed ribbons trigger tight packs and rapid blitzes.
- Wahoo: Patrol the downhill lanes and the clean blue lanes next to the edge. Their windows are sudden and brief — speed and presentation matter.

Bait seasonality matters: small pilchards and sardina in spring, larger anchoa and flying fish in the warm months. A floating paddy on the fringe changes a troll lane into a feeding zone.

## When the window is open and when it shuts
When it’s on: the fringe is clean and distinct — blue on one side, greener water on the other — with birds and foamers pinned to the line. Marks appear quickly once you commit; bites are often explosive and short. The fleet will ladder troll the edge then convert to live bait or chunk when bait compresses.

When it’s dead: water color is homogenized or chocolate, bird activity is gone, and bait is scattered or holding deep with no surface response. NW swell can rip the fringe apart or drive bait so deep teasers won’t touch it. On dead days boats usually move outward to deeper banks instead of lingering on the flat fringe.

## How the fleet treats the ridge
Locals use the ridge as a short‑buy: get there fast, test a lane, move if nothing shows. When the edge is narrow and clean, a single confirmed report will move the fleet quickly.

Typical approach: run to visible color or the ridge contour on the plotter if color’s faint, ladder troll along the ridge, then hold inside the edge if birds or marks appear. Because the productive band is narrow, several boats can collapse the window quickly; fish often go quiet under repeated pressure. Experienced skippers will back off and try a fresh lane 2–3 miles along the ridge.

Unconfirmed calls are checked visually before commitment — the ridge will confuse anyone relying solely on a text report.

## Practical setup and quick checklist
I work this spot fast. Find the edge, hold a lane, convert quickly.

Signs to commit:
- 2–4°F surface jump across a few hundred yards or a visible color break
- birds working a tight band; dolphins that stay on the line
- floating debris or an isolated weed seam

Initial steps:
1. Run to the visible color change or the plotted ridge contour.
2. Ladder troll across the edge with a mixed spread: rigid plugs, skirts, and a couple of deeper baits that track the flank.
3. Vary speeds for wahoo (faster) while keeping at least one rig at tuna speed.

If you find uphill marks:
- Deploy live bait on circle hooks immediately. Active yellowfin (and occasional marlin) prefer live presentations in tight windows.
- Prepare to chunk — hold the bite rather than trusting lures alone.

Working a paddy on the fringe:
- Fish both open‑water reaction and suspenders: mahi will hit shiners right at the weed edge while wahoo often skirt just outside.
- Keep a tight spread and be ready to move as the paddy drifts — paddies blow out fast under pressure.

When bites move subsurface:
- Add heavier leaders and slower, deeper baits. The ridge contains small vertical pockets that respond to deeper presentations.

## Common mistakes
- Committing to a single small flasher without birds or color support.
- Overfishing a visible paddy until life scatters.
- Ignoring swell — short, steep seas often collapse the usable window even when color looks right.

## Sea state and safety
The ridge is near offshore but exposed. Northwest swell builds on the run out — if the entrance or first mile is breaking, the short run becomes a slog. Favor earlier departures on marginal days and pick the closer flank of the ridge when the sea stiffens.

What matters most out there: where the color change sits relative to the contour, how compressed the bait is on the uphill side, and whether the fringe is a clean held line or a ragged wake. GPS coordinates are secondary — the edge moves with current and wind.

Fish the ridge opportunistically and quickly. When that narrow thermal fringe is firm, Cabo Ridge gives short, high‑value windows for yellowfin, mahi and wahoo. When it fades, push along the contour or head to the deeper seamounts — the ridge seldom holds the day by itself.
