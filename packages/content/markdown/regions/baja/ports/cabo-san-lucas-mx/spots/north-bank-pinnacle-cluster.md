## Where the cluster sits and how it reads on sonar
This is the first real bank once you run north out of the harbor — the clean-blue line you hit after the bar. It isn't a single ridge but a handful of steep pinnacles and short ledges spread across roughly 10–20 nm north of Cabo. Each rock comes up fast from the slope and creates downhill seams, pockets and quick relief that bait likes to hug.

On your screen the cluster shows as tight knots of returns with clean-water lanes between them. From the boat it fishes like compact, workman’s ground: short drifts, quick repositioning, and the option to deploy teasers and chunk without committing to a long run.

## How current, tide and wind set the play
The place runs on small-scale current fingers and color seams more than broad thermal fronts. That determines whether you get tight bait and tuna or scattered bait and bottom work.

- Productive setup: a clean-blue lane pushed along a pinnacle flank with a green/brown upwelling finger nearby. Fish compress on the blue side; bait pins on the seam.
- Tide matters more than absolute direction. A steady tidal sweep running down the slope creates a tidy downhill current off each pinnacle. Slack tide tends to scatter bait into troughs.
- Wind tweaks the geometry: northwest swell steepens the exposed flank and funnels birds and sargassum into the lee; southeast winds flatten the entrance and tighten seams.

Micro-observation: the working water is narrow here — think 100–500 yards over a flank, not a broad edge. That narrowness is why small changes in angle or speed move you from nothing to a working mark.

## What the bait does and the signals that matter
Bait is the currency here. The pinnacles interrupt shelf flow and give small schools places to hold.

Common forage includes flying fish, small sardina and occasional squid when the water warms. Floating weed and debris collect on the lee sides. When current pushes, bait sits tight on the downhill face; when tide fades it scatters into the troughs and often moves deeper.

Practical signals that the cluster is on:
- tight, uphill trolling marks over a single pinnacle
- a neat blue line running between darker water on one flank
- birds sitting in the lee and not actively working
- sonar showing bait pinned to the edge of rock

A persistent bird patch is a better signal here than scattered birds. Dolphins, when present, will round up bait into tighter balls and often bring yellowfin quickly.

## Two distinct fishing modes and how they behave
The cluster flips between two modes. Treat them differently.

Tight-structure mode
- Current is pushing, bait on the downhill face, bottom-game dominates.
- Expect short drifts, vertical hookups, heavy contact with amberjack, snapper and grouper.
- You fish the flank and hold the boat; vertical presentations and chunking work best.

Blue-edge mode
- A narrow clean-water lane forms along a flank or between pinnacles, bait stacks coherently and yellowfin (or billfish) patrol the edge.
- This window is short-lived and pressure-sensitive. Tuna can arrive fast and leave faster.

When the bait stays coherent, fish stay close. Once the bait scatters everything goes deeper or ghosts the area.

## How I run it: positioning, drift and presentations
Start on the downhill side and hold that flank. If you find bait mid-water, stay with it and work both trolling and live/chunk presentations.

Drift and positioning
- Short controlled drifts angled to present jigs or live bait down the slope. Move quickly between marks; don’t slog through a dead pinnacle.
- When chunking, keep the spread tight and be ready to switch to whole baits. On a steady tide you can anchor a pattern and hold position; on variable tide you want to be mobile.

Rigs and presentations
- Bottom window: vertical jigs in the 80–200 ft range and live bait on a short dropper. Current pins fish to the rock faces.
- Pelagic window: slow the troll, add larger skirts/teasers and run live baits quickly on the uphill side.

Sonar cues matter: look for tight bait pockets hugging relief and marks that climb as you slow. Those climbing marks are often the places yellowfin will exploit.

Sea-state judgment
- The cluster pays when the entrance is ugly and you want a short run.
- On a strong northwest swell the exposed flanks become dangerous and inefficient to work—even if fish are present.

## Fleet behavior and pressure patterns
Because it’s a short run it’s a common fallback when the bar or sea state rules out longer trips.

How boats work it
- Ladder drifts between pinnacles, quick hook-and-hold on a mark, then move. Trollers sweep the flanks; chunking boats hold a tight pattern on a steady tide.
- Local boats call a working pinnacle and others converge quickly. Experienced skippers split to less-pressured rocks inside the cluster rather than fight a single mark.

Pressure reactions
- If multiple boats stack one pinnacle, tuna go subsurface or shift to a neighbor.
- Bottom fish tuck deeper into vertical faces and stop responding to light jigging; chunking or heavier presentations become necessary.
- Persistent pressure often moves the productive lane down-current to the next rock.

## Simple priorities and the common mistakes I see
What usually matters most:
- a neat bait ball on the downhill face
- a steady tide sweep
- minimal boat pressure on a single pinnacle

What usually matters less:
- exact GPS coordinates of a brown patch
- long, noisy runs across open water without holding an edge

Common mistakes
- Overworking a single pinnacle until the bait disappears — move sooner than you think.
- Flipping presentations before you’ve dialed depth and speed — small changes find the bite zone.
- Ignoring lee pockets of debris — some of the best dorado and wahoo reactions come from debris lines tucked between rocks rather than the obvious blue lane.

The cluster is practical ground when the sea keeps the bite tight and the tide gives a tidy downhill sweep. Find the flank, hold the bait, and don’t overstay when the water breaks — that’s when the place stops being productive and becomes just a rock.
