The tongue of water between Punta de Mita and La Cruz fishes like a conveyor belt—small, repeatable, and very much tide-driven. It’s ten‑to‑twenty minutes from the marina, easy to run, and worth checking often because the window shows and closes fast. When the tide lines up the right way, bait compresses into narrow shallow lanes and roosters and jacks turn that mess of small forage into short, intense strikes.

## Why this tongue concentrates bait
Observation: on a push tide you can watch the bait ride tight along the sand fingers and the mouths of the tidal sloughs. Those little scour channels and runoff fingers funnel water and bait; where they meet shallows and scattered rock the bait slows and piles.

Explanation: the headland peels current and creates a tight lee alongshore. That creates a narrow conveyor—sand-to-rock transitions and short kelp patches act like teeth on the belt. Bait pushed by the flood gets funneled, compacted, then pinned against the shallow points. That’s the simple physics; predators use the result.

## When the lane matters
Observation first: the bite fires when there’s a steady flood pushing into the lee. Early morning and late afternoon pushes are the sharpest—low sun and calm water give you sight windows that matter.

Patterns:
- Best: the last hour of the incoming tide into slack, and the first hour after the tide eases. Bait is compacted; roosters hold predictable station.
- Secondary: a strong mid-flood when wind is light. Bait piles fast and the strike lane is cleaner.
- Avoid: dead-flat or a long, weak run. The bait scatters and predators thin out.
Also practical: when the outside bite is blown or marginal, local boats come inside and this tongue becomes the go-to first‑mile rooster and jack water.

## The structure you should be watching
The working lane sits on a shallow bench with a couple of small rocky points and short kelp patches. Depths across the lane run roughly 6–30 ft. The places that matter are where sudden sand-to-rock transitions occur and where slough mouths create tiny conveyor belts. Look for the shallow fingers and small scour channels—the bait rides those and stalls at the points.

## What the bait and predators do
Observation: common forage shows up as tight schools of small sardina/pilchard and mullet pushed from the estuaries. They get compacted along the foam line and shallow fingers.

How predators use it:
- Roosters generally hold just inside the bait band near rock and kelp, often visible cruising the foam line. They don’t sit inside the baitball; they stage 10–25 yards inside the lane waiting for prey to be pushed to them.
- Jacks patrol the deeper edge and ambush from the channel teeth. On windier days jacks become more aggressive and take metals when roosters go quiet.
- Birds help but aren’t always reliable here; inside work often shows as sudden surface commotion rather than long stretches of bird pressure.

## Signs the lane is building
Watch for these signals—each one is a useful confirmation:
- a steady push of bait into the shallow fingers
- bait blown into the foam line and holding
- surface boils or diving birds over a 50–200 yd stretch
- visible fish cutting the surface along the edge on a tide push

When you see a couple of those together, you can commit.

## How I work the lane (tactics and handling)
Start with observation, then choose presentation. Present upstream of the bait push and let your offering move across the lane—roosters expect prey to pass them.

Positioning and boat handling:
- Stay upstream of the bait band; present across the lane rather than directly into it.
- Use short bursts and controlled drift instead of hovering in prop wash. Spot‑lock or gentle motors to hold angle; avoid churning the bait.
- Long casts from a stopped drift often outfish anchoring directly on top of the bait.

Live bait:
- Small live sardina or free‑swimming mullet on a short leader run naturally through the strike lane is the most reliable conversion tool. Let the bait run with the current; don’t force it.

Surface offerings:
- When roosters are surface‑minded, long casts with topwater poppers, walking sticks and medium pencil plugs across shallow rock work. Pick a cast, work it down the lane, then reposition quickly if you don’t get attention.

Metals and jigs:
- For passing jacks or when surface lures fail, tight‑ to medium‑weight jigs and small metals fished down the deeper edge trigger reaction strikes. Work the channel teeth where jacks ambush.

Micro-observation matters:
- If you can see a rooster stage—hold 10–25 yards inshore of the bait and present across. If the bait is churned or the tide softens, the roosters will leave the fingers.

## Fleet behavior and local practice
Locals run quick checks at first light and commit when a sustained bait push shows. Calls are simple and specific—if a panga radios “two boats on the north spit, bait pushing,” expect a handful of boats to hold the lane. If you arrive late and the lane is pressured, shift to the next slough mouth or fish a quieter bank inside until the tide cycles.

Common pressure reactions:
- repeated passes push schools deeper
- roosters move off shallow fingers if the bait gets churned
- jacks can dominate on blown days and take metals when surface work dies

Respect the lane and move when it’s crowded. The bite is often short-lived; being first on a clean lane wins.

## Common mistakes and quick fixes
Mistakes are predictable; fixes are straightforward.
- Anchoring on top of the bait and waiting: don’t. Anchor only if you can present upstream.
- Overcasting into prop wash: make long, clean casts at an angle.
- Ignoring tide direction: fish a push tide. A weak or opposite tide scatters the bait.
Contingencies when conditions go ugly:
- Fish the inside hard: short casts to kelp paddies, rocky points and slough mouths—roosters and jacks will still take live baits and surface offerings.
- Move to protected river mouths and jets if available—mullet concentrate there independent of offshore conditions.
- Tighten presentation: switch to live bait, shorten leaders, slow the drift and fish the bite lane.
- Work the deeper edge with metals and jigs when sight fishing is impossible.

## What usually matters here
Listen to the water: tide angle and strength, bait concentration, and a clean presentation tell you more than exact GPS numbers or how many boats were there yesterday.

The La Cruz tongue is short, simple water. When tide, bait and presentation line up the lane gives quick, obvious bites. When they don’t, there’s enough micro-variation inside to run a practical plan without burning fuel on the outside. Check the tide first, read the bait second, and work upstream to downstream.
