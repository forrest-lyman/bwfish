## Why El Anclote matters

Observation first: El Anclote is the first real bit of structure inside the lee of Punta de Mita — a handful of shallow rock fingers and pinnacles that step down into short downhill ledges. On good days you can watch bait stack in the seams between those fingers and then watch roosters and jacks work the edges like clockwork.

Why that happens: tide-driven flow squeezes micro-eddies and a narrow band of current against the rocks. The bait can't run out of the seam, so it piles into visible pockets. That concentration creates two practical lanes where predators hunt — one tight and shallow, the other just off the downhill face.

Read the bait first. If the bait is stacked and steady, the place fishes like a streetlight. If the current is shredding the bait past the rocks, it fishes like a dead shoreline.

## Two feeding lanes: inside boils and outside spill

Start with the observation, then match presentation.

Inside lane — the shallow pads and pockets
- What you’ll see: small, confident boils tucked into the shoulders of the rocks, minnows pushed into sand patches and weed edges.
- How the fish behave: roosters often show as visible chases and short surface commotion. They hunt tight to structure and react to topwater noise and natural live-bait presentations.
- How to fish it: quiet flylined live bait through pockets, aggressive short popper bursts along the shadow line, long accurate casts into seams. Keep presentations natural; roosters take a bait threaded into a pocket more than an obnoxious, splashing offering when the bait is tightly stacked.

Outside lane — downhill faces and the spill
- What you’ll see: bait peeling off the pinnacles into a narrow down-current lane; marks hugging the face on your sonar.
- How the fish behave: jacks cruise both sides but will blitz in the spill zone; roosters will sit just off the edge and hit baits coming uphill.
- How to fish it: short uphill trolling, slow retrieves of soft plastics across the runoff, chunking into the seam, and controlled casts across the spill to trigger subsurface strikes.

Both lanes live or die with tide angle and how the current wraps the point. When the current favors the lee, both lanes can be active; when it doesn't, neither will be worth the effort.

## Tidal angles that open the window

Watch the tide component, not just speed.

You want a steady push that has an onshore component into the lee — enough to shove bait into pockets, but not so strong it strips the bait back out. Floods that lean into the point or ebbs that cleanly peel around the fingers set up the two lanes. Swell and wind shift the exact angle, so the "right" tide can move a few hundred yards either way.

Also watch for river or lagoon outflows after rain. Those small plumes often compress bait and can trigger short, violent rooster windows. The window is short; it builds and fades with minutes, not hours.

## Signs the window is building

Watch for these, in this order:

- Small, consistent boils inside the rocks — not scattered nervous water.
- Bait visible in the seams between fingers on calm days.
- Birds working tight to the shoreline or sitting on adjacent weed patches.
- Sonar marks hugging the shallow face rather than appearing as a dispersed column.

Those are hands-on signs. If you get two of them together — steady boils plus birds or tight sonar marks — the pinnacles are worth holding for a stretch.

## Small-boat and shore access

Observation: El Anclote is close and practical. Small pangas and skiffs can make short runs from Punta and work the inside without committing to outside conditions.

Practical notes:
- Launch timing: mornings when NW swell is smaller, or afternoons on southerly wind days, open the lee and make long casts or kite work possible.
- Shore anglers: on calm days you can sight roosters from headland rocks. Accurate long casts and steady reading of tide movement beat brute distance.
- Safety and handling: the rocks are shallow. In chop stray lines and anchors foul easily. Keep the engine ready for quick repositioning — the fish come and go on tight windows and you need to present across the face, not sit static on an anchor that fouls the seam.

## Tactics that convert

Short observation first: fish here respond to presentation that matches where the bait is holding — quiet when tight in pockets, noisy and chunk-driven when bait is spilling.

Effective options:
- Live-bait flylining: best when bait is visible. Thread the bait through the pocket and keep the presentation quiet and natural.
- Early morning topwater: poppers and stickbaits along shadow lines draw roosters. Use short, aggressive retrieves with pauses.
- Soft plastics and small jigs: when the surface is blown out or fish are subsurface, burn or twitch a weighted plastic across the spill; jacks take a smaller profile under current.
- Chunk-and-hold: small chunks tossed into the seam with a tight drift past the downhill edge will bring jacks and holding roosters in stained water.
- Controlled drift angles: set a drift that keeps the boat moving parallel to the fingers so your baits cross the face rather than running straight down-current.

Match your gear and drift to the lane you’re targeting. Static anchors across the seam tend to kill bites — you want movement that presents across the face.

## Fleet behavior and pressure

What I watch in the radio and on the water: local captains use El Anclote as a quick test. If the pinnacles are holding bait, the point or nearby canyons might be worth the day. If they’re empty, most skip the spot.

Pressure is real. Short windows collapse when more boats stack into the same lane. If you find one or two boats holding a clean line, don’t fight the crowd — look for the same tidal angle a mile east or west. Captains will call boils, birds, and temp; they rarely broadcast size. A tight "rooster" call usually means an actual local window.

## Common mistakes

Look and then act. These are the habits that waste time here:

- Fishing the wrong tide angle — a heavy, shredding current that strips bait is a dead spot for roosters.
- Overworking topwater in stained water — switch to chunk or subsurface presentations sooner.
- Anchoring across the seam — a static bait in a stale column rarely triggers roosters; controlled movement across the face does.

Avoid those and you spend time on signs that matter.

## What usually matters — and what doesn't

Matters:
- tide angle and small bait distribution
- a clean pocket of bait visible in the fingers
- light wind so surface presentations are readable

Matters less:
- yesterday’s numbers — the pinnacles flip quickly
- long trolling spreads — El Anclote is close, sight-oriented water

## Micro-observation

The roosters will show and then vanish. When the boils start in the lee pockets, the day often makes sense: make the right presentation and you’ll get immediate attention. If bait is scattered and the tide is blowing past, move on. The place gives clear signals; read them and act fast.

## Final note

El Anclote is honest water — small, sight-driven, and pressure-sensitive. It’s a quick check that tells you how the point is behaving. Watch tide, read the bait, and match your presentation to pockets or spill. When those three line up, the pinnacles fish like a streetlight. When they don’t, you’ll be better off fishing somewhere that still has a window.
