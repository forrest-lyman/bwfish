A string of short, steep mouths and shallow throats sits a few miles southeast of Punta de Mita. The shelf drops hard and close here, so the current and temperature contours meet the edge in tight, visible seams. Bait funnels against the lip, the flow chews at the drop, and predators either push uphill through the gate or stack just above it. The geometry is compact enough that you can check, mark, and fish a canyon‑scale window inside a single 15–30 minute run.

Why these short mouths act like drop gates

The observation: bait and water properties compress right at the mouth, and fish pile in a narrow vertical band uphill of the lip. The explanation: short, steep relief keeps the bait layer compact; downhill current stalls at the lip and then peels outward, creating a seam that both concentrates and exposes bait.

What that gives you in practice is a small, intense window—if the temp step and seam line up you can convert with uphill trolling, kites, or quick chunking without committing to a long transit. But the gate changes fast: a warm filament or a shifting weedline can open or close it in under an hour.

Why the spot works (short list)

- Steep relief compresses bait vertically and keeps fish stacked uphill of the exit.
- Downhill current against the lip forms a clear seam and a sonar band of marks.
- Close proximity to Punta de Mita makes it a practical, repeatable check during a day trip.
- Small eddies and wind‑pushed weedlines often thread the mouths, which attracts dorado and other opportunists.

Signs the gates are worth holding

Watch for these signals together — one alone is noise, two together matter:

- Sonar: a tight bait band hugging the downslope at the canyon lip.
- Temperature: a 1–3°C step within a few hundred meters of the mouth.
- Birds: working low and tight over the exit, not scattered.
- Surface life: flying fish or a visible weed patch/slick threaded across the throat.
- Current feel: an obvious uphill presentation when you motor uphill from the lip.

When the gates play and when they don’t

You learn the rhythm quickly. The gates fire when there’s moderate downhill current that pins bait without blowing it apart, when small warm filaments or slim blue patches hug the mouths, and when seas are southerly or light so you can kite. They fall apart with a NW swell that scatters bait, with green/stained water and no temp step (fish go deep or leave), or under heavy pressure—repeated fast passes push schools down and out of the surface window.

Micro‑observation: one short sequence

The sonar showed a compact school stacked uphill. Birds came in and circled tight for five minutes. Then a weed line threaded through the mouth and dorado ate first. Tuna followed after the bait scattered. Small events and timing make the difference.

How captains work the gates

Search, mark, convert. That’s the pattern. Don’t commit until you see bait and temperature together.

Search then hold
- Run the contour past the mouths at trolling speed, mark bait and depth, check the thermometer, then convert to a localized pattern. If nothing shows, move on quickly—these mouths don’t reward blind faith.

Uphill troll vs kite
- Observation: when marks are moving and the temp step is firm, an uphill troll will get reactions. High‑speed plugs and lures placed uphill of the mouth draw chasing tuna and wahoo. Keep your speed and direction aimed uphill from the bait band.
- Observation: when flying fish are present and seas are calm, a kite with flying‑fish baits parked over the seam is a natural presentation for yellowfin and marlin. Kite gives you a soft, visible bait right at the gate; uphill trolling gives you speed and noise through the same seam.

Mark‑and‑hold
- If you see a compact cloud on sonar at the lip, make short controlled motors across the mark and then tighten the spread. Fish will often hover just uphill of the bait; slow your presentation through that layer rather than blasting past it.

Chunk/live conversion
- In cooler or stained water switch to chunking or slow‑trolling ballyhoo behind planers. The exit concentrates bait vertically; introducing scent and noise into the column and shortening the spread gets hooks in the water where the fish are holding.

Weed/debris strategy
- Run small skirted lures and a live option through any weed line that threads the mouth. Dorado and opportunistic tuna take first. Don’t ignore roosterfish on the inside mouths when the lee is calm—they show up on occasion.

Drift backup
- When a mark persists and the wind allows, anchor or set a controlled drift upcurrent of the canyon and drift back through the seam. Vertical presentations from a drift often hold fish longer than a passing troll.

Signals the window is building

These are the short, actionable signs I look for while approaching:

- Bait marks compressing at the canyon lip.
- Birds lowering toward the seam instead of chasing randomly.
- Thermometer showing a firm step within the troll spread.
- Weed or flying fish concentrating along the exit.
- The fleet going quiet and holding position.

Pressure reactions and simple fixes

Repeated fast passes change fish behavior quickly. Schools will sink and stack deeper after the first two boats make aggressive runs. Foamers that accepted casts early will refuse and relocate under pressure. Sometimes the edge stops producing surface hits but still shows on sonar—don’t give up on a mark; change presentation depth and add scent or noise.

Fleet behavior here

Local skippers run short exploratory passes along the canyon string, fan out to mark, then converge on the mouth with the best signs. One boat usually fans and marks while the rest convert. When the gates are firing you’ll see a mix of uphill trolls, kite baits, and a few short drifts. When the gates are quiet the fleet tacks back to the point or checks inside structure for roosters and jacks.

Common mistakes

- Chasing brightness without confirming the temp step: surface foam can be nothing more than small‑bait noise.
- Over‑trolling through the mouth: if marks sit, slow presentations and shorten the spread.
- Skipping the drift/anchor option: sometimes the best conversion is vertical, not fast.

Quick calls for the day

- If birds, a temp step, and a bait band line up in the same mouth — slow down and mark‑and‑hold.
- If flying fish and calm seas are present — kite the nearest mouth instead of solo trolling.
- If a weedline runs through multiple mouths — work the line from one exit to the next; the first weed‑studded mouth you hit often holds the most dorado.

The Litibú gates reward disciplined reads more than brute effort. Find the bait, confirm the temp, match the presentation to the layer and the seam, and then sit down and fish the gate while it’s open.
