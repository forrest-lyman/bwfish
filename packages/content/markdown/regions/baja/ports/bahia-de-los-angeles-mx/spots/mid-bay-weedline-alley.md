The Alley runs like a seam through mid-bay — not a fixed bank but a transient lane where wind, tide and the narrower shelf collect floating weed, kelp mats and debris into linear slicks. When it’s holding, the fish are close enough to the harbor to make a short, worthwhile run; when it breaks apart, you’ll find scattered patches or nothing at all.

## Where the seam forms and why it holds dorado
Observation first: the weedlines we call the Alley form inside the island chain where warm surface water can sit up against cooler seams pushed through the islands. Wind direction and the tighter shelf geometry concentrate the mats into a corridor that runs roughly parallel to the islands. The proximity matters — warm water and floating structure inside the islands keeps dorado and other surface pelagics within a short run of the harbor instead of out on the shelf edge.

Explanation second: those warm patches attract bait, bait attracts birds, birds point to where the fish will stack. The Alley is a surface-fish window powered by surface temperature contrasts and floating structure, not a bottom feature.

## When the Alley shows up
I watch for these conditions because they reliably separate “try it” days from “don’t bother” days.

- Best window: light southerly winds to calm conditions, often with a residual NW push from earlier in the day. Warm surface water must be present.
- Worst: steady, strong NW wind. The seam fragments, weed shreds, and the mats get pushed outside the island chain.
- Seasonality: midsummer into early fall is the sweet stretch. You can also get short-lived Alley action on late-spring warm pulses.

Think in terms of surface flow and warm water available inside the islands — without both, you’ll see debris but not dorado.

## Reading the line: what to look for
The Alley gives you clear surface signals if you’re watching.

- A darker or greener slick line against surrounding water.
- Birds working along one edge of the slick.
- Floating weed, kelp, or occasional debris riding the seam.
- A small temperature bump or a color change on a surface thermometer/IR.

Those cues stack: birds first, then bait, then dorado. Miss the first cue and the window often closes or moves.

## Bait and fish behavior around the mats
Small slicks concentrate surface bait — flying fish, small sardina and tight sardine pockets. That bait draws dorado into the mats; when the seam is firm, fish will work the warm side of the weedline. You’ll also see small tunas and bonito chasing tight bait balls just off the mats.

Behavioral notes from the water: dorado hits are often short and explosive. They appear in furious patches rather than long steady encounters. Birds tend to pattern the windward edge when bait is pinned; if the birds are holding, there’s usually something worth attacking underneath.

## How the fleet treats the Alley
Because it’s close, the Alley gets quick checks and fast compression.

- Fast checks: boats leave the harbor for short sweeps across likely seams early. The runs are short, so commitment is easy.
- Compression: when a patch with birds and dorado is found, the fleet converges quickly. Pressure can thin the bite inside one to two hours.
- When wind swings NW, most boats switch to lee-side island ledges or inside reef work instead of chasing shredded mats outside.

Expect a short decision cycle: find, work, and move on if the bite fades.

## How I fish the Alley
I treat it as a shallow, surface-first game. The tactics below reflect what I change when I see the cues.

Search pattern
Run across the expected seam at different angles — a 45° pass through the mats brings birds and fish up faster than a slow, long parallel drift. Crossing reveals edges; parallel drifts can miss the active seam or run you past too quickly.

Trolling
Place daisy chains and large skirted baits on the warm side of the weedline. Keep a spread you can hack back quickly — most hits happen inside the first 100–200 yards of a patch. If fish are showing on the troll, increase speed to trigger follow-bites; if birds are tight, slow the spread.

Float-and-live
When mats are sizeable, run a live sardine on a float alongside the mat. Hold the bait up in the surface current when the weed moves slowly — dorado take high and fast.

Drift and short anchors
Small protected pockets near island lee allow drifts or brief anchors to present baits into the seam without bottom hangups. Use them when the mat is forming off a lee shore and the current is gentle.

Speed and gear notes
Control speed through the mats — too fast and birds and dorado spook; too slow and you miss quick strikes on the troll. Expect tangles: use quick-release clips, stout leaders, and rigging that allows rapid bait changes. Practical gear beats exotic lures here.

## Quick cues and early adjustments
Short checklist to act on when you see the line:

Signs it’s worth a run
- Visible weedline with birds on the lee side
- A warm surface color inside the mat
- Bait flicks on the surface or rolling fish near debris

Early adjustments when you arrive
- Slow one bait to hover in the seam
- Put a live on a float at the outer edge
- Spread larger-profile lures toward the windward side

Those adjustments are tactical and reversible — try one, watch the response, and move to the next.

## Common mistakes I see
- Blowing through a patch at full trolling speed when birds are working — slow down.
- Parking on top of a dense mat and trying to pitch into it — you’ll tangle and lose baits fast.
- Ignoring temperature readings — the fish are usually on the warm side; color alone can mislead you.

## Micro-observation that separates good days from junk
The Alley is honest on sequence: birds first, then bait, then dorado. If you miss the first cue, you’re reacting to the aftermath instead of getting ahead of the school.

## Why the Alley matters to boats out of Bahía de los Ángeles
It’s a compact, opportunistic dorado spot that changes faster than shelf banks. You can be on a working mat from the dock in under an hour, which makes the Alley a routine morning check in summer. Expect short, intense windows — read the birds, watch the surface color, and control your speed. When weed, warm water and birds line up, the Alley pays quickly.
