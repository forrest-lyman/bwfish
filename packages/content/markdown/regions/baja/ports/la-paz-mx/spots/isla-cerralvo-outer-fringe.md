## Where the flank stops being island and starts being blue water

If you run off Cerralvo you can see the transition with your own eyes. Inside 20–30 nm the island still shapes water — lee pockets, ridges, channel outflow — but beyond that the shelf drops and the summer thermocline settles into a 10–40 m layer. The outer fringe is the seam where warm surface filaments peel off the flank and collide with colder basin water. That seam concentrates bait and creates rolling corridors for dorado, yellowfin and marlin more reliably than any single rock or contour line.

In practice that means you get patches and lanes instead of scattered singles. The flank supplies the bait; the thermocline gives predators a depth to sit on. When both align you stop guessing and the action gets readable.

## How currents and the thermocline shape the filament

The island isn’t just structure — it pushes water. Channel exchange and island push shear warm surface water into narrow roads that run off the flank. Local upwelling along the peninsula keeps the basin cooler, so warm fingers only form where wind and current let surface water consolidate. In summer the thermocline is usually sharp; the warm side sits over the flank and thins as it peels into blue. Predators either stack on the warm side or hold just below the thermal break.

Visible signs follow the physics: weed and debris collect on the warm filament and along the seam where the color changes. That visual seam is most skippers’ commit signal.

## Two bait behaviors — island bait vs warm‑patch bait

There are two bait worlds here and they behave differently.

Island bait: tight, compressible schools — sardina, caballito, small saury — that show up as dense sonar marks near ledges and channel mouths. Tuna will often sit deeper under a compressed ball; those marks tend to be compact and change quickly when pressured.

Warm‑patch bait: looser, surface‑oriented life associated with sargassum, flying fish and bits of debris. Dorado live in and around that surface life; marlin and yellowfin move through when bait is abundant. The warm patch attracts surface feeders and creates a different strike profile than a tight ball off the rocks.

Read the bait first, then pick presentation.

## When a warm patch is worth running 20–50 nm

You don’t commit to the outer fringe on a rumor. The warm patch is worth the run when several signals line up:

- a clean blue tongue bordering green or brown island water
- a continuous line of floating weed or scattered debris
- birds working in a patterned way — dropping and lifting along the same line
- surface temp a degree or two higher than surrounding water on the thermometer
- porpoise or consistent surface boils along the filament

One signal alone is flimsy. Two or more usually justifies the 20–50 nm commitment; three or more means you go with a plan and stick to it.

## Trolling vs chunking — how I choose

I treat this like a decision tree: start mobile, confirm, then commit.

Trolling — when to use it
- Use when the filament is continuous, birds are active and you have open blue on one side of the seam.
- Presentation: a spread of flatlines and teasers for dorado; cedar plugs or large skirted lures and skirted ballyhoo at 8–14 kt for yellowfin and marlin. Keep a mix of shallow and deeper offerings — the thermocline changes your strike band.
- Why: trolling lets you find where fish intersect the filament. Dorado hit visible weed and moving plugs; tuna and marlin take trolled lures when bait is being herded.

Chunking / live‑baiting — when to switch
- Use when bait is tightly compressed against an edge or you see sustained boils or singles holding in one area.
- Presentation: slow circle or controlled drift with chunks to build a slick; live baits on short leaders presented directly into the school.
- Why: chunking turns a transient mark into a feeding window. When predators are committed below the bait ball, scent and chumming hold them and convert short encounters into hookups.

Start mobile. If you see consistent indicators, slow down and shift to chunking. If the fish won’t hold, revert to trolling and cover more water.

## How I work a filament — a practical sequence

1. Run along the seam and watch color, weed and birds. Mark temperature every few miles to confirm the thermal contrast.
2. Separate bait and structure marks on the sonar. A shallow bait layer with a thermocline beneath is classic dorado; a tight bait ball pressing on a slope with marks below points to tuna.
3. Make a first pass with a light spread — a couple flatlines and one deeper lure — to test depth and species.
4. If you get signs (birds fix, a strike, persistent boils), slow and investigate: put out baits, drop a teaser, or start a short chunk slick.
5. Commit methodically. If birds and debris hold, dedicate the next hour to the filament. If you pull tuna that don't hold, switch quickly to short leaders and chummed live baits.

Move in measured steps. Don’t scatter the bait with frantic passes; a methodical approach converts more consistently.

## Thermometer and sonar discipline

Thermometer first, sonar second. A couple degrees over a small distance changes the whole strike zone. Mark bait separately from "structure" — bait can sit 10–30 m shallower than where predators hang. Watch marks as you troll: tuna will slide under bait balls as you pass; dorado ride the top 5–10 m.

Keep a clean habit: log temps every few miles, note the thermocline depth, and mark bait layers independently. That discipline tells you whether to troll shallow, run deeper lures, or set up to chum.

## How the fleet moves and what pressure does

From La Paz the logic is predictable: island faces first, flank shoulders next, then the basin. Small pangas rarely commit beyond the flank; sportboats and the boats with fuel and patience will push the 20–50 nm line when reports or wind lines promise it. When a clean filament with weed appears the fleet converges fast — pressure can collapse a patch inside an hour.

Good boats:
- make a couple methodical passes before switching methods
- keep a chunking kit ready when they find tight bait
- watch the wind window; a good patch at 40 nm is useless if the nor’easter comes up

Pressure mistakes I see most: boats push too close and scatter bait; repeated passes through a small patch shut dorado off; a long chunk slick left without rotation attracts sharks and forces tuna to depth.

## What usually matters here (and what doesn’t)

What usually matters more:
- a sharp temperature contrast
- a continuous weed or debris line
- bait compressed against a flank or slope

What usually matters less:
- yesterday’s single hookup report
- an isolated foamer with no supporting bait marks
- exact GPS numbers — the filament moves and shifts with wind and current

Trust the water, not the chat. Read the seam and adjust.

## Quick gear calls

- Dorado: 30–50 lb flatlines, small skirts, ballyhoo; short leaders when birds are tight.
- Yellowfin/marlin: 30–80 lb spreads, cedar/flatline + skirted lures, run 8–14 kt.
- Chunking: 50–80 lb main, short 30–60 lb leaders, steady slick and a controlled drift.

Those are starting points. Tune leader length and skirt size to what the birds and bait are telling you.

## Small observations that matter

The weedline usually shows where warm water is riding the flank. If birds drop on the same line for 20–30 minutes you stop guessing and put out baits. A warm patch with no weed can still hold tuna — look for a deeper bait ball and the thermocline signature on your sonar.

When the flank is working you either troll until something breaks or you find the bait tight and convert it with chum. The best days are the ones where the water gives you a readable seam and you stick to one plan long enough to see which species the patch wants.
