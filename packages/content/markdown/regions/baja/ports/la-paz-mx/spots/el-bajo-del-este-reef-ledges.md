## Where El Bajo del Este sits and how it fishes

El Bajo del Este is the first real hardbottom after La Paz’s mud flats — a stitched series of ledges, boulder fields and lone pinnacles running parallel to the island faces and the eastern channel mouths. Depths sit mostly 15–40 m. Relief is often abrupt: short drops, caves, and scattered bommies interrupt otherwise flat sand.

It’s the kind of spot you run when you want a reliable bite without committing to a long blue‑water trip. Short run, predictable tidal funnels, and a mix of bottom and structure predators make it a dependable fallback for small boats and pangas. When the local fleet can’t gamble offshore, they work this reef.

## Tide funnels — how the current sets the bite

Tide through the eastern channels and the lee of Isla Espíritu Santo organizes the place. The reef faces the main ebb and flood funnels; when the tide runs it pins bait along the downhill side and creates short, powerful seams. Those seams are ambush lanes for yellowtail and amberjack; the bottom fish work the holes and overhangs.

- Strong flood: bait compresses against the face; fish hold high on the lee and feed out from the edge.
- Strong ebb: fish tuck into pockets, holes and under overhangs; drifts swing heavier and you’ll need more weight.
- Slack: the hunting slows. Best time for methodical, slow bottom work; worse for live‑bait verticals.

Micro-observation from boats that fish it: the marks climb and narrow on the first flood—bait tight to the reef and fish moving uphill toward the seam.

## What the bait looks like and where it stacks

Forage here is local and compact — sardina, caballito, small saury and juvenile mackerel. They form fingered clouds along the reef and in scour gutters between pinnacles. Birds rarely give it away; you use sonar and active checking (stickbaits or a cast net) to confirm.

Watch for:
- tight, dark bait patches hugging the reef
- small foamers over shallow bommies at first light
- isolated kelp or weed on pinnacles with a few birds working nearby

Those tighter, compact schools are the windows. When the bait is loose and gray on the screen, the reef fishes like any other hardbottom — steady, single hookups, not a window.

## Who the reef holds and how they behave

This is a two‑speed reef: dependable bottom fish all the time, and structure pelagics that light up when tide and bait line up.

- Bottom species (groupers, snappers): the constant. They sit in cracks, caves and on downhill sides. They prefer heavy drops, slow retrieves and slack or slow flood presentations.
- Yellowtail and amberjack: appear when bait is compressed and there’s a clear downhill seam. They chase moving bait along the face — live bait or fast vertical jigs get them.
- Occasional small pelagics (sierra) and jack crevalle show up near the flats and on tight tides, but they don’t replace the bottom work.

If the marks tighten and climb, you’re looking at yellowtail feeding. If the marks are low and gray, you’re looking at bottom fish that will take slow, bottom‑hugging presentations.

## Reading the window — signs a yellowtail run is building

You don’t need a miracle. The reef tells you with a few simple signals:

- Bait tightened against the face.
- Sonar marks streaking upward along the ledge.
- Short, sharp bird work or single foamers.
- Compact, moving sonar targets split from the main bait cloud.

When those line up, present baits uphill into the seam and have fast fall rates ready. When they don’t, switch to proper slow bottom tactics.

Common mistakes to avoid:
- Drifting straight downhill into the face — present from uphill.
- Running the same seam repeatedly once pressure builds.
- Dropping heavy bottom rigs on a strong flood without sufficient weight.

## How the fleet runs it and why positioning matters

Local pangas and sportboats use El Bajo del Este as a first check or a fallback. The repeatable pattern is either anchor/hold on the uphill side of a seam or make a controlled drift down the face. Experienced crews watch the tide clock closely and avoid running the spot into a strong offshore northerly unless they expect a bumpy, broken drift.

Typical fleet behavior:
- Early morning: live‑baiting and pitch‑jigging along the face.
- Midday on a steady tide: drifting with short leaders and heavy drops for grouper and snapper.
- When a yellowtail shows: boats converge fast and the bite window shortens under pressure.

Positioning is primary. If you can present bait uphill into a marked school, you’ll usually outproduce a boat that’s simply drifting through the bait.

## Tackle and presentation that work

Keep rigs simple and positionally strict. The reef rewards correct angles and clean presentations more than clever rigs.

- When to fish: start the first hour of flood or the first hour of ebb depending on where the wind has pushed bait — both can work, they just feel different.
- Live bait: sardina or caballito on 6–10 ft leaders for yellowtail; 40–80 lb mainline. Present uphill into the face so the bait drifts toward the fish.
- Vertical jigging: 80–120 g jigs, neutral‑to‑fast fall for yellowtail; short, aggressive strokes. If fish are high on the face, keep the fall quick.
- Bottom rigs: heavy 60–130 lb leader with circle hooks and enough weight to hold in current. Drop on a slow flood or slack to reduce twist; fish ledge edges and holes between bommies.
- Leader length: shorten leaders in strong tide to reduce hang‑ups and improve hookup angles.
- Sonar work: mark structure and bait separately. Run the boat to present baits uphill into marked bait schools rather than cutting straight through them.

## Slack vs running tide — when to change gears

Slack: best for slow ground‑huggers and picking apart caves with a heavy drop. Expect fish deeper and slower.

Flood or ebb: best for yellowtail and jacks — fish hunt compressed bait. Expect more hangups, quicker hookups, and shorter windows.

Adjust leader length, weight and presentation to the current. When the tide tightens bait to the face, shorten leaders and speed up fall rates. When slack, slow everything down and fish the drop.

## Final observation

El Bajo del Este fishes like a neighborhood reef: short run, repeatable, and governed by simple rules — tide, bait and presentation. Work the seam, present uphill, and respect the swing of the tide. Do that and the reef will produce when the rest of the bay is slow.
