The Tongue is the first sensible stop outside La Paz harbor. From the wheel it reads like a simple feature: the main channel narrows, peels into a fan of sand fingers and exposed reef, and the tide accelerates. That tight flow over mixed sand and rock scours bait into the cuts and stacks it along downhill faces. Predators sit on those faces and the bite tends to be tidy and repeatable when the currents line up.

How the Tongue Concentrates Bait
The process is straightforward and consistent. Fast flow through a narrow throat over alternating sand gutters and hard edges sweeps tiny bait into the cuts. Where the sand fingers end and rock begins the bait piles against the face. Predators—yellowtail, jacks, and reef species—hold on the downhill side of the faster water and intercept the drift.

You can see it on sonar if you read it: bait a few meters shallower in the gutter, cleaner, denser bands hugging the rock, and darker predator marks on the face below. Feel it at the helm—the boat pulls differently as you cross the tongue. Don't trust the chart alone; the feature is obvious when you look for flow and edges together.

Three Lanes Worth Running From La Paz
From the harbor it's a short hop. I work three predictable lines and cover most of the fishable geometry without burning time.

- The narrow throat: where the channel funnels and the flow speeds up. Good for quick checks and catching fish as they stage.
- Down‑current side of the shelf fingers: the gutters that run between sand and rock. Bait piles here; fish stage on the downhill edge.
- Lee pockets behind small rock islands: calmer water with concentrated bait and easy pitching angles.

Work those lanes in sequence—throat, gutters, then lee pockets—and you won't be chasing wide water. The Tongue shifts slightly with tide, so don't be married to a pixel on GPS.

When the Tongue Turns On
This place is tide‑sensitive. Two things get it going:

- A steady flood or ebb that creates a clear downhill seam and pins bait to the rock.
- A post‑northerly cleanup that tightens the thermocline and compresses bait just outside the mouth, which often triggers yellowtail to push shallow.

Timing matters. Early morning floods and the hour around mid‑tide changes—when flow begins to define—are often the most productive windows. Slack middle‑of‑the‑day tides can be dead unless something else (wind push or color change) drives bait compression.

Working the Bait Alley: Live Bait and Slow Drifts
Short, decisive presentations win here. The geometry is simple: deliver baits uphill so they drift into the predator’s face. Stop your trolling and set up a controlled drift when you find the stack.

Tactics in practice

Live‑bait set
Pitch sardina or caballito on short leaders (6–10 ft) into the face. Position the boat slightly uphill of the target and let the bait work toward the rock. When current is heavy shorten leaders to reduce tangles and improve hookup angle. A small chum stream—used carefully—will keep bait tight long enough to convert a scouting mark into a sustained bite, but don’t spray chum until you’ve confirmed a pattern.

Slow‑drift bottom
Run a controlled drift over the sand gutters with heavy drops. Rigs in the 60–130 lb range on bottom setups hold better and stay in the strike zone during a slow flood or steady ebb. The idea is to reduce swing and keep baits in front of fish that are hugging the outer face.

Vertical jigs
When yellowtail sit tight to the rock, fast‑fall jigs on neutral reels will trigger reaction strikes. Fish the vertical face—don’t waste time on the wide flats. Short, aggressive jigging strokes where the predators are stacked pay off more than long, wide presentations.

Sonar discipline
Mark bait schools separately from hard edges. Bait will usually sit a bit shallower in the gutter than the predator marks on the face. Don’t troll through the stack; stop, mark, set a drift, pitch or live‑bait. Treat the target as a three‑dimensional problem: depth of bait, location of the face, and current speed define the hookup window.

Boat Size, Launch Constraints, and Tide Windows
This is short‑run water but not always panga‑friendly. The bay mouth can work from small boats on calm days, but nortes and residual swell make it uncomfortable or unsafe for light craft.

- Small pangas: quick to the Tongue and nimble in shallow gutters and lee pockets. Best on calm mornings and modest tides.
- Sportboats: handle bigger tides and chop better; they hold position better for a controlled chum/live program across multiple cuts.

Tide window advice: prioritize a working flood or ebb. Mid‑tide build is often better than absolute high or low. Avoid the peak of a strong northerly when entrance water becomes confused and bait scatters.

Signs It’s Worth Stopping
Watch for these quick, simple signals from the wheel and sounder:

- Birds sitting tight on the down‑tide side of the tongue.
- Bait on sonar in narrow, dense bands hugging the rock.
- Surface boils or short directional runs in lee pockets.
- A visible color change where the channel water peels off the sand fingers.

If you see two or more, make a controlled stop and set up. If you see bait but no defined flow, the fish are likely not committed.

Fleet Behavior, Pressure, and Common Mistakes
Locals treat the Tongue as a morning choice—quick checks from the harbor and back before sea state builds. When it works, boats stack in lee pockets until a tide swing or pressure collapses the bite. When it’s quiet the fleet moves inside to kelp and paddies or runs the outside shelf.

Common mistakes I see:

- Trolling through gutter marks instead of stopping to live‑bait and present uphill.
- Fishing absolute slack; this place wants defined flow.
- Over‑chumming and turning a tidy window into a pressured, short-lived bite.

Pressure reactions are predictable: foamers often sink or scatter after boats arrive; repeated chum shortens windows; fish that were shallow move to the outer face or deeper gutters as tide softens.

What Usually Matters More (and Less)
More: tide angle and speed, bait pinned to the hard edge, and your ability to present live bait uphill into the face.

Less: exact GPS points on individual sand fingers (the Tongue shifts a bit with tide), or long trolled spreads—this water rewards controlled drifts and live bait work, not wide trolling runs.

If You Run From La Paz
Plan a short, tactical day. Watch the tide, bring live bait, set up short leaders and heavy bottom rigs, and be ready to stop and present uphill. When bait, tide, and structure line up the Tongue gives efficient, repeatable yellowtail and reef days without a long run. Keep your sonar discipline, manage chum, and don’t be surprised when the window closes quickly—these fish want current, and they stop feeding the moment it goes slack.
