These pinnacles are the first real structure you hit northwest of Isla Partida — a tight string of spikes and boulder fields that break the bay’s contour into short downhill faces. Tops sit shallow—often 10–25 m—with bases down into 50–70 m. They don’t read as one long reef so much as a set of fingers and small ledges that funnel the tide and compress bait into lanes where predators stack.

From La Paz the run is short enough to treat this as a morning panga job or a quick sportboat check:
- Distance: commonly 6–10 nm from the marina.
- Typical transit: pangas 20–40 minutes; sportboats 10–20 minutes depending on sea and engine.

Where the pinnacles work and why
The pinnacles do one simple thing well: they turn moving water into defined downhill faces. When tide, bait and edge line up the fish stack on the down-current side and stay there until something changes. That repeatability is what makes this cluster useful — you can plan a vertical approach instead of running wide bluewater patterns.

Current, seams and holding lanes
Tide is the organizing force here. Narrow channels around Partida shove water across the pinnacles and create short, sharp seams; fish prefer the downhill side of those seams.

Observations and patterns:
- Best windows are mid-ebb and mid-flood when the seam is crisp. Slack often scatters the bite.
- Bait compresses against the face; yellowtail and jacks sit in the 10–30 m band along the downhill slope, while amberjack commonly hang lower on the face or just off pinnacles.
- If the tide is strong expect fish to orient hard into the flow — shorten leaders and present clean, uphill drifts.

Boat positioning is simple: hold slightly uphill of the face and let baits drift into the seam. If you sit over the mark you push bait and fish away; if you present from uphill you control angle and hookup geometry.

Bait, signs, and what to watch on the sounder
Sardina and small caballito/anchoveta are the attractors here. The pinnacles act as a staging ground between the island flats and the deeper basin — bait moves through them on tidal exchange.

Signs that the cluster is worth working:
- tight bait balls hugging the face on sonar
- birds working low and focused on one flank
- foam lines or surface debris aligning with a down-current face
- consistent marks on 20–40 m under a thin surface band

These signals are the readout. I don’t fish guesses — I fish what I can see on the sounder or observe on the surface.

Who shows up and how they hold
- Yellowtail: the primary daytime target. They stack on the downhill face and respond to live sardina and fast-fall jigs from the 10–30 m band.
- Amberjack: appear on stronger currents and deeper structure; they’ll take heavier jigs and larger live baits and usually sit lower on the face.
- Snapper and rockfish: tight to the rock; reliable on a slow drop when the current softens.

Timing: when the window opens and when it slams shut
A clean, defined seam with concentrated bait produces fast results. The bite builds quickly and collapses just as fast when the tide flips or the seam softens. Micro-observation: marks ran uphill on the first flood and vanished into the base once the tide softened — that sort of rapid transition is common here.

How the fleet usually works it
- Pangas: work tight to the pinnacles, drift live bait over the face and pitch jigs into working marks. They move fast and trade location when the bait drifts out.
- Sportboats: set up longer drifts, anchor for controlled vertical spreads, or hold position to present multiple live baits into the seam. They’ll troll the uphill edge if they want to tempt bold fish away from pressured lanes.
- Pressure pattern: the cluster takes pressure poorly. Multiple boats on one pinnacle scatter bait and push fish deeper; the better crews move 200–400 m and re-present rather than crowding a face.

Vertical-jig and live-bait approaches
This spot is a vertical-attack piece of water. The job is to present where predators are sitting and match the drift angle.

Vertical jigs
- Zone: work from surface down to 40–60 m depending on marks; yellowtail usually inhabit 10–30 m.
- Action: neutral-to-fast jigs with a tight flutter and quick pickups work when fish are active. Slow, grinding lifts are the ticket for deeper amberjack on heavy tide.
- Boat handling: hold uphill, let the jig fall into the mark, then use short, sharp lifts and fast returns to draw strikes.

Live bait
- Presentation: drift live sardina or small caballito uphill into the face. On strong tides either shorten leaders or anchor for a cleaner presentation.
- Tuning: shorten leaders on hard tides to keep hookup angles clean — fish pin into the flow and a long leader reduces hookups.
- Chum: a small, controlled chum slick holds bait tight to the face; if predators sit and refuse live, switch to jigs and change the action.

Tactical checklist
- Start uphill so baits enter the seam and hit the downhill face.
- Watch sonar: separate bait from structure and target the bait band depth.
- Commit when the tide makes a steady seam; slack or reversed seams usually scatter fish.
- If marks flatten or move deeper, shorten leaders and try heavier jigs or larger live baits.
- If pressure builds, move 200–400 m and re-present rather than crowding the same pinnacle.

Common mistakes and pressure reactions
- Crowding one pinnacle — repeated disturbance pushes fish down or out.
- Trolling through active bait marks — turns a tidy window into a scatter.
- Grinding on when the tide softens — when the seam collapses, your better play is to relocate or pause and re-present later.

What tends to matter most here
- tide direction and strength
- where bait sits relative to the face
- boat positioning uphill of the seam

What tends to matter less
- exact GPS waypoint — the cluster fishes along a set of faces, not a single point
- long leader experiments when the tide is strong

Final note
This is a dependable local piece when the island channels are working. It rewards clean reads: bait on sonar, a defined seam, and a clear downhill face. When those three line up you can pick a method and work it precisely. When they don’t, move on and find the next working face.
