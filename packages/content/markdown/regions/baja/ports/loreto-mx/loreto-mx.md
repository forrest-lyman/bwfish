# Loreto, Mexico

## How the island chain frames the fishery
Loreto is a sheltered staging point with islands a short shot from the harbor. You’re not running an hour before you find structure — the chain of islands, submerged ridges and point-to-point ledges are the fishery. Those features create steep drop-offs, narrow channels and simple places for bait to stack against current seams.

Typical first water you’d call “meaningful” shows up within 5–20 nm: island edges, the outer flanks of the bay and the channels between islands. Beyond that the shelf often steps off into deeper basin water. The stepover ledges and channel mouths are where bait gets pinched and pelagics first concentrate. The harbor itself is straightforward for staging and fuel; the tricky bit is wind against tide through the passes — short runs that can feel punchy when that happens.

## When tides, upwelling, and clarity line up
Conditions here are all about tidal jets around islands and intermittent upwelling. The tide through the channels sets narrow seams and downhill flows on lee sides. When the tide is pushing past a point you get a thin, productive edge; on slack that edge fades and fish scatter.

Water clarity turns quickly. Light wind plus steady tidal flow gives clean water and visible bait balls. Strong onshore wind or a tropical swell brings color and higher seas and tends to push fish closer to structure. Summer builds a stronger, more stable thermocline; winter cools surface temps and compresses the vertical distribution. Fog can hide bird work during seasonal transitions. The best windows are calm mornings with a tidal push through the channels and a distinct temp break on the sounder.

## Where each fish shows up and when
The fishery splits cleanly between island-edge pelagics and inshore structure/bottom work. Timing and where you choose to work are predictable if you read bait, current and contour together.

- Pelagics: Dorado and yellowfin dominate the warm months. They stack on warm surface bands and along temp breaks where bait concentrates. Wahoo shows on fast bait pops and clean-water edges, especially when bait balls break up. Marlin and the larger tunas turn up when blue water and steady bait line up — more conditional than the dorado and yella.
- Inshore/structure: Roosterfish and jacks follow bait along exposed points, beaches and the lee sides of islands where current funnels prey. Snapper and grouper live tight to hard bottom and ledges; they’ll sit on steep drop-offs and canyon mouths and bite best on steady tidal flow. Amberjack hang the same structure when bait is pushed over the edge.
- Timing: Late spring through early fall is your prime for offshore tuna and dorado. The shoulders give more variable but often very productive windows for wahoo and roosters. Bottom bite tends to be steadier in transitional months when current concentrates bait over ledges.

There’s a practical rule here: bait, current and a contour that forces bait into a seam — have two and you’re hopeful, have all three and you commit. Presence without all three is window-shopping.

## How the fleet searches and reacts
Local crews divide by range and objective. Small boats and pangas push first to island points and ledges for roosters, jacks and quick dorado. Larger sportboats stage for longer runs to open-water tuna and dorado when conditions allow. On the radio you’ll hear bird-work coordinates, temp reports and distance to the nearest edge — shorthand for what’s hot right now.

Search behavior is repeatable. Boats run island contours until they find birds or a temp break. If birds are working and bait is on sonar, crews commit. If you only see a temp line, most slow-troll or put a live bait in the water to test it. If neither birds nor bait are present, the fleet usually re-stages closer to the islands or moves up and down the shelf rather than committing to long runs.

Weather windows control range. Runs beyond 30–40 nm need a steady sea; most local boats avoid long runs when wind opposes the tide through the passes. The first thing crews look for is bird activity tied to a temp edge — that pair usually sends boats offshore.

## Practical tactics: what to try first
Fish here read current and contour more than calendar days. Treat temp breaks, birds, bait stack and tidal push as a system — one alone rarely carries the day.

1. Read the sounder and surface temp first. Narrow warm bands hugging island points or shallow ledges are primary targets for flatlines and live bait.
2. Run island contours before committing to open water. Contour work finds the first birds and bait; trollers mark edges and live-bait boats prospect inside seams.
3. Use current to your advantage. When the tide pushes, fish often concentrate on the downhill side of a point; when a channel flips, the uphill side lights up. Anchor or slow-troll over canyon mouths and steep drop-offs when bait is stacked.
4. Set your spread with variety. Keep at least one offering shallow across the surface and a couple deeper when the water is mixed. In calm, clear water don’t hesitate with teasers or rigged baits to trigger visible activity.
5. Live bait and chunking for structure fish: present bait directly over the structure with a steady drop for snapper and amberjack. For roosters, live bait pinched near a current seam or cast to a working bird flock beats long trolling runs.
6. Jigging and vertical work: canyon walls and ledges can hold pelagics and bigfish in relatively shallow water. Vertical jigs on an edge will often produce sudden, heavy hits when fish are stacked.

Decision point: if you have a temp break plus visible bait on the sounder and the tide is pushing, commit. If you have only a temp break and no bait, work the contour slowly and be ready to switch to live bait or slow-troll the moment birds or bait fragmentation shows up.
