The channels between Loreto’s islands don’t behave like open-water patches. They act like highways—water funnels, speeds up, and a narrow band of warm surface water peels down the flank of an island. Those downhill seams are a running pattern: you sight them while transiting and, when bait, tide and temp line line up, you convert the transit into an intercept.

## Reading the downhill seam (what you see first)
You’ll usually pick it up visually or on the surface temp readout before the sounder locks anything meaningful. Look for a thin warm ribbon hugging a point or running down a flank, birds working a tight ribbon rather than a broad patch, and bait marks on the downhill face.

Observation first: the temp band is the map. Birds and bait confirm it. The tide gives it teeth. If the warm band is present but there’s no bait or no push, the band is usually noise; when the current is pushing, the same band concentrates bait and fish.

Why it matters: the channel concentrates flow into a narrow jet. That jet compresses bait and creates a short, intense road fish use to move and feed.

## Running the channel on transit
Most crews treat these channels as search roads. You run contours along island flanks at transit speed, watching surface temp, birds, and the sounder. If you see a warm finger pushed down the flank or a temp line hugging a point you slow and set the spread across the seam rather than ahead of it.

Practical sequence:
1. Ride the flank at transit speed with temp probe and eyes on birds.
2. When the band narrows and bait shows on the face, slow and set the spread across the seam.
3. Commit quickly if birds are tight and the tide’s pushing; hesitation scatters the patch.

The range from Loreto harbor is short—meaningful channel water is often inside 5–20 nm—so you can commit on a window and still beat the afternoon wind.

## Signals that convert transit into a bite
The seam only becomes fishy when several pieces lock. Watch for these together rather than in isolation.

- Temp band hugging a point and extending downhill.
- Birds working a narrow ribbon instead of a broad patch.
- Solid bait marks on the downhill face within trolling depth.
- Current pushing through the channel (not slack).
- Surface fragmentation or popper blow-ups near the seam.

Any single signal alone can be false. Temp alone is only a road map; the tide and bait are the fuel.

## Short commit checklist
Keep it short and decisive at the helm.

- Temp + visible bait + tide push = commit.
- Temp only, no bait = slow down and prospect with live bait/chum.
- Bait on sounder but slack tide = hold and mark; fish may be deep or scattered.

If one piece is missing, treat the channel as prospect water and keep moving until the system forms.

## How the fleet runs it (typical reactions)
Crews set the spread across the seam, not ahead. When bait shows and the tide pushes most boats commit immediately—tease, flatline, or live-bait depending on what the birds and bait say.

Common responses under pressure:
- Multiple boats on a narrow seam scatter bait and make surface offers less effective.
- Repeated passes push fish deeper; then live bait or chunking often outperforms surface rigs.
- Late-morning wind builds break surface patches; follow birds and deeper marks rather than the surface color.

## Spreading and presentations across the seam
Observation first: dorado hit the shallow seam. Yellowfin will take both shallow and mid-deep depending on clarity and push.

Spread shape I favor: one shallow offering tight to the warm band (feather/rigged bait or flasher), one mid at 20–40 ft, and one deep rod 60–120 ft depending on thermocline and clarity. Keep the shallow bait on the seam—that’s the dorado lane.

Presentations:
- Flatline and rigged offerings—work best when birds are striping the surface ribbon. You want something that swims and flashes close to the seam.
- Live bait / slow-troll—when bait is stacked but reluctant, set a live bait inside the seam and slow-troll down the face. It converts temp-line windows into committed strikes.
- Chunking—short-range chunk spreads over canyon mouths or steep drop-offs attached to the channel; hold station into the downhill current and let chum create localized commotion.
- Teasers—a small teaser or teaser-and-flasher ahead of the shallow rig helps hold attention without blowing a tight patch.

Match the offering to what the birds and bait are saying. If birds are ripping surface strips, keep something lively and visible shallow. If bait sits mid-water, move your mid-rod in.

## Tackle and leader choices (practical)
I keep this operational, not exhaustive.

Dorado:
- Rods: 30–40 lb class conventional or 20–30 lb spinning when fish are smaller and water is warm and clean.
- Leader: 30–50 lb fluorocarbon is plenty; 60–100 lb is overkill for most dorado in these seams.

Yellowfin:
- Rods: 50–80 lb conventional for larger tuna and open runs. For lighter yellowfin (small schools or animal-sized fish) 30–50 lb setups work if you plan to hit and hold quickly.
- Leaders: 30–80 lb fluorocarbon depending on size. Use 80–130 lb wire only when you’ve seen tuna hit lures and fight into the spread.

Lures and terminal:
- Small cones, feathers and skirted hoochies tied high in the spread for yellowfin.
- Rigged baits, cedar plugs or small lures pulled along the seam for dorado.
- Keep leader lengths short when trolling a channel seam—too much leader and the offering swims out of the bite lane.
- Wire for toothy visitors (wahoo shows up in these seams); otherwise favor fluoro for action.

## Interpreting temp lines, birds and tide
The temp line is the map; birds and bait are the field reports; tide is the engine. Clean water with a strong push favors yellowfin and fast dorado. Colored water or onshore chop tends to push fish tight to the lee and closer to structure. Slack tide often means deep or scattered fish—don’t commit hard to a temp spike with no bait.

If the temp band narrows and the sounder cleans up, that’s usually when you stop running and start fishing.

## How pressure changes the seam
Pressure alters behavior predictably. If a seam gets run repeatedly, bait scatters and surface foamers refuse repeated casts. Repeated passes make fish sink or move deeper; live bait or chunking becomes more effective than surface offerings. When multiple boats converge, the patch often fragments—switch to deeper or hold-and-chum tactics.

## Micro-observations from a run
The best runs start as transit. The temp band tightens, the sounder picks bait on the face, birds drop in tight, and the day changes in a handful of minutes. When it’s working you can intercept multiple schools in a single channel run. When it’s not working, the channel still serves as prospect water for points, canyon mouths, and lee pockets.

Decision point is simple: do you have temp + bait + tide? If yes, set the spread and commit. If not, treat the channel like scouting water and keep going until the system forms.
