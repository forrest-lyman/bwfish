These passes are simple geography doing complicated work. Narrow necks between Loreto’s islands — shallow reefs, point-to-point ridges and tight channels — force the tide into focused jets. Those jets peel bait off the point, press it against a face, then spill into a quieter lee. That spill is the arresting point: roosters and jacks line up on the downhill side and wait for the seam to break.

You can run to these cuts in ten to forty‑five minutes from the harbor. They’re short trips with short windows. Get the tide right and you can run three passes before breakfast; get it wrong and the whole cut goes quiet.

## Where the jet actually matters

Observation first: the current gets narrow and fast through the cut. On the map these are nothing special — just a channel and a shallow ridge — but on the water the flow concentrates. Two structural things matter most: the point or face where the jet peels and the lee where that jet spills and slows. Fish use the energy change.

What you’ll see before you see fish: a tight, thin bait seam hugging the edge and a darker sounder mark where the jet detaches and tumbles into lee water. That darker shadow is a working ambush zone — roosters and big jacks sit there waiting for the seam to fragment.

## Reading the push: timing that actually produces strikes

Tide is the switch. On a push through these channels you get three reliable effects: bait compressed along the point, a clean lee pocket to ambush from, and a steady downhill current that holds fish in predictable spots. Slack erases the seam; bait scatters and the bite turns into searching.

The most productive minutes are the first 90 of a push and the run immediately before the tide flips. Treat those minutes like currency — work them hard, then be ready to move if the compression softens.

Micro‑observation: the bait almost always hugs the edge on the push; birds typically follow a minute later. Don’t wait for a boil to get into position — when the bait hooks up the birds will usually show.

## Bait behavior and sounder signs

Bait here runs small: sardina, glass minnows, that sandeel‑type stuff that clings to current seams. It forms thin, dense layers on the shallow face; on the sounder you’ll see a high‑density line right at the break and often a darker pocket where the jet peels into lee water. Birds are helpful but not guaranteed — on tight passes there may be only a handful working the seam even when the sounder shows bait.

Read the sounder first, then the birds. When the sounder shows compression and a clear spill shadow, that’s where you pitch offerings. When the sounder goes flat and bait scatters, you’re in search mode.

## What’s usually caught and how they behave

This corridor fishes like a roosterfish and jack crevalle road. Roosters use the current to build speed into their strikes — they exploit the downhill flow. Jacks come both as resident pressure fighters and as transient smashers when a bait pop develops. Dorado, small yellowfin or wahoo show up sometimes along the outer edges when warm filaments push through, but treat those as extras. The passes are a point‑and‑cast game first.

Roosters respond well to live‑bait presentations into the seam. Jacks will take chunks or small chum lines and also respond to aggressive topwater when a bait pop fragments.

## How the fleet plays the cuts

Local pangas and small boats fish these channels because they’re quick to reach and quick to cycle through. Watch how boats behave and you’ll learn the local pattern:

- Morning push: crews stage the inside passes first; if the first cut is slow they run two or three nearby cuts in sequence.
- When birds show: boats commit and spread out along the seam to avoid stepping on each other’s sight‑casting.
- When there’s only a temp line without birds: crews slow‑troll live baits down the downhill face until fragmentation or reaction appears.

Experienced boats favor downhill casts into the seam rather than long downstream drifts. Less experienced crews often sit on the outer edge and miss the shallow ambush pockets.

## Presentations that work here

The presentations are straightforward and situational: get bait into the seam and present it with either speed or deception depending on what the fish expect.

- Live bait, hooked in the nose and free‑running, pitched or floated into the spill — primary presentation for roosters.
- Short, aggressive casts with poppers and stickbaits when the bait pops or fragments — fast strikes happen here.
- Light chunking or very small chum lines in cooler months to hold jacks near the lee pockets.
- Quick switches: if birds show but won’t commit to casts, drop a longer live line and slow‑troll into the patch until something reacts.

Spread note: keep one shallow offering and one slightly deeper live bait. In calm, clear water roosters can be picky; present naturally and be ready to twitch.

## Signs the window is building — and how to tell when it’s over

Watch these signals in sequence rather than isolation. A single sign can be misleading; a cluster is a window.

Signs the window is building:
- bait compressed tight against the point on the sounder
- a narrow band of cleaner water hugging the lee
- birds intermittently dropping into the seam
- a consistent downstream drift angle on the surface (not cross‑chop)

When slack arrives the seam dissolves, bait scatters, and the bite becomes a search again. Pressure shows in predictable ways: repeated boats over the same hole scatters bait and pushes fish to the far side of the lee; early chum or persistent anchoring makes birds back off and roosters flatten out; too many long trolls blow the seam apart and the window closes.

Micro‑observation: when one boat anchors a pass to chunk, neighboring boats often lose the sight‑casting game in fifteen minutes.

## Safety, decision points, and where to move when it goes wrong

Short runs don’t mean short seas. The passes amplify existing conditions.

- Wind against tide through a channel creates confused, steep chop on approach and inside the lanes. That makes accurate casts difficult and can punish small boats quickly.
- Fog and low light can hide working birds; don’t commit on a temp read unless you have a plan to work the contour.

Decision framework: if the tide is pushing and wind is light, commit. If wind opposes the tide through the pass, run the leeward side of the islands or wait for the next push.

What usually matters more:
- direction and strength of the tidal jet
- bait compression against the face
- whether clean lee pockets form

What usually matters less:
- how many boats are already there (if the seams are right, the bite redistributes)
- yesterday’s numbers

Be nimble. The passes give short, high‑probability windows: read the tide, watch the sounder for bait compression, present quickly, and move when the jet softens. If you treat the minutes like currency you can run three productive cuts in a morning and finish on a good lee.
