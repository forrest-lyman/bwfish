Why this ribbon matters
The water here isn't the broad, slow thermal gradient you get out beyond the bank. It's a thin, warm ribbon that clings to island points, lee faces and the first downhill ribs outside the bay. Those ribbons break the flow, trap bait, and line up predators. When current, contour and bait stack, that narrow band turns from a temperature reading into a feeding lane — short, violent and often decisive.

The band and how it organizes bait
You can expect the band to be narrow — roughly 50–500 yards across — and sitting tight to points, channel mouths and downhill seams. On the surface it will show as a color line, a thin strip on your surface temp trace, or a change in bird work. The band corrals bait: a tight slick of sardina or midwater fish, or a fractured cloud chased by frigate birds. Pelagics treat it like a road.

Read it first, then explain it. The band concentrates bait by holding warmer surface water against the lee of structure while tide or channel jets push through the gap. Bait stacks against that shear, and predators follow the stack more reliably than they follow raw temperature.

Where to run the boat
Start on the contours. The inside flank of a point and the lee side of small islands are where these bands form. Run the high side of the island, work back along the drop, and watch for a narrow rise and fall on your surface temp readout.

Practical sequence
1. Run points and ledges at a slow cruise and watch the surface temp gauge.
2. Mark narrow warm spikes and slow back over them.
3. Scan for birds working the edge, then pull the sounder tight over the band.
4. If bait shows as fractured marks or streaks, commit.

Those four steps are the sequence. You can skip ahead if birds or sonar are already loud, but this is the reliable order when you start cold.

Signs the band is worth committing
Watch signals together — one alone is trial fishing, three together is a strike lane.

- birds sitting and riding the line instead of milling
- bait on sonar fragmented and streaking upward toward the surface
- foamers or focused darting birds
- a surface temp jump of about 1–3°C over surrounding water on your probe
- a downhill current or tidal push through the channel

Micro detail: birds give you the early warning almost every time. They show before the rest widens. Treat them as your first confirmation, then verify with temp and sonar.

Surface/flatline tactics for dorado and yellowfin
When the band is thin, shallow and clean you'll get surface action — often early and on calm mornings.

Setup:
- a shallow offering running 15–40 ft back that fishes the surface shear (popper or skirted lure)
- a second offering 10–30 ft down for fish sitting a little deeper
- longer leaders and natural‑motion teasers when water is glass‑clean

How to run it:
- flatline across the band at 6–8 kt for dorado; bump speed up for nervous yellowfin
- small poppers or wakebaits to trigger the initial strikes — dorado usually crashes first and drags yellowfin into the mess
- cast if foamers are tight and visible; a well‑placed popper or stickbait to the edge converts pressure‑sensitive fish
- keep live bait in the spread on a short wire or float to convert surface hits into sustained action

Fast fish — wahoo windows
Wahoo show on two common patterns here: broken, fast bait pops; or when the band has clean blue water on one side and the presentation can be fast and clean.

- Present fast, deep‑running skirted plugs or high‑speed cedar plugs 80–150 ft back.
- Run a clean daisy chain — wahoo want a clear approach, not a messy spread.
- If you see rapid, high‑arched birds and extended splashing beyond the ribbon, clear shallow offerings and bring a fast lure in.

When the action goes deep
The band compresses vertically as the day moves or the tide changes. Switch when marks compact below about 30–40 ft, when the surface warms but marks are dense and holding, or when birds disappear while the temp line and bait marks persist.

Switch to:
- deeper plugs and rigs in the spread (70–120 ft back)
- slower boat speed and live bait set at mid‑column depths
- vertical jigs on the outer ledge or canyon mouth if marks hug the face

Tactical breakouts and common pressure reactions
Signs the window is building: a narrow, bright temp jump on the surface trace; bait marks streaking up the column; birds lowering to the water and holding a seam; steady tidal push through the channel. Those signs usually precede the violent bite.

Pressure reactions are predictable. Fish sink and spread when multiple boats arrive and everyone casts. Foamers lock out after heavy casting; if that happens, follow the band rather than grinding in place. A wide, sloppy spread kills the window; narrow, targeted presentations extend it.

How the fleet behaves
Locals run the island contours looking for these ribbons. Small pangas commit fast with live bait and casts; larger boats stage and run a spread until birds or sonar force a decision. When a clean band shows, boats line up along the same axis — the first few to lock it down usually set the day. If the tide softens, the band often collapses and the fleet slides to the next point instead of staying put.

Decision point: when to commit
Commit when you have all three: a surface temp band + visible bait marks (fragmented or streaking) + a tide push. One or two signals are exploratory. With all three, you’re in a strike lane and should narrow your presentation and commit resources.

Small, practical additions
- The band rarely holds on slack tide. If the tide is soft, work the contour slowly and favor live bait over insisting on a flatline.
- The window opens and closes fast. It can go from quiet to explosive in minutes and disappear just as quickly.

Final note
Read your sounder, watch the birds, and let tide and contour tell you when to run in or back off. The island edge gives you short windows; stack your signals, keep presentations tight, and the ribbon will give you decisive bites.
