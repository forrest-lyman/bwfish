This place matters because it’s close, quick, and predictable in a way that lets you test water without burning a whole day. The harbor mouth and the lee of the main jetty corrals sardina and anchovy into short seams along paddies, isolated bulls, and shallow ledges. Boats run ten to twenty minutes from the slip, check a few spots, and either finish fish or move on. Treat it like a quick exam—the best days show a clear bait seam and a small window where yellowtail stack or patrol.

## Why the jetty creates two useful lanes

Observation: the jetty gives you an outside face where current peels off and a protected lee where eddies hold bait. Both lanes hold yellowtail, but they behave differently.

Explanation: the outside face is where water accelerates and strips bait off kelp; fish will stage on the downhill side and take reaction baits. The lee seam behind the breakwater traps slow water and concentrates bait—fish sit and pick. On a flood that meets the southward coastal flow you’ll see bait tighten on the inside edge; on an ebb the bait spreads wider and forces more searching. NW swell and onshore wind push dirty water into the mouth; when a narrow slip of clearer water shows along the paddies you get the most reactive tail behavior.

## How bait and tide set the window

Observation first: when sardina or anchovy are pinned under paddies, birds work a tight line 50–300 yards off the jetty, and sonar marks hug the kelp edge, the window opens. If the paddies are clean or bait is a loose column, the window is often closed.

Explanation: yellowtail here are structure‑attached opportunists. They use kelp rows and shallow downhill ledges as travel lanes when bait gets pinned. Typical forage is sardina and anchovy in the paddies, with occasional squid and small rockfish near the ledges. Commercial baitboats can compress or scatter bait quickly; their lines change the patch in minutes.

Signs the window is building are simple and visual:
- sardina under paddies or a steady bird line 50–300 yards off the jetty,
- sonar marks tightly hugging the kelp edge rather than a loose vertical column,
- a narrow slip of cleaner water along the paddies,
- fish taking a single well‑presented live bait instead of only long runs.

Those signs come before the bite. When you see them, prepare to tighten everything and go small on the presentation.

## Reading a live patch — what to look for on approach

Start with the approach. If birds are on the jetty face and working tight, you’re likely seeing bait pinned to structure. Birds working 400–800 yards offshore usually mean a different scale of water and fish — a move offshore may be required.

On sonar, a healthy patch shows marks hugging the kelp edge and short, dense arches rather than a wide column. A clean slip of water against the paddies and visible sardina under kelp are the clearest early signs. If you can trigger a reaction on the first live bait drop, the patch is committed—don’t tiptoe.

Approach through the lee when possible. Windward, noisy runs or a high bow profile often scatter fish before you see them.

## Working the patch: baits, runs, and boat handling

Observation: when a paddy lights, the right presentation is often a lively, natural bait in the inside seam. When tails are moving between paddies, short, aggressive runs work better.

Live‑sardina and free‑lining: present to the inside edge on the uphill side of a kelp row. Keep the bait lively—short tugs and small pauses. These fish prefer motion; a limp chunk won’t draw the same response. Use a moderate leader—60–80 lb for boats that work the kelp—and a short dropper or stinger on larger baits.

Short runs and artificials: when fish are patrolling, short 20–30 second runs with metal jigs or small surface irons trigger reaction strikes. Alternate a live bait drop with a quick cast of a small stickbait to break a pattern and force a reaction.

Chunking and chum holds: if fish are stacked tight and committed you can motor‑hold or anchor downwind of the paddies and feed a controlled chum ribbon into the interior seam. Motor perpendicular to the edge when chunking to keep the ribbon clean and prevent the boat from dragging the chum into the kelp.

Boat handling details matter more here than far offshore. Use the motor for subtle drift control along paddies; approach angles and throttle noise are decision points for the fish. When the tide strengthens through the mouth keep the bow into the current—yellowtail will tend to sit on the inside edge, not the outside, and a bow‑into‑current position helps keep presentations in the target zone.

## Signs a patch will shut and common mistakes that close a paddy

Observation: the same things that light a patch can shut it down—noise, the wrong approach, or staying too long.

Common mistakes:
- overstaying on one paddy after fish scatter. Patches rebuild elsewhere quickly; move once the first passes don’t return fish.
- Running noisy or from the windward side. Fish spook easily in this corridor.
- Treating every bird line the same. Birds tight on the jetty face usually mean pinned bait; birds farther out signal a different setup.

If you’ve dropped three live baits and only get spooked fish, tighten leaders, shorten droppers, or change presentation. Don’t sit idle hoping the patch comes back—test another paddy.

## Fleet behavior and baitboat effects

Observation: local boats probe early, hang on a productive patch, and move fast when the picture changes. Commercial baitboats compress or scatter bait and will rearrange the patch in minutes.

Explanation: in busy mornings expect short windows. If you hold a patch, make it count—shorten leaders, keep the boat quiet, and tighten baits. If commercial baitboats are running lines nearby, expect more scattered marks; look for the remaining loose schools on sonar and be ready to shift. The fleet’s patterns are part of the environment here; watch how others approach to learn whether the patch is worth holding.

## When to stay and when to push beyond the harbor fringe

Observation: the harbor fringe is a good scouting ground. Full paddies with responsive yellowtail mean there’s rarely a reason to run far. Clean paddies with birds or a temperature break suggest the bank or nearby ledges are worth the run.

Decision rule: if paddies are full and tails are eating, tighten everything and work the patch. If paddies are clean and birds or a temp break show, run the nearby ledges and first bank lanes—use the fringe to decide whether to commit offshore.

## Launch, jetty, and visibility considerations

Practical notes: the launch is short and sheltered—fuel up, grab bait, and get on the water fast. Time your launch around the tides; the jetty mouth concentrates current on big tides and with NW swell, so plan recovery for lower current or calmer seas when possible.

Fog typically burns off later in the morning. If you start socked in, expect harder navigation and slower bird spotting; rely on electronics and a solid compass heading. Keep situational awareness for local commercial and sport traffic—boats appear fast around the jetty and paddies.

Jetty caution: the strongest current concentrates through the entrance on big tides. Treat the mouth with respect on launch and recovery.

## Micro‑observations and a simple rule

A paddy will light with one clean bait run and a line of birds; if it rebuilds after a pass, hold it and tighten up. If it doesn’t, move before the sun and wind push the picture into the face.

If you know one thing about this spot: treat it like a test—short run, quick read, and either finish the patch or use it to scout the bank.
