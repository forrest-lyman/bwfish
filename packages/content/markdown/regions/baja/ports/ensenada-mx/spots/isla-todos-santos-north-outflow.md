What makes the north side of Todos Santos worth the run is simple and repeatable: a fixed piece of relief and a changing line of moving water. The island builds a tidy lee on its north flank and a handful of short canyon mouths sit just outside the kelp. When southward coastal flow, a tidal pulse or an eddy meets that relief, bait compresses into a clear band along the kelp and the downhill face. The island doesn’t invent life — it organizes what’s already moving through the water.

## The north ledge and where the canyon mouths sit

The ledge itself is a mix of kelp rows, isolated rock and a downhill contour that steps toward those canyon exits. The canyon mouths are short downhill runs you can pick out on sonar: cleaner water pockets and tighter marks where current fingers accelerate. Fishermen break the spot into two connected pieces: the inside kelp/ledge for yellowtail and the immediate outflow lanes for chunk-and-live work on tuna and other pelagics.

## How currents and outflows make a bite window

The currents that matter are the seams the island creates by sheltering one side of the flow. When NW wind or swell pushes parallel to the coast the north flank builds a solid lee; a strong southerly push will soften or erase that seam. The canyon mouths act like fingers that peel bait off the kelp and shove it downhill. When a tide, warm filament or eddy rides a canyon mouth the outflow tightens into a working edge 100–300 yards wide.

Temperature edges are a big part of the switch: a warm patch stacked onto an outflow converts kelp-bound fish into open-water feeders quickly. Visibility tightens or loosens with wind and swell — calm mornings give a clean band along the north face; once the sea chops, bait scatters and the seam becomes soft.

## Bait, birds and how fish position

Typical forage starts with sardina and anchovy inside the kelp, then small bait pushed off the ledge into the canyon throat. When the outflow organizes those bait balls compress and hold in a downhill column. Birds usually show early; working birds on the lee line or anchored over a canyon mouth often lead sonar by minutes.

Behaviorally, yellowtail pin to the inside edge — tight to kelp and rock. Bluefin and other tunas sit just outside, usually on the downhill side of the bait column or on the first deep marks the outflow produces. That split — kelp fish tight, tuna just outside the column — is what changes the work from free-line/kelp to chunk-and-live.

## Signs the window is building and when it’s worth committing

You get a bite window when several things line up: birds tightening over a canyon mouth, sonar showing a vertical, compact bait column just outside the kelp, and a stepped change in water color or temperature at the outflow. Porpoise or floating debris working the lane are good secondary signals. If those pieces aren’t present you’ll be chasing ghosts.

Signs that bluefin work is really building:
- birds converging on a single seam
- compact, vertical sonar marks outside the kelp
- a clear temp or color step on the outflow
- surface frenetic activity near the throat (porpoise, foamers)

## How the fleet plays it and pressure behavior

Morning probes usually start inside on kelp and paddies. If yellowtail are present and the tide is holding crews grind the kelp until the school slips or the current softens. Boats commit offshore when birds, a stable bait column and a temp line line up at the canyon mouths — that’s when you slow-troll into the leading edge and switch to live-bait/chunk setups.

Experienced crews try to own a seam: one boat will establish a controlled drift or chum line near the canyon exit while others circle the perimeter. Avoid anchoring right on the edge; motor control matters more than being pinned to a GPS number. The north ledge is pressure-sensitive — too many boats and the yellowtail shut down on sight, and deeper tuna tend to sink or spread and refuse the chum.

Common pressure reactions:
- yellowtail fleeing tight kelp after repeated free-line passes
- tuna slipping deeper or widening into scattered marks
- foamers shutting down once wind or repeated casts add noise

## Approaches, positioning and practical setups from Ensenada

This is a committed run from Ensenada — plan fuel, bait and daylight. The north side works best early or late when the lee and tide combine. Aim to line up the island’s north flank so you arrive up-current of the seam and can make controlled, perpendicular passes across it.

Positioning options break into two clear tasks:
- Kelp/ledge work: angle the boat to control drift along the inside edge. Free-line a sardina on light braid with a stout leader (60–80 lb) and be ready for short, explosive runs back to the edge.
- Canyon/outflow work: run into the leading edge, slow-troll in with teasers, then transition to live-bait chumming. Set a short chum line down-current of the canyon throat and motor-perpendicular to keep a clean column.

If the wind is NW and building the north lee tightens — stage inside and work downhill presentations. If the wind shifts southerly the seam blurs; back off or move to a different flank.

## Tackle choices and presentations that work

For yellowtail on the kelp: free-line live sardina on light braid with heavy leaders, short-run artificials to trigger reaction bites, and constant motor control to keep the bait in the strike lane.

For bluefin on the outflow: slow-troll into the leading edge with teasers, then commit to live-bait chumming when birds and marks commit. Chunking while motor-controlling the boat perpendicular to the canyon mouth holds the column best. If marks sit deep, vertical jigs or drop baits under a balloon are useful — expect the tuna to be more pick-y and pressure-shy than kelp fish.

## Micro-observations and what actually moves the needle

The fish leave the kelp the moment the canyon starts pumping the bait downhill. If birds tighten on a single seam, go vertical with chum before you start swapping lures. What usually matters most is a clean, repeatable seam, a compact bait column, and the correct approach angle with steady motor control. What matters less are exact GPS numbers or one more lure in the spread.

Common mistakes that kill a window:
- arriving wide and running past the seam without staging up-current
- turning a chunk line into a scatter by over-chumming or noisy, tight-radius maneuvers
- fishing the kelp hard after the bait has already peeled off into the canyon

## When to leave

If the bait column dissipates and the birds quit the mouth, the area will often rebuild elsewhere along the island. Don’t sit through a slow, fading seam — reposition or move on before the day’s texture changes.

The north side of Todos Santos rewards tidy observations and clean execution: a seam that repeats, birds that commit, and a steady hand on the motor. When those pieces line up the work moves from hugging kelp to running a controlled chunk/live program on deeper marks. When they don’t, the island stays only a pretty piece of relief.
