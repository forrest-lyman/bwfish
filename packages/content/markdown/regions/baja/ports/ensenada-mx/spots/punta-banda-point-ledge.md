## How the point sits and why yellowtail stage here

Punta Banda is the south corner of Ensenada Bay — a compact rocky point that drops quickly into a short, steep ledge before the shelf opens. It reads like a mini‑shelf notch: cleaner inside lee water, a defined downhill face, and seams that peel off the point on a tidal push. It isn’t a big bank; it’s a staging area.

What I watch for is bait pushed tight against kelp collars and rock pockets. The bait compresses on the uphill side of that kelp/ledge, funnels down the face, and yellowtail use the downhill seams as intercept lanes — short, predictable runs from kelp to seam and back. When the three pieces line up (bait, current, and a temperature edge) the spot goes methodical instead of lucky.

## How currents and tides build the lee seam

The dominant pattern is a southward coastal flow that gets interrupted by tides and local eddies around the point. Productive seams form when a tidal push meets the offshore current just right and peels cleaner water along the downhill face.

A few practical currents to read:
- NW wind tends to blow the clean water off the tip and creates a tidy lee on the inside. That lee pins bait against kelp/rock and makes controlled drifts possible.
- Soft south‑setting current and small eddies hold bait tight to the inside edge.
- A hard southwest or east tide will wash the seams out fast.

Read the angle. The useful drift angle is the one that produces a steady uphill‑to‑downhill movement across your free‑line presentation. If the drift goes sideways and bounces, the bait won’t funnel the way you want.

## Bait, signs, and what to watch for

Forage here is usually sardina and anchovy pushed tight to kelp collars and rock pockets. Birds will often give you the first hint, but good sonar will reveal the bait road before birds commit.

Signs the window is building:
- Small bait marks rising from the ledge toward the kelp.
- Birds sitting and working the inside edge instead of scattering.
- Sonar marks forming a band that slopes down the face.
- The fleet slows and runs perpendicular checks rather than blasting past.

Yellowtail behavior on the point is specific: tight, short runs from kelp to seam, intermittent rolling, and quick direction changes when wind or boats disturb the lane. If you see that, you can set up around pattern rather than hope for a random hookup.

## How we fish it — tactics and boat control

This place fishes best early and late when the shelf is borderline. Tactics are simple and deliberate; boat control matters more than flashy gear.

Drift‑and‑live
- Free‑line live sardina along the inside edge. Present on the uphill side and let the current carry the bait toward the drop. Keep the bait between the kelp and the downhill seam.

Short, controlled runs
- If live bait isn’t holding, do short artificials tacks to provoke reaction strikes. Keep the runs tight; long, wide passes spook compressed bait.

Chunk and motor‑control
- When fish sit tight to the face, motor perpendicular to the edge and chunk a steady line. Motor‑perpendicular chunking holds chum in a neat line and prevents bait from piling under the boat.

Positioning
- Sit higher on the inside edge if the tide is pushing offshore; sit lower (closer to the face) if the tide pushes into the point and tightens the bait. The idea is to control where the bait exits the kelp.

Boat work is constant — neutral drift with quick throttle corrections when you need to reduce swing across kelp. The angle you approach from matters more than swapping lures.

## Tactical checklist — live‑bait work

This is a short, practical checklist for running live sardina on the point:
- Live sardina on a single hook, thin wire leader for visibility, short trace to limit tangles.
- Free‑line length long enough to ride the surface into the seam but short enough to control noise and steer.
- Boat control: neutral drift, but be ready for quick throttle corrections.
- Crew spacing: one on the rod, one on sonar/birds, one up front spotting bait and adjusting drift.

Those are the mechanics. The subtle part is keeping the bait shape and compression consistent as the drift evolves.

## When to fish the point and when to stage for a bank run

Fish the point when:
- bait is thick and the seam is holding direction,
- birds sit and work the inside edge,
- sonar shows repeated bait marks on the ledge,
- wind and swell keep the line accessible and pressure low.

Stage for a bank run when:
- a warm patch or color break shows offshore,
- birds and porpoise appear beyond the shelf,
- sonar shows widespread bait balls away from the point,
- the seam becomes a transit line instead of a holding lane.

Micro‑observation: a tidy, persistent lee seam at Punta Banda usually means a local bite. A wide, messy bird line farther out usually means your next stop should be the bank.

## Fleet behavior and pressure signals

Locals treat Punta Banda as part of a wider search pattern. Expect a few boats to check it early and either fish it hard or use it as a staging point.

Low pressure: small groups hold the point and fish deliberately — best for tight drift work.
High pressure: fish scatter, bait gets nervous, and the productive window shrinks. Experienced crews will move off sooner to chase isolated kelp or banks.

Common pressure reactions you’ll see:
- Schools sink below the surface sonar range when too many boats push the area.
- Foamers give a single pass and then disappear instead of committing.
- Bait scatters along the kelp instead of compressing.

If you notice those signs, don’t burn time trying to force the drift — reposition or head offshore.

## Practical handling notes

A few observed details that change results:
- Approach kelp quietly. Angle of approach matters more than another lure swap.
- Motor‑perpendicular chunking keeps the chum in a neat line and prevents bait from piling under the boat.
- When sonar shows marks on the downhill face, slow down and tighten presentations — consistency beats sudden changes.
- If the tide softens and the seam breaks, move along the kelp line instead of trying to fight a worn‑out drift.

## What actually matters here

Usually matters more:
- Drift angle
- Bait shape and compression
- Whether the lee seam holds direction

Usually matters less:
- Yesterday’s report of numbers
- Exact GPS coordinate when the seam is gone

Punta Banda is where yellowtail wait when local water lines up. Treat it like a staging area: patient with live bait, deliberate with boat control, and ready to move the second the birds and temp edge point offshore. Keep the drift tidy. If two of the three — bait, current, temperature — are working, you can finish; get all three and the day becomes methodical, not lucky.
