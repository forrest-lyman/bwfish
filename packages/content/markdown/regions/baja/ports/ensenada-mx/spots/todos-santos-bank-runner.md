## What the Runner Is and why you use it

The inside flank of Todos Santos isn’t a single ridge so much as a run — a shelf corridor that sits between kelp/rock nearshore and the bank proper. It collects warm fingers, weed, debris and transient bait, and does it in a way that makes the decision to troll or stop simple and practical. The contour is steady enough to focus life, shallow enough to switch to live bait or chum without turning the boat around.

Use it as a transit lane when you want to pick up a warm patch on the way out or as an early staging area before the bank gets crowded. The run is where warm water first meets cleaner shelf water — where birds and porpoise show up early and an on‑the‑spot decision (push and spread or slow and chum) still matters.

## When the edge lines up — the three signals that make the run meat

Productive days on the Runner feel brittle: you want two of three signals and when you get all three it fishes like a finishing lane — bait, birds, temperature change.

Signs the window is building:
- birds actively working a moving bait line, not just loafing
- sonar showing concentrated bait balls or a rising column
- a clear leading edge on the thermometer — surface temp a few degrees warmer than adjacent water
- a narrow corridor of weed or debris with porpoise‑type commotion
- foamers holding direction instead of milling

A micro note: birds sitting over a clean temp edge is a commitment signal. If the birds disappear and the temp edge remains, you’ve likely hit pressure or a passing front — expect the bite to fade.

## Starting with a spread — treating the run like a highway

Default is to troll into the leading edge and treat it like a highway. The first pass tells you what to do next: hold and chum or keep moving.

I run a mixed spread on the Runner: teasers and skirts up top with irons and plugs 150–300 yards back depending on speed and water color. If porpoise or tight bird lines show, tighten the spread and bring baits inward — big yellowfin prefer tight, fast presentations. Speed matters: dorado and wahoo want it faster; yellowfin will take a steady trolling pace but respond to a faster bait near debris. Always keep a couple heavier irons or a large skirted lure ready for fish that push shallow and hot.

What changes the decision: if you have clean warm water with tight, moving birds — stay moving and trolled. If you find dirty water with birds holding, slow down and convert.

## When to slow, chum and fish live bait (bluefin considerations)

Bluefin on the Runner vary by school mood and depth. The rule of thumb is simple: slow when the marks are solid and holding, push when they’re moving.

Slow when sonar shows solid marks, birds and bait are concentrated and not moving fast, or a temp edge is nailed to the contour. For a live‑bait/chum setup, motor control is everything — set the engine so the chum slick hangs straight and natural along the edge. Run short natural drifts and free‑line live sardina or mackerel on stout leaders. Keep a steady chop of small cut bait to hold them; you’re buying time and feeding the decision.

Vertical jigging is a conversion tool when marks are deep and consistent. Jigs often turn tentative fish into committed hookups. Don’t over‑commit early: if marks and birds are mobile, chumming just holds nothing and you’ll lose the school by the time you try to pick them up again.

Micro note: bluefin that take a slow free‑lined bait will usually take the second; those that ignore it are often looking for speed or a jig.

## Weedlines and debris — how the Runner fishes like a southbound highway

When the Runner collects floating life it fishes fast. The leading side of debris often produces dorado and fast yellowfin bites.

Work the leading side of rafts with fast baits and long leaders for dorado; for yellowfin use large single baits or big skirted lures. Keep a long leader and a bright skirt on dorado runs, but be ready to tighten up and present slow, dense offerings if fish push under rafts. Porpoise and small foamers near weedlines usually signal yellowfin. If debris is sparse, spread the troll wider to cover lanes — if it’s thick, compress the spread and make your baits look like the food in the raft.

## Common mistakes and quick observations

You see the same errors from boats that don’t read the run:

- Chumming into a moving porpoise line — you’ll hold nothing and lose the lead on a traveling school.
- Treating every debris patch the same — some patches hold dorado, some are sparse and want speed for nervous yellowfin.
- Ignoring the temp probe — a 2–3°F change along the run is a practical boundary; birds without temp change are less reliable.

Micro observations to trust: foamers holding direction mean a controlled feeding lane. Birds over a clean temp edge mean commit. If birds vanish but temperature stays, expect pressure or a front.

## Pressure, fleet behavior and how experienced crews hold it

The Runner lives and dies by how visible the signs are and how fast they draw boats. Productive patches attract converging pressure from Ensenada and beyond. Experienced crews secure the edge early — either by holding position with live bait and a tight chum slick or by making the first pass and setting a spread that commands the lane.

What experienced boats do differently:
- Verify first: run temp and sonar before committing. The first pass usually tells you whether to set a spread or hold and chum.
- Hold the line: if you plan to chum, set a clean motor setup so the chum line reads naturally. Directionless motoring scatters bait and skittish tuna.
- Pivot quickly: have baits and teasers staged. A fast spread can convert an oncoming school the instant you see a porpoise pack slide across the run.

Common fleet reactions to pressure:
- schools sink and spread below sonar range
- foamers bolt and scatter after repeated close approaches
- dorado abandon thick patches under heavy boat traffic
- yellowfin speed up and jump between stops when pressured

## Quick checks from Ensenada — launch and early morning habits

Make your launch and early probes count. Go when wind is light and the shelf shows stable color change. NW swell and strong onshore wind shorten the useful window. Early morning, probe kelp and points first — if yellowtail are tight, don’t sacrifice a sure bite for a distant warm patch. When bird reports and temperature readings match, commit early; the inside flank rewards timely decisions more than brute distance.

## Tactical checklist (what I keep ready on the run)

- Spread gear for mixed targets: fast skirts and irons for yellowfin/dorado, heavier jigs and live‑bait rigs on deck for bluefin.
- Run teasers/skirted lures up top, irons and plugs 150–300 yards back depending on speed.
- Keep the temp probe and sonar paired; marks with no temp change are often transients.
- Preserve a clean chum line if you intend to hold a bluefin patch — steady baiting and position usually convert tentative schools.
- Be ready to tighten or widen the spread as birds and porpoise dictate.

## How I decide on the run

Make the early verification pass a habit. It’s usually one correct decision on the first pass that makes the day: commit to a fast spread when water and debris demand speed; slow and chum when marks solidify. Read the edge, manage the chum, and be ready to pivot — the Runner rewards timing and respect for the signs more than brute force.
