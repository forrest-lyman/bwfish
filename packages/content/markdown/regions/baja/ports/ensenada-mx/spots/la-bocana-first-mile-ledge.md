## Why the first mile matters
I’ve run this stretch enough to know it pulls trips back inside when the outside isn’t worth the fuel. That first mile outside La Bocana compresses bait into a narrow band along kelp paddies, sand/rock ledges and tidal gutters. The geometry and the channel’s push make a predictable line — yellowtail treat it like a short highway. You’ll get quick hookups with live bait, tight chunking results, and useful action on days the outer shelf is blown off.

It’s compact and pressure‑sensitive. When bait sits and the current has a clean push, the first mile will outproduce longer runs. When it doesn’t, it shuts down fast.

## Tidal exchange and the kelp‑ledge geometry
Observation first: the harbor alternately pushes cleaner ocean water in on flood and peels water back on ebb. That movement sculpts the kelp necks and ledge edges into the places bait and predators line up.

On flood the kelp takes a tidy inside edge; on ebb the exchange strips bait off the shallow heads and sets narrow downhill seams along the outside ledges. The useful bite usually comes on the transition — near top of flood and the first hour of ebb — when the current is definite but not ripping. Push too hard and the seams collapse; too soft and the tails roam.

Two local modifiers to watch:
- NW onshore swell spreads turbid water across the entrance and destroys the clean downhill seams; fish tighten to kelp or go deep.
- An eddy commonly forms on the east side of the mouth and holds cleaner pockets behind the first kelp lines — those pockets are likely the best places to stop if the main line looks messy.

## Reading bait, birds and sonar
Start with bait. If sardina or small schools are sitting in the kelp paddies, you’re on the money. Birds making short, controlled drops along the outside edge in early light is a good sign too. On sonar a thin, dense bait band 10–30 ft deep hugging the kelp/ledge edge is the pattern that usually yields.

Signs that the neck is working:
- Live sardina holding in the kelp paddies.
- Short bird drops along the outside edge at first light.
- Sonar showing a narrow, dense bait band just off structure.
- A tight cluster of boats anchored on the inside edge — the fleet’s basic tell.

Those observations come before tactics. If bait is there and the current has a clean angle, you set up. If bait’s scattered or the current is soft, don’t pour time into the wrong side of the neck.

## How yellowtail use the neck
Pattern: yellowtail hunt the compressed bait line and shift quickly with the tide’s character. On a steady push they sit tight to the uphill side of the kelp neck — semi‑suspended and ready for a short run. On soft tides they chase and roam, making the bite patchy and reaction‑based.

A couple of behaviors to keep in mind:
- Schools can go from visible to gone with a single tide change; bait often stays longer than the tail. If the bait lingers and the tail disappears, expect them deeper or moved off by pressure/noise.
- When the line is defined, tails will do short, aggressive intercepts rather than long chases. That favors short presentations and live bait work.

For decisions on where to place the boat, prioritize the uphill side when the push is steady; broaden your spread when the tide softens.

## What to throw and when
Short, aggressive presentations are the consistent winners here. Nothing elaborate.

- Live sardina free‑lined to the inside edge on light drag when fish are sitting tight. Let them run a short, natural line into the seam.
- Short surface runs with small stickbaits or plugs when fish are visible and reacting — quick bursts, keep the presentations in their strike zone.
- Slow trolling just downhill of the kelp on a steady push when fish hold midwater and won’t chase long.
- Controlled chunking/chum and hung baits on the uphill side when fish are tight and not willing to chase — make a steady, quiet presentation.

Gear: 30–50 lb outfits give comfortable control; stout leaders for contact with kelp and ledge. Boat work matters more than lure choice — control the drift, hold angle, and manage noise.

## Boat work, common mistakes and quick tactics
Observation: most failures here are boat failures, not lure failures.

Common mistakes you’ll see:
- Parking on the wrong side of the neck and letting the motor shove the drift across the school. That ruins the inside edge.
- Chasing every bird. Random surface activity is often bait pushed by a bigger school elsewhere; the reliable strike zone is the kelp/ledge transition.
- Running the entrance when the bar is sloppy. The first mile is useless if you can’t get in or out safely.

Tactical quick checks before you commit:
- Tide timing beats exact GPS. The line shifts with tide and wind — set up for the tide window rather than pinning to coordinates.
- If you get the bait, keep your approach quiet, set a controlled chum pattern, and stage live bait on the uphill side. Use the motor to hold angle and drift perpendicular through the kelp throat on chunking sets.

## How the fleet fishes it
Morning traffic follows the same script: local pangas and small boats check the nearest kelp paddies, work the obvious necks, then either finish inside or run out to outer ledges if birds or sonar pull them. Pressure shows quickly — repeated noisy passes push fish deeper or make them move off.

Experienced boats:
- Approach quietly, anchor or drift with a controlled chum, and present live bait on the uphill side.
- Use precise motor control to hold angle through the neck on chunking sets rather than blasting in and out.

If you want to keep a spot, stop early and work quietly; the fish will often fire before the fleet tightens.

## Bar, launch and safety
La Bocana’s bar is the practical limiter here. The entrance works when you can run it cleanly; it isn’t worth the risk when the bar is running.

Notes from the wheelhouse:
- Check local bar reports before committing. If the bar is running, the first mile isn’t worth the risk.
- Early morning slack floods are usually the most manageable run times; avoid big ebb through a sloppy bar.
- Navigation: follow markers and local traffic. Sandbars shift seasonally and the channel can shoal. If the harbor launch officer is holding launches, respect it.

Safety decisions overrule fishing ones every time.

## When to pull off the neck
The first mile is short and unforgiving. Leave when bait disappears, sonar shows scattered marks, or the current softens into a flat drift. If those things happen, either back off to the harbor kelp paddies or run outside to the next ledge lane with less pressure.

Practical rule: when the neck stops producing within one or two clean passes, move. The spot rewards quick, clean work and punishes noisy, unfocused effort.
