Why these paddies matter
The paddies here aren’t a big continuous forest — they’re small isolated kelp pads, short rock lines, a few gutters and broken edges tucked close to the harbor. That proximity is the operational fact: you can be fishing them in twenty minutes on a calm morning. Because the structure is compact, when bait gets pinned these patches behave like a tight pocket of life instead of a long wandering edge. That concentrated relief produces short, sharp bites if everything lines up.

The three things that turn them on
If you boil it down, these paddies live and die with surface cleanliness, bait shape/location, and how the wind and swell push through the bar.

- Surface cleanliness: clean blue or only slightly stained water slipping over the bar lets fish see and commit. NW wind tends to mud the inside and kills the opportunity; a southerly swell or light morning wind flattens the bar and lets cleaner water slip in.
- Bait shape: sardina/anchoveta and other small, glassy bait that tuck under blades or stack in gutters are the key. When bait is pinned tight to the kelp edge the paddies act like traps.
- Wind/bar flow: the angle and strength of the push through the mouth determines whether bait gets pinned into lee pockets or gets washed back to shore.

Tide and the micro-traffic
Tide controls the micro-traffic around each pad more than anything visible from the cove. A steady push through the harbor mouth will shove sardina into the lee of a pad or squeeze them into the rock gutters; that’s when the yellowtail compress vertically and sit under the blades ready to explode upwards. Slack or reversing tide scatters the bait back into the shallows and the paddies go quiet. Watch for small eddies and narrow currents between pads — those are the ambush lanes.

Typical bait and what else shows up
Sardina and anchoveta dominate. You’ll see small glassy bait tucked under the pads and occasionally small squid or juvenile mackerel in warm months. Birds here aren’t the long-working, wide-open flocks you get offshore — they’ll loaf or sit just behind a productive pad, which is a subtle but reliable sign that something’s holding underneath. Yellowtail are the main ticket; dorado and other pelagics show only when warm surface patches push through.

When the paddies pay
You’ll get fast casts and explosive hits when a handful of conditions line up:

- Clean or slightly stained water inside the bar.
- Bait pinned tight to the kelp edge or stacked in a narrow gutter between pads.
- A steady tide push through the mouth that keeps bait from washing back to the shallows.
- Early morning calm or late-afternoon easing wind. Midday onshore chop usually shuts them down.

How the fleet treats them
These paddies are a short-day option for the fleet. On a working inside field boats run quick cycles: short runs, quick casts, and rapid changes of live bait. Captains who know them keep one engine ready and a full live tank — run a short spread to find the bait, kill the motors, and get into pitching or casting the moment fish show.

Typical sequence most captains use:
1. Slow perimeter run to mark gulls, bait concentrations, and the clean patches.
2. Shut down onto a pad and position to pitch or cast into gutters or the kelp shadow.
3. Keep the drift and presentation tight; the strike window is short and almost always inside the pad’s shadow.

Tactics that actually catch fish
Don’t overcomplicate — presentation and drift control beat fancy lure choices here.

- Live-bait pitch: bring a live sardina or anchovy tight, short leader, direct presentation into the lee of the pad. That’s the highest-percentage play.
- Surface casting: poppers and metal slugs on calm days — fast, sharp retrieves that keep the bait out of the blades and provoke surface explosions.
- Short jigs: in the deeper gutters between pads, quick small-metal or soft-jig work will trigger aggressive yellowtail holding below.
- Drift control: slow the drift, adjust angle, and present into the bait pocket. If you’re over the pad, the drift matters more than whether you’re running a popper or a jig.

Signals a paddy is worth the work
Look for these signs before you commit fuel and time:

- Bait hugging the base of the kelp or stacking in a narrow gutter.
- A few birds sitting and nodding directly over a pad rather than scattered out working.
- Cleaner water flashing through an otherwise stained inside field.
- Fishfinder marks hugging the kelp base or a tight vertical column near the pad edge.

Common mistakes that cost fish
There are a few repeatable errors that kill this bite fast:

- Running right over the pad at trolling speed instead of slowing, circling, and presenting.
- Expecting a big open-water foam breaker here — these are small, tight windows that need short casts.
- Chumming broadly and scattering bait. Offshore chunking works elsewhere; here you want live bait or tight, accurate casts that keep bait in the pocket.

When to walk away
The bite usually starts on the inside edge of the paddies and is short-range and high-speed when it comes. But wind can turn these pockets into a mess quickly — when the wind brings in dirty water or the tide stops pushing through the mouth, the paddies stop being pockets and just become kelp. Have a Plan B: if the inside field isn’t holding, the nearby ledges and canyon mouths off this port are the usual money spots.

Quick, practical note
If you find a pad with pinned sardina and a steady push, set up the way you would for a tight ambush: short leaders, live bait, controlled drift, and be ready for the bite to be over as soon as the tide slacks or the wind shifts. The window is small, but when it’s right the strikes are immediate and heavy.
