# Bahia Tortugas, Mexico

## Where the kelp ends and the canyon begins
Bahia Tortugas sits on a narrow shelf that falls off fast. The harbor is shallow and open—there’s usually a shallow bar and the first fishable water is often outside that bar and the outer kelp and rock lines. Close to shore you’ll see scattered hard bottom, isolated ledges and kelp paddies; a few miles out those pockets give way to steep contours and canyon mouths.

That geography dictates the run. When bait and yellowtail are stacked on kelp or the inside rocks, most boats keep it short. When bait, birds and temperature breaks push to the shelf edge or canyon mouths the whole game changes—you plan fuel and daylight for longer runs and shift your presentation to slope tactics.

Read the transition first: if bait is tight to paddies and the bar looks active, you’ve got predictable inside work. If birds and temp breaks are on the edge, expect scattered, moving pelagic schools and commit to searching the slope.

## When the windows open: wind, upwelling and warm patches
The water here switches between cold, upwelled water and warm, patchy offshore water. Spring and early summer bring strong upwelling and a steady northwester that roughens the inside and churns green water. Mid-summer into fall normally breaks into warmer, cleaner patches as winds ease.

Watch three physical signals before you burn time:
- Wind and swell at the bar — NW wind muddies inside water and builds a steep, uncomfortable run; light winds open the long-run windows.
- Surface temperature patterns and eddies — the coastal current and transient eddies carve temperature edges and seams along the shelf; those edges are where pelagics aggregate.
- Tide interaction at canyons — a tide push through a canyon concentrates bait and creates downhill vs uphill troll lanes.

Clean, blue water in a warm patch with a steady tide push through a canyon is a simple formula for workable days. Cold, upwelled water can still hold fish, but they scatter and you rely on finding the warm seams that concentrate bait.

## Who shows and when
The fishery is fundamentally pelagic on the shelf and slope, with predictable inshore structure work. The main players are yellowfin tuna, dorado and wahoo, with seasonal marlin. Yellowtail and smaller structure species live on the kelp and ledges year-round when bait is present.

Seasonal behavior in practice:
- Late spring through fall: warming produces discrete warm patches and temperature edges on the shelf. Yellowfin and dorado show in those patches—working birds and stacked bait on the ledge or spilling from canyon mouths are telltale signs.
- Early spring: strong upwelling pushes water colder and the fish scatter or move deeper; you hunt warm seams on the graph and chase the edges.
- Year-round inshore: kelp paddies and rocky ledges will hold yellowtail and dorado on calm days when bait is present; those bites are more predictable than slope work.

Presence alone isn’t enough—what makes fish bite is a clear temperature edge or current seam with bait and birds. Without that, you’ll find species near the area but not fishable schools.

## How the fleet reads the water
Boats here pick lanes based on sea state and water, not fixed GPS dots. When the kelp is firing the fleet clusters inside; when the slope is working the fleet spreads along the shelf edge and canyon mouths, running methodical grids to find seams.

Typical operating choices:
- Short-day: work kelp, rocks and inside ledges when wind or the bar make long runs risky.
- Long-run: commit to canyon mouths and the slope when reports or your temp graph show breaks—these runs are planned for fuel and daylight.
- Information flow: everyone checks radio and visual sign, but most captains still trust their own temp line and sonar sweep over hearsay.
- Conservative calls: big NW swell or unsettled wind puts boats inside; calm windows trigger those aggressive long runs.

The three things most captains check first are wind/swell at the bar, SST contours on the graph, and working birds on the horizon. Those three decide whether you stay tight or go look for the edge.

## Read seams, then change presentation fast
The winning habit here is reading seams, contour and bait together — and switching presentation quickly when the pattern shows up. The classic sequence I watch: temp edge and birds, bait stacked in a canyon or spilling from the ledge, then commit to chunking or live baiting. Move fast; pelagics don’t wait.

High-value, repeatable signals:
- Temperature edge plus working birds: high probability for yellowfin and dorado. Run the edge until they show you a preference, then narrow your spread.
- Tide push through canyon mouths: bait piles up. Set up downhill troll lanes and be ready to stop and chunk or pitch live bait.
- Kelp paddies in clean water: reliable yellowtail and dorado casting—keep your baits tight to the kelp.
- Current seam on a hard edge: prime troll lane; vertical jigs pick up fish on steep drop-offs.

Practical tactics and gear calls
- Trolling: run a varied spread across temp breaks and seams. Use teasers or strip baits on the inside lines to register bites, then bring fish to the boat for live bait or popper work.
- Chunking/live-baiting: when bait balls are stacked or a tide push is active, stop the spread. Chunking or pitching live bait into a concentrated school converts roaming tuna into hookable fish.
- Kelp/paddy work: cast jigs and live baits close to paddies and hard kelp edges. Keep light contact and be ready for sudden, high-speed runs.
- Vertical jigs and slow pitch: on steep contours and hard bottom, work the top 100–200 feet. That range triggers tuna and large structure fish when they sit deep on the edge.
- Wahoo: use wire leaders and fast-trolled lures. Mark fast edges and return—wahoo often intercept through warm seams.
- Bottom drops: when you’re on ledges, use stout gear and heavy weights. Set up on the uphill side so your presentation swings naturally with the tide.

The single sharp adjustment that wins days is the switch from searching with a wide trolling spread to committing to chunking or live-bait work when birds and bait persist on a temperature edge. Make that switch decisively and the bite usually follows.
