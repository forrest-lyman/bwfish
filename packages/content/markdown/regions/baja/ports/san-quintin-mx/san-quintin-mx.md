# San Quintin, Mexico

## How the bay and the first edge set the day
San Quintin is a broad bay that funnels quickly to a narrow, steep shelf. That transition—flat sand and kelp near the mouth into a rapid drop and offshore banks—is the engine for most of the fishing. Inside the mouth you get kelp paddies, rock reefs and flats that hold yellowtail, bass and halibut. A few miles out the bottom breaks into ledges, pinnacles and ramps where bait stacks and bigger pelagics show up.

The practical decision boils down to the mouth: if you run out and see nothing—no bait, no birds—you’re usually still in “holding” water and should plan to fish structure. If you find birds and bait on the first downramp or the first edge off the shelf, that’s where most of the fleet treats the real water. Short runs to kelp and rock give steady yellowtail and bottom fish; the outer banks and slope are the place for tuna and dorado, but only when surface signs and temperature back it up.

Read the edge more than the GPS. Yellowtail sit on the uphill side of kelp and on the heads of ramps. Offshore pelagics key to visible bait and warm streaks along the first break.

## When the water opens: wind, upwelling, and the morning window
This place is driven by wind and upwelling more than a calendar. Spring is cold—strong upwelling and cool surface temps. Late summer into fall the upwelling eases and warm-water pushes sneak in. Northwest wind is the normal summer afternoon; winter and spring bring stronger NW swell and sloppy cross-seas that make long runs uncomfortable. Fog and cold mornings are common during peak upwelling.

What actually changes a day is combinations, not dates. Watch for these patterns:
- After several days of light NW wind followed by a calm morning you often get clean water and a visible temp break. Bait stacks on edges and fish become visible—this is the morning window you want.
- Strong NNW swell with onshore wind strips paddies and scatters sargassum. Bait hides tight to hard bottom and fish move close to structure.
- Sustained onshore wind or downwelling pushes bait into the bay and concentrates fish shallow. That’s good for inside runs, bad for looking for tuna and dorado.

If you find a stable surface temp and working birds, you can make manageable runs and expect active bait. Without that combo, you’re usually better off fishing structure inside.

## Who shows where and why
San Quintin runs as a split fishery: reliable nearshore structure and a narrow offshore window for pelagics when the water lines up.

Yellowtail — backbone of the port. They live paddies, first ledges outside the bay, and the heads of banks. They’ll eat live bait aggressively when current is pushing and bait stacks on the ledge. You’ll catch them on live bait, slow-trolled plastics and jigs when the bait is concentrated.

Dorado and tunas — transient and temperature-driven. They arrive with warm-water pushes and visible temp edges. When they’re around they feed hard on scattered bait and around debris or paddies. They’re a by-product of a warm corridor hitting the shelf and bait being available.

Halibut and inshore bottomfish — take over when wind and swell keep boats in the bay. Sand/rock transitions near the mouth consistently produce halibut and mixed demersal catches; these fish respond to slow presentations and are position sensitive to tide.

Bonita and small tunas — often the early indicators of a warmer corridor. If you see them working ahead of larger tuna, the system may be moving through.

The pattern that decides a day is bait concentration and a temperature gradient. A few birds and bait stacked on the first downramp will turn otherwise ordinary water into steady action. Absent those signs, you’re fishing for structure or you should run further.

## How the fleet reads the water and commits
Local boats make practical choices: weather, how far they want to run, and what the coastal screens show. When yellowtail are working, small boats stay near and run kelp lines and close ledges. When pelagics are suspected the fleet splits: scouts make reconnaissance runs, the rest commit if scouts report birds and clean edges.

Common habits I see on any given morning:
- Early reconnaissance runs to check birds and temp edges before committing to long runs.
- If bait is stacked on the first bank outside the mouth most boats stay inshore and work paddies and edges instead of pushing farther.
- Larger boats will push to the shelf or overnight grounds when satellite or on-screen temps show a persistent warm corridor; small boats usually won’t risk those runs.

Scouts watch three things first: birds, sonar bait signatures, and the surface temperature gradient. Those three signals make the decision to stay, search, or commit.

## Tactical notes that actually matter
This fishery rewards quick, correct reads. Read the bait, the temperature and the contour together and adjust immediately.

Read the edge, not the GPS point. Yellowtail and small tunas stack on the uphill side of kelp lines and on the heads of ramps. Drift, kite or slow-troll live bait along that uphill seam and you’ll see the difference.

Downhill current on the shelf concentrates bait on the ledge. When you find downhill current and a 1–2°F warmer streak, set a search pattern perpendicular to the break. Troll slow baits, run a few jigs on the edges, and be ready to pitch live bait once the echo shows concentration.

Working birds with scattered surface bait is the clearest signal for dorado and larger tuna. First find where the bait is holding on the sonar, then put spreader bars and small high-speed lures in the water or get live bait on a kite if fish are tight to paddies.

Kelp paddies aren’t static. Check the down-current end and the uphill side. If a paddy is producing, run a tight drift or a tight drift-kite along the edge rather than circling wide.

When bait is tight but birds aren’t working, slow-pitch metals and heavy jigs will pick off suspended yellowtail and smaller tunas over pinnacles. Keep a vertical presentation where the echo shows bait stacked on the ledge.

For halibut and sandfish, anchor or slow-drift the sand/rock transitions on an incoming tide with live bait or large plastics. These fish want slow, positive presentations and respond to tide-driven push.

The simplest reliable adjustment: if bait is stacked on the first offshore ledge and you have a noticeable warm streak on the surface, commit to the edge with live bait and a downhill drift. If that combination isn’t there, work kelp and rocks close to the bay.

Fish the signals you can see—birds, bait on sonar, and temp breaks—and make movements based on what those three give you. That’s the pattern here; everything else is detail.
