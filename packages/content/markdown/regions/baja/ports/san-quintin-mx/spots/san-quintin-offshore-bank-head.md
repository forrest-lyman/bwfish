This is the head of the first offshore bank outside San Quintin’s shelf — not a lone rock, but a ridge‑on‑ridge trend where the shelf peels into one or two downhill ramps. Current shears off the crest and pins bait into a tight column along the uphill seam. Locals call that seam the live‑bait ridge because it’s where sardina and small mackerel pile when the current and temperature line up.

Think of it as a decision point. Run the head, check the echo and birds, and you decide whether to fish yellowtail and bottom structure or convert to a kite/live‑bait spread for dorado and smaller tunas.

## Why boats keep stopping at the head
Short run and repeatable geometry. The bank head is close enough for small boats to reach without burning a full day, and the downhill current creates a reliable seam where bait compresses. When the upwelling relaxes and a warm filament slips over the ridge, the same shallow structure that holds yellowtail becomes a launch point for dorado and yellowfin.

Species you can expect in the mix: yellowtail on kelp and ridge heads, bonita and small tunas marking the corridor, and dorado or yellowfin when a warm streak threads through.

## When the surface window matters
The head matters most when three conditions come together: bait density, tight bird work, and a warm surface filament.

- Late‑summer to fall warm pushes — small warm filaments slipping over the bank can turn a normal yellowtail ridge into pelagic water.
- Calm mornings after a light NW wind — surface clears, temp edges firm, and bait stacks on the uphill face.
- NNW swell with onshore wind — paddies strip and fish hug hard bottom; the head can look dead until the swell eases.

If those signs aren’t present, treat it like a yellowtail/bottom check and move on.

## Signals that the live‑bait window is building
Watch and confirm these signals; they’re simple and dependable.

- Birds working tight to the ridge instead of spread across the flat.
- Sonar showing bait stacked 20–80 ft on the uphill face.
- A visible warm streak (even a 1–2°F edge) crossing the bank.
- Porpoise or bonita blitzing in short, concentrated breaks.

A single foamer with no sonar backing isn’t enough — you want at least two of the above before you convert.

## Scouting and first moves
Run early, make perpendicular sweeps across the crest, and mark the uphill seam on your echo.

Sequence to follow when you first arrive:
1. Find the depth where bait concentrates on the screen.
2. Note your drift angle and which side of the ridge the bait is holding.
3. If birds are working, circle once to confirm consistency before committing your spread.

Micro‑observation: the first scout that reports birds tight on the uphill face usually decides where the fleet stops. Treat that call seriously, but verify with your echo.

## How fishermen convert the ridge to a live‑bait/kite play
My rule of thumb is simple: anchor the decision on three things — sonar bait, working birds, and a warm filament. When all three line up, the ridge becomes a practical live‑bait conversion point.

What we do:
- Kite a live sardina or small mackerel on the uphill side of the seam to present natural bait where the school is pinned.
- Run spreader bars and high‑speed lures on the opposite side to intercept moving tuna and dorado.
- Keep a couple of live‑bait droppers free so you can immediately fly‑line if fish tighten to paddies near the ridge head.

The kite holds the bait uphill while the spread covers moving targets — that layered presentation converts a transit into pressure the pelagics respond to.

## Spreader‑bar and trolling notes
Spreader bars are the workhorse when birds are active and fish are moving.

- Use small, high‑speed lures when bird work is consistent; they cover direction changes and trigger fast fish.
- Drop one or two swim baits near the ridge crest at moderate speeds to tempt cautious yellowfin; keep the outer lines on spreaders to cover running lanes.
- When birds are sporadic, tighten the spread and slow the baits — dorado often reappear on a slower, more bunched presentation.

Adjust speeds and spread based on how the birds and sonar respond rather than a preset RPM.

## Vertical and jigging options
When the surface window collapses but the echo still shows bait, go vertical.

- Heavy jigs and slow‑pitch metals pick off suspended yellowtail and small tunas over pinnacles and deeper shoulders.
- Keep a vertical rig ready when bait stacks directly over the top of the ridge — fish will often sit mid‑column and take short, vertical offerings.

Vertical work is the contingency for when the topwater pattern fades but the fish remain on the ridge.

## Kite practicalities and timing
Kite early in the window. If fish are keyed to paddies or very shallow bait, kite live baits to present a natural offering above the ridge.

- Use the kite to hold bait uphill of the boat while spreaders work the outside. That combo forces passing tuna and dorado to choose.
- If the fish are moving fast across the seam, add an extra high‑speed lure to cover the outside lane. If they’re holding, tighten up and bring baits shallower.

Kite setups win the early moments of a window; delay and you miss the easiest bites.

## What usually matters more — and less
Things that usually decide the day:
- Drift angle across the ridge.
- Density and depth of bait on the echo.
- Consistency of bird work.

Things that usually matter less:
- An isolated foamer with no sonar backing.
- Yesterday’s numbers from a ridge a few miles away.

Read what’s under your hull now, not last trip’s story.

## Small‑boat vs larger‑boat decisions
Small boats: treat the bank head as a high‑probability, short‑run target. It’s reachable and fuel‑efficient; when the window opens you can get sustained yellowtail and dorado action without an all‑day grind. Don’t over‑commit offshore unless scouts confirm a persistent warm corridor.

Larger boats: use the head as a staging checkpoint. If the head is firing and the warm filament holds, push beyond the ridge for wider dorado and yellowfin lanes or an overnight ground. Bigger boats can deploy more kites and wider spreads to work multiple layers.

Fuel callout: commit extra fuel only after the initial check—birds, bait, and temps need to hold.

## Fleet behavior and pressure reactions
Scouts run quick perpendicular sweeps; the fleet splits into commit and fallback groups based on those reads. If one boat hooks up consistently, expect schools to tighten and either sink or scatter. The typical pressure reactions I see:

- Schools drop below sonar after repeated passes.
- Foamers speed up and run the edge, refusing repeated casts.
- Bait thins on the ridge and often returns later in the tide cycle.

If pressure builds, the winning move is to change drift angle or presentation rather than repeating the same pass.

## Tactical reminders
A few practical rules that save time and hooks:

- Read the edge, not the GPS. The crest shifts with current angle; fish where bait pins, not where the chart says the head should be.
- If current softens, fish often move deeper — switch to vertical presentations and heavy jigs.
- If birds are tight but your sonar shows no bait, you’re probably seeing transient surface activity. Check surrounding water before burning fuel.

Run early, scout cleanly, and convert to kite/live‑bait when the three signs line up. If the surface window collapses, be ready to switch to vertical or jigging work — the ridge will usually still hold fish if you change how you present.
