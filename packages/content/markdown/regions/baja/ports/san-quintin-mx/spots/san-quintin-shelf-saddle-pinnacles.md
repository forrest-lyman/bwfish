The cluster sits where the shelf first falls away outside the bay mouth — a short, irregular saddle with several shallow-to-mid depth pinnacles rising from a 120–200 ft terrace. It’s not a single obvious bank; it’s a stitched-together piece of relief: low saddles, isolated cones, and short, steep ribs that break the sweep of the outer face. From the surface the water often looks ordinary. On electronics the contours grab bait and make a tidy vertical column.

Why this little mess of relief matters
The feature organizes vertical water. Bait drifting down the slope will hit the saddle and pinnacles and pile in a compact column instead of sliding cleanly over the terrace. Small bait schools get caught on the uphill side of a cone and hang in the water column. That geometry is why yellowtail and smaller tunas show up suspended rather than tailing on the flat.

Current and temperature that make the saddle produce
Observe these three signals in order of importance:

- Downhill current across the saddle. When water moves off the shelf toward deeper water the bait compresses into tight echoes above the pinnacles. That’s the money signal.
- A narrow 1–2°F warm streak riding the top of the slope. It keeps bait active and will turn a good mark into a fishable gate — tuna and dorado chase those fingers.
- Wind/upwelling conditions. NW wind and active upwelling usually mask or push away the warm filaments. Several calm days or an early-morning glass-off lets the warm finger settle on the edge.

Reading the sounder here
Look for shape and behavior, not big numbers.

- Tight, narrow columns sitting 20–80 ft off the bottom over a small high — true vertical bait stacks, not long diffuse schools.
- Marks that streak upward when birds arrive — bait being pushed off the ledge into the column.
- A scatter of smaller marks off the saddle heading away from the bank — smaller tuna/bonito working the flank.

What the cluster typically holds
Think suspended predators first, bottom life second.

- Yellowtail: hold mid-column over the pinnacles and on the uphill sides of cones. They’ll come up from the face to take live bait and respond to vertical metals when the drift is right.
- Smaller tunas/bonito and occasional yellowfin: show up when birds and warm streaks intersect the cluster.
- Demersal life on the saddle edges: halibut and large sandfish sit low on the flats next to the highs.

When the spot fishes — and when it goes quiet
Practical signs to steam on or leave.

- It fishes when: downhill current + tight sonar bait over a small high + visible temp streak or active birds.
- It looks dead when: bait is smeared into long tracks; surface color is uniform and cool from strong upwelling; or NW wind is scrubbing paddies and pushing life tight to nearshore rocks.
- Timing note: the same saddle that produces vertical marks in the morning can be empty by noon if the tide softens or wind chops the water and buries the paddies.

Micro-observation
On a clean day with a good downhill drift the fish stay mid-column, not on the bottom. That’s the place to present to.

Fleet behavior and how the other boats read the bank
Locals treat this cluster like a short bluewater checkpoint.

- Small boats usually run a quick check of the line — if the first paddy’s productive they settle or push deeper.
- Larger boats stage across the saddle and run perpendicular search passes looking for consistent stacked marks.
- If several boats converge on the same small paddy, the fish can disappear; often another cone 200–400 yards away has fresh bait.

How they search and set up
Scout perpendicular passes to pick off the warm finger and the tight bait signatures. When scouts report stacked echo over a cone most boats set a downhill drift and go vertical. If birds are working away from the saddle, boats widen the search and shift to spreader bars or troll lanes.

Vertical first — the primary tactics
Start with the column. The structure holds fish in place; coax them up.

- Presentations: slow-pitch metals and heavy jigs are the primary tools. Drop to the bait, keep the presentation vertical, and work short, aggressive lifts when the sonar shows fish hugging a column.
- Live bait: a short drop with a live sardina or small mackerel converts better than long runs when marks are tight and current is steady.
- Kite/live-bait conversion: if birds are pounding surface schools around paddies near the saddle, switch to kiting live baits at the top of the column and be ready to run spreader bars on the troll lanes.
- Pace: slow, controlled vertical presentations beat fast, flashy offerings here. The cluster rewards a measured drop and a patient lift.

When the vertical plan should change
Watch the bait and birds.

- Bait rises and birds go to work on the surface: move from vertical metals to trolling/spreader bars and put a live bait on the kite.
- Marks wash out into thin, long echoes: consolidate and fish the paddies or shift to nearby ledges — the saddle likes concentrated bait.
- Repeated drops without follow: the current angle has likely changed; re-chart your drift and reset the vertical zone.

Practical checklist before you commit
- Find downhill current across a cone or saddle.
- Confirm a narrow bait column on the sounder (20–80 ft off bottom).
- Set up a downhill drift keeping baits and rods vertical.
- Run slow-pitch metals and have live bait ready.
- Be ready to switch to spreader bars/kite if birds and surface bait spell a change.

Common mistakes to avoid
- Circling paddies wide instead of running a tight downhill drift along the uphill seam.
- Treating every flat mark as a bank; these pinnacles are small and fish hug the uphill side.
- Switching to fast, flashy offerings too soon — the first bites usually come to a vertical, controlled presentation.

Pressure reactions and quick pivots
Under pressure bigger yellowtail tighten to the high and tuck deeper. That’s when heavier jigs and a measured drop win. If the fleet punts the same warm finger and it dies, back off 200–400 yards and re-evaluate the sounder — another cone often has fresh bait.

The saddle reads well if you pay attention to the echo, the current, and the birds. Fish the column first; when the column softens, fish the contour around it. Patience on the drop and a fast switch to kite or troll when the surface lights up is how you win here.
