## The ground — sand fingers into rock

You’ll find a set of sand tongues carving into broken rock and kelp necks along the bay’s southern approach. It’s not a steep shelf—more a repeatable pattern: soft flats, short scours and gutters, and patches of darker rock that hold bait. On the screen the bottom “cleans up” into wide soft areas with darker blobs of rock and bait clouds riding the troughs.

That mix of sand and nearby rock is why this spot holds halibut: the sand gives them a feeding lane and the rock keeps bait in place long enough for a slow pass or an anchored presentation. In practice that means the seam—the down‑current edge of the sand where it meets rock—is where you put your attention.

## Tide and movement — fish the flood

This place fishes with the tide. Incoming (flood) is the work tide: current pushes bait off the rocks and into the gutters and pins fish along the down‑current edge of the transition.

Practical notes from running it:

- The best window is the first 1–2 hours of the incoming tide.
- Aim for a slow, parallel sweep along the gutter rather than a steep cross drift; halibut want time to turn on a bait.
- If you can hold a 0.5–1.0 kt drift over the gutter you’re in the sweet spot. Faster than that and the baits sail past; much slower and you won’t cover the seam. Adjust weight or scope before changing hook size.
- Light onshore wind usually compresses bait and helps the push. Heavy NW wind tends to bury paddies and push fish tight to the rock.

Observation: the fish will often show on the drop before they show on the cast. That little hesitation on the graph—bait holding in the column—is a direct signal.

## Signs it’s firing

Watch for three things together and commit:

- Clean sonar traces that outline a sand gutter with scattered, denser marks hugging the rock edge.
- Live bait hanging 10–20 ft above bottom instead of evaporating immediately—bait in the column is the clue.
- Short, sharp follows or quick inhalations on a slow retrieve rather than long runs.

When boats start lifting fish on the first or second presentation on the drift, you’re on a good run. If marks vanish as the tide strengthens, back up your drift and fish tighter to the rock head.

## Anchor vs drift — how the fleet works it

Locals pick between a controlled drift and a short anchor set. Tide strength, wind and how tight the fish are to structure make the call.

When to drift
- Use the drift when the tide is moving cleanly and you can hold that 0.5–1.0 kt sweep along the gutter.
- Drift when you need to locate fish marks and cover the seam without scaring fish with too many passes.

When to anchor
- Anchor when the tide softens but there’s still a push and the fish want slow presentations.
- Anchor when wind is light and you can set a stern anchor without swinging into rocks.
- On an anchor set you want bow/ stern control so the boat stays pointed into the push and the baits ride directly off the stern.

Decisions explained: drift finds fish and exposes active schools; anchor holds them in a narrow zone for deliberate presentations. Don’t force one when conditions favor the other.

### Drift setup checklist

This is short and operational—what I do before the first pass.

- Mark the gutter head and the first rock blob on the sonar.
- Run an initial drift line roughly perpendicular to the gutter, then feather throttle until you’re moving parallel and slow.
- Keep baits 6–18 inches off bottom on soft sand. A half‑meter lift often triggers follows without burying the bait.
- Use a moderate bank of weight to keep the bait near bottom; droppers or short pennel rigs are reliable.
- If your drift is too fast, add weight or shorten scope instead of changing hooks.

## Bait and presentations — slow, deliberate, position‑sensitive

Halibut here respond to presentations that sit and read like natural prey. They’re position‑sensitive; small changes in height and angle matter.

What works:

- Live bait: medium sardina or local small live baits on 2/0–4/0 circle or striper‑style hooks. Let the bait wander slowly just above bottom.
- Large plastics: slow, soft plastics on 3/4–1.5 oz heads (match head weight to depth/drag) with gentle hops—think fleeing sand shrimp or crab rather than slugging it.
- Slow‑pitch jigs and vertical baits: short lifts and soft falls around rock edges where halibut cruise; avoid big, fast strikes.
- Chum and scent: light, targeted chumming near an anchor can keep halibut in the hole. Don’t over‑chum and scatter bait.

What tends to matter more here: bait in the column, drift angle and tide timing. What tends to matter less: long, heavy spreads and running long trolling drags.

## When inside beats outside

The sand fingers are the practical inside choice when conditions outside are marginal.

Fish the inside when:

- Onshore wind or persistent downwelling is pushing bait into the bay and the outside edges are stripped of paddies.
- Heavy NNW swell has scrubbed off paddies, leaving debris and bait tight to rock.
- Fuel, bar or entrance conditions make long runs impractical.

This isn’t defeat. When the incoming tide pins bait against the sand/rock seam and there’s no usable warm streak outside, the sand fingers will outproduce a marginal offshore hunt.

## Common mistakes

A few repeated errors I see:

- Circling a paddy or sand finger too wide and losing the down‑current edge—halibut live on the seam.
- Drifting too fast to cover ground; trying to be everywhere kills hookups. Slow, controlled sweeps win.
- Anchoring without stern control—boats swing and risk fouling lines or dragging into shallow rock.

## Electronics and reading the day

Run the sonar for the seam first. The gutter head will read softer, then a darker rock patch—that seam is where fish stack. Watch for bait arches that hang in the column; they’re where halibut patrol. If those bait marks disappear as the tide grows, pull the drift tighter to the rock head or switch tactics.

Final operational call:
If the incoming tide pins bait against the sand/rock seam and the sonar shows tight marks, fish the gut deliberately—slow drifts, live bait or large plastics, and short, repeatable passes. If those signs are missing and birds or warm streaks appear at the bay mouth, run the first ledge and test the shelf. This ground rewards respect for position and timing; you won’t outfish it by speed.
