The North Point pinnacles are a short, readable piece of water you can reach from Los Barriles in twenty to forty minutes on a calm morning. They’re not a continuous reef—think of a string of teeth: 8–12 isolated cones and rock piles, 10–30 ft on top, with narrow sand gutters between them and a steep contour a few miles outside the cluster. The features break bait on a push and create short downhill fingers where roosters and jacks do most of their work.

## How the pinnacles sit on the shelf and why bait concentrates
Observation: bait stacks right on the shallow tops and funnels into the down-current pockets. Explanation: the pinnacles are the last shallow teeth before the shelf drops; when a tide pushes across them the flow peels around each pile, making downstream seams and lee pockets. Those seams force bait up the cones and pinch them against the edges—you get tight, short-lived packs rather than long slicks.

Run the cluster and you’ll see the same pattern: birds tighten over one pile, sonar shows a column that looks like it’s being squeezed along the down-current side, and the surface boils where roosters work the edge.

## Run time, entrance and exposure
Observation: short run but exposed. Explanation and decisions:
- Distance: 3–5 nm from the marina, fuel-friendly for half-day work.
- Time: 20–40 minutes in calm; longer when the thermal builds.
- Entrance: the harbor exit is straightforward, but long north swells can make the mouth sloppy. If the mouth’s dirty, move your schedule earlier or wait for a light tide.
- Wind/thermal: mornings are best. Afternoon NW thermals build short chop and smear seams—baits break into lines or disappear. Winter northerlies can make the first mile unpleasant; don’t force it.

## How the tide peels the pinnacles and where to position
Observation: the spot fishes like an edge. Explanation and approach:
- Best windows are a steady push across the piles. That push can be flood or outgoing depending on the day, but it must create consistent downhill flow across several piles.
- Position up-current of the pile, hold station and let the current carry your presentation into the seam. If you run over the pile you’ll scatter bait and spook roosters—approach each pile into the push, not across it.
- With light thermal the seams stay tidy; when NW wind cranks the seams smear and the bait spreads out or sinks. In that case shorten your spread and fish the column instead of relying on surface commotion.

## Signals that tell you a window is open
Watch for these practical cues; they’re what we act on, not theory:
- Birds repeatedly checking one pile rather than wide, random working birds.
- Tight sonar returns over a pinnacle—bait concentrated through depth.
- Boils or small slashes on the down-current side.
- A tide push that creates a consistent downhill flow across multiple piles.
- A clean pocket or thin slick between piles with visible bait or flying fish.

When several of those line up you get a short, hard window—sometimes minutes, sometimes an hour. When they fall apart, the fish usually move off quickly.

## Bait, birds and seasonality
Observation: small sardina, glassy sardines, threadfin/flying fish and small jacks are the common forage. How that changes the game:
- Birds (terns and occasional shearwaters) tighten on working bait. Persistent, low birds over a single pile are better signals than a single crash.
- Roosters are present year-round here and respond to tidy tide pushes in cooler months and shoulder seasons. Amberjack and larger snapper trend warmer, but any tidally active day can turn them on.
- Summer sees more scattered surface bait and some dorado moving through, but the cluster remains a structure-plus-tide play.
- Remember the short-window reality: tight bait packs can dissolve fast, especially when a thermal smears the surface.

## Presentations and boat handling
Observation: the fish prefer the downhill seam or the deeper face depending on species. Practical presentations:
- Roosterfish:
  - Approach early, before thermals build. Hold up-current and cast live bait or large strip baits into the downhill seam.
  - Short casting, heavy pops, or a live sardina/mullet on a short, stiff leader with a quick retrieve mimic fleeing bait along the seam.
  - If bait is stacked, station just outside the top and present into the moving pocket rather than across it.
- Amberjack:
  - When marks show on the deeper faces, vertical work wins. Drop heavy live baits or jigs tight to the rock and be ready for hard, short runs toward structure. Use stout leaders and a plan to get heads up fast.
- Snapper and mixed bottom:
  - Work the sand gutters with controlled drifts or short anchor drifts using small chunks or slow-sink rigs. Fish the rock-to-sand transition where snapper stage between relief and feeding lanes.
- Boat handling:
  - Shorten the spread in chop; slow your approach when wind smears the slick.
  - Re-position up-current after each pass—don’t keep circling the same angle. The current finger will often rebuild ahead of you.
- Electronics:
  - Watch for rising marks and increased target size on the edge. When marks tighten and shallow, change to surface or shallow presentations; when they stack deeper, switch to verticals.

## Common tactical mistakes
Short, practical list of what I see boats do wrong:
- Running over the pile and charging the bait—spooks roosters and scatters schooling bait.
- Presenting uphill into the bait—do the work upstream and let the bait move into the fish.
- Ignoring tidal shape—flat, slack tides often leave the piles empty.
- Overworking a fading pile—if birds and bait stop reforming, move on or wait for the next push.

## Pressure, spacing and when to give it time
Observation: the line is short and predictable, so pressure shows quickly. Explanation and behavior:
- Too many boats on the same pile pushes fish deeper or down-current. Work different angles, come back later, or let a pile rest for an hour and fish the next clean push.
- The best boats use small moves—try the next pile up-current or a nearby channel drop rather than fighting for the same rock.

## Why experienced boats keep checking it
Observation: quick reach, readable water, and repeatable windows. Explanation: on tidy tides and calm mornings the cluster gives short, focused action—roosters on the edge, amberjack and snapper on the faces—without long runs or guesswork. When the thermal and swell conspire the cluster still offers quick, manageable opportunities close to port. It’s a compact piece of water you can test reliably and move between without burning a full day.

Fish the signs, respect the tide, and keep your approaches off the bait. When the pieces line up, the cluster pays off in short, high-probability runs.
