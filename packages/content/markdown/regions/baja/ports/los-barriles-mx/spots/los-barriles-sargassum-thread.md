## Where the thread sits and how far you'll run
The Sargassum Thread isn’t a single mat you can mark and forget. It’s a finger of weed and drifting debris that traces the first canyon mouths and shelf shoulders east–southeast of Los Barriles. On good days it becomes a long, narrow alley—broken mats, tight seams, a few cleaner edges—that funnels bait, birds and predators along a predictable line.

Expect to check close patches at 6–8 nm, and only commit on longer runs (12–18 nm) when you see warm blue water and birds stacking on a seam. The alley is streaky: long clean runs of blue water sit beside heavy, stringy weed and short, tight mats in the same pass.

## Reading the morning window and planning the exit
Mornings usually give you the best picture. The NW thermal builds toward late morning and either compresses the weed into tight seams or scatters it, depending on wind angle. If you want calm presentations, time the exit with the tide and watch for short‑period chop—an otherwise straightforward exit can make slow baits impossible when the harbor is lumpy.

Winter north swell steepens the approach and shreds surface mats; if the exit is messy, plan for a later start or a short‑run day. When the NW thermal is steady the alley often slides into a lee outside the shelf shoulders; when the wind shifts it can move a mile or more in short order. In practice that means short visual checks early, and committing only when birds, blue water and a seam agree.

## Why the thread forms where it does
The thread sits where thermal wind, surface current and tidal flows meet the shelf and canyon mouths. Canyon pushes and tidal peel compress floating weed into linear mats and downhill seams. The important physical feature is the canyon mouth and the downhill seam alongside it—the weed often lines the up‑current side of that seam where bait funnels and predators like to run.

Look for a hard blue band next to greener, flakier water; that temperature/clarity change is the usual signature. But expect patchiness: clean gaps, tight mats and long stringy sargassum can all occur inside the same alley.

## Early signs the window is building
Start with what you can see and hear—birds will usually tell you before the electronics do. Signs that the window is forming:

- Birds—frigates, terns, shearwaters—lowering and picking repeatedly on a patch.
- Flying fish or small mackerel riding slicks inside or ahead of the weed.
- A visible temperature notch where blue water meets greener, weedier water.
- Weed mats holding small jacks, juvenile dorado or triggerfish under them.
- A steady current pushing through a canyon mouth compressing surface life.

When those pieces line up the patch fishes fast and impatient. Miss one of them and the window often collapses.

## When mahi and wahoo show
Mahi peak late spring through early fall—warmer blue water and steady thermals build the long threads that bring them in earliest and fastest. Wahoo show more regularly in summer and early fall; they use the same alley but prefer cleaner, faster edges to run on.

Transitional seasons (spring and fall) can be the most reliable for short, intense windows—clean blue fingers pushing against weed mats bring both species in quick succession. Winter gives less consistent weed and therefore shorter, more filament‑dependent action when it happens.

## How I fish the thread
Scout first, then commit. A single working patch with birds is worth the run; a long quiet weedline is not.

1. Visual scout. Use the bow, binoculars and birds. If birds are committed, slow down and make controlled passes.
2. Slow, precise entries. Slice the boat through the weed—full‑speed runs scatter the life and kill the bite. Keep the transom clear for mahi and be ready to cast.
3. Station and feed the edge. Hold the boat just outside the mat and feed baits into the seam; fish often approach from deeper water to cut through the mat. Present baits downhill into the compressed bait field—uphill presentations through a seam rarely work.
4. Presentations:
   - Mahi: small skirted lures, surface irons run through the seam, short accurate casts to mat edges, teasers to pull followers up, and live bait when fish are committed. Short spreads and a tighter pattern usually outfish a wide, diluted spread.
   - Wahoo: when clean blue water sits beside the weed, switch to faster offerings—plugs, trolled cigars or single‑hook skirted lures on longer leaders. Test speed over the edge and be ready to slow the presentation if fish are following but not committing.
5. Short casting routine. When mahi are around, a methodical cast pattern—bow, port, starboard—beats random long casts through the middle of the mat.

Think in terms of feeding the seam, not plowing through it.

## What usually matters more, and what doesn’t
What usually matters more:
- A canyon push or steady current through a seam.
- Bait and flying fish inside or just ahead of the weed.
- Birds committed to the patch.

What usually matters less:
- The prettiest, longest weedline—length doesn’t equal life.
- Running a maximum spread before confirming birds or bait.
- Making long runs when short checks are producing.

Let the three signals (current, bait, birds) drive your commitment. When they agree, slow down and focus. When they don’t, move on.

## Common mistakes that kill the patch
The mistakes I see most often are simple and avoidable:

- Plowing through a live mat at full speed and scattering everything.
- Keeping a wide spread when fish are tight to the weed—too many baits dilute the opportunity.
- Sticking to a single mat after birds and bait drift to a new patch—weed moves fast under strong thermal wind.
- Waiting for the fleet to commit before testing a clean edge—wahoo and bigger mahi often sit on the blue side.

Fix those and your hit rate improves quickly.

## Small observations that change decisions
The weed can collapse in fifteen minutes. Birds will usually show you the patch before your sounder will. Fish commute between a mat and the blue edge—present into the water they use, not just into the debris.

In practice: a tight mat, a canyon push and birds equals a high‑value window. Lose one of those three and the spot becomes a time sink. Be prepared to punch out, check, and re‑run the alley until the pieces line up.
