Why short sand fingers concentrate bait
The inner lee here is a compact shelf — shallow sand tongues that step into scattered rock and small pinnacles. What you see first is bait getting squeezed into narrow lanes: sand fingers form troughs and edges, rock pimples break the flow, and the nearby canyon mouths focus the tidal push. Observationally, when the tide runs into the canyon the bait stops spreading and piles up along the sand edges and in the protected lee pockets. That’s your window: a short, tidy feed rather than an open‑water blitz.

Where this sits and how to get there
This is first‑mile water, usually 0.5–3 nm from the harbor. On calm mornings the exit is straightforward; with a winter northerly swell the shallow approach can get sloppy and the sand tongues churn up. The daily NW thermal typically builds by mid‑morning, so the pockets fish best before the wind breaks the slicks and scatters bait. After a hard northerly swell the best roosterwater often shows the day after it drops, when tides have reworked the sand and the pockets clean up.

Reading the tide push — when the window forms
Observation first: the place really lives on a push. You’ll see birds tighten to a narrow band, sonar marks narrow along the edge of a trough, and foamers working a specific lee pocket. That’s the window forming.

Why it matters: flood or a strong push into the canyon compresses bait against the sand fingertip and creates downhill seams where fish can hold and ambush. Slack or a softening tide lets bottom species settle on ledges, but roosters prefer an incoming/flood push or the early outgoing that channels bait past points. In short: watch the direction and strength of the push — it usually beats everything else.

Structure and how the water organizes life
Start with the features and watch how they behave.

- Sand fingers: low bars and troughs off the beach that funnel bait into narrow lanes. When bait is stacked on the edge, surface boils and tight sonar marks follow the contour.
- Lee pockets: small, protected patches of cleaner water behind points and rock. A clean push into these concentrates bait and focuses surface action.
- Isolated pinnacles and rock piles: not huge structure, but enough relief to hold snapper, grouper and to act as ambush points for roosters.
- Canyon influence: nearby canyon mouths accelerate and focus tidal exchange across these short shelves, turning a benign flat into a feeding lane.

Bait, birds and what to watch on the sounder
Observation first: bait here is mostly sardina and small mullet pushed along the surf. When the window builds you’ll see tight, shallow sonar marks on the sand edge, birds working tight to the beach, and localized foamers and boils.

Use those signals together. Birds or foamers that are tight and moving along a specific band almost always mean compressed bait. Sonar that shows narrow, shallow stacks along the sand trough tells you where the bait lane is. When marks narrow and hold, position for the push — the fish will use the seam rather than the flat.

Where the fish actually hold
Short answer: the fish come from the deeper troughs and holes adjacent to the sand, not from the flat sand itself.

Roosterfish are the defining species, showing year‑round but strongest on warm months and during solid tide pushes. Snapper and grouper sit on the rock pockets and pinnacles year‑round and often bite cleaner on calm days or on tides that let you hold a controlled drift. Jack crevalle are the noisy first responders — they show when bait starts breaking and are a reliable signal that something larger is behind the commotion.

How the fleet treats this water
Locally this is quick‑check water or the fallback when canyon heads aren’t worth the run. Boats will run in early for rooster checks on the tide push; if it’s quiet they’ll move on. The fleet often spreads early to avoid spooking wary roosters and comes back tight if birds or foamers report inside the first mile. Repeat passes and lots of traffic will shut these small pockets faster than open water, so discretion and a clean approach pay dividends.

Tactics — approach, positioning, and presentations
General approach: leave early, beat the thermal, and get into the push. Position up‑current of the bait so your presentation runs downhill through the seam. Hold station just outside the shallow edge and present into the stacked bait — most hits come from the adjacent troughs or holes, so keep casts short and presentations natural.

Species‑specific notes

- Roosterfish
  Observation: roosters light up with an incoming/flood push into the pocket or a late‑afternoon funneling tide.
  Where: just outside the surf break and up‑current of the bait lane or point lee.
  How: live baits or natural‑running sardina that move downhill through the seam; short casts into boils and seams. Quiet approach and natural drift beat theatrics.

- Snapper
  Observation: steady on slack or a clean outgoing that lays bait over the rock edge.
  Where: along the rock/sand transition, over pinnacles and holes at the sand edge.
  How: vertical presentations or slow drifts with live bait or small jigs; keep the drift tight to the relief.

- Grouper
  Observation: hold when current pins bait against rock piles and ledges.
  Where: downhill sides of pinnacles and small ledges.
  How: slow, bottom‑oriented work — let baits settle into holes and off ledges; short controlled drifts or anchoring in a legal lee pocket when practical.

- Jack Crevalle
  Observation: first to show when bait is breaking; they make the surface chaos.
  Where: on top of boils and inside compressed lee.
  How: fast, aggressive offerings — poppers, metal jigs or quick live‑bait casts; be ready for surface scrambles.

Common mistakes and pressure effects
Observation: the window here is easily shut by poor approach or too many boats.

- Charging foam or birds straight on will scatter the bait and kill the push. Approach along the seam and present downhill.
- Presenting uphill — trolling or offering baits that move away from the bait — usually fails. Fish want baits pushed into them.
- Overfishing small pockets with repeated passes quiets the spot. Sometimes the right play is to angle off or give the area a reset.

Tactical priorities
What matters most: tide push into the pocket, bait compression against the sand edge, and your approach angle (up‑current into the seam). What matters less: running straight to the first bird without checking current, or fishing the center of the sand finger instead of the drop‑off.

When to commit — quick checklist
If you can get there on an early tide push and the water is calm enough to read bait and birds, commit. If the thermal has already chopped the slicks, or the inlet is rolling from a northerly swell, you’re often better served running farther to a canyon head.

Before you stop, confirm:

1. tide direction and strength — is there a push into the canyon?
2. visible bait on the fishfinder at the sand edge
3. birds or foamers holding a narrow band
4. a clean approach angle into the lee (not across or uphill)

Short, tactical water
This is short, tactical water — not a canyon head, not a long run. Read tide and bait first, then put the boat so a natural downhill presentation runs through the fish. If you get it right the action is tight, visible, and efficient; if you get it wrong, the pockets go quiet fast.
