# San Francisco, California

## Gate, Bar and Where the Shelf Starts
Look first at the Gate. The bay dumps through a narrow, turbulent throat onto a steep shelf—bar, current, and drop-off all packed into the first few miles. That geometry is the story: inside water, a mixed inner shelf, then a sharp outer ledge where canyons and hard edges begin to collect bait and fish.

In practice that means two kinds of trips out of the Gate. If the bar is behaving you can be on bait-rich inner-shelf marks in 20–50 minutes. If you want true blue-water structure—Farallones, Cordell, the outer ledges—plan on a few hours and a careful look at the tide/swell combo before you run. The Gate isn’t just a waypoint; it controls whether you fish tight to the headlands or commit to the banks.

## How Tides, Wind and Upwelling Organize Fish
The ocean out here is organized by three moving parts: wind-driven upwelling, the tidal squeeze at the Gate, and the occasional thermocline offshore. Summer is the classic setup—persistent NW wind, cold upwelled water and low cloud with a shallow, distinct thermocline farther out. Winter throws in southerly or westerly storms, bigger swell and warmer surface patches after fronts.

The Gate’s tidal flow reverses quickly and sculpts uphill and downhill currents along every point and ledge. Where that downhill current meets slower water you get seams and bait concentration. Clean water and visible bait show up most on moderate NW wind with a swell that gives surface structure without closing the bar. Strong onshore wind with an outgoing tide will dirty things fast; you’ll still have wide current seams, but the visibility and working-bird cues go down.

Operational rule: treat the Gate tide table like a run card. Slack and short windows of reduced current are when most boats choose to commit west.

## Two Operational Fisheries: Nearshore Structure vs Offshore Patches
San Francisco runs two distinct fisheries that rarely fish the same day under the same tactics. Each responds to different signals.

Nearshore demersals and kelp-edge fish
- Rockfish, lingcod, cabezon and halibut hold tight to kelp, rocky ledges and sand-to-rock transitions inside the shelf. These bites are structure-driven and can be steady year-round when bait stacks on the ledge. Work the downhill sides and keep gear that gets into crevices—slow pitch jigs, bait rigs and tight bottom contact are the payers.

Dungeness crab
- When the season’s open it’s a predictable economic bottom fishery. Pots and buoys shape small-boat routes around the bay mouth and points; navigation around gear is a routine consideration.

Salmon and coastal migratory fish
- Chinook show when bait (anchovy, squid) gets pushed against the shelf and a temperature edge sits near the break. They hang on the contour—trolling uphill with plugs and downriggers along the edge is the pattern when the bite is on.

Pelagics—albacore, bluefin and transient sharks
- Summer albacore show on warm surface pockets with working birds and surface bait 20–60 nm west and southwest of the Gate, usually along the thermocline over outer ledges. Bluefin are patchier but follow the same collision of warm water and dense bait. Threshers and makos turn up when the same bait balls and surface chaos occur. If you see birds, warm pocket and tight bait, you’re looking at the same playbook whether it’s tuna or a big shark.

The practical separation between a fishable day and a long search is alignment: a manageable Gate, a clear temperature break near the shelf, and bait concentrated on a hard edge with birds or sonar confirming. Lose two of those three and you’re searching more than fishing.

## How The Fleet Decides To Run
Locals split into two operating modes: short-hop headland boats and longer bank runs. Small boats tend to hang the shelf and headlands; big boats commit to the Farallones, Cordell and beyond when conditions let them through. Commercial gear—crab pots and gillnets—also shapes where small boats will troll or anchor.

A common decision flow runs like this:
1. Check the Gate—if the bar is closed or noisy, plan inside-water or nearshore trips only.
2. If the Gate is passable, listen for reports: surface temp at the break, working birds, and sonar bait stacks from boats already on the banks.
3. Commit when reports corroborate—one boat calling birds is noise; multiple boats calling birds plus temp at the break is usually enough to run.

Search patterns are methodical: grid the contour from inside-out, then run uphill trolls across seams; if nothing, run farther west. When several boats find a patch, the fleet disperses along the edge to avoid stepping on each other’s spread. The three variables captains watch first are surface temp, first-moving birds and the Gate tide—those decide whether the fleet skates the outer shelf or hangs on the headlands.

## Reading Bait, Current and Temperature—Tactical Notes
Success here is practical: find moving water and bait, then put the right presentation where fish expect it.

Current seams and tide push
- The Gate produces strong tidal pushes. Fish set on the downstream side of points and at the foot of ledges where downhill current meets slower water. For pelagics, present lures on an uphill troll so they run into the current. For bottom species, drift the downhill side with baits held just above the rock.

Temperature edges and clean water
- Tuna and salmon key to thermoclines. Look for color changes on the screen and surface temp pockets. When a warm pocket sits on the outer ledge and birds are working, tighten the spread: shallow and midwater offerings first, deep rigs second.

Bait concentration and sonar
- When bait shows stacked on the ledge, change from search to attack. For pelagics, send trolled iron and flat lines into the ball and keep speeds that trigger strikes. For salmon, hit the uphill troll with downriggers and plugs to cover the strike zone.

Bottom structure and demersal rigs
- Rockfish and lingcod live where rock meets relief. Favor tight bottom contact and jigs that get into crevices. On a drift, keep the boat slow enough to let baits sit in the bite window. Foots of ledges and sand-to-rock transitions are consistent holding zones.

Kelp and obstruction management
- Kelp patches hold bait and small fish year-round. Use float rigs and heavy single leaders around kelp; expect fouls and be ready to change angle, re-anchor or pick a new drift.

Weather and bar management
- Run plans around slack tide and short windows of reduced current at the Gate. If wind and swell exceed your tolerance, shift to inside-water marks rather than force long runs into dirty water.

Practical local rule: find the temp break on the outer ledge, bait stacked on that ledge, and working birds within a manageable run from the Gate—then commit and concentrate on uphill and downhill zones. If one of those signals is missing, tighten the radius and hunt the structure closer to the headlands.

## What Really Matters Out There
You can fish San Francisco from the kelp edge to the blue water, but the same three signals will beat guesswork every time: the Gate window, a temperature edge in the right place, and concentrated bait. Watch how the fleet reacts to those signals; their moves are the clearest indicator you’re on a real bite rather than chasing noise.
