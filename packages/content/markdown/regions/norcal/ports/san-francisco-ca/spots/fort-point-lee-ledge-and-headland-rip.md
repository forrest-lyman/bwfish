The water off Fort Point is the sort of first‑mile fishing most boats underuse. It’s close, repeatable, and dependent on a simple interaction: the Gate’s push makes a tight rip and downstream eddy, the headland and its downhill ledges compress bait into a narrow seam, and predators sit where that bait first hits structure. You can run a full day here without burning fuel if you read the tide angle and the lee pockets.

## Why the seam is tighter here than farther west
The Gate focuses a jet; the headland peels water off the point and creates a clear shear line and eddy on the downstream side. That focused push makes a seam that’s narrow and visible compared with the fuzzier seams out on the bank. Which side of the point hosts the seam depends on tide angle: a strong, steady Gate push produces a tight, productive rip; a soft or crosswise tide spreads the seam and dilutes bait into a broader mess.

Expect the seam to sit either close to the rocks or peel several hundred yards off depending on tide strength and swell. Slack windows—the short drops in current—are the practical moments to get set up. If the Gate is noisy, you move inside to tidy lee pockets instead of trying to wrestle the outer seam.

## The bottom and where bait collects
The bottom is a mix: isolated rock ledges and pinnacles stepping into sand gutters from roughly 30–120 ft. Kelp patches and paddies form in the lee and act like magnets. Bait gets funneled into the eddy, rides the seam and bumps the downhill faces. The ledges act like a conveyor—bait moves along the edge and predators pick it off.

Pay attention to these signs the seam is organizing:
- birds working low and tight to a narrow line
- sonar showing a band of bait hugging the slope
- foam or a surface rip aligned with the headland face
- kelp paddies clustered just off the downhill ledge

Micro note: fish usually hold where the bait first bumps the face—not always on the shallowest rock.

## Who you catch and how they behave
This is bait‑driven structure fishing.

- Rockfish and lingcod: consistent first‑mile targets. They sit on the ledge faces, boulder clusters and kelp necks. Vertical presentations—tight dropper rigs or slow, touchy jigs—pull them out of cracks and holes.
- Chinook (transient salmon): when migrants run through, the seam turns into a short staging lane. They rarely spread widely; they ride the contour in a band.
- Halibut and sand‑pocket fish: halibut take the sand gutters adjacent to the ledges, especially on outgoing tides when bait drops from rock into sand.

## Reading the Gate push and timing a drift
Everything here maps to tide angle and strength. A tidy Gate push organizes bait, tightens birds and fills your sonar. Cross‑choppy pushes or heavy onshore winds turn the seam into a wide, dirty mess—marks scatter and fish bury into messier water.

Operational points:
- Fish the downstream side of the headland relative to the Gate push.
- Use slack or short reduced‑current windows to set up drifts or short trolls.
- If the bar is noisy, don’t try to force the outer seam—work lee pockets and cleaner water closer in.

## How I work the ledges
This is practical, repeatable work. My approach centers on angle, contact, and moving when the pocket proves soft.

Drift and angle
- Cross the ledge uphill-to-downhill so your bait or jig passes through the strike zone where bait meets structure. Parallel drifts tend to miss the active strip.
- Start each pass above the structure and fish down through it. Small reefs and pinnacles need repeated starts.

Bottom rigs and jigging
- Rockfish/lingcod: tight single‑hook dropper rigs or vertical jigs are the staple. Expect to fish 30–120 ft.
- Jigs: 2–6 oz slow‑pitch jigs for the shallower ledges; heavier jigs for deeper ribs. Keep constant contact—lingcod live in cracks and respond to a touch‑and‑pause retrieve.

Salmon approach
- When salmon stage, the seam becomes a short uphill troll road. Troll across the seam uphill and be ready for bites on the first or second pass.
- Present plugs or downrigger baits into the current. Early in the window I set downriggers shallow to mid (20–60 ft) and adjust to what the sonar shows.

Kelp and leader management
- Float rigs and single heavy leaders when working near paddies. Kelp fouls gear fast; if kelp is killing the spread, move a few hundred yards—the bait may still be there but the gear won’t be.
- Use stout leaders around crevicey rock and paddies—lingcod and bigger rockfish will chew light knots.

Common tactical mistakes to avoid
- Anchoring into kelp instead of moving 100–200 yd to a cleaner drift line.
- Running the drift parallel to the face instead of crossing it uphill‑to‑downhill.
- Ignoring the Gate tide angle and fishing the wrong side of the headland.

## Signs the window is building
These are short, practical signals you can act on:
- birds tightening into a narrow line
- sonar showing bait stacked against the ledge
- a foam/colour seam running parallel to the face
- a steady Gate push with a readable downstream eddy

When these come together the place goes from fair to useful fast.

## How pressure changes the bite
Locals treat Fort Point two ways: as the day’s meat or the contingency plan. When the fleet shows, expect fish to change quickly.

Typical reactions to pressure:
- fish sink tight into cracks and stop rising into the column
- foamers (top-water feeders) refuse repeated casts after two boats
- salmon move deeper and tighten on downriggers when the top water is blown out

Two corroborated radio calls—“birds tight off Fort Point” and “Gate slack—Fort Point set”—will get the run started. One call is noise; two makes the fleet move.

## Hazards and constraints
The Gate’s current and the Rip can be sharp. Don’t overcommit when bar reports are marginal. Practical cautions:
- crab pots and commercial traffic are constant hazards; watch for buoys and transient crab floats inside the lee
- fog hides birds and the fleet; when visibility drops fish slower, rely on sonar, and keep radio calls tight
- when the bar is noisy, scale back to kelp necks and sand gutters instead of grinding the outer seam

Final read
Fort Point isn’t flashy. It’s dependable when the Gate lines up and the headland seam organizes bait. You can run a tidy day of vertical work and short uphill troll salmon checks inside the first mile. When the Gate is sloppy the same water becomes a guessing game—watch the tide angle, work the kelp necks and sand gutters, and move when the pocket shuts down.
