## Why this neck matters

Sutro’s flank — the water between Lands End and the Cliff House — is small but reliably productive when conditions shut down the Gate or the shelf. The geometry is the thing: a headland that makes tidy downhill seams, a string of kelp necks and detached paddies that trap bait, and nearby rock fingers that give lingcod and rockfish ambush points. It’s not a broad bank; it’s concentrated, pressure‑sensitive, and repeats when tide and bait line up.

Start here when the big plan gets messy. The patterns are short and sharp: tide angle, paddy placement, and whether bait is stacked.

## How the tide and headland move the water

Observation first: current here doesn’t behave like open shelf current. It’s a tight choreography of Gate-driven tide bending around Lands End and the headland deflecting the flow.

- On a tide push you get a narrow uphill face at the headland and a downhill sweep past the kelp. That downhill sweep creates a downstream gutter where paddies and debris collect.
- Even a modest NW swell will pin paddies on the lee side; onshore wind breaks them apart. The same swell matters a lot for how clean the pockets stay.
- Water color and pockets change quick. In fog you’ll often see cleaner pockets on sonar before your eyes confirm anything.

Put simply: a tidally driven downhill sweep across the kelp line with a lee pocket downstream is when bait compresses and station‑holding predators get predictable.

## What hides under the paddies

You won’t always get drama on the surface. The paddies attract small anchovy, juvenile sardine, paddy fish and shrimp‑stage critters, usually shallow — often inside the top 30–60 ft. That drives everything below.

Observe first, then act:

- Birds are blunt instruments here; they hit a paddy briefly and leave. Don’t mistake a quick bird show for sustained bait work. Sonar showing a rounded bait ball beneath a paddy is the truth.
- Predators treat the kelp edge like a conveyor: bait drifts past and lingcod tuck under paddies while rockfish hug faces and cracks.
- Depth matters. If bait is shallow, your bite window is tight. Surface presentations sometimes work, but most productive work is vertical into the bite zone.

## How the spot behaves through a tide

When it fishes: as the tide pushes (or right after slack) bait stacks beneath paddies and along rock fringes. You’ll see marks climb on the sonar; fish get tight to cover — lingcod in holes under paddies, rockfish on faces and in small cracks.

When it’s dead: paddies can be visible but empty, wind has scattered the line, or the fleet has pressured the best edges. Strong onshore wind turns paddies from concentrators into dispersants and muddies the water. The paddies that held bait in calm conditions can be worthless in a blow.

Micro-observation: a busy-looking paddy can be empty. Trust the screen.

## Fleet patterns and how to run the line

Locals treat Sutro like a first‑mile contingency.

- Small boats use it for short days when the Gate barbles or a shelf run isn’t practical.
- If the Farallones or outer shelf are firing, Sutro is quiet. When the Gate closes, the fleet mills here.
- Good boats move fast: check a paddy, mark bait, make a short vertical pass, and shift before the kelp fouls the spread.

Tactical read: if one paddy shows bait on the screen, set on the downstream edge and fish the next two or three paddies down the line — bait tends to migrate along the string.

## Presentations that work — vertical and float rigs

This is short‑range vertical work. Presentations must get into the bite window cleanly and not hang in the kelp.

Start with verticals: tight, punchy jigs in the 2–6 oz range. Drop to the face, short hops, pause. Lingcod often eats on the pause or on that first lift into cover.

Float and bait rigs: small live anchovy or sardine under a small float fished on the paddy edge keeps bait off the canopy but in the strike zone. Use short leaders and single heavy leaders to survive kelp and rock.

A useful compressed setup:
- 2–4 oz pencil or small cigar float
- 18–30 in 30–50 lb leader
- 1/0–3/0 single hook for live bait
- Quick‑release clips for speedy retrieval

A few setup notes from the boat:
- Keep leaders short. Long leaders hang in the kelp canopy and kill hookups.
- For bottom contact on rockfish, run tight to the foot of the ledge, not dragging across the top.
- If the paddy line has surface bait and birds peel, short troll runs with small irons or feather rigs can trigger fast surface strikes — uncommon, but handy when it shows.

## When the kelp fouls the spread

Kelp fouling is the limiter. Expect fouls; plan around them.

- Work one paddy at a time. Retrieve quickly and redeploy two to three boat lengths down to avoid tangles with the next raft.
- If fouling escalates, change angle — drift slightly offshore so baits ride the trailing edge instead of cutting through the canopy.
- The first five minutes of a calm window usually give the cleanest retrieves; repeated passes pull weed into the spread.

Common fouling signals:
- paddies shift with wind and your next drift angle will foul faster
- two clean drops and you’re likely to have weed in the second retrieve — move

## When to use Sutro — and when to leave

Use it when:
- the Gate is noisy or closed and you need a practical day
- there’s a short slack or tide turn that will form a downstream pocket
- fog obscures bird and temperature cues and you want close‑in sonar work
- the shelf search goes blind and you need reliable bait and hardbottom chance

Leave it when:
- strong onshore wind disperses paddies and fouls the water
- the fleet has pressured the neck within the hour and marks are flat
- paddies are widely spaced with no downstream sweep — bait is likely off the edge

Signs the window is building:
- rounded bait cloud on sonar beneath a paddy
- birds briefly working a single paddy then leaving
- marks rising on the face as tide begins to push
- downstream gutter holding small debris and foam

## Common mistakes and the quick checklist

Most mistakes are tactical and avoidable: running the same paddy too long, using long leaders that hang in the canopy, ignoring tide angle, or casting surface offerings when bait is deep.

If you want a short operational flow, follow this order:
1. Check the Gate. If the bar is foul, plan Sutro instead of the shelf.
2. Run to the downstream side of the kelp line on the tide push.
3. Sonar the paddies — look for rounded bait balls under the canopy.
4. Drop vertical jigs and float rigs on the paddy edge; keep retrieves short.
5. Move after two clean attempts or when fouling increases.

## Final note

Sutro is honest water. It won’t give the open-shelf drama, but it will give short, repeatable bites when big plans fail. Fish the angle of attack: tide, paddy placement, and quick, clean presentations. When those three line up a small neck will fish like a lot more water.
