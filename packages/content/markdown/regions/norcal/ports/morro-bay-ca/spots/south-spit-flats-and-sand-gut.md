What makes this place work is plain: a shallow sand tongue off the south spit funnels water and bait into a narrow scour channel that runs between flat sand and the first nearshore rock. You get steady relief — flats in the 10–30 ft range, the gut scours down to 30–60 ft — with pockets and a few small holes where halibut tuck and feed. The bar outside the spit is part of the system; whether you can run the gut or have to stick to the lee seams depends on how tidy that bar is.

## How the sand‑gut organizes bait and halibut

The gut is a conveyor. On the ebb water peels through the trough and bait lanes form along the current seams. Halibut orient into the sweep and face downhill; they hold on the edge of the trough or in little depressions where the current slows. When bait is moving cleanly down the gut the fish will show as slow, low arches on the bottom and you’ll see the occasional mud cloud or tail‑flash when a fish turns on a bait.

That pattern — bait lane, steady sweep, halibut facing current — is what you chase here. When any one of those pieces breaks down, the bite shifts off the gut or shuts down.

## Signs the window is building

Watch the sonar and the current first; birds are secondary here.

- steady strips or small schools moving down the gut on sonar  
- consistent downhill current along the gut edges  
- slow, low arches hugging the bottom (halibut marks)  
- intermittent mud clouds or tail‑flashes on the sweep

If you see those things come together you’re looking at a practical window.

## Tide, the bar, and why timing matters

The run starts and ends at the bar. The ebb that holds the gut together is mid‑ebb through the first hour of the run down — not the last sloppy hour when current peels into pockets and the sweep becomes ragged. Slack kills the motion the fish are keyed to.

A few practical notes:

- a falling tide with a west swell widens the breakers; if a strong west swell overlaps the outgoing tide the bar closes the window.  
- when the bar is sloppy shorten the run: fish the outer sand/rock seams on the lee side instead of risking a late crossing.  
- a clean, light ebb with a calm bar and soft wind makes the drift manageable and the halibut willing to chase.

Respect the bar forecast. A green run can go bad in 30 minutes once the wind and swell combine.

## Bait and presentation for a soft, mobile drift

Natural bait is the currency here: live anchovy/anchoveta, fresh squid strips, small sardine pieces taken from the bay. Halibut on these flats prefer a light, soft presentation — they’ll follow a bait more readily than take a dead, pinned offering under heavy lead.

How I set up:

- use a light running sinker and a long leader so the bait can swim naturally on the drop  
- main line light enough to let the bait move; leaders around 18–30 lb fluoro depending on wind and visibility  
- sinker 2–6 oz depending on drift speed — heavier only when the sweep pushes you off bottom  
- keep drag light; let the fish move. Tight drag and heavy pressure cost fish where there’s nearby structure.

The goal is to present a freely swimming bait that reads natural in the current seams, not a nailed‑to‑bottom jig.

## When to fish the gut and when to push to the seam

Fish the gut when bait is moving down the trough and sonar shows a clear bait lane. The gut organizes the energy; halibut ride that energy and orient to it.

Push to the adjacent sand/rock seam when one of these happens:

- the bar is marginal or the ebb is erratic  
- birds and bait have moved outside the first mile or outside the trough  
- sonar shows bait leaving the gut and lighting up the seam instead

If bait disappears from the gut and appears on the seam, move immediately — the fish follow the bait, not your plan.

## Common operational mistakes I see

These are the ones that burn time and fish:

- waiting for full ebb. The last hour often goes sloppy and unpredictable.  
- running too heavy. Heavy leads kill action on a shallow flat.  
- anchoring in the wrong spot. Too far in and you lose drift; too close to the gut and you stack on other boats.  
- ignoring the bar forecast. A marginal bar will change everything quickly.

Fix those and your odds improve even on an ordinary day.

## How the local fleet treats this water

Small boats and local skippers treat the south spit gut as first‑mile, high‑probability halibut water when the bar is closed or the day is short. They run early, get a steady drift, and keep it simple.

Party boats and boats unwilling to risk a marginal bar tend to favor the outer seams or nearby ledges; they’ll only push the gut when bar reports are green. Information moves quietly — one consistent report of bait working down the gut and the fleet reorganizes fast. When the bite consolidates it eats pressure: fish tuck into holes or stop feeding if boats pile up.

## Tactical checklist for a controlled drift

- run early on light swell; aim for mid‑ebb through the first hour  
- set up downstream of the gut edge and drift across the trough at a slow, consistent speed  
- light main line, 18–30 lb fluoro leader, 2–6 oz running sinker depending on drift speed  
- live bait or fresh strips on a long leader; give the bait freedom to swim off the bottom  
- watch the sounder for mud clouds and slow bottom arches — those are often the hook points  
- be ready to shorten the drift and recast quickly when marks tighten

Use the checklist as a sequence: find the bait, set the drift, present light, and react fast when the sounder changes.

## Short tactical notes on transitions and bite shape

- as the tide fades fish will close into patchy holes. When marks tighten and go deep, slow everything and pick the bait off the bottom rather than forcing a fast presentation.  
- birds are a weak indicator here; let sonar and current be your lead instruments.  
- when the gut goes quiet but the sand/rock seam shows noise expect single fish, not balls. Shift to a slightly heavier sinker, shorten the leader, and chop or jig the bait lightly.

This place isn't glamour water; it's predictable when the tide, bar and bait line up. Respect the bar, fish the ebb, keep your presentation light and mobile, and move with the bait when the basin shifts. That's the practical rhythm that takes fish here.
