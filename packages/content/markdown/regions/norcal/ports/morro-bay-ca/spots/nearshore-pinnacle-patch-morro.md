The Patch

A tight string of shallow-to-mid pinnacles and broken rock sitting a few miles west of the Morro Bar. Nothing here towers like an offshore knob—most tops run 30–120 ft over bases that sit between about 60 and 220 ft—but together they form the first real hardbottom the coast offers after the sand spit. It reads like a broken fence: knobs, holes and short ledges that catch current and pin bait where the shelf otherwise slopes away.

Why you check it first is simple: it’s close, it’s fishy, and it anchors bait when the bigger shelf is pushed off. When the bait stacks here you get rockfish and lingcod without a long run; when the bait peels off the patch it’s a clear signal to go or go home.

How the pinnacles organize bait

Observation first: bait concentrates on uphill faces and in the narrow seams formed where flow peels off each knob. You’ll see three consistent patterns.

- Downhill seams form on the flanks. Incoming or along‑shore flow peels off the knobs and creates a narrow downhill conveyor where sand eels, squid and small forage ride the current.
- Pinnacles act as anchors. Bait stacks on the uphill faces and in overhang holes; predators patrol the uphill side and the deeper gutters between pieces.
- Temp/color lines often pin to the outermost knobs. In spring upwelling a cool band hugs the patch, with a warmer skin outside that corrals surface life.

Sonar usually leads the birds here: dense, stacked clouds sitting right over relief before the birds tighten into working arcs.

When the patch fishes (and when it doesn’t)

Productive
A tide that moves across the knobs—either an outgoing that peels across the faces or a push that creates a downhill sweep—plus bait that sits tight on the uphill side. Birds working low and sonar showing bait at 60–150 ft is the classic short‑run signal. You can park up and fish vertically; the bites often come off the stack.

Patchy
Steady upwelling with heavy NW wind makes the water noisy and frayed. The patch may still hold bait but the birds vanish and fish hang deep. You switch to vertical work and heavier jigs, and count on slower returns.

Dead
A single transient bird event, a cleaned slick with bait scattered, or a steady offshore wind that strips the surface—those are the quick kills. The sounder can look alive and the bite be dead; don’t flog thin marks.

Micro-observation
The bait stacks on the uphill face; the fish sit just inside the downhill flow. That’s where the hooks meet teeth.

Tactics and setup

Work vertically and keep your presentation into the seam. This feature is short and forgiving—anchor pulls and tight work win more often than broad covers.

Anchor placement
Anchor slightly uphill of a knob or over the deeper trough beside the piece. Present baits into the downhill current; drift angle across a face matters more than dropping dead center on top. If you anchor on the downhill side you’re presenting away from the fish.

Rod spread
Keep one rod shallow and one deep. Stretch the vertical column with different leaders or staggered dropper rigs so you cover the stack from top to bottom.

Vertical jig specifics
Heavy vertical jigs with short, sharp lifts on the drop get most hookups; many bites come on the fall. Fish the holes and overhangs—stop the jig over deep cuts and work slow returns with pauses. If bait is shallow, shorten the drop and use lighter jigs to keep the lure in the upper half of the stack.

Sonar and sounder reads

Look for dense bait clouds hugging the top of the pinnacle with brighter, tighter targets just beneath—that’s the bite column. Marks that compress and then thin quickly when boats arrive are pressure‑sensitive; move a short distance and re‑check the uphill face. If the sonar shows thin, spread marks, slow down and work vertically rather than high‑speed covering.

Bar state and day planning

Calm bar, short run: the patch is a staging ground. Boats will sit longer, make more anchor pulls, and experiment with vertical patterns as bait rides shallow.

Marginal/sloppy bar: it becomes the fallback. Boats shorten trips, focus on deeper bits and heavier jigging, and avoid surface trolling. Windows are shorter and your tolerance for long searches drops.

Afternoon wind: bites compress early. Run early, find a working knob, and plan to pull off if wind rips and the bar gets messy.

What usually matters more
- Tide sweep across the knobs
- Whether bait sits tight to relief
- A persistent downhill seam

What usually matters less
- A lone bird or a single surface boil

Fleet behavior and pressure

Locals treat the patch like a pocket watch: if it’s holding bait they stay; if not, they move. Expect a short crowd on tidy mornings. When boats anchor directly on top, schools sink or scatter—anchor just uphill and keep drifts tight. Foamers that refuse repeated casts often mean fish are pinned deep; switch to heavier slow jigs or bait drops. Boats that arrive late and linger can tear the window down—get in early, fish efficient patterns, and move if the marks thin.

Common mistakes

- Anchoring on the downhill side and presenting baits away from the current.
- Chasing every bird; focus on birds that track across the patch and match a stable sonar noise.
- Waiting for perfect light; this is about getting on the stack and working the column, not sight casting.

If the patch dies

It usually dies cleanly: bait scatters, the sonar thins, and birds lift off. Don’t flog a flat mark—run the shelf edge or hop to a nearby ledge. The decision to commit offshore often starts here: when the patch loses bait and the temp map shows a warm finger west, that’s your signal for a longer run.

Short, practical close
Fish the uphill side. Anchor into the seam. When the patch holds bait you can do the day without a long run; when it doesn’t, you’ll know fast.
