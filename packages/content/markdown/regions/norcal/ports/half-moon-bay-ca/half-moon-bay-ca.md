# Half Moon Bay, California

## Why Half Moon Bay is the short run to mixed water
Half Moon Bay sits on a narrow shelf where deep water comes in fast. You don’t have to run forever to find hard edges, pinnacles and canyon cuts — within about 5–20 nm the bottom switches from soft sand to structure. That quick transition is the practical reason boats launch here: you can be fishing varied water without burning all day on fuel.

The entrance is exposed. Northwest and west swell over shallow angles throws steep, closing sets across the bar and around the jetty. When that happens most boats either stage well inside the harbor or wait for a lull. Treat the harbor as the last sheltered point before you commit to offshore transit: plan your gear, your livewell and your route there. The first useful bait and meaningful marks generally show beyond the 40–100-fathom line; whether you push north toward San Francisco canyons or south toward Monterey tracks comes down to swell direction, season and where the warm pockets sit.

## How swell, upwelling and eddies define the window
The usable window out of Half Moon is the result of three working patterns: summer upwelling with persistent NW wind and fog, winter storm swell from the NW–W, and a southward California Current that spawns eddies and shear off the shelf. Watch how these three interact — they tell you when you can go and where the fish will stack.

Observations you’ll use every trip:
- Northwest or west swell over shallow angles makes a steep bar and dangerous entrance. Small onshore wind plus any significant swell is a red flag for the run.
- In late spring through early fall upwelling pushes cold, nutrient-rich green water close to shore. Warm pockets and eddies will sit offshore or on the break; those pockets are the places tuna and bluefin show, while salmon prefer the cooler rips nearershore.
- The dominant flow is generally southward, but local tidal pushes and eddy-shedding create seams and accelerations along ledges. Current hitting hard bottom produces “uphill” bites — fish stack where current speeds up off a ledge.
- Summer fog hides birds and weedlines until the sun clears it. When visibility is low, rely more on sonar, temp checks and listening to your mates on the radio than on sighting birds.

In short: the swell/wind combo decides whether you can run. Upwelling, eddies and ledges decide where bait concentrates and where you should fish once you’re out.

## When and where the fish show
Everything here follows bait, temperature edges and current over structure. Species change with season, but the pattern is consistent: warm pockets + structure + bait = action; cool rips + bait = salmon; hard bottom + current = rockfish/lingcod.

Seasonal patterns to lean on:
- Summer–early fall (June–October): Salmon and albacore are the main show. Salmon set up in rips and current seams close to shore, often 3–15 nm. Albacore and occasional bluefin punch into warm patches and sit on sloping hard bottom beyond the upwelling band; they key to bait balls and working birds.
- Year-round nearshore bottomfish: Rockfish and lingcod hold the hard bottoms, pinnacles and ledges in roughly the 20–120-fathom range. When weather pushes boats inshore these grounds are reliable.
- Late fall through spring: Dungeness crab dominates commercial effort. Expect pots, buoys and denser gear in the typical crab lanes — the fleet routes adapt around that.
- Squid and baitfish: When market squid or dense schooling bait are present nearshore, predators compress tight to shore and the run shortens dramatically.

Presence alone doesn't win the day. Look for the intersection of bait concentration, the temperature edge and a current seam. Two of three gives you a pattern you can work; all three aligned gives you a fishable run.

## How the fleet chooses its runs
Locals make conservative decisions out of necessity. You’ll see the same decision tree every time: birds and bait first, temperature second, structure last. Without at least two signals most boats stay inside or on the close edge of the shelf.

Common behaviors I watch:
- Commit thresholds: Long runs happen after birds are working, sonar shows bait, or a warm pocket is confirmed with a thermometer or chart. One signal rarely sends the fleet past the 40–100-fathom line.
- Search patterns: Boats commonly start uphill trolling, scanning for weedlines and birds. If birds appear the spread goes shallow and livebait becomes an option. If birds are absent they’ll push to likely canyon cuts and set deeper trollers or drift for salmon depending on the season.
- Information flow: Sightings move fast on VHF and working groups form quickly around persistent marks. When a port is hot runs cluster; when it's off boats fan out chasing different canyons and eddies.
- Commercial gear: Crab pots and netting compress some nearshore lanes during their seasons. Expect the fleet to shift lanes to avoid gear and to honor closures.

If you want to predict where boats will be, listen for birds, bait marks and temp calls — those three determine whether guys stay inshore, sit the shelf edge, or run for deeper canyons.

## Tactics that consistently work from the edge
The single most reliable principle here is to fish the convergence: current seam, temperature edge and hard bottom. When two line up you have something actionable. When all three do, you have a fishable run.

Practical, repeatable tweaks:
- Read the seam before you commit. If a current seam crosses a ledge and bait is stacked on it, set a spread to cover both shallow and medium depths. Seams compress bait — fish will often stack on the uphill side.
- Mark temperature breaks and run passes across them. Tuna species key to warm pockets; salmon prefer cool-water rips. Run uphill/downhill across the break rather than cruising parallel and hoping to meet bait.
- Work birds and weedlines first. Birds circling tight over bait balls are the clearest signal you’ll get. Troll a mixed spread — shallow plugs, flatfalls — to intercept fish coming up from depth. Don’t abandon solid bird work for a lone temp reading unless the birds go quiet.
- Adjust trolling to the species. Albacore and smaller tunas prefer medium speeds with smaller plugs or cedar-style lures. Bluefin, when present and deeper in clean water, respond better to slower-trolled larger lures and livebait off downriggers or flat-lines.
- Bottom fishing is simple geometry: anchor or drift over hard edges and ledges for rockfish and lingcod. Vertical presentations and heavy jigs work on steep contour. Avoid drifting soft sand unless you’re specifically marking isolated hard patches.
- Livebait and wells matter. Keep livebait in shaded or warm pockets when sea temps vary and match bait size to the target. When bait is scarce, downsize offerings and use chum or scent to hold fish.
- Bar and transit decisions are conservative. Stage inside and wait for a lull if the swell looks sketchy. Daylight crossings are safer in fog months. Plan extra fuel and time for slow transits when swell and current are working against you.

Fish the intersections: seams concentrate bait, temp edges concentrate species, bottom concentrates the hold. When you see two of those signals tighten your spread and cover the missing piece; when you see all three, you run a different playbook and bring the heavier gear.
