What this place looks like and why it matters

The Inner Flats & Channel Cuts are the close sand-and-gutter country just outside Pillar Point: broad, shallow flats cut by scour channels, small sand gutters and the occasional cobble pocket. It isn’t one seam you run down — it’s a system of mouths and uphill faces that repeat on every drift. You fish it when the bar looks marginal, the run is short, or you need a quick, tide-driven score. Short transit, quick checks, and properly timed drifts turn a marginal day into something worth fishing.

How the water behaves here

On the productive bits you’re typically in 3–15 fathoms: flat sand with deeper gutters and cut mouths. Tide is the driver — not wind, not birds. A clean, forceful flood will shove bait into cut mouths; a firm ebb peels bait off the flats and funnels it down the gutters. Slack is mostly dead. The state of the bar changes everything: when the bar is up or breaking, boats stage inside and the inner cuts become primary; when the bar clears you can push for the outer ledges. Summer fog will hide bird sign and wake up the sounder as your primary search tool.

Why fish use the cuts

Observation: bait holds on the uphill faces and piles at the mouths; predators stage exactly where the current forces prey to compress.

Explanation: sand eels, small anchovy-type schools and episodic squid ride the seams. The cuts act like a conveyor — bait lifts off the flat, funnels through the mouth and becomes concentrated where predators can ambush. Halibut will pick the gutters and mouths to sit with their heads downhill; migrating salmon (early chinook and summer coho) will hold along tidally driven seams on the uphill faces. When squid show, everything tightens to the nearshore and action spikes.

Signs a window is forming

Watch for these practical signs — they show up before the birds do:

- sonar marks stacked on the uphill side of a cut
- short, directional boils along a gutter mouth
- a tide forecast that promises a strong, clean push (flood or ebb), not a weak sloppy run
- squid or small bait holding tight to the flats

Reading those signs gets you on the drift before the fleet converges.

When it fishes and when it doesn’t

This spot is best on a tidy flood or ebb that creates a clear flow through a channel mouth, or right after a tide turn when bait is being pushed off the flats. It’s also reliable the morning after a clean bar window, when bait has reorganized nearshore. It dies on slack, long onshore wind that muddles the seams, or when heavy pot fields compress lanes. Even on marginal days a well-timed cross-drift through a cut will often produce — not every run, but often enough to justify the stop.

How I run a cut

Observation first: the productive angle is across the uphill face into the mouth, not running straight along the flat. I set a controlled drift that angles across the face, pauses as the bait reaches the mouth, then rides down the gutter where halibut and salmon commute.

Drift setup (sequence matters)
1. Start uphill of the cut so you cross the uphill face rather than chase the mouth.
2. Set a spread covering near and mid columns (0–20 ft and 20–60 ft).
3. Cross the face, hold into the mouth long enough for baits to be in the strike zone, then run the gutter.
4. Adjust drift speed so baits stay in the strike zone during the tide surge.

Anchoring and other passes: anchor when you get a narrow slack window or a promising pocket — position so current comes across the face rather than down it. When salmon are staging, slow uphill/downhill troll passes through the seam with shallow plugs or spoons; keep the speed down and the presentations shallow. When live bait is present a short chum lane or one-to-two live baits on a short drop converts more bites. Live bait in shallow, cool water dies fast — keep them in shaded wells and rotate often.

Bait and presentation cues

Observe how the bait is holding and match it simply. Small, lively bait on a short drop for halibut. Slow, shallow plugs or spoons for salmon that are low in the column. If squid show, downsize hooks and add scent/chum — predators will compress and get aggressive; shorter drops and smaller offerings pay.

Common mistakes I see

- Fishing slack as if the tide doesn’t matter — these cuts are tide-driven, not magical.
- Running straight along the flats; you want to cross the cut and work into the mouth.
- Expecting birds to point the spot — fog or tight bait pushes mean the sounder and tide clock are the reliable indicators.
- Overstaying a dead cut once the tide shifts — the pattern changes fast; move.

Pressure, fleet behavior and hazards

This is short-run, local water. When the bar is dicey the inner cuts get busy — VHF reports spread fast and working boats form quickly. Experienced skippers split: some work the flats and push outward if the bar allows; others stage inside and wait for cleaner signs. Expect pot buoys and lines during crab season; they compress lanes and require cautious routing.

Micro-observations that matter

- The cuts live by tide. When bait pins at a mouth, halibut tend to orient head downhill and bite short.
- Fog hides the birds; on gray mornings the sounder and tide clock tell you more than the binoculars.
- A steady set of sonar marks with short directional boils is a better indicator than a single surface boil.

Why check this first on a Half Moon Bay trip

Practical reason: when the entrance or swell makes a longer run risky, or when time and tide favor a short play, the Inner Flats & Channel Cuts are the pragmatic stop. They won’t replace a good outer ledge on a firm thermal edge, but they’re repeatable. Read the tide, watch the sounder, respect the bar—and the cuts will pay off more often than not.
