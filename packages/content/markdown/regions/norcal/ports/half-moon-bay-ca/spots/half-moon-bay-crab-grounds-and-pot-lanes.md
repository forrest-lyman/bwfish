The Half Moon Bay crab grounds aren't a single mark on the chart. They’re a seasonal pattern of buoys, pot strings, and the small hard patches and sand gutters that hold Dungeness on the inside shelf. You learn to read around them because they change where and how you fish: when lanes are dense the fleet moves, when tides clean up the flats the crabs come active, and when hauling starts the whole neighborhood rearranges.

Where the lanes sit
The working footprint lives on the shallow portion of the shelf — roughly the first 5–20 miles out. Structurally it’s long sand pockets, intermittent shell beds, and little knobs of rock or ledge that break up the flats. The hard stuff is usually small and scattered; what matters is the gutter lines and the way tide squeezes bait along them. On the surface the buoys are the dominant feature — they show before the bottom does.

Why crabs and boats share this water
Observation first: pots sit where hauling is practical and catch rates are steady. Practically that means nearable water, manageable tides, and flats that hold organic material. The crabs follow food and shelter — sand gutters and shell beds collect detritus and small crustaceans, and routine tidal flushing creates short windows when crabs are actively feeding and grabable. Commercial gear gets placed where those bite windows line up with a reasonable run back to the harbor and safe hauling.

What lives here
Dungeness crab is the reason the lanes exist. You’ll also see rockfish and lingcod around the scattered hard patches, occasional halibut on sand gutters during tide change, and salmon staging along edges when bait and tidally-driven seams compress forage near the lanes.

Current and water movement to watch
Tide is the primary driver. The flood and ebb move clean water and food across the flats; high catchability for crabs often lines up with clean tidal exchange. Wind can push weed and surface debris into the buoys in light summer conditions, which concentrates bait near the surface. Nearshore upwelling bands can tighten bait close to structure and sometimes pull salmon into the same corridors the boats want to troll. Read the tide first, the wind second.

How the spot behaves through a day
When it fishes: tight tide changes, clean water after a wind lull, and calm enough conditions for haulers to run a regular schedule. That steady hauling rhythm is useful for you too — halibut and rockfish drifts often work best on a stable tide.

When it's dead: heavy onshore wind, rough NW swell, or dense pot coverage. If the bar is rough and boats are forced inside, hauling drops and so does the active window; likewise, lots of hauling can scatter bait and quiet the area after a short spike.

Fleet behavior and what that teaches you
The buoys move with business needs more than with fish. Commercial boats haul on schedules dictated by tides, seasons, and windows; the strings follow hauling patterns. Recreational skippers respond predictably — avoid dense fields, shift to adjacent ledges, or run outside the string. A radio call about “string at X” will clear an area faster than any thermograph.

Micro-observation: the pots show up first on the horizon. The fish often show up around the pots later, but not always. Don’t assume the presence of gear means bad fishing — sometimes hauling compresses bait and creates a short-term spike. Assume instead that gear changes the timing and the drift you can safely run.

Reading buoys and choosing lanes
Approach a lane like you would any piece of structure: identify the buoys, note spacing, and decide if you can safely and effectively present to the adjacent gutter or ledge. If pots are wide-spaced you can often thread a vertical or jig drift between strings. If buoys are dense, leave it alone and fish the next gutter or small hard patch. Always give hauling boats lots of room; tangled gear will ruin a day.

Tactical notes (practical, not theoretical)
- Navigation and safety: approach new fields at idle, ID the GPS position of every buoy you can see, and keep a sharp lookout. Foul lines are real and costly. Keep a radio watch when hauling is happening; haulers maneuver unpredictably during a set.
- Drift control: run short, controlled drifts across sand pockets early and late in the tide. For halibut and rockfish keep presentations vertical and present baits slightly upstream so they fall naturally into the fishes’ zone.
- Troll and salmon work: when salmon use the lanes they’re usually hitting bait squeezed by current fingers. Troll uphill passes across seams; shallow spoons and flatfalls near working birds are often a higher-percentage play than deep spreads around buoys.
- Timing: avoid peak hauling windows unless you want the bustle. Hauling compresses the working window and scatters bait; your best near‑lane work is either before hauling starts or off the lane on a nearby ledge.
- Gear awareness: keep lines short and don’t deploy pots or long slow gear inside crab fields. Lost rigs are expensive and entanglement has real legal consequences.

Signs the lane is worth checking
- tidy strings of buoys with sensible spacing (not mid‑haul chaos)
- visible, clean tidal movement across the flats
- birds working low near buoys or in adjacent gutters
- sonar marks of bait hugging a ledge off the lane

Common mistakes I see
- running wide‑open drifts through tight buoy fields
- assuming pots mean the area is dead — sometimes hauling concentrates bait
- abandoning persistent birds for a pot field when the birds are clearly working

Hazards, practicalities, and red flags
Fog hides buoys — in low visibility slow down, use radar and sonar, and prioritize safety over a short opportunity. Check current regulations and notices before you run near lanes; seasonal closures, reporting requirements, and buoy rules change and you don’t want a surprise. Watch hauling boats’ sterns and tenders — they’ll swing or drop pots unpredictably during a set.

What usually matters more than your snapshot
- tide timing
- buoy density
- where the nearest hard patch sits

What usually matters less
- exact buoy spacing you saw yesterday
- a single satellite temp read without birds, sonar, or tide confirmation

Final note
The crab grounds are part natural and part human pattern. Learn to read the buoys like you read current and birds. When lanes, tides, and bait line up you can run efficient nearshore work out of Half Moon Bay; when they don’t, the lanes simply steer the fleet and the fish elsewhere. Adjust your drift and timing, not your confidence.
