The Pillar Point jetty and bar are the gate out of Half Moon Bay. It’s the shallow first mile where onshore swell, shifting sand and the long jetty sculpt a seam that peels off the coast. When that tongue is boiling the harbor is effectively closed; when it cleans up you can be on real water in minutes. The state of the bar is the single most immediate decision you’ll make here.

How the jetty and bar shape bait and current
The entrance is simple geometry with complicated effects. The long jetty and exposed channel create a predictable funnel: water funnels, bait concentrates, and a surface seam peels down the outside face. Shallow sand and nearshore gutters step quickly to harder edges beyond the surf; within a few miles the bottom moves from flat sand to mixed sand, isolated rock and the start of steeper ledges. That rapid change in relief is why you get halibut in the gutters, rockfish on the isolated rocks and salmon staging along the seam.

When the bar is calm it’s short, efficient water—meaning frequent returns, quick checks and the chance to fish a useful window without committing to the outer shelf.

When it fishes
Pillar Point isn’t a long-haul blue‑water stop by itself. It’s a gate that sets the table. Use it as short-run, high-frequency water.

- Halibut: Sand gutters and near-jetty sand pockets line up with tide pushes. The best drifts happen on a steady flood or ebb that pins sand-flat bait against the channel edge.
- Salmon: Early-season and migrating fish stage on the peel of the jetty seam and in the first channel cuts. They tend to show in a narrow depth band and often come with a bait line and birds.
- Rockfish and nearshore bottomfish: Isolated hard patches and the outer jetty face hold rockfish and lingcod on tide changes, especially when you can make vertical drifts or controlled anchors.

Reading the morning window
You’ll know a useful window when three things line up: seam, bait and birds. Watch the surface for a tidy peel rather than random, boiling sets; listen to the birds and check sonar for stacked bait low in the first mile. Live bait that calms in the well right after clearing the channel is a practical sign—those fish haven’t been hammered by traffic yet.

Signs that a window is forming
- Birds tightening over a narrow surface line near the jetty.
- A clean boil or peel off the outer tongue instead of random breakers.
- Sonar showing bait stacked low in the first mile.
- Live bait behaving calmly in the well after clearing the channel.

Running the gate — transit checklist
The entrance changes fast. These steps are about timing and patience.

1. Wait for daylight and a lull if fog hides texture across the bar. Seeing the sea state is worth the delay.
2. If the bar looks marginal, stage inside and wait for slack or a small offshore breeze. The entrance can close quickly with a NW swell.
3. Run the entrance on the tide that minimizes cross sets. A strong outgoing on a short NW swell is the worst combo.
4. If you commit, plan for a slower, fuel‑conscious return—conditions can double your inbound time if wind and swell switch.

Work patterns on the water
Keep your fishing plan tight. Pillar Point rewards quick adjustments and short, controlled work.

### Halibut drifts
Put the boat in a controlled drift across the sand gutters. Use live or fresh cut bait on a sliding sinker, keep light contact with the bottom, and favor short hops over long, uncontrolled drifts when the current is messy. The most consistent bites come when bait is pinned to the edge on a steady tide push.

### Salmon
Troll shallow spoons and small plugs from the jetty mouth uphill toward the cleaner faces. When birds are tied to the seam, switch to surface plugs and slow the spread—salmon here set up in a narrow depth band and will take in a small window. Be ready to collapse the spread into the seam when birds tighten.

### Rockfish and verticals
Short, nailed‑down anchor drops or controlled drift passes along the outside jetty face and isolated rocks work best. Heavy jigs and vertical presentations win when you can keep the boat pinned off relief. Don’t waste long drifts over random sand; focus on the outside face and the small hard spots that break up the flats.

Short checks
If the bar is open but the outer shelf looks rough, run a quick check off the jetty before committing to the outer ledges—many days the best work stays inside 3–7 nm.

Pressure, pots and hazards
This entrance concentrates people and gear.

- Crab pots and buoys: Expect gear around the entrance and on the near flats during crab season. Give markers and commercial lanes a wide berth.
- Fleet compression: When the jetty seam is working, the whole harbor shows up. Trolling lanes get busy, bait gets nervous, and repeated passes spook fish. Pick an approach that avoids constant boat traffic.
- False positives: A pretty boil or isolated foam isn’t always fish. Confirm with birds, sonar bait and repeat marks before committing a full spread.

Micro patterns that matter
The jetty peels a seam most mornings. The fish that ride it are rarely spread out—if birds stay low and tight and bait shows stacked on sonar, the action is predictable and concentrated. If the surface is sloppy and birds scatter, the bite fragments quickly.

What separates good days from bad days
Good days: a stable seam off the jetty, birds that stay low and tight, and a predictable tide push across the sand gutter. Bad days: a sloppy, boiling bar with cross sets, scattered birds, and no coherent bait on the sounder. Read those signs early and either move quickly to exploit the window or save the fuel for the next one.

Bottom line
Pillar Point is a short‑run workhorse when the bar allows it. Fish it like a gate: respect the surf, watch the seam, and move fast when birds and bait agree. When the bar is up, don’t argue with it; when it’s down, the first mile often pays better than a long run.
