Sand turning to broken rock in a few miles—that’s the thing that defines this water. The shelf drops from soft pockets into stepped ledges, pinnacles and boulder fields with narrow sand gutters between. Kelp paddies sit on the outer shoulders and along seams; when tide and current line up they compress bait into those pockets and the bite concentrates fast. It’s a short-run place that fishes like an edge: tight, local, and decisive.

## Sand-to-ledge transition and what to expect on the bottom
You can run from 3–8 miles and go from soft sand to abrupt relief. Typical working depths are roughly 20–60 fathoms. Inside that band you’ll find shallower paddies and necks; beyond the main face there are deeper pinnacles stepping off. Expect isolated high spots, pinnacles that stick up on their own, and sand gutters that funnel bait along the face. The ground is irregular—don’t assume a gentle slope.

Why that matters: the paddies gather the first life and the rock finishes the job. When bait is forced into the gutters or held on the uphill side of relief, predators stack quickly and the bite is compact enough to work without a marathon run.

## Small-scale current fingers control the bite
Observation first: when it’s fishing you’ll see current shearing across the face in fingers, not a uniform alongshore flow. Those fingers create uphill and downhill bites; a clean uphill pull along the face piles bait on the outside edge. When the current runs strictly parallel and strong, it strips bait off the paddies and the day goes quiet.

A couple of timing notes:
- Summer upwelling usually leaves a cold, green band tight to the coast. When a warmer filament or eddy peels into the paddies you get short, intense windows—bait tight to kelp and fish stacked on the uphill side of the seam.
- The bite commonly fires on tide changes or when a current shear begins to form across the face. That’s when bait accumulates in predictable places.

Practical observation: fog mornings hide the birds. Whoever reads sonar and bait marks first in that fog usually finds the fish before the fleet converges.

Be aware of sea state: northwest swell and onshore wind make paddies dicey for small boats. When the bar is rough, the productive water is whatever you can get to and hold, not the prettiest mark on the chart.

## Kelp paddies: bait radar, not a guarantee
Kelp paddies are the bait concentrators here. They’re the first life you look for; the rock is the finishing piece.

What you’ll typically see:
- Paddies with surface structure and birds—anchovy and market squid show most often.
- Sonar with bait stacked on the face: arches and slow-moving columns over high spots.
- Birds hugging paddies when bait is exposed; when birds get quiet, the marks usually thin fast.

Interpretation: a string of paddies with birds and a bait column on the face is a high-probability line. A warm surface patch with no bait or a lone bird without sonar marks is usually not worth investing a long drift.

## What fishes here and when it’s best
This is bottomfish water first. Rockfish and lingcod are the dependable targets; halibut and smaller nearshore species use the gutters and sand pockets less frequently. The spot fishes year-round when weather keeps boats inshore, but the best windows are tied to tide changes and warm pockets pressing against the ledge.

When it fishes (signals you’ll see):
- Bait tight to kelp or stacked on the uphill side of the face.
- Sonar shows discrete marks hugging relief instead of a scattered cloud.
- Birds working low and persistent over paddies—fish commonly follow within hours.

When it’s dead (what to watch for):
- Clean water with no birds and only sparse, scattered marks.
- Strong, parallel alongshore current stripping bait from paddies.
- Heavy swell that washes the kelp and scatters life.

## How the fleet treats this water
Locals use this as a first‑mile check or an inshore fallback. On calm days the fleet fans out to different paddies; when a mark holds the crowd squeezes the face fast.

Experienced operators tend to:
- stage uphill of the mark to control drift angle,
- keep a spread of vertical and slow‑troll presentations,
- avoid anchoring in the foam unless they know the ledge well.

Pressure shows up quickly: foamers refuse repeated casts and marks sink or scatter after repeated passes. The better boats switch to vertical work (drop) or move to the next paddy instead of flogging the same surface cast.

## Drift, presentations, and bait management — the practical calls
Observation first, then action.

Drift setup
- I run uphill of the ledge and let the drift pull me across the face. A 30–60° drift angle across the contour keeps baits in the strike zone longer than a parallel run.

Presentations
- On steep relief, vertical jigs and bait strips fished tight to the rock are higher percentage.
- When bait is concentrated on paddies, slow‑trolled plugs and small flatfalls over the outside edge trigger reactive strikes.
- If birds and bait are tight to the paddies, start with surface offerings and be ready to switch to verticals when marks tighten on the face.

Anchor vs drift
- Anchor only on known, charted high spots or when you intend to work one pinnacle thoroughly. Anchoring into a seam can put you out of the productive angle or too deep—use it deliberately, not instinctively.

Sonar reading
- Look for concentrated arches hugging relief. Slow, dense arches close to the bottom are classic rockfish/lingcod. Tall, mobile columns rising off a pinnacle often mean squirming bait and an ambush predator.
- A clean sonar face with no birds is rarely worth repeating.

Bait management
- When squid are present, downsize hooks and present multiple small strips.
- When bait is scarce, go lighter and keep the offering natural—smaller profiles catch more strikes than heavier, obvious baits in thin water.

## What usually matters — and what doesn’t
Things that usually matter:
- drift angle across the face
- tide movement shearing across the ledge
- bait tight to kelp versus bait dispersed in the gutter

Things that usually matter less:
- an isolated warm surface patch with no bait
- a single bird working without sonar marks

## Quick read checklist and common mistakes
Signals that a window is building:
- birds holding low over a line of paddies
- tight bait arches on the uphill side of the ledge
- a visible temperature filament or cleaner pocket hugging the paddies
- current starting to shear across the face instead of running straight alongshore

Common mistakes:
- running parallel to the ledge and missing the uphill drift angle
- chasing one foamer repeatedly when the school is moving off
- anchoring on top of the seam and fishing out of the productive angle
- ignoring crab pots and buoys—commercial gear is common near some paddies

## Micro-observations worth remembering
- The paddies gather the first life. The rock finishes the job.
- When the tide starts to move across the face, fish show up fast.
- Fog hides birds; read sonar early or you’ll lose the window to the fleet.
- If a mark refuses repeated surface work, switch to verticals or move on.

This is practical, accessible ledge work for boats that respect the bar and the wind window. Treat paddies as your bait radar and the ledge as the final exam—when both line up you can fish a small area hard and leave with fish. When they don’t, move; there’s always another paddy or high spot a few minutes away.
