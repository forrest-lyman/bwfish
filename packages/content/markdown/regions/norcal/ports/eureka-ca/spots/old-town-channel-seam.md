The Old Town Channel seam is short and decisive. It isn’t a big, persistent feature — it’s a lane that exists when the tide, the bar, and the bait all line up. When it’s on, you’ll see cleaner water peel off the channel mouth, bait compressed against that first ledge, and early‑run Chinook parking just long enough to feed before they commit to the shelf. For a small skiff on a calm‑bar day it can be the day’s best fish; for a larger boat it’s a quick check on the way past.

## Where the seam forms and what it looks like
You can think of it as the bay’s outflow meeting the first change in substrate outside the sand spit. The Old Town throat funnels a shallow, accelerating flow straight out of the bay. Once that flow hits the spit and the first backside ledge the cleaner, faster water peels away as a visible seam.

Observations:
- The seam reads as a darker or warmer band against the greener upwelled water outside — birds and porpoise will work it when bait is present.
- The bottom near the mouth is mostly sand and scoured channels; a mile out it shifts to mixed rock and scattered ledges. That substrate change is where bait stacks and salmon orient.

Why it matters: where clean, moving water runs against structure the bait gets compressed and concentrated. Salmon use those concentrated lanes to stage and feed before moving offshore.

## When the seam actually becomes a salmon lane
This seam is a tide feature. It’s not about swell or wind so much as the tidal exchange and how the bar is behaving.

- Most active during a clear tidal exchange: a strong ebb pushing bait out of the bay, then the early flood when the outside current eases and creates a holding lane.
- Bar state sets the limit. A hammering ebb across the entrance will blow bait apart and make the seam irrelevant. A calm or easing bar compresses the window and concentrates life into the lane.

In short: you want a tidal push to create the seam, but not so violent at the bar that everything scatters.

## How we work the seam
Start with the obvious: check the bar and the tide. If the bar is passable and the seam shows, stage just outside the mouth and run uphill passes along the edge looking for repeatable marks and birds.

Tactics in practice:
- First passes are exploratory — tight, uphill runs along the edge to find consistent depth and direction on the troll.
- When sonar shows bait stacked against the inside face of the first backside ledge, slow into a controlled pass. If fish are holding, convert to a drift or anchor and fish the downhill side.
- If nothing shows, short‑hop the seam west and watch for thermal fingering and other point seams — the seam can be a small piece of a larger bait field.

Small skiffs treat this like a day‑maker: tight spreads, frequent passes, and quick conversions to drift/anchor when a stack appears. Bigger boats will often run past a thin seam and search adjacent point seams or the first canyon mouths where the feeding window may be broader.

## Signs the window is building
Watch for alignment — the seam only becomes consistent when several signals lock in.

- Birds tightening and working close to the edge rather than just milling.
- Sonar showing bait stacked off the inside face of the first ledge.
- Surface color change that holds direction, a steady band instead of peeling apart.
- Repeating marks on repeated uphill passes that hold depth and course.

Those signs tell you the bait, current, and structure are cooperating.

## How pressure changes the play
The seam compresses the fleet fast. Once one boat marks and commits, everyone piles in and things alter quickly.

What you’ll see:
- Bait scatters faster than you expect once the seam is fished hard; foamers can appear and then shut down under pressure.
- Fish will push deeper or disperse if too many boats run the same pass repeatedly. In practice a controlled drift after commitment usually out‑converts nonstop trolling through the same lane.

Read the pressure as you would current: adjust angle and tempo rather than brute force the same pass.

## Tactical reads and immediate adjustments
We make small, clear adjustments more than big gambles.

- Troll uphill along the edge first. Salmon commonly move from outside into the seam and meet the bait on the inside face.
- If bait is high and current starts to push it off the ledge, slow down and pick a drift angle to fish the downhill side — that often turns a transient school into a holding situation.
- Watch the tide clock and the Humboldt Bay NOAA station. The seam can flip from rideable to dead inside an hour as the bar and exchange change.
- Don’t treat the seam as automatic. If birds and bait aren’t together, odds favor running a little farther to the prominent point seams or the first canyon mouth.

These are operational decisions — how you move and present once you read the bait and birds.

## What matters more — and what’s usually noise
Keep priorities simple.

What usually matters more:
- bait, birds, and current aligning
- bar condition and timing
- bait stacked on the inside face of structure

What usually matters less:
- the number of boats calling it in (noise more than guarantee)
- a single isolated floater or random foam patch without consistent marks

A repeating mark and working birds beat radio traffic and a lone foam line every time.

Micro‑observation
The seam tightens most reliably on a weakening ebb. When that happens, boats that slow and drift the downhill edge convert better than boats that keep powering through.

The Old Town Channel seam is local and repeatable when the tide and bar allow. If the bait clings to the first ledge and the birds are working, it can make a day. If those pieces aren’t there, it’s an expensive check — read the bait, respect the bar, and commit only when birds and sonar confirm the edge.
