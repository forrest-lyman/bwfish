West Head fishes the way close-in spots do: a simple change in water texture that concentrates bait and concentrates attention. It sits inside the first mile outside the Humboldt Bar — a short kelp neck with a shallow inside face and a harder downhill ledge that breaks into mixed rock and sand. That contrast — calm lee water inside, a tidy downhill seam off the point — is the reason bait compresses here more often than on the neighboring sand.

The ledge itself isn’t one big cliff. Think strings of pockets and boulder ribs with kelp patches that break the current. When tide and swell line up, the inside face stacks bait into a tight column just off the kelp and predators use it like a conveyor. Birds will sit low over the kelp; sonar will show compact, dense bait marks hugging the inside edge.

## Why the kelp neck concentrates bait

Observation first: bait piles up tight on the inside face, not scattered across the sand. The neck creates a small patch of cleaner water where current peels off the point and the kelp slows flow. Bait coming off the kelp will sit in those downhill pockets instead of blasting across open sand. Practically, that means one vertical stack becomes the focus for everything: birds, salmon pushes, and anything big enough to eat stacked bait.

Explanation: the kelp breaks the surface texture and creates a narrow downhill seam. That seam keeps bait in a column close to structure, which is exactly what predators prefer — easy to find, easy to ambush.

Micro-observation: bait stacked tight on the inside face. The fleet often commits to that single stack.

## When West Head fishes best

You want three pieces to line up:

- A weakening tide or a slack period after a moderate NW swell — surface needs to be tidy and the bar passable.
- Current that will peel off the point and push bait from the kelp into the downhill pockets.
- Bait visible on sonar and birds working low over the kelp.

What happens on a typical tide:
- With tide pushing bait off the kelp, you’ll see consistent bottomfish and lingcod marks in the downhill pockets.
- When a warm finger rides toward the point and the bait lines up on the inside face, salmon will stage. That window is short but can be loud.

## How fishermen work it

You can work West Head three ways depending on what’s showing. I line my approach to what the water and sonar are telling me — not the calendar.

### Salmon
Short, uphill trolls along the outside of the kelp neck. Follow the warm band into cleaner lee-water; an uphill pass that keeps a lure or plug over the stacked bait will trigger hookups. When salmon hit repeatedly on an uphill pass, shorten the spread and leave a bait or two inside the kelp seam for immediate follow-up casts.

### Rockfish & Lingcod
Controlled live‑bottom drifts over the downhill pockets or anchor on the lee when the school is tight. Heavy vertical jigs through stacked marks often convert easier than a long slow drift; the fish are sitting in pockets and will hit a straight vertical presentation. For lingcod, favor slower vertical work — they won’t chase far.

### Halibut
They show on the sand cuts just inside the kelp run. Work the downhill edge with short leaders and large baits, drifting across the sand/rock transition instead of parking squarely on soft sand. Expect short, decisive bites when you cross the cuts.

## Signs the window is building

When I’m watching the spot these are the signals I trust first; they come together more often than any chart.

- Birds lowering and working slowly along the kelp line.
- Sonar showing a dense, narrow bait column on the inside face.
- Current fingers that peel off the point and make a tidy downhill flow.
- A narrow patch of warmer surface water pressing against cooler green water — but only meaningful when matched with birds or stacked marks.

What usually matters more:
- bait depth relative to the ledge
- whether the tide is pushing bait off the kelp
- an honest look at the bar and swell

What usually matters less:
- one isolated foam-er with no bait on sonar
- a temperature chart without birds or stacked marks

## Pressure and common mistakes

Because it’s the first mile out, West Head attracts small boats. On busy weekends that single stacked bait column disappears fast — boats that show up late often find only scattered marks and pressured foamers.

Common mistakes I see:
- Trying to force a long troll through the kelp line. It usually fails. Better to make short, methodical passes through the bait lanes, then set up a drift or anchor if fish hold.
- Running out past the point on a hard ebb or with a rough bar. West Head is valuable because it’s close; don’t gamble a marginal bar run chasing a thin temp finger without birds or bait.

If you want to keep a place in the stack, commit early and fish it tight. If the stack shutters under pressure, move off and work nearby pockets rather than running a long spread through stressed bait.

## Tactical callouts

- When the inside face shows bait piled high on sonar and the tide is beginning to push off the ledge: slow down, put a spread through the lanes, and be ready to drop down if fish hold.
- Shorten the spread when salmon are repeatedly striking on an uphill pass; keep a bait inside the kelp seam for quick follow-up.
- Favor controlled speed and vertical presentations for lingcod drift work — the downhill pockets hold sit-and-wait fish that won’t chase far.
- Don’t run a long, fast troll through kelp. You want methodical passes that keep lures where the bait is sitting.

## Final note

West Head isn’t dramatic, but it pays when the pieces align: stacked bait on the inside face, a tidy downhill seam, manageable surface conditions and birds to confirm what the sonar shows. For boats limited by bar conditions it often gives more usable water than farther, rougher runs. Fish it like a pocket feature — short reads, quick adjustments, and respect for the bar.
