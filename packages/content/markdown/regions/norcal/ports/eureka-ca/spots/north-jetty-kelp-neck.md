This is the first useful ledge a few minutes outside Humboldt Bay when the bar is passable. Think of it as a narrow machine: jetty stones, a shallow rocky shelf, a kelp ribbon bolted to the inside edge, and a sand gutter that drops off to the outside. The whole setup organizes current and bait over a few hundred yards. When tide and swell cooperate you get a tight downhill seam that pins life along the inside face — prime holding water for lingcod and rockfish without a long run.

## Why the neck pins bait

The jetty forces the tide into a narrow corridor. On a flood the flow peels around the stones and lays a downhill push along the kelp’s inside edge. That downhill seam compresses bait into a narrow band where rock meets sand. On the ebb the same geometry channels water off the ledge into the gutter — a tidy drift for halibut and rockfish when your angle is right.

Swell matters. Long-period swell tends to soften the seam and blur surface signs; a shorter NW swell with light wind usually cleans the pocket and makes the seam visible. Temperature fingers can tighten into the kelp seam too: a warm surface wash over cooler water will concentrate bait and, occasionally, pelagics or salmon. Those are bonuses — structure and tide run this spot.

Fog changes how you read it. In low visibility you stop looking for birds and start fishing the transducer and tide angle. That’s where you separate real opportunity from wishful thinking.

## Bait and who follows

Anchovies and small surf smelt are the core bait here. They stack tight on good tides inside the kelp and over the short ledge. Smaller schooling bait and sandeels use the kelp fringe as cover; that fringe draws rockfish and the lingcod that sit under the kelp’s shadow. When there’s a real compression you’ll see birds and sometimes porpoise working low and tight — the first honest visual clue the inside lane is alive.

## Where the fish sit and how they behave

Lingcod and rockfish are the primary targets. Lingcod prefer the kelp shadow and the inside face where they can ambush falling bait; they’ll take heavy, vertical presentations and hold tight even when current surges. Rockfish stack on the structure and will ride up off the bottom as the tide picks up.

The adjacent sand gutter is a secondary play — halibut will take advantage of good drift angles off the edge, but that depends on how exposed the bar is and which way the tide is running.

When the spot is on:
- Bait shows as a narrow, high stack along the inside face.
- Birds and porpoise operate very close to the kelp seam.
- Rockfish marks lift off the bottom as current increases; lingcod stay tucked under shadows and hit vertical baits hard.
- The fleet compresses into the first mile and stays; one boat finds a stack and it rarely stays quiet.

When it’s dead:
- The kelp ribbon looks neat, the surface is pretty, and the sonar is empty.
- A warm finger farther offshore or a sloppy bar will leave the neck empty.
- If the bar is running hard the inside lane still gives marks, but small boats often avoid it because you lose drift control.

## Reading the flood-to-slack window

The best window is a clean flood into slack. That’s when bait is compressed against the inside rock and kelp and predators are pinned to the fall zone. On a hard ebb the tide tends to pull bait and fish off the inside face and into the gutter; you’ll lose the tight seam and the bite goes scattershot.

Signs the window is building:
- Sonar shows bait stacked tight along the inside kelp face.
- Birds working low in a narrow line over the kelp.
- Fish marks separating from the bottom and rising with the tide surge.
- The surface shows a tidy downhill flow rather than chaotic chop.

If those signs aren’t present, don’t force it. You can spend a morning drifting pretty water and catch nothing.

## How the fleet uses it

Small boats treat this as a reliable first-mile option when the bar is open but the long runs aren’t worth it. Expect a tight park of boats on slack and early flood when bait stacks up. Experienced skippers set parallel drifts and leave room for each other; pressure kills the topwater signs fast. If everybody starts casting and chasing foamers the surface activity often disappears and the marks go tight for a while.

When the bar is sloppy the neck’s advantage vanishes — most crews will fish the inside headlands instead. On foggy mornings the smart guys trust transducers and tide charts; the birds will show themselves later, and by then the best window is usually gone.

## Tactics I use here

Start vertical, then broaden out. If sonar shows bait stacked on the inside edge I drop heavy jigs or a stout bait rig tight into the kelp shadow first; that isolates lingcod and the larger rockfish. If you pick up fish on vertical work, then try longer casts and heavier soft-jig profiles to cover the uphill water.

When you have the inside face working, a controlled drift that keeps baits moving uphill along the kelp edge is effective — short leaders and big natural baits or heavy soft-jigs that hang close to structure are standard. When the sonar gives you a real stack, anchor or short-drift into the fall zone and present live or cut bait vertically.

A short sequence to follow when you see a stack:
1. Slow down and mark the edge with your transducer.
2. Drop a heavy vertical presentation tight to the kelp shadow.
3. If fish are responsive, widen to controlled uphill drifts to draw fish off the kelp.
4. Back off noise and constant casting if the marks go tight.

Two practical habits: watch the tide angle (clean flood to slack is the window), and keep noise discipline. This water is shallow enough that loud running gear or constant casting scatters surface signs. Drift quietly and keep the boat low in the water when you’re working tight.

## Operational callouts and micro-observations

The jetty can hide how good the inside lane is until you’re right on it. Scout slow and read your transducer; birds alone can be misleading in the kelp patch. If the bar is sloppy, move on to inside headlands — the neck loses its edge when entry is risky.

A common micro-observation: the kelp neck often looks quiet mid-morning, then tightens into a feeding lane on the next flood. When that happens the boats stop moving.

Why check it? It’s close, repeatable, and the first place the Humboldt fleet tests a day. When stacked bait, a tidy downhill flow, and a soft bar line up, you’ve got a compact, productive length of water that doesn’t burn fuel on a long run.
