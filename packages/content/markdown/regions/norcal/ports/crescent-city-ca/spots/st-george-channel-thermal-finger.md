The St. George Channel is the obvious working lane off Crescent City. It’s that narrow strip between the headland and the first reef line where shelf geometry, current and a small patch of warm water line up and make a tidy troll corridor. The feature is a finger—warm, cleaner water peeling along the slope, tucking up against colder, greener surf. When it holds bait it turns predictable: birds mark the edge, the meter clears, and you can run repeatable passes that keep bringing strikes.

Why the thermal finger concentrates bait
---------------------------------------

Observation first: the warm water doesn’t sit in a broad patch offshore. It funnels. A small warm pocket drifts near the shelf, the reef and headland squeeze it toward the channel, and it rides the seam like a river. Bait and birds sit on the warm side; colder upwelling water stays inside the channel. Where the channel pinches the seam often narrows to a few boat-widths—great when it’s stable, useless when it fragments.

The mechanics matter because the shelf and reef create an upslope/downhill interaction. That interaction traps the filament against structure; the result is tight bait concentration rather than scattered marks over open water. Barometer, wind and recent upwelling history decide whether the pocket detaches cleanly or gets shredded before it reaches the channel. A steady southward California Current plus local eddy action is the usual reason the seam will hold long enough to troll.

Reading the seam: signs it’s real
--------------------------------

You don’t need a textbook to tell when the finger is worth fishing. Look for:

- a sharp SST jump on the screen across a short distance;
- birds moving low and steady along a narrow line rather than a scatter of foamers;
- bait marks stacked on the warm side and a distinct surface color change;
- a fishfinder that shows scattered, clean marks instead of murky bottom clutter.

Those signals together are the ticket. One by itself—say a single bird with no meter marks—means little. The seam’s value is its concentration and repeatability.

How fish use the finger
-----------------------

Watch where the fish sit relative to the warm edge and the slope.

- Albacore: they tend to hang tight at the warm-side edge and respond to faster, uphill troll speeds. Typical setups: 45–60 ft of line, clean-swimming cedar plugs or feathers that ride in the up-thermocline layer. They like that sharp contrast—warm bait on one side, cooler water on the other.
- Salmon (migrating Chinook): they’ll often ride slightly deeper or off the outside edge of the finger. Run troll lanes uphill across the seam and count strokes to reproduce the depth that produced the strike.
- Birds: when birds are working here they usually mean the finger has bait tight enough to hold predators. If birds are committed, slow the boat into controlled circles or tight uphill passes—don’t blare through the band.

Micro-observation: seam movement vs fish
---------------------------------------

The seam moves more than the fish. If the thermal line slides south, the fish will often follow within a day. If the seam fragments, the fish scatter faster than the fleet can react. That means your immediate job is to stay keyed to the line, not to chase every single mark that pops off the screen.

Short operational tactics
-------------------------

Layout your spread across the seam so you can cover the edge cleanly. Inside rods in smaller feathers and spoons; outside rods with plugs or larger spoons. Recreate strike distances by counting strokes and noting speed through the band—those two data points matter more than a lure color.

On a bite: stop fast and chum or put livebait if you have it. Albacore often build when you stop cleanly and get bait on the water; a hurried, sloppy stop usually loses the school. If a stop produces marks and the birds linger, make repeat uphill passes rather than long runs away—schools in the channel tend to slide with the current, not bolt.

If the warm finger pins on a ledge or small pinnacle, short vertical jigs or chunking behind the boat can convert finicky birds into strikes. And don’t overwork the zone: repeated aggressive passes will push fish deeper. If you get two clean stops with fading results, change depth or move off the immediate lane.

When the finger breaks
----------------------

Common failure modes are simple and visible.

- Light wind combined with a long, broken swell will shred the seam. The SST break smears out; birds leave and the meter quiets.
- Pressure and overworking the band push fish deeper and less willing to commit. If bites drop after two stops, you’re probably the problem.

If the seam fragments into multiple thin filaments, fish scatter. That’s the time to pull back, re-scan for a dominant line, and either tighten the spread to a single pass or switch to structure work on the ledges.

Access, bar and timing
----------------------

The channel lives outside the bar. Don’t run for a finger without a conservative window—the Crescent City bar flips fast with tide and swell. Late summer is the most reliable period for these thermal fingers, but only when the bar is passable in the morning. Plan fuel and time for multiple passes; these fingers can be picky and they can re-form in the same spot if you’re patient.

Fleet behavior and etiquette
----------------------------

Locals will check the first ledges and fan into the channel if SST or buoy reports show a warm pocket. Expect the fleet to compress quickly once birds and bait show. Experienced boats hold distance and focus on reproducing strike angles instead of simply piling on. You’ll see early-converging trollers and a few boats set to chunk or bait the stop—play smart: reproduce the pass that worked and keep your angles clean.

Seasonal shape
--------------

- Late summer to early fall: prime for albacore when thermoclines and warm filaments develop offshore and drift into the channel.
- Late summer into fall: migrating Chinook stack on channel seams during transit past the headland; fall tides with a warm fringe give the better bite.
- Spring and strong upwelling periods: the channel will hold bait, but water tends to be greener and the migratory bite is less consistent—use the channel more for structure work and short halibut/rockfish drifts.

Tactical takeaway
-----------------

If you find a narrow SST seam with bait and birds in the St. George Channel, change program immediately: tighten the spread, reproduce the strike depth, and treat the seam like a moving conveyor. If the seam isn’t there, don’t waste a long-run troll—work the ledges and sand edges close to the headland until the finger returns. The feature rewards discipline: repeat the pass that produced a strike, respect the bar, and follow the seam, not yesterday’s report.
