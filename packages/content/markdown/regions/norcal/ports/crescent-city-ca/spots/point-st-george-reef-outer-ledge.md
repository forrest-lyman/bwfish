The outer ledge off Point St. George is the first real piece of shelf that drops into deeper water west of Crescent City. On the meter it shows as a clean contour break — pinnacles, boulder patches and a pronounced downhill face that channels current into a narrow shelf trough. That downhill direction is the thing: water pushes off the point, peels into the ledge and either pins bait against the rock or strips it away, depending on tide and swell. Predators follow the direction; lingcod and bigger rockfish sit the downhill shoulders, and when a warm finger rides the outer shelf the same geometry gives you a very repeatable intercept for salmon.

## How the ledge organizes water and bait

What you see first is direction. The face creates seams and eddies in predictable spots.

- A steady downhill push across the face will compress bait against the shelf edge and light up the downhill shoulders.
- Eddies form on the lee side of the point — those are the pockets where bait piles and where fish will stack if wind and tide line up.
- If the current runs offshore too fast it strips bait into the trough instead of compressing it, and the numbers go thin.

Observation before theory: when the face is working you’ll see a distinct surface seam or color change just outside the ledge and tight meter marks on the downward slope. The fish stay where the water moves; read the seam first.

Micro-observation: the best mornings often start with a clear thermocline 2–4 miles off the point and a steady line of birds that travels along it.

## Reading the morning window

The ledge produces short, clear windows. Pick the signals that repeat.

Signs the window is building:
- birds traveling low and steady along the contour
- bait compressing into tight meter marks on the face
- a narrow SST stripe visible on the surface or on electronics
- one or two troll bites that repeat on the same count

If you get two or three of those, set a plan: either work the structure hard with vertical presentations or commit a short troll across the seam and then replicate the count.

## Access and the bar — the practical clock

This is outside water. Crescent City’s bar sets your schedule. If the bar is blown or the NW swell has steep faces, the outer ledge becomes a poor choice — you fish inside pinnacles or kelp lines instead.

Plan runs on slack or in tide windows where incoming/outgoing aligns with a manageable swell. Expect to make multiple passes across the seam to find a productive line; leave fuel and time in case the bar goes gnarly on the way back.

## Two ways boats work the ledge

Boats approach this ground in two distinct modes: structure work and intercept/troll work. Don’t mix both in the same pass unless you’ve already confirmed what’s on the seam.

Structure work
- Long, slow drifts or controlled anchor drifts over pinnacles and downhill shoulders when the current is pushing across the ledge.
- Vertical jigs and heavy bait presentations. Start above the ridge, tag bottom without dragging and let the jig sweep the face.
- If you see marks suspended on sonar, tighten leader length and present jigs vertically on the edge — the bite changes when fish suspend.

Intercept/troll work
- When a warm finger or a visible SST break sits on the outer contour, push a few miles offshore and run an uphill troll across the seam.
- Use 3–5 rods with a mix of inside feathers and outside spoons. Run the seam at a higher speed and troll uphill into cleaner water.
- Count strokes across the ledge and repeat the count — a repeatable count between the edge and the warm pocket is gold.

## Tactics by species

Lingcod and rockfish
Fish the downhill shoulders with long, controlled slow drifts. Tag bottom, hold contact without dragging, and let a jig or bait sweep the face. On good marks that suspend, shorten leader, get vertical, and make your presentation tight to the numbers.

Salmon
Run the seam as a lane. If the warm band is present, you want an uphill intercept. Three to five rods, mixed identities (feathers inside, spoons outside), run a consistent speed and count your strokes across the edge. When bites repeat on the same count, you’ve found a lane you can replicate.

Short baiting and chunking
When birds and electronics both show a dense bait patch in a lee eddy, short chunking can turn a passive school into a committed feed. Use caution where crab gear and other boats are present; short casts and controlled drifts work better than wide, messy spreads.

## When the ledge goes flat

This ground can look right and produce nothing. Typical failures:
- Current slides offshore too fast and strips bait from the face.
- A broad cold pulse spreads bait thin over the shelf instead of compressing it.
- Fleet pressure: repeated passes push fish off the high spots or deeper than your gear reaches.

Common pressure reactions:
- fish sink below your sonar range
- foamers refuse repeated casts
- bites only on extreme edge drifts away from the obvious numbers

If you see those signs, either change tactics (deeper, tighter presentations) or move. The ledge punishes running blind.

## Seasonal shape

- Spring: upwelling pushes bait tight to the nearshore face. This is primarily a structure game — lingcod and rockfish respond to tidal pushes more than surface warmth.
- Late summer–early fall: a narrow warm finger can ride the outer shelf and open short troll windows for salmon. When that happens the ledge shifts from a bottomfish stop to an intercept corridor.
- Winter: big NW swell and wind usually put the outer ledge off-limits. Fish inside or wait for calm spells.

## Fleet behavior and practical notes

Locals treat Point St. George like an early check and a nearby fallback to deeper runs. The common routine: fan out along predicted seams, test the first ledges for bait, then commit farther offshore only when SST and birds confirm a warm pocket.

Respect commercial gear — pots and place-holders sit on the shelf — and avoid long troll spreads over known trap lines. When you get bites, note the count and the current phase. This ledge rewards repeatable patterns and punishes improvisation without signals.

The fish stay where the water moves. Read the seam first; the rock will follow.
