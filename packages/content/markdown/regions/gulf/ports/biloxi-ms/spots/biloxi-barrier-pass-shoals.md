Motion is the feature here. The barrier islands off Biloxi don’t give you a single rock or reef to fish; they set up a repeating pattern of shoals, narrow cuts, and lee pockets. Tides shove water through those cuts, lift bait onto the shoal crests, and then dump it into pockets or channel margins. Where that motion constrains bait into a narrow strip—that’s the seam. That seam is the show: kings, cobia and jacks use it like a highway and a feeding lane, but they don’t sit on one GPS point — they ride the tide.

## Depth and bottom — where the bait gets stacked
The ground tells you where the bait will go.

Shoals and flats: shallow, usually 3–20 ft, with sand, shell and scattered rock. On a push tide the bait crowns the shoal crest and sits tight to the top.

Pass channels: narrow cuts that drop off sharply — you’ll often see a 10–30 ft swing from the flat to the channel. Those uphill and downhill faces are what predators use: uphill for ambushes, downhill for trailing seams.

Lee pockets and holes: small, deeper pockets immediately behind the shoal. When the tide slackens or falls the bait funnels into these holes and holds there until the next push.

Read the bottom and you’ll know where the bait will compress as the tide moves.

## How the tide builds (and kills) the seam
Tide is the engine. The passes locally invert the shelf pattern: a steady push through a cut pins bait against the uphill face, and the outgoing phase compresses it into channel margins.

- Incoming (flood) shoves bait up onto the shoal and against shallow relief.
- Outgoing (ebb) compresses bait into the channel edges and lee pockets.
- Short, strong tidal pulses make the seam narrow and very productive, but brief.
- Wind will change everything: light southerlies with a clean tide sharpen the seam; onshore breeze or chop buries it or blows the seam off.

The seam is a timing cue more than a place — find the tide and the seam will show.

## Bait sequence and which predators follow
Observe the bait first; the rest follows in predictable order.

Threadfin and pilchards show earliest, tight to the crest. As the tide sharpens the seam, small jack-type baits and menhaden get pushed into the cutting flow and birds move in hard.

Who turns up:
- King mackerel and Spanish run the moving bait along the channel edge.
- Cobia cruise the uphill faces, targeting larger pinned bait and rays pushed into shallow cuts.
- Jack crevalle patrol the flats and drops; they’ll smash surface presentations and live baits.

If the bait is tight and the seam is defined, you get a quick succession of species rather than a long, single‑species bite.

## Signals the window is building
Look for a set of simple, repeatable signs — they stack.

- Bait pinned tight on the shoal crest instead of scattered across the flat.
- Birds concentrated over the cut, not scattered on random patches.
- A steady mark or streak on sonar hugging the channel edge.
- A clean strip of water along the cut: a narrow, clearer band while surrounding water stays murky.

If two or three of those line up, the seam is about to produce.

## How the bite unfolds and how long it lasts
The passes are short‑window work. When a seam forms it generally earns you 30–90 minutes of real action. Expect turnovers: fast-moving kings and jacks first, then cobia and larger cruisers as bait compresses and larger targets show.

When it’s dead: water is flat, muddy, or wind-stirred; bait is spread thin across the flats; birds aren’t working a seam; sonar shows dispersed marks or nothing. The pass still moves water, but without contrast or a firm push it’s ordinary.

## Running a pass — practical calls and moves
Locals treat the cuts like tactical check‑ins: hit a pass on a good tide, work it for the window, then rotate. Big boats avoid crossing the barrier on swell days, so many productive pass windows are small-boat plays.

When you approach:
1. Confirm tide direction and look for birds on approach.
2. Slow down — make short uphill tacks across the seam rather than a long drift through it.
3. Keep a live bait ready on light gear for quick presentations when birds push bait to the surface.
4. When pressure shows up, rotate off and let the spot settle.

What to run and when:
- Live threadfin or pilchards swum above the uphill face: decisive for cobia and kings.
- Fast-trolled spoons and small skirted plugs to rip through the incoming seam for immediate strikes from kings and jacks.
- Chunking/controlled chum near the channel when bait is deep but compressed — quick chunks for quick windows.
- Vertical jigging on the channel edge when sonar shows concentrated marks just below the surface.

Short practical points: position up-current of the seam, make short uphill drifts across the edge, and keep a bait tied on light gear for surface pushes.

## Fleet behavior and pressure management
The passes get worked hard when they’re on. Small boats will cycle a cut for the 60–90 minute window and then hop to the next nearby cut or a nearshore wreck. If you stay on one pass too long you’ll spread pressure and scatter the bait.

Common mistakes to avoid:
- Hanging on a pass after the window fades.
- Hitting a cut at slack tide — rarely productive.
- Fishing the lee (dead) side when the uphill face is where the bait is pinned.
- Trusting birds alone: they’re a signal, not proof—confirm with sonar and temperature when you can.

If the pass is crowded, rotate and look for the next seam rather than crowding the same drift.

## Micro-observations and the simple operational call
Small observations matter: bait tight to the crest before the bite, and then piling on the downhill face right before kings move in. The seam will show on sonar and GPS before birds fully commit.

Operational call: if you see a clean strip of water, a tight bait mark hugging the pass edge on your screen, and the tide is moving — run short uphill passes with live bait ready. Commit to the 30–90 minute window, work it hard, then rotate. The barrier passes aren’t long‑haul grounds; they’re punchy, tactical work that pays when the tide does the job.
