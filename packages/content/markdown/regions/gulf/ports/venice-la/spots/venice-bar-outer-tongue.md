The tongue at the ship channel mouth is the port’s gate. You can see the geometry from a mile out: a dredged trench cuts through muddy flats, throws a narrow sand tongue into cleaner water, and often sits right under a visible plume edge. For boats leaving Venice the decision is rarely poetic — it’s practical: can you get across safely, and is the color, current and bait worth burning fuel and daylight to chase the outside slope?

What the tongue looks like is simple and the consequences are immediate. A sand tongue and ebb ridge form off the channel mouth; a sand rip and a small lee pocket sit on top of that. That lee is the first bluewater signal out of Venice. When cleaner water pushes over the bar you’ll see birds, a temperature or color step, or bait on sonar. When those things line up it’s a stake in the ground. When they don’t, the right move is to work the near ledges and platform ties until something shows.

## Current, color and the bar decide the run

Observe first, explain second. The plume, the tongue and swell angle are the three variables you check in that order.

- The plume. Muddy, low‑salinity water from the river often sits inside the tongue and stains the first several miles. If the plume peels offshore it either buries the bar in stain or creates a tight color line you can run along.
- The tongue/rip. On a strong ebb the sand tongue and rip sharpen; clean water pushes over the bar and produces a downhill seam on the outside face where bait and predators stack.
- Swell angle. Opposing swell across the channel ruins the lee pocket and makes the pass dangerous. A tidy run with swell aligned with the channel is the practical window — otherwise the ride costs the day.

Put another way: color and current tell you where the bait will be; swell tells you whether the bar will let you work it.

## Signs the decision is leaning outside

When you’re at the tongue you want corroboration. A single bird or a radio tip is not enough. Look for a compact package of signals that line up.

- a crisp color break sitting on or just outside the tongue
- working birds tight to the channel face
- a visible uphill/downhill current shear over the rip
- sonar bait stacking off the face in the 40–120 ft range
- a bar run with swell aligned to the channel and a predictable ebb

At sea that package looks the same: the water suddenly cleans up outside the tongue, birds wheel on a tight band, and boats that were idling in shallow water cut and commit. When those markers align, yellowfin, mahi or billfish on the outside is a reasonable gamble — but only when the signals are real and repeated.

## When to stay inside and work structure

Outside water is exciting, but the inside options are real fish and often the safer, smarter play. If the plume covers the first 3–5 miles and the color is uniform, you’re usually wasting fuel on a long run. Opposing swell or a confused channel mouth kills a usable lee even if the outside looks good from the bridge. If you have no birds, no temp step, and sonar only shows scattered bait near the bottom, the near ledges and platform rows will usually pay.

Top‑of‑slope ledges, platform rows and the first hardpatches consistently hold kings, blackfin and the snapper/grouper complex even when the outside water is dead. A short, clean pass across structure or working current often beats a long run into marginal blue.

## Common mistakes and how the fleet reads the bar

Fleet behavior around Venice is practical and quick. When a solid outside signature shows, the fleet elongates and runs: first boats cut hard, the rest follow, radio chatter goes from “check” to “working,” and pressure builds on the edge. When the bar is marginal most boats refuse the outside and pile into the channel lee and platform rows, which kills bites quickly if everyone repeats the same GPS mark.

Typical mistakes I see:

- committing on a single bird report and arriving at an empty break — wait for color/bait corroboration
- trying the outside with opposing swell — passable doesn’t mean productive
- repeating passes over the same GPS mark instead of working contour lines — outside edges are linear; you need to work along them

Reading how the fleet behaves is itself a signal. If boats split and run, that usually means the markers checked out. If everyone sits and grinds the near ledges, the outside is probably marginal.

## How I run the tongue (practical checklist)

Make decisions quickly but based on the signals. My checklist when I’m at the mouth:

- Confirm the color line: look for a clear SST/blue band sitting outside the tongue.
- Scan birds and debris: working birds over a tight band are better confirmation than one patch.
- Check swell angle on the bar: opposing swell kills a usable lee even if the outside looks hot.
- Start uphill passes first: run uphill at speed to provoke a reaction, then slow through the bait if birds appear.
- Have a fallback: if the outside doesn’t corroborate, immediately work the top‑of‑slope ledges, platform ties or the ship‑channel lee instead of burning fuel farther out.

Small decisions at the tongue win days. Confirm the package or fall back fast.

Micro‑observation: the bar will decide the trip before the fish do.

This tongue is a decision point, not a destination. When the tongue, rip and plume line up with clean water, bait and birds the port flips to bluewater. When they don’t, the practical job is to work structure inside the channel or save fuel and try again on the next tide or weather window.
