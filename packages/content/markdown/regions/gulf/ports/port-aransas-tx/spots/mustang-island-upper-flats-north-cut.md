## The first mile: flats, potholes, and sand fingers

This is the shallow water immediately behind Mustang Island — the first mile of flats and the mouth of the North Cut. Depths are thumb‑deep out on the flats (6–18 inches), a few feet in the small cuts, then drop into a patchwork of shallow potholes, oyster edges and narrow sand fingers. Substrate flips between short grass, sparse shell and open sand. That geometry is what makes a steady tidal push visible: bait roads form, fish tail in the shallows, and you can often see the whole sequence from the boat.

We check this spot because it’s practical sight‑fishing when the bar or sea state makes a longer run unnecessary. You’ll put trout and reds in the boat without leaving the lee of the island. The payoffs here are usually about timing — a short, explosive window — not fancy tackle.

## Tide and the North Cut: where the engine is

The cut and its adjacent sand tongues act like a tidal engine. On the flood tide bait and shrimp peel off the flats and funnel toward the channel mouth; predatory fish ride that push and stage on the outside grass edges and in the troughs. On the ebb the same fish ghost into potholes, cuts and the lee of oyster heads.

Useful operational rules:
- Flood tide: expect fish staging on outside grass edges, tailing along sand fingers, and holding ahead of the channel mouth.
- Ebb/falling tide: fish tuck into potholes, cuts and deeper holes — that’s when you work the troughs and holes.
- Slack tide: generally flat; only useful if it coincides with good light for sighting, otherwise reposition to the tide neck.

The pass and jetties change everything. A hard pass current produces a clean mudline and a tight current seam in the cut — bait compresses there and the flats light up. If the pass is blown or southerlies stain the water, the sight windows vanish.

## Bait and forage bands

Primary forage here is small glass minnows, grass shrimp and finger mullet. In clean water they show as tight bands; with a running tide they break into choppy pushes. Birds are a blunt but reliable indicator — small gull flocks and terns collapsing on a narrow band usually means a working push of bait.

Watch how the bait behaves more than how many birds are present. The bait lanes tell you where the fish will stage; birds tell you that the lane has protein.

## How the bite typically looks

You’ll see fish before you feel them. Tailing trout and skittering mullet give you better information than the first rod pull. When the flood hits the cut at dawn the bite is often short and intense — 45–90 minutes of frantic sight fishing if conditions line up.

Wind changes the picture fast. A light offshore or parallel breeze keeps flats glassy and lets you pick apart tailing fish. Onshore chop hides tails and pushes fish to deeper cuts and edges.

## Signs the sight window is building

Look for these cues; they’re the short list that tells you to get in position:
- water cleaning rapidly across the flats
- grass shrimp rising in the shallows
- bait strips moving into the sand tongues on the flood
- tailing trout ahead of the channel mouth
- birds working low and tight over a narrow band

If several of those line up on the flood you have a real shot at sight fishing.

## How boats work this water

This is a short, deliberate job for local boats. The running style is quiet and positional:
- Pole or drift into the tide; stealth beats power here.
- Anchor only to present into a single pothole or cut when fish concentrate on a falling tide.
- Keep the motor off until the last 50 yards — vibration and wake spook these fish.
- Skiffs, shallow flats boats and paddles do the real work; larger boats mostly use the cut mouth as a staging area.

If the sight lane is small, don’t force it. Approach the seam on an angle and intercept on the edges rather than plowing into the middle.

## Presentations that catch fish

Keep presentations simple and match the visibility:
- Trout sight rigs: light leaders (10–12 lb fluorocarbon), small live shrimp or finger mullet under a popping cork, or freelined shrimp in very clean water.
- Redfish: stout single‑hook live‑lining on a long leader, or a slow short drift with a small weight when they’re in deeper troughs.
- When the water stains: switch to chunkier, scentier baits and concentrate on cuts and potholes instead of open sight lanes.
- Pocket work: short casts across the edge, let the bait fall vertically into the pothole, and expect a slow initial take from reds before they commit.

Match leader and approach to the water clarity. Heavy leaders in glassy water spook wary trout; light leaders in rough or stained water get thrashed.

## Common mistakes

The mistakes I still see from good fishermen:
- Waiting too long to get on the flood — most sight activity shows early as the tide begins to fill.
- Over‑weighting leads and dragging presentations through thin water.
- Running straight into the mudline instead of approaching the seam on an angle into the tide.
- Using heavy leaders in glassy water and spooking wary trout.

Fix one mistake at a time: stealth and tide timing usually buy you more fish than one extra lure.

## Fleet behavior and quick decision rules

This is high‑frequency, short‑day water. When a sight window forms anglers compress into the lane quickly; even a few boats can scatter bait and kill a tailing bite. The practical response is to relocate slightly off the visible seam and intercept fish on the edges or in deeper cuts.

Two simple decision rules I use:
- Tide + light > run the flats sight fish; the flood in good light is worth the short run.
- Wind or brown water > quit the sight work and fish the cuts and potholes with chunkier baits.

The fish show on the grass before the birds commit. If the flood hits with light and clear water, get in there early. If it’s blown or brown, save the sight stuff for another day and work the deeper structure.
