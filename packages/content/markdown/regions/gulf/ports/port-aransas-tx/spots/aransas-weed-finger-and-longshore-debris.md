This strip isn’t a fixed structure — it’s a temporary road carved by wind, tide through the pass, and the longshore sweep. Pieces of sargassum, cut bait from shrimp boats and tide‑chiselled wood collect into narrow, ragged fingers that ride a shallow longshore current just outside the first shelf. When a band of warmer blue water or a tight temperature filament sits next to those fingers, they become short, very productive pelagic lanes.

## Why the fingers form where they do
Observation first: the weed lines run parallel to Mustang Island, usually as thin, irregular ribbons. They’re most obvious on an east–southeast wind with a steady alongshore push to the south. The pass matters — outflow tongues and eddy detachments at certain tidal stages peel debris away from the mouth and braid it into the longshore sweep.

Explanation: wind pushes floating material off the beach; the alongshore flow concentrates it into threads. A moderate southward current along the outside shelf keeps those threads aligned with the island. Slack tide or a fresh northerly disperses the fingers fast. You’ll also see a clean color edge where greener or bluer water meets stained inshore water; the weed almost always sits on that edge.

## What lives on the seam — bait, birds, and early signs
Watch the bait and birds before you commit. Small schooling fish — glass minnows, the occasional pogie or finger mullet pushed by the pass — pile into the weed. Those little schools attract more bait and then predators.

Birds are the first reliable sign. If terns and shearwaters tighten on a ragged line and start diving, pelagics usually show up within minutes. The sequence I look for: bait pushing against floating weed, birds working the seam, then surface action. When warm water is present the mahi and kings arrive quickly; cobia and Spanish will use the larger debris and lanes too, but mahi/kings are the reason most boats run this strip.

## When the finger matters — timing and how long it lasts
These fingers run on short windows. A productive section will fish great for hours and sometimes only minutes. The useful window rarely spans more than a tide unless wind and current hold steady.

It’s most effective when a clear blue band sits close by. A weed finger in tannic inshore water can hold life, but not the same explosive pelagic bite you get when that finger sits against an offshore temp edge. In my experience late morning through afternoon is prime — surface warming and bird activity peak then — but after an onshore night wind an early check pays off.

## How the fleet searches and commits
Look at how local boats move: the search is visual and fast. Crews run a few miles southeast of the pass, pick off threads, then grid across them at trolling speed. When birds and a temp edge pair with weed you get a funnel — boats converge on the working section instead of spacing out.

Experienced captains approach from up‑current, cross the line to judge bait density, then set a troll down the edge. Less experienced boats often circle foamers and spend time in obvious commotion, missing the cleaner lanes a few hundred yards downwind. Shrimp boats and commercial traffic can throw up temporary debris that looks productive; the difference is whether birds commit to the line or just ride the nets’ wake.

## Approach and spread — fish the boundary, not the mat
Observation: the fish use the clean/weed interface. Running into the mat puts lures where the fish aren’t.

Practical approach: come from up‑current, cross the seam to see how tight the bait is, then set a spread that fishes the edge. Troll the edge rather than the middle — run lures just outside the weed and strip back through the action. When boats bunch, find the clean lane and work the edge; when the line thins, move along it quickly.

Suggested tackle choices without fuss: small to medium skirt baits, small diving plugs, and planers with fast, flashy teasers. Planer boards and outriggers get lures under the surface and clear of floating material. When birds are working tight, switch to live bait or free‑lined ballyhoo/small mullet. Topwater and surface popping work when fish are visible and birds are tight — be ready to switch in a heartbeat; the action is often short and aggressive.

If the line holds bait but fish aren’t committing, a short anchor drift or live‑bait drift on the up‑current end will catch the slower cobia and kings that use that structure more methodically.

## Depth and presentation notes
Mahi strike very near the surface; kings often sit a little deeper. Use planers and outriggers to vary presentation: a few lures just under the surface, a couple at surface, and one deeper bait. Keep the spread mobile — the working section moves along the seam and can tear apart quickly.

## Signals the window is building
The good window shows a pattern, not a single sign:
- Birds tightening and repeatedly diving on a narrow ribbon.
- Surface bait being pushed up against floating weed — white backs or streaks in the mat.
- A visible color change or a slim thermal edge running parallel to shore.
- Other boats committing to a short run down the line.

Two of those together — birds plus a color/temp edge — is the signal I trust to commit.

## Common mistakes that waste time
- Running into the weed and throwing lures over the mat. The fish live on the boundary.
- Chasing a lone foamer while ignoring the rest of the line. The working patch moves; check upwind and downwind before circling.
- Waiting too long to switch to live bait. When birds sharpen the action, live bait often turns an intermittent mark into a committed feed.

## Operational realism — quick checks, quick decisions
The finger is ephemeral. One afternoon it’s a clean road with birds and mahi; the next it’s dispersed tangles and only small bait. Treat the line like a temporary highway: quick in, quick checks, and commit only when two signals line up. When they do, the alley fishes fast and then is gone.

## Micro‑observation
I ran up on a black seam in green water one afternoon. The birds found it first; the mahi hit five minutes later. Simple sequence — spot, cross, set on the edge.
