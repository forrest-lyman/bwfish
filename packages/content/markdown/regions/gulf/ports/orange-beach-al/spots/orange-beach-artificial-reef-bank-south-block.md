## What the bank looks like and how you spot it

South Block isn’t a single towering ledge—it's a short stitched line of concrete modules, rubble and isolated rock sitting inside the 15–40 fathom belt south and southeast of the Orange Beach pass. On sonar it reads like a broken arc: high-contrast tops separated by sand gutters and short downhill seams. On a calm day you can make that arc with your eyes; on anything windier the marks compress and the bank just fishes where current and bait meet it.

That pattern matters. The reef creates pockets and short ledges across a sand plain, and the fish show up where those pockets funnel bait and current together.

## Tide and current windows that make the bank work

The pass dictates the short-term current here. Flood stacks bait and debris on the northern faces of blocks and along the jetty; ebb pulls that bait off the outside slopes and into the gutters between pieces. Practically, the bank wants a steady, measurable current—either a clean flood that piles bait on the tops or an ebb that turns the gutters into funnels.

Observations and what they mean:
- Flood: you’ll see bait stacked on top and the inside faces. That’s prime for live-bait work and presentations that ride the top of the reef.
- Ebb: bait streams off into gutters. That’s when chunking, jigging the face, and controlled drifts pay off.
- Slack: the marks become ghostly; fish tuck deeper and become hard to coax.

If the tidal shear is up, the bite builds. When the tide softens, the top often stays noisy but the hooks go quiet until the current reshapes the column.

## Bait, birds and the sonar signatures to trust

Forage here is small schooling bait: glass minnows and pilchards from local pushes, occasional pogies, and seasonal shrimp and crab life around the modules. When the bite is forming the tops show a dense fuzz on sonar; when a single species commits those targets stack tight.

Birds are unreliable as a first signal. More often the first reliable indicator is the arc on your screen or a cluster of boats. When birds do show, it’s usually because the bait’s been pushed to the surface by spanish or jacks. The steady snapper and grouper work, though, typically starts below the surface.

Species you’ll see regularly: vermilion and gray snapper, gag and red grouper, amberjack, triggerfish and the occasional cobia on the larger pieces.

## When to go and when to sit this one out

South Block eats steady, clean current and bait pushed through the pass. It’s a go-to when:
- the pass is pushing bait on a steady tide and the inside water is bait-rich, or
- a few days of southeast wind have cleaned the water and the fleet finds blue patches over the bank.

Hold off when a northerly chop has smeared the water or the pass is green and fouled—bait gets skittish, and bigger reef fish slide deeper or toward better relief. Also note: the bank can look alive on sonar and still be quiet at the rod if the bait is scattered or a temperature edge is holding well offshore.

## How the fleet runs South Block

Locals treat this as first-mile reef water. Typical behavior you’ll see:
- Quick checks on the incoming tide to see if bait and livies are stacked.
- Short drifts along the line with a spread of livies and a couple of jigs.
- Light chumming once the live-bait bite locks up, working the whole top-to-bottom column.
- Switching to vertical jigging or heavier slow-pitch when marks are tight but shy.

Pressure shows fast. A solid bait stack will bunch boats inside a few hundred yards; after repeated passes the fish often tighten up or move a touch deeper. The quiet boats that hold in the bite zone and fish deeper usually convert more than the ones that bounce around.

## Holding the bite — practical tactics

Observations first, then what we do.

When bait is stacked on the tops, start with live baits on the flood or the first strong ebb and free-line them near the reef top. If current is ripping, move to heavier live-bait rigs or slow-pitch jigs so the presentation stays in the strike zone. For snapper that are actually on the top, oversized hooks on livies hold better than light jigs—holding bottom matters.

For amberjack and grouper use heavy leaders, stout hooks and fish a short tie to the bottom with controlled lifts; those fish respond to heavier, slower presentations. When marks sit mid-column and won’t tighten, chunk and drift the outside gutter to lay down a scent trail and slow fish into commitment.

A micro-observation worth remembering: after the tide softens the top can still show life, but hooks go quiet until the current redefines the column. Patience and holding bottom through that second wind often pays.

## Common mistakes that kill time on the bank

These are habits that degrade the bite here:
- Chumming hard at slack: churned water and no current usually kills the bite.
- Fishing only the top when marks show a column—the snapper may be suspended off the face.
- Staying shallow with light tackle when amberjack are present; you’ll either lose fish or miss the right hookset window.

Avoid those and you’ll convert a higher percentage of the bites you see on screen.

## Reading marks — what to trust

Take sonar marks as an argument, not a promise. A clean, dense arc over a block with streaks down into a gutter usually equals committed fish. Thin, noisy marks that smear when you move the boat often mean bait only or reluctant fish. If the arc tightens after a pass—especially on a first strong current change—the odds tilt quickly in your favor.

Also watch the fleet. If boats are converting on livies and the top-of-reef marks tighten, that’s a strong signal to stay and work the area rather than chase birds or a temp edge elsewhere.

## When to stay and when to chase something else

Stay if:
- bait is stacked and current is clear,
- top-of-reef marks tighten and boats are converting on livies,
- the pass is pushing clean water and the bank shows a color difference.

Pass if:
- a steady south/southeast fetch has built a temp break offshore and birds are working over blue water—those edges often outfish the bank,
- the pass is green and the bite is only on tiny bait that won’t hold larger reef predators.

South Block is practical, not glamorous. On the right tide with bait stacked it will give steady snapper and grouper work and the occasional amberjack surprise. Fish it by watching current first, keeping live-bait rigs ready, and having the patience to hold bottom when the bite narrows.
