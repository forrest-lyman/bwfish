What makes this piece of water worth running is obvious once you see it: long, narrow seagrass fingers running parallel to the island, shallow oyster bars and cuts slicing across them, and a pass that forces most tidal exchange into a few predictable lanes. The features are subtle — a grass ridge here, a muddy cut there — but they control where bait stacks and where game hides. This is corridor fishing, not a single hotspot.

## Why the pass controls the alley

The pass is the engine. On the flood the jet against the inside jetty pushes mullet and shrimp into the upstream grass fingers; on the ebb that same body of bait peels off the grass and runs past oyster lips and shallow bars. When the pass is clean the current is narrow and steady and bait sits tight along predictable lanes. After a northerly blow or heavy rain the pass dirties, the bait thins, and the fish either move deeper or suspend. That pattern determines whether you fish the grass edges, the cuts, or run for the outer rows.

I watch the pass first, then the grass. If I see a clear jet and bait sheeting into the jetty on sonar, I plan for a tight window around the tide change. If the pass is muddied, I expect muted marks and start looking for muddier channels or deeper structure.

## How bait organizes on the grass and oyster edge

Seagrass fingers act like shallow gutters for small life. Glass minnows, juvenile mullet and shrimp hold tight in blades and troughs; the trout and reds patrol the downhill edges and ambush anything pushed off the grass. Oyster bars collect barnacles, crabs and small crustaceans; sheepshead relate to that vertical, rough structure more than open grass and show on the high tides around oyster lips and pilings.

Birds are a useful but not definitive signal. Terns and gulls tightening over a cut usually mean fleeing bait, often a boat or two away. More often you'll see the bait on sonar before the birds commit. Treat birds as confirmation, not the cue.

## Reading the window — when the fish line up

The practical bite window here is small: the last hour of outgoing and the first hour of incoming are when fish line the downhill side of grass lanes and the outside face of oyster bars. On a clean pass those minutes are sharp — bait sheets, trout show as tight single marks over grass, and boats start easing into a particular cut instead of running the whole shoreline.

Signs the window is building:
- a clean, steady jet through the pass and along the inside jetty
- bait sheeting on the grass edge on your sonar
- trout appearing as tight, singular marks rather than a scatter
- other boats easing into a cut rather than hammering the shoreline

If those signals aren't there, the practical option is to recon the pass mouth or check outer oyster rows where reds and sheepshead will stage at high tide.

## How boats work the water

Small skiffs and poling boats are the natural platform for these fingers. They approach quietly on the down-current side and place short casts across the holes and edges. Center consoles will run a reconnaissance along the inside jetty on the incoming tide; if bait is stacked they'll fish live shrimp or small popping plugs before committing to longer runs. Charters usually stage on deeper cuts and anchor or controlled-drift the downhill water where trout and reds hold.

Pressure matters here. This water fishes out fast: once several boats sit on a tight cut the fish scatter or slide off the edge. When you arrive to a crowded cut, check sonar and bait before dropping a hook — busy doesn't always mean productive.

## Presentations that actually work here

Start with observation and match what the fish are keyed to. The presentations that consistently connect on these edges are simple and precise.

- Live shrimp on a 1/0–2/0 circle or live-fly rigged, fished on a 6–10 ft leader for trout and reds in the cuts. Hold small baits tight to the grass edge or present them just off oyster lips.
- Light topwater plugs and small ticking poppers first light and last light over shallow grass — when bait is tight trout will commit aggressively.
- Soft plastics on 1/8–1/4 oz jigheads cast across potholes and slowly retrieved are the go-to when current softens and fish focus on smaller bait.
- Sheepshead want small things close to structure: downsize to small crabs or shrimp on a small hook and make short, accurate vertical drops next to oyster faces.
- Controlled drifts with the engine in neutral and a small drogue keep the boat moving the correct angle along oyster lines without burying the prop.

Those are not academic options — they're the stuff that turns marks into fish in these cuts.

## Micro-observations that help decisions

- Redfish tend to show late in the outgoing when bait peels off the grass and runs toward the bars. If you want reds, time the late ebb and target the lips.
- When grass is clean and baited, trout get twitchy and will take topwater at first light. That early pulse is short but explosive.
- Sheepshead hold high tides on oyster edges almost exclusively; you'll rarely find them out on open grass.

These are small patterns, but they save hours of running. Fish the downhill edge first, not the center of the grass run.

## Shallow-water navigation and anchoring

Depths change fast. Mud and shell hide shallow fingers that will ground a prop in minutes. Run the route once at slow speed before committing to a drift or pole pass. Know the day's tidal range: low will expose oyster racks and sandbars; high opens holding water but speeds current through cuts.

Practical boat handling:
- Keep a shallow-running prop and trim for slow planing. A sudden throttle surge can bury the bow and shove you onto a bar.
- Watch shrimp boats and skiffs — trawls disturb the surface, scatter birds and move bait. Treat them like mobile structure and give them room.
- Anchor only on firm bottom. If you must anchor on grass or near oysters use short scope and be ready to reset; anchors drag on soft mud and sticky shell.

These precautions keep you fishing rather than fighting boats or gear.

## Common shallow-water mistakes

Most mistakes here are about angle and attention rather than gear. The frequent errors I see are:

- Fishing the middle of the grass run instead of the downhill edge. Fish hunt the funnel, not the top of the grass.
- Overdrifting at speed. Too much boat speed collapses your presentation window and pushes fish off the edge.
- Dropping on a cut because it looks busy on GPS. A crowded cut can be fished out; always confirm with sonar and bait checks before committing.

Avoid those and you keep your runs productive longer.

## When it's dead and where to look next

If grass looks clean but your arc shows no bait after a northerly blow, expect the bite to have moved into muddier channels or offshore. Pretty water can be empty of bait. In those conditions the efficient move is a short recon to the pass mouth or a quick check of the outer oyster rows where sheepshead and reds will often hold at high tide.

When to come back: after several days of steady southeast wind the inside tends to clean up, bait tightens, and grass-edge windows return. If you see a clear, narrow jet with stacked bait at the pass, plan a morning run on the tide change rather than a midday drift.

## What usually matters here (and what doesn't)

What usually matters most:
- tide timing
- bait concentration on the downhill edge
- current cleanliness through the pass

What usually matters less:
- the exact GPS point on the grass
- random isolated surface boils that don't show bait on sonar

This water isn't showy. It rewards quiet approaches, attention to tide, and simple, accurate presentations fished where the grass meets the cut or the oyster face. Fish it patient, respect the shallow hazards, and bail when the pass dirties; come back on a steady southeast run.
