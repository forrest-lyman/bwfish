What makes this place work is simple and repeatable: a short, clean run through the pass, two jetties that create lee pockets, and a narrow tongue of sand where current and structure compress bait. Most of the worthwhile water sits inside sight of the jetties but behaves like open Gulf—fast, changeable, and oriented to tide. Read the water and you’ll see the bait lines; follow them and you’ll find trout, redfish and sheepshead on tidy tide windows.

## Where the bait compresses: inside jetty, lee pockets, outside gutter

If you picture the pass cross-section, three lines stand out. First, the inside face of the jetties where flood stacks bait into the lee. Second, small lee pockets behind oyster heads and pilings where fish wait for tidally pushed forage. Third, the downhill gutter on the outside where ebb peels that same bait off the flats.

Those are the lanes you work. When bait is pinned to the jetty face the bite gets concentrated and sightable; when the tide turns the bait peels into shallow sand gutters and off grass edges and the action moves to the downhill seams.

Micro-observation: the fish stack on the lee. When bait leaves the jetty face the window is usually closed.

## How the tide and wind build the window

The pass is short, so current changes hard and fast. On flood the jetty lee and inside face collect moving bait; on ebb the concentrated bait streams out through shallow gutters and around oyster flats.

Wind and fetch rewrite clarity. Southeast winds tend to clean the pass and let you sight‑fish the inside face. Northerlies chop and stain water quickly and shorten the useful window. Expect a hard, useful run for a short period—plan your transit to the tide rather than the clock.

A typical window shows this shape:
1. Last hour of ebb — fish leaving the flats; look in gutters and downhill seams.
2. First two hours of flood — bait consolidates on the inside jetty and the lee; best live‑bait and sight opportunities.
3. Mid‑flood slack — often quiet; if the bait disappears, the window ends fast.

## What lives on the tongue and where they sit

Forage is compact: finger mullet, glass minnows, small shrimp and crabs around hard structure, and worms/grass shrimp in the flats. Birds are usually not the signal here—the bite is driven by current and compressed bait, not big surface blowups.

How the species use the lines:
- Spotted seatrout: show at the tail end of the ebb and first into the flood. They hang just off grass edges and in the lee where current slows.
- Red drum: take stronger downhill flows and sit in the sand gutter on ebb; larger reds turn up predictably when mullet are being pushed into the seam.
- Sheepshead: lock to pilings, jetties and oyster heads on the incoming tide; they hold tight and feed hard on crustaceans.

## Reading the three lanes during the window

The pass normally fishes like two distinct lanes once the tide pins bait: an inside lee for live‑bait and sight shots, and a downstream gutter that fishes well on drifting artificials or freelined livies.

When you’re on the inside face, position slightly upcurrent of the lee and let the livie run naturally into the seam. On the outside gutter, run a short cross-drift across the downhill face and sweep your bait through the bait line. If the inside face is full of bait and clean water, start there—fish density and shot quality are higher. If the bait peels off, move to the gutter quickly; the fish follow the food.

## Tactics: live baits, small artificials, and boat work

This spot rewards tidy presentations and controlled boat handling more than brute force.

Live bait and light tackle
- Trout and reds: live shrimp or small finger mullet on a 1/0–2/0 light live‑bait hook with 20–30 lb leader. Use a popping cork around grass edges or freeline at dawn and dusk.
- Fly‑line livies in the lee: hold the boat slightly upcurrent of the line and let the bait run into the seam. That slow, natural presentation converts percentage bites into hard fights.
- Sheepshead: small crab halves, fiddler crabs or green crab on a 1/0–2/0 circle hook, short leader. Fish tight to structure and be ready to lift them out before they bury under oysters.

Artificial options
- 1/8–1/4 oz jigheads with soft plastics along grass edges for trout.
- Paddle tails worked slowly around the mouth produce when fished across the downhill gutter on ebb.

Boat handling and drift
- Drift slow in the lee with the engine idling and a controlled angle into the seam. Too much throttle makes the livie look wrong; too little and you’ll yaw and lose control.
- For the outside gutter, set a short drift across the downhill face, cast upcurrent and let the bait swing through the bait line.
- Anchor only where holding is certain—failed sets on the outer tongue end the day fast.

Safety and transit
Time the pass. A calm run on incoming tide is the single most useful habit here because flood stacks bait and reduces outbound swell over the bar. If a northerly chop builds, don’t push it—the bar steepens fast. Keep the bow into swell when crossing and keep rpm sufficient for steerage; be ready to reverse off rocks if a crossing goes wrong.

## Signs the window is building — and common mistakes

Watch the bait and water clarity. Practical signals the bite is about to go hot:
- Bait piling on the inside jetty face.
- Fish following bait into shallow lee pockets.
- A clean strip of water along the jetty while surrounding water stains.
- Consistent livie takes while you’re still 50–100 yards from the outside gutter.

Common mistakes that waste the window:
- Waiting for slack. The best work is while the tide moves, not on high slack.
- Drifting straight through the jetty instead of treating the inside lee and downstream gutter as separate lanes.
- Using too light a leader for sheepshead—small hooks but stout trace and short leaders that keep baits on the bottom work better.

## Fleet behavior and operational calls

Locals run tight. Charters and private skiffs pile up during the window; when the flood pins bait on the jetty the area fills quickly and pressure alters how fish feed. Experienced skippers cycle through lee pockets quietly and will move a few boatlengths to the first gutter rather than stay crowded.

If visibility drops, favour blind presentations along the inside jetty face with livies rather than fighting sight shots. If you find several boats on your spot, shift to an adjacent lee pocket or the first outside gutter—fish often relocate a short distance rather than vanish.

Final reality check: this is short, practical water. The pass mouth gives fast feedback—if the bait’s there and the current is right you’ll know in an hour. If it isn’t, don’t hang: run inside flats or a nearby reef and let the pass reset for the next tide.
