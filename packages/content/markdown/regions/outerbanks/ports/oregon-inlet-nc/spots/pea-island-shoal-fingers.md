The place looks simple until you stand on the bow and watch the water. Low sand-and-shell fingers peel off Pea Island and angle into the longshore. The inlet’s flood pushes across those fingers; the ebb slides down their outside faces. Where a finger tip meets the longshore pulse you get a tight seam — shallow, fast, and loaded with bait for a short, violent period.

Why the fingers make tight seams
The geometry is the deal. Fingers focus the flow into narrow lanes and create lee pockets where bait and surface life pile up. You don't get a long line of scattered bait here; you get short, 20–50 yard strips where the water churns and food compresses into a shallow column. Predators that can see the column — stripers, kings, cobia — will blitz those strips until the pulse relaxes.

Reading the window
The window is brief and usually shows twice: the first flood out of the inlet and the early ebb when the longshore pulse organizes. Slack is tiny; the first hour of a push is often the money.

- On the flood the inlet rides out warm, bait-rich water and pins mullet, glass minnows and small bay herrings against the up-current face of the fingers.
- On the ebb the longshore pulls a tight tongue down the outside edge; birds, chasing stripers and migrating kings appear along that downhill strip.

The seam is fragile to sea state. A rough bar or onshore swell doesn't eliminate the seam but raises the cost and risk of getting there. Most skippers bail when the bar looks unhappy.

Bait sequence and how fish react
Watch the bait before you pick a lure. On these fingers the bait sits high and thin early, then compresses into a tight ball near the tip as the tide pins it. Birds usually pick it up first; their behavior is the clearest early signal.

Typical feeding sequence I see: bait pushed off the flat by the choke, funnels to the tip, birds tighten and mark the lane, the bait ball condenses, then stripers or kingfish charge in short runs. Cobia show up opportunistically on the lee when the bait mass is big enough to hang around the structure.

What fishes the seams and how they behave
Striped bass turn up early and late in the run—sight and live-bait work best when you can see the bait ball. Kings run through in spring and early summer and take spoons, plugs and livebait along the downhill face. Cobia sit on the lee of fingers or nearby wrecks when rays or larger bait are pushed by; they prefer steady tides and visible bait concentrations.

When the spot is firing you'll see birds in a narrow ribbon, sonar showing bait stacked in the top third of the column, and boats getting short, explosive bursts of action. When it's off the longshore smears the seam into a broad slick, birds thin out, and boats end up crawling the fingers looking for pockets.

Signs the seam is building
- Birds tightening into a narrow ribbon over a finger tip.
- Sonar showing bait from surface into the upper column.
- Current direction pinning right along the finger face.

When those three line up you should be on it.

How the fleet runs it
Local boats treat Pea Island like first-mile insurance or dawn meat. Dawn work is tight to the inlet and the first fingers—quick checks for false albacore and early stripers. If birds and bait pin, boats hold and fish live baits into the ball; if signals are weak they fan out and work tip-to-tip.

Small boats sit inside and grind with jigs, livebait and slow-trolls. Bigger boats will run the longshore seam farther along, chasing cleaner pockets or bigger schools. Pressure matters: foamers get worked over and will sink or scatter after a few boats; experienced skippers either back off to let the bait reform or move a few hundred yards to an untouched pocket.

When the seam dies
A north or northeast wind kills the seam fast. Light north or east winds can be tolerated if they let the inlet pulse run clean. Once the seam smears into a wide, shallow slick the useful bite is gone or moved into numerous small pockets — you have to transition from holding a point to searching.

Tactical notes — what to try and when
Match presentation to the current and the shape of the bait.

- Hold inside versus fan out
  - Hold inside when you have clear bird work, stacked bait, and a steady tide. Small boats can convert with live bait and short drifts.
  - Fan out when bird work is weak, the seam looks smeared on the surface or sonar, or when you want to find the next untouched pocket before the fleet crowds it.

- Presentations that work
  - Live bait under a balloon or free-lined into a tight bait ball for stripers and cobia.
  - Fast spoons, plugs or surface irons down the downhill face for kings and false albacore.
  - Short jigs and 1–2 oz bucktails when bait is close to the bottom along a finger slope.
  - Anchor or short drifts in lee pockets for cobia and tog as slack approaches.

- Tackle and setup
  - Heavy leaders and stout hooks for kings; stripers prefer lighter leaders but keep abrasion resistance in mind around shell and rock.
  - In strong current add weight to keep presentations in the strike zone; in light current strip baits slowly or troll uphill at low speed.
  - Keep a livewell ready. Local triggers are goggle-eyes and cigar minnows.

Quick operational callouts
- Move early. The seam that’s perfect at first light often blurs by mid-morning as tide and wind change.
- Read birds before you read sonar here; birds usually show the seam first and sonar confirms stacking.
- Watch the bar. Pea Island is close, but inlet conditions set your margin for error.

Why fishermen keep checking it
These fingers produce concentrated, sight-driven bait columns that offshore usually don't give you—short runs, fast action, and decisions you can make on the bow. The pattern is repeatable: tide-driven compression into narrow lanes. The trick is to catch the pulse early, choose the right presentation for the current and bait position, and avoid flattening the mark by crowding it. Get in, get on the ball, and get out before the seam blurs or the fleet turns it into a shallow slick.
