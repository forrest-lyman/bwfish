What defines this piece of bottom is geometry. A shallow shoal peels into a short, steep downhill face with pockets of rock and scattered wreck wood. The inlet jet and the longshore flow meet a few miles seaward of the mouth. That collision builds a tight lee on the down‑current side and pins bait to the high spots. On the screen you see stacked marks over seams; on the deck you see short, intense windows of action.

## Why the lee pins bait and why that matters
Observation first: when the first pulse of flood runs, warm bait‑rich water peels over the shoal and slams into the lee side. The high rocks break the flow into fingers and create pockets where forage collects.

Explanation: those pockets turn the site from broad water into a concentrated food patch. Wreck and ledge fish hold in the pockets and on the edges; roaming predators work the outside flank. The result is a short, high-value bite window tied tightly to the inlet pulse and water clarity.

## When the useful bite window opens
The timing is almost always the same: the initial push of flood through the inlet. That first flood brings a distinct outbound/flood pulse that pins bait on the lee. If you meet that first hour of flood the odds are in your favor.

A few caveats:
- Heavy ebb and strong longshore rips shear the seam and push bait off the highs. Marks smear and fish either stack tight on the downhill edges or vanish into the gutter.
- Clean water—light east or northerly winds and minimal surface mixing—gives the clearest bait stacks. When the surface churns, birds and sonar are less reliable cues.

## Where the bait and fish actually sit
On sonar you’ll see tight, dense marks pressing against the high rocks and the immediate downhill slope. Surface bird work is intermittent; often the bait is mid‑column and visible on the screen well before birds commit.

Pattern:
- Bait pins in the lee pockets, on the high rock tops, and along the immediate downhill face.
- Wreck/ledge species (snapper, sea bass, tog) hug the pockets and take presentations that drift past the structure.
- Cobia patrol the outside flank and will hit a live bait or a bait stripped through a ray of chum when a chunk of bait drifts clear of the rocks.

Read the marks before committing. If the bait is compressed tight and high in the column, vertical work and short leaders win. If the marks are smeared on the bottom, you go heavier and slower.

## How the spot fishes—what to do first
If the lee is building, treat this as vertical, structure‑first water:

- Start on the downhill face and fish down into the current. Snapper and sea bass commonly sit 10–30 ft off the rock edge; you want your bait or jig to pass them on a controlled fall.
- Use short, powerful lifts with quick, controlled falls—this makes your offering look injured in the current and invites reaction strikes.
- Keep leader length tight when bait is stacked over wood and rock. Short leaders reduce wraps and keep the hook zone close to the fish holding on the edge.

Why that order? The face funnels presentations. If you fish broad or slow drifts across a hard flood you tend to bury baits and miss the vertical strike zone.

## Anchoring, drifting, and presenting baits
Practical, hands‑on notes from the deck:

- Anchor timing: set up on slack or in the first hour of flood. Fish hold best on the initial push; anchor too early on an ebb and you’ll sit in dead water as the bait slides away.
- Anchor placement: anchor on the lee of the high spot so your bait drifts down the face rather than hanging over the rock. When the flood is strong, use a stern anchor and keep the bow up‑current. That lets you present baits down the slope while maintaining tension and reducing scope.
- Controlled drifts for cobia: if cobia show on the outside flank, work a slow parallel drift along the contour rather than straight down the slope. Keep the boat moving so live baits read as natural and reduce hangups.
- Hangup avoidance: shorten leaders, use single hooks or short droppers, and fish weights you can pull through snags. Avoid long wire traces over the tight rock pockets unless you plan to fish wide of the structure.

If you hang up, mark the spot, back off a boat length and re‑drop. Often the fish are sitting on the edges you just grabbed.

## Tactics when the mobile fish show
Cobia are the mobile bonus here. They’ll show up when a ray of bait drifts free or when stray warm water pushes a temp break near the face.

Tactical calls:
- Switch to live goggle‑eyes or small bluefish when cobia appear. Present them on a slow drift along the outside flank.
- Use short leaders and a steady presentation; long leaders and heavy gear increase hangups and reduce hookup ratios.
- Don’t anchor dead on the rock if the cobia are roaming—stay mobile and parallel the contour.

## Fleet behavior and how experienced skippers set up
Locals run this as a close offshore option on marginal days. Small boats will pick off lee pockets on a tidy flood or slack; larger boats tend to make controlled vertical passes with jigs and back off if the bite fades before running farther out.

Experienced skippers:
- plan to meet the first flood at the lee,
- mark the high spots and set anchor positions that present baits down the face rather than directly over the rock,
- and adjust quickly—if sonar compresses marks up column, shorten leaders and fish under the birds; if marks smear to the bottom, go heavier and slower.

## Signals that the lee is building—or breaking
Watch for these observable cues:

- Bait marks compressing against the high spots on sonar.
- A distinct inlet pulse on the display—the first outbound/flood ring.
- Birds tight on the edge rather than scattered wide.
- A slight temperature gradient on the face compared to adjacent water.

When the lee breaks, marks smear and birds go wide. If that happens, either change to heavier bottom work or move off the high spot before you anchor into dead water.

## What usually matters most (and what doesn’t)
What usually matters more:
- timing with the inlet pulse,
- finding and marking the pockets on sonar,
- setting your anchor or drift to present baits down the downhill face.

What usually matters less:
- long, slow drifts straight down the face during a hard flood (they bury baits),
- staying pinned to one high spot once the bait has pushed off.

Micro‑observation worth remembering: bait pins hard on the lee on the first flood. When that lines up with clean water, the face fishes like someone flipped a switch.

Short window, structure‑first water. Read the tide, find the pockets on the screen, present down the face, and be ready to snap to live‑bait cobia work if the mobile fish show.
