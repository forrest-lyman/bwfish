The harbor mouth is the practical first stop out of San Diego. A solid jetty, a narrow channel throat and nearby sand gutters make a small, active system — lee pockets and downhill edges sit close together and change with every tide. Boats that treat this water as a proper option, not a consolation prize, win more days. Shallow, variable, and quick to punish bad timing: get the tide, the bar and the drift angle right and you run reliable halibut drifts and tight rockfish marks without a long run.

## Why the braided entrance matters — jetty, channel and sand tongues

The entrance isn't one thing — it's a braid of channel through the jetty that peels into sand gutters and rock/kelp fingers. That tight mix of sand and relief gives short, useful ranges within minutes of the marina. When the bar is marginal or the swell rules out the banks, this water is your contingency. Because everything sits close, the tide angle immediately changes where baits work: a sand tongue can go from dead to hot across a single tide change.

## Tide angle and the sand gut — how each tide fishes

Tide angle is the core signal here. The jetty and channel bend the flow; that creates predictable downhill and lee drifts.

- Ebb — the day‑to‑day halibut window. Outbound current peels off the channel, scouring the gutters and sweeping bait down the outside edge. A steady ebb gives a long, controllable drift that lets whole baits sweep the flat‑to‑edge transitions.
- Flood — better for rockfish and lingcod in tight pockets. The flood pins bait to the jetty and lee pillows; fish stack tight to rocks and kelp necks. Drifts are shorter and more positional, so you work vertical drops and short-range presentations.
- Slack — usually kills surface feeds and confuses sand‑gut drifts. If the bite is on the surface or dependent on a downhill sweep, slack collapses it fast.
- Angle matters — aim for a 30–45° drift into the gut relative to the face. Too steep and your baits bury in the sand; too shallow and you drag off the edge and miss the transition.

The practical habit: watch how the channel peels and set your throttle to keep that 30–45° sweep. If you can’t hold it cleanly, move a few hundred yards up or down until the boat rides the current predictably.

## Signs a useful drift is building

You don’t guess here — you read small signals.

- Surface color and foam: the sand tongue often shows as a change in color and narrow foam lines running off the jetty.
- Sonar: bait hugging the downhill edge or a dark ledge mark at depth is the most reliable confirmation.
- One boat holding a clean edge beats a scattered fleet. When a single boat sits quietly on the edge with bait working, that’s usually the real mark.
- Birds feeding over a tight boil on the outside of the jetty matters — especially when sonar confirms bait below them.

Don’t confuse random gulls over the harbor with a true bait field. Confirm with electronics before committing a run.

## What fishes here and when

This is close‑in, mixed‑bottom water. Know the windows and target accordingly.

- Pacific halibut — the primary target. They live the sand gutters and outside sand tongues and show reliably on steady ebb drifts, especially when a bar window lines up.
- Rockfish and lingcod — work the jetty toe, kelp necks and scattered rock patches on flood or tide turns. Short drops and vertical jigging pay off when bait holds tight to relief.
- Sandbass and white seabass — occasional; short bursts around kelp necks and nearshore ledges when bait tightens.
- Small tuna/bonito — very occasional. Schools can chop through on a warm finger close to shore, but don’t depend on it.

Read the tide first, then pick the species you can present to consistently.

## How I set up the drift and the why behind it

Keep it simple and positionally strict.

- Control drift on the gut: motor into the drift, run a flasher or whole bait and hold throttle to maintain the angle. Mark the initial drop and watch how the boat rides the current for a couple passes before changing.
- Multiple passes win: fish will often commit after one or two clean presentations. Don’t tinker the drift on the first pass unless you see something wrong.
- Vertical checks on the fingers: stop for short, tight drifts or a short set‑anchor near kelp/rock fingers and vertical jig the face where bait stacks.
- Sonar first: in tight water birds can be misleading. Dark marks on down‑imaging and bait on the top 20–40 ft are the confirmations you trust.

Crew roles matter here: one runs throttle and keeps the drift, one watches sonar and marks, one tends rods. Tight teamwork over noisy radio chatter.

## When to push to kelp and canyon shoulders

This spot is the default when the banks are blown, but it’s also a staging ground for pushing out.

- Work the kelp/ledge line inside the first 5 miles if the bar or swell rules out a long run. Live bait under a kite or fast chum passes pick off yellowtail and seabass when water cleans near kelp.
- Only commit to canyon shoulders when you have three signals: clean water, a stable temp break and birds or sonar bait. Don't go on rumor; verify as you run out.
- Move offshore in stages: run cross‑lines toward canyon heads rather than ripping straight out. You’ll confirm bait and current as you go and avoid wasting a long run on no marks.

## Common mistakes and pressure reactions

Know the usual screwups — they repeat every season.

- Fishing the gut on slack: too many boats wait for a "magic" slack and then wonder why the bite evaporated. The guts work on a moving tide.
- Anchoring the wrong edge: dropping anchor across a natural sweep blocks the drift. Anchor only to work a specific rock or kelp neck short and tight.
- Assuming birds equal entry: harbor birds often feed on small forage unrelated to the gutters. Confirm with sonar.
- Crowding the jetty toe: pressure scatters bait. When the jetty gets busy, pull a few hundred yards downcurrent to the sand edge and keep quiet presentations.

If you want more reliability, favor the edge a few yards outside the busiest line — fewer boats, cleaner bait and steadier drifts.

## Bar, seam and safety notes to respect

This is small‑boat friendly water but it’s unforgiving when the bar is rough.

- Check the bar and harbor release every time. A one‑hour window is not a blanket pass—conditions change quickly with NW swell.
- Watch the ebb tongue: a strong ebb can produce a short, steep breaking tongue at the jetty that will pull a small boat off heading. Time your shallow approach early or late on smaller runs.
- If the bar is marginal, you’ll still get worthwhile days inside kelp and inner ledges. If the bar is closed, do not force it.
- Keep crew on the bow when maneuvering near rocks and maintain speed control on the approach — currents braid around the jetties.

Respect the bar. If the seam looks wrong, work inside and fish smart.

## Quick tactical checklist

- Best tides: steady ebb for halibut; flood and tide‑turn pockets for rockfish.
- Presentations: drifted whole bait or flasher rigs over the sand gut; vertical jigs and short drops on kelp/rock fingers.
- Electronics: mark the sand edge, watch bait on the top 20–40 ft, and use down‑imaging to find the edge between sand and relief.
- Crew work: one person running drift/throttle, one watching sonar, one tending lines.

## Fleet behavior and a final micro‑observation

Locals split decisively on run distance. Short‑range boats treat the harbor entrance as primary water; larger boats check the channel and keep going if the seam and birds show farther out. Reports move fast — when one boat finds a solid halibut drift it tends to stay quiet; when surface signs build, radio chatter sends the fleet outbound.

Micro‑observation: slack often kills a surface bite. The best halibut mornings are steady, small‑ebb drifts that let baits sweep the outside edge without burying. If the banks are blown, fish the gut like it matters — because most days, it does.
