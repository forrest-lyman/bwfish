What separates this strip from a single reef or charted bench is movement. Out here the structure is made of kelp rafts, floating weed, and short downhill necks on the near shelf. They behave like micro‑reefs when bait is pushed off them — a short, obvious downhill presentation appears and the yellowtail bite can be explosive if you find the seam and present live bait correctly.

Below I lay out the observable patterns: how the paddies form and die, what the sonar and birds usually tell you, how the best boats set kites and chum, and the safe limits for doing it. I’m not rehashing basics — this is about reading a moving patch of kelp and deciding fast.

Why mobile paddies matter: they act like micro‑reefs

The paddies themselves aren’t special until current gives them a downhill. When a raft sits so bait can be squeezed and pushed off the lee side you get a corridor — bait funnels down 100–500 yards and yellowtail use that downhill as a hunting lane. The raft becomes an ambush edge; without the downhill it’s just weed.

Observations first:
- Productive paddies show bait stacked tight against kelp trunks and a visible downhill sweep off the face.
- A good raft lights up for 20–90 minutes, then it either gets picked clean or drifts out of the window.
- If bait is milling and the raft stalls, the school will often sink or move on.

Reading downhill flow and color seams

Start visually, then confirm with a short troll and sonar. A slim blue tongue along the kelp trunks, a slick line where bait is compressed, or a visible surface slick are the signs that current is squeezing bait off the pad and making a downhill neck.

Why the color seam matters: that contrast is where the water direction becomes obvious. The fish don't care about an isolated raft unless the current gives it a direction to hunt. In practical terms, we chase seams, not names.

Current and swell behavior that changes everything

The active pattern is a downhill sweep combined with a modest along‑shelf push. Even a few degrees of downhill angle is enough to carry bait from the pad toward the face and make kites and droppers present naturally.

Northwest swell and wind are the binary switch here. A clean, light NW keeps paddies intact and bait on the face. Once NW or west swell gets raw — or NW wind exceeds about 15–18 knots — paddies scatter, kites foul, and the good water is driven inside. When the swell builds above roughly 4–5 ft the outer paddies go trashy and the place becomes hazardous for kite work.

Bait, birds, and what the sonar tells you

Primary forage is sardines and small mackerel/anchovy pushed into the kelp fringe. Those bait balls strip off as current squeezes and roll down the downhill face. Birds can be quiet here; we often see yellowtail, amberjack, or tuna without much bird activity. Sonar showing bait stacked 10–40 ft off the kelp trunks is a more reliable indicator than sporadic bird reports.

Watch for:
- Tight bait stacks near trunks — that’s immediate interest.
- Small foamers, a slick line, or subtle color contrast when birds are absent.
- Paddies acting as staging points: predators will sit off the pad and hit the downhill corridor rather than feed openly on the kelp for long.

Species and how they behave here

Yellowtail are the headline — they convert consistently when you place live bait under a kite or with downhill chum. White seabass and larger calico/kelp bass turn up when paddies sit over mixed hardbottom. Occasionally a smaller bluefin or surface tuna will hit a dense, clean bait patch, but that’s not the rule; yellowtail dominate.

Fleet behavior and pressure

When a paddy lights up the boats swarm. Small boats and day charters will circle, set kites quickly, and convert fast. Experienced skippers don’t linger on pretty paddies — they crossline and check the downhill with a short troll and sonar pass. Pressure matters: first boats get the best bites. As a paddy gets crowded fish go deeper and pickier.

Search strategy — practical steps

Move fast and check the downhill before committing.

- Run crosslines into and across every visible kelp string. The productive raft rarely looks like its neighbor.
- Circle each raft and do a slow pass with sonar. If bait is present but not stacked, push on.
- If you don’t see birds, look for foamers, slicks, or a subtle color change that indicates compression.

These aren’t philosophical points — they’re what tells you to set a kite or leave.

Kite and live deployment — what wins

Set kites when you have a clear downhill direction, surface push or birds, and 200–500 yards of working water ahead of the raft. The goal is to present livies so they drift downhill over the face, not parallel to the kelp line.

Typical spread that works in these conditions:
- Two to four livies on the kite, with shorter droppers closer to the boat.
- One bridled presentation 30–60 ft off the kite.
- A couple of heavier livies free‑lined behind if fish are aggressive.

Keep the kite angled into the downhill flow. When a hookup happens close to the kite cut the kite line and convert fast — the school will often give a run of short hits. Reset another live immediately; prolonged presentations lose the fish.

Chum technique and quick conversions

Chum light and fast. Heavy, continuous chum in a paddy scatters small bait and turns the kelp into a mess. Run short, active chumming that simulates a fleeing bait ball and pitch fresh live bait right into the slick.

If the first live is refused, pitch another immediately rather than letting the presentation sit. When fish begin to spread and mill, pause chumming and move to a neighboring raft — picked‑over behavior is real.

Common mistakes to avoid

- Circling a pretty paddy without checking whether it has a downhill.
- Over‑chumming until bait scatters and fish sink.
- Running kites into raw NW swell and fouling lines on kelp.
- Waiting too long to convert a strike — first conversions are the most reliable.

Safe operating limits and when to change tactics

Kelp work is close‑in work; respect the sea. If NW wind is above 15–18 knots or the west‑component swell builds above ~4–5 ft, paddies stop being an advantage. Kites foul easily and retrieving big fish near kelp risks tangles and prop issues.

When conditions go rough:
- Shift inside to belly‑water kelp necks and nearshore ledges where swell is moderated.
- Prefer free‑lined livies or short drifts over kiting in fog or reduced visibility.
- Slow your search, rely on sonar, and keep careful watch on fleet traffic.

What usually matters most — and least

What usually matters more: downhill angle, bait shape, and drift control. Get those right and you win the day.

What usually matters less: the exact paddy name or one prior day's report. These rafts move and the pattern today is what counts.

Operational note

Treat every kelp raft like a short, tactical piece of structure. Move fast until the downhill shows. Set a tight, responsive kite spread, chum in short bursts to convert quickly, and pull out when the NW builds. The paddies are a high‑probability play when they’re behaving — when they aren’t, they’ll cost you time and risk.
