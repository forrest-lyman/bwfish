## Why Lido Ledge fishes like a close bank

You feel it before you see it: a compact string of rocks and tight paddies sitting on a short downhill face, minutes from the channel. It behaves more like close bank water than open shelf—short runs, concentrated bait, and fast windows. Because the structure is small and right off the harbor, you can go from dock to live bait on feeding fish inside an hour on a tidy morning. That proximity makes Lido a first‑check spot. If one paddy is working, you’ll know quickly; if not, you can move inside without wasting the day.

Why it works: the paddies have clear up‑current faces and immediate lee pockets downhill. Bait funnels and compresses on those uphill faces; yellowtail stage on the downhill seams and ambush bait pushed off the paddy. The geometry is tight—current, bait and structure line up over a few hundred yards—so the bite is compact and pressure‑sensitive.

## Reading current through the paddies

Observation first: when a paddy shows an uphill ripple and birds concentrate on the lee, the zone usually fires for 30–90 minutes. If the paddies sit flat and birds are absent, the bite’s probably off.

Key moving parts:
- up‑current faces of paddies and ledges — this is the feed side where bait compresses.
- lee seams between paddies — calm pockets where fish will hold and eat.
- tide angle through the harbor mouth — incoming tends to shove bait into necks; a strong ebb can pull bait off the face and shut the bite.

Practical read: check the direction of the push into the harbor mouth. If tide angle feeds the uphill face, stage for live bait or quick high‑speed work. If tide goes cross or strong ebb, don’t bail out of the area entirely—look for neighboring lee seams or deeper fingers where fish relocate.

## Bait signs that mean the paddy will produce

What to watch for: birds, bait behavior, and sonar. Those three tell you whether to set up or move on.

Typical build looks like:
- bait popping on the up‑current side
- birds hovering tight over one or two paddies
- sonar showing dense, shallow balls hugging the face
- occasional foamers or small porpoise pushes on warm days

Dolphins and persistent diving terns are the confirmation. A neat, dense ball on the face with birds locked on it is the signal to commit — live bait or a fast chum sprint will usually produce.

## How the fleet shapes the bite

Lido is short water; pressure shows fast. An early fast boat can convert a tidy paddy into a classic 60–90 minute yellowtail window. Once several boats sit on the same paddy the fish either sink, go quiet or move to the next reasonable lee seam.

Typical fleet behavior:
- small, quick boats check paddies first and spread high‑speed chum
- larger boats will anchor a tidy lee if live bait shows and feed it with chilled sardines
- the fleet fragments quickly toward any thermal finger or better canyon water

Practical read: if you arrive early and score, expect the window to be short. If you’re late and the paddy already has boats, either set up on an adjacent seam or move on. Don’t overcommit to a single pressured patch.

## High‑speed chum vs live‑bait anchoring — when to pick which

Observation: when bait is clean and the lee is tidy you can work either method effectively. The decision comes down to fish behavior and how compressed the bait is.

When to high‑speed chum: use it when fish are aggressive on the surface and you need to get them to commit quickly. Fast sprints bring fish up from a short depth and are effective on tight yellowtail windows.

When to anchor live bait: anchor or micro‑drift on the up‑current face when live sardines hold and you want cleaner hookups. Live bait is more selective and converts better when fish are settling into the lee seam.

Short checklist for high‑speed chum:
- start light and build steady; don’t blow bait immediately
- short burst chumming, then quick high‑speed spread to get fish surfaced
- run two to four lighter feathers with a couple heavier stickbaits behind the spread
- be ready to switch to live bait or drop a bait if fish sit down—chunking rarely needed here

Live‑bait anchoring/drift notes:
- anchor or drift so live sardines ride naturally into the lee seam
- keep minimal rode to feel the bite immediately; long anchors let you swing and lose the natural presentation
- micro‑drift: when tide lightens, motor down a paddy line and present live bait on light leaders—yellowtail often hit instantly when the bait tracks naturally

## Presentation, leaders and hooks

Observation: the right leader and hook give you immediate hookup feel and let the fish play without constant leader failure.

Live‑bait yellowtail:
- mainline: 40–60 lb braid
- leader: 30–50 lb fluorocarbon, 3–6 ft
Keep leaders short enough to present naturally but long enough to absorb head shakes.

Feathers and stickbaits:
- run 30–60 lb leaders depending on visibility and fish size
- balance lighter feathers with a couple heavier rods so you can convert a fast scramble into a cleaner hookup

When bigger fish or white seabass show:
- step up to 60–80 lb leader; for chunking into larger fish go short and stout—80–120 lb
- hooks: circles for live‑bait yellowtail when retention is the goal; big singles or 5/0–8/0 for chunking conversions into larger fish

## Common mistakes that kill windows

You’ll see the same errors over and over. Avoid them.

- overcommitting to a single paddy after the first two boats pressure it—fish relocate to neighboring lee seams quickly
- anchoring broadside to the current—presentations look unnatural and live bait gets eaten by small bait fish
- blasting through the kelp line at trolling speed—set on the edge and work the face first

Micro‑observation: a tidy live‑bait hookup within the first 20 minutes usually means the paddy will produce for the next hour. If you haven’t had that in 20, reassess.

## Signs the window is building and when to leave

Signals the window is building:
- birds tightening over one paddy instead of skittering
- sonar marks hugging the up‑current face
- live bait holding on the up‑current side without immediate scattering
- a small color change or a 1°F uptick across the neck over a few hundred yards

When to pull lines and move:
- bait breaks into scattered dots on sonar and birds lift off
- tide shifts from a clean push to a cross or ebb that pulls bait away from faces
- larger canyon or temp reports come over the radio—if better water shows, don’t fight it

## Short operational sequence from Newport

1. Check the nearest paddies uphill of the channel mouth first—close and quick.
2. If birds/sonar line up, set a small chum line and stage live baits on the up‑current faces.
3. Anchor or micro‑drift into the lee; present live bait and keep a tight spread of feathers.
4. If the zone fades, move incrementally to adjacent paddies—don’t hang on a single pressured patch.

Lido Ledge fishes like a busy little bank: quick checks, tidy setups, and the willingness to move when the water changes. Read the paddies, feel the tide, and feed the lee before the fleet picks it apart.
