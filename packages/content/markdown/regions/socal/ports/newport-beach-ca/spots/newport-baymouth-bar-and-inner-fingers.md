## How the baymouth bar organizes the whole trip
First observation is simple: the bar sits between the launch and the canyon like a decision gate. It's a shallow sand tongue with a shoal skirt, a handful of small sand pockets and little rock fingers in the first mile outside the harbor. From the dock it's visible and fast to run into—so when the mouth is calm you steam through and keep going. When it’s marginal the bar becomes the day.

That shallow tongue matters for two reasons. One, it reshapes the harbor outflow into short, tide-driven gutters. Two, those gutters drop into small downhill holes within very shallow water—exactly where halibut and bait organize. The area is also exposed to short-period chop and NW wind; compared with nearby ports the bar can be a harsher gate. Practically, that exposure means the bar is as much a weather test as a fishery.

## When the bar decides: run plans and safe transit
Observation first: the state of the bar decides whether you commit to the canyon or put the day into the fingers.

If short-period wind chop or an onshore push piles into the mouth, the tidy gutters you want for halibut drifts get messy or disappear. That’s the time to tie your plan to a bar report and build a conservative fuel and turnaround margin. Time your run on the slacker end of the tide to reduce bar push. Watch the harbor queue and shallow markers—after storms that shallow tongue shoals quickly.

Typical run reasoning I use:

- If the bar is calm and swell period is long or southerly, I’m inclined to commit to the canyon head.
- If the bar looks marginal or there’s onshore short chop, I plan for inside work: shorter drifts, more anchors, earlier recall.

If you must work inside on a forecasted offshore day, pick a lee pocket and run a single, controlled drift rather than long, exposed runs across the tongue.

## Where the fish live: gutters, sand holes and current seams
Start with the patterns. The tidal exchange through the mouth carves short gutters and small downhill flows off the sand tongue. Bait congregates in those gutters; halibut park where the gutter frames a sand hole. On an ebb you’ll see a distinct ebb tongue that lines up gutters and bait seams in 10–25 ft of water. Those seams show up as tight, slow-moving arcs on sonar.

Two important caveats: fresh swell or onshore wind will hide or reverse those seams and make the bite difficult. Long-period swell, however, tends to smooth the runout and often improves visibility and bait behavior—so a cleaner run with long swell can be better than a glassy, short-chop day.

Micro-observation: the bait lives in the gutter; the halibut live where the gutter drops into the hole. Read the seam first, then place your presentation.

## Bait and life on the fingers
What you’ll see: sand-eel runs, small anchovy/young sardine pushes and crab/ghost shrimp on the flats. Bird work is less frequent here than outside the harbor, but gulls and terns show up on tide pushes and where bait balls compress in gutters. Often the bar holds bait that’s been pushed out of the harbor on an ebb; on sonar that looks like tight arcs hugging the gutter.

That bait pattern explains timing. When bait is tight on the downhill edge of a pocket the halibut are positioned to ambush. When bait scatters, fish refuse—or they move into the lee of small structure between drifts.

## Sand holes and halibut drifts — practical workups
Observation: the highest-percentage drifts start on the uphill side and work across the gutter into the pocket. The transition from a shallow gutter to a deeper sand pocket is where halibut sit.

How I run it in practice: mark the pocket on sonar, set a short, controlled drift uphill into the current seam, and present cut or live baits across the drop at slow speed. Repeat the run from slightly different entry angles rather than blasting the same line repeatedly. If you get soft marks or nothing at all, shorten the drift and try a different angle—halibut in these shallow pieces respond to angle and subtle current changes.

Signs a hole will hold halibut:
- Bait on the downhill edge of the pocket.
- Sonar marks that hold tight to the bottom at the edge rather than scattering.
- A steady tide moving through the mouth, not a completely slacked condition.

Those are practical, actionable signals—not theory. Mark, enter uphill, present slow, and repeat.

## When you choose to stay inside: seabass and sandbass windows
The inside picks up work when a few triggers occur: dirty water outside, onshore wind making the canyon unsafe, short-period chop, or a cold pulse that kills pelagic surface activity but still compresses bait into the gutter mouths. Under those conditions the fingers and small rock patches fish like a full plan, not a backup.

Tactics here shift toward slow presentations and edge work. White seabass and other basses show in low-light windows along nearshore edges, rock fingers and kelp patches—early morning and late light matter. Sand bass and croaker are reliable on short days, especially when southern water shuts down the canyon.

Priority tactics:
- Focus on downhill faces and lee sides of pockets.
- Present vertically or slowly; pressured flatfish are often spooky.
- Treat low-light periods as your primary seabass window.

## Fleet behavior, pressure and common mistakes
When the canyon is closed the fleet compresses on the bar and fingers. Bite windows shrink under pressure. Experienced boats will spread drift angles, mark a hole and give it breathing room, or run controlled repeated uphill drifts instead of long noisy passes. Less experienced boats often blunder through the same seam, which pushes halibut deeper and shortens seabass daytime activity.

Common mistakes to avoid:
- Treating the bar like a parking lot without checking a bar report. The mouth changes quickly with wind and swell.
- Running long, fast drifts that cross the gutter too quickly. Shorter uphill drifts convert better.
- Fishing inside marks like offshore structure—these halibut respond to subtle changes in current and angle; blasting through will lose fish.

Pressure signs to watch: halibut sink deeper and stop chasing flashy presentations; seabass bites move toward low-light or nocturnal timing as more boats pile in.

## Tactical checklist (short)
- Confirm bar and transit state before you leave.
- Mark the gutter and sand pocket on sonar; commit to uphill entries.
- Run short, slow presentations across the drop and vary entry angles.
- If the canyon’s closed, treat the fingers as a full-day plan rather than a backup stop.

## Final observation
The baymouth bar is not just a navigational obstacle—it’s a system. When it’s marginal it becomes the fishery. Read the gutters, time the tide, fish uphill into the current, and only push for the canyon when the bar and swell period give you the seam you want.
