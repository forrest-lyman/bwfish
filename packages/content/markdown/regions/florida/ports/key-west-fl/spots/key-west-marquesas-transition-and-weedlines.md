Where the reef steps off and the Stream starts to finger
The Marquesas Transition is that stretch where the Keys’ shallow reef gives way to cleaner shelf and the Florida Current starts sending warm filaments along the top. It isn’t a single line on a chart — it’s a place where color breaks, weed and blue water meet, and the Current makes short, productive lanes. When the pieces line up you get stacked weedlines with mahi, wahoo on the blue fringe, and blackfin tucked under the bait.

How far and when to commit
Expect 25–35 nm west‑southwest of Key West. With most boats that’s 1.5–3 hours one way depending on sea state and speed. Run it when three practical signals come together: clean blue water, working birds, and a visible color break on the horizon. If you don’t have those, you’re usually burning fuel on a soft omen.

The water, the seam, and what to read first
Look for a narrow strip of darker blue or green against paler water with birds sitting on the edge and floating weed holding small bait. The pattern I watch for is simple:

- A warm filament or blue patch peeling off the Current and riding the shelf.
- Tidal and along‑shelf shear that slows the water over the outer shelf and lets bait stack.
- Wind driving sargassum and debris into narrow, holdable weedlines.

Sonar will confirm it: bait streaks with marks stacked down‑current of a slick are the best sign. Marks scattered without a color break or birds are usually noise.

What the bait and life tell you
Primary forage here runs to flyingfish and small burlap‑school bait under sargassum. On the surface you’ll see little concentrations of mahi on the weed, birds strafing the edge, and the occasional dolphin working the lanes. Down a bit, blackfin will sit under bait when a thermocline sets over the slope. On cleaner seams with a firm temperature jump you can see sailfish or a lone marlin; that’s rarer but it happens.

When the corridor fires
Windows are short and sharp. Typical triggers are:

- A narrow warm filament or blue patch riding tight along the shelf.
- The tide or Stream laying a clean seam against a weedline.
- Birds and debris coinciding with a visible temperature break.

When those three line up you get fast spread‑trolling hits and quick live‑bait conversions. When the tide’s wrong — cross‑shelf or slack — the same water often looks dead.

How the fleet approaches it and why spacing matters
Local boats treat this like lanes and traffic. Early scouts run out to find the filament and call color and bird work. When a lane firms, boats stage along its length and space wider than reef or inshore crews would. Two reasons: surface feeders spook easily, and the productive zone is often a dozen yards off the weed, not on it. Experienced crews move gear fast between presentations — fast skirted lures on the blue edge, teasers and small plugs over weed, and live baits or downriggered offerings where blackfin stack.

Signs the window is building
Watch for these on approach — when several appear the lane is usually about to pop:

- Birds concentrating on a narrow band instead of being scattered.
- A weedline holding a steady line of debris rather than breaking apart.
- Sonar showing bait streaks with fish marks underneath.
- A visible surface temperature difference across 200–500 yards on a probe.

Tactical staging and how I choose presentations
I run multiple presentations and pick which to emphasize as the lane talks back.

- Fast lures on the blue fringe for wahoo and the quicker mahi strikes. Wahoo want speed and clean water; be ready with wire leaders and faster trolling when birds leave the weed and chase into the blue.
- Skaters, teasers, and small plugs over the weed for mahi feeding the surface. Mahi like slow to moderate speeds around sargassum; slow down if the weed holds fish.
- Live baits or downriggered weighted baits toward the inside of the seam for blackfin that are sitting under bait at 30–60 ft. If tunas are deeper, downriggers or heavier weights will beat surface‑only options.

If mahi stack on the weed a small slick and a bit of chunk will hold them. Keep the boat oriented with the current — sloppy corrections and over‑anchoring the prop scatter a school faster than anything else. Run a few clean passes, mark the best shows, and move on; most lanes close quickly under pressure.

Depth and speed notes
Bait often sits 30–60 ft here. If you see marks down in that band don’t be shy with downriggers. Speed is the other lever: slow to moderate for weedbound mahi, faster and cleaner for wahoo. Match the speed to what the birds and the weed tell you — they’ll show if the fish want pace or a hang.

Common mistakes that kill a window
The mistakes I see most often are repeatable and avoidable:

- Committing to a single foamer without checking color and temp — foamers can be wind‑pushed bait, not a filament.
- Running only skirted lures into a weedline when blackfin are holding under the bait — you need a live bait option inside as backup.
- Parking too close to a tight weedline; the fish often work a dozen yards off the edge and pressure kills fast.

Micro‑observations that separate trips
The best lanes I’ve fished are where a warm finger rubs an offshore weedline and birds sit at that seam. When that happens the bite switches from sparse to instant. The hard part is finding that moment, committing before the patch peels offshore, and moving when the window slams shut.

Why boats keep checking this corridor
It’s the closest repeatable piece of bluewater that turns a Key West reef day into a pelagic trip. You don’t need a canyon or big swell — just a filament, a weedline, and the Current doing what it always does: moving food along the shelf. Find the moment when all three line up and you’ll know why local boats keep running back.
