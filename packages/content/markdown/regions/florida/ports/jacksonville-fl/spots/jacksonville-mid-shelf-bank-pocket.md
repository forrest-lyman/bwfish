## Where to run and what you’ll see on the surface

Run 20–40 nm ESE from the St. Johns jetty, keeping the inlet mouth to your west. On the surface the pocket reads as a cleaner blue lane inside a warmer patch, or a short, loose weedline hugging scattered relief. It isn’t a single reef—think patchwork: low ledges, shell ribs and sand pockets. Those small features act like Velcro for bait when a thermal fringe stalls over the mid‑shelf.

Observation first: when the filament stalls the surface warms, birds tighten, and boats start moving from the inlet ledges to that 20–40 nm band. Explanation second: the shelf contours and scattered relief slow current fingers enough to compress bait along that warm fringe.

## Bottom structure that matters

The charts don’t show anything dramatic. What matters is the mix:

- alternating sand and shell ridges
- isolated low relief ledges and occasional rock outcrops
- shallow shelf contours that step down in short runs

Those pieces are enough to pin pogies and threadfins. The pocket normally sits where a warm surface filament stalls against the mid‑shelf contours and small ledges—bait piles on the down‑current side of the relief.

## When the pocket actually works

The pocket answers to three signals: a visible surface temperature uptick, bait compression on the ledges, and a slowdown of the offshore current finger. Seasons and behavior follow patterns rather than fixed dates.

- Spring / fall: you get tight, fast blackfin and sail windows when warm filaments drift close to the shelf. Birds show early; boats sweep ledges looking for bait.
- Summer: sargassum riders and a stronger warm surface layer turn this into a mahi and yellowfin spot—fast, short-lived schools.
- Winter: pockets are rare. When they form the bait is tightly stacked and sightings are brief.

In practice: the pocket is a timing and connection play. If the filament isn’t touching the shelf in the right way, the marks will be ghostly.

## Getting there: the bar, tide and commitment

This is mid‑shelf work. You need a clear bar window and a willingness to commit to a 3–6 hour day.

1. Time the outside run to a clean bar report and a favorable tide—early flood or slack outbound usually makes the first mile easier.
2. Expect 1.5–3 hours transit one way depending on conditions and speed.
3. If the bar is sloppy, fish the inlet ledges and wait.

Rule: don’t force the bar. When wind and offshore tide line up against the channel the window can shut quickly. If you have a marginal bar report, the pocket isn’t worth the risk.

## Bait first—how life stacks and what to watch for

Bait drives this pocket. Pogies, threadfins and small glass minnows that slip off the inlet ledges will stack on the down‑current side of a ledge or in shallow sand pockets. When a filament stalls, the bait compresses under a thin warm slick; birds and dolphins often show before sonar picks up the marks.

Work the bait first—your odds improve when the bait is clearly stacked above structure.

Signs the window is building
- birds working in tighter arcs instead of milling
- a surface temperature rise of a couple degrees on the plotter
- isolated weed or foam lines running in the pocket
- sonar marks climbing toward the surface over a ledge

Those are observable, repeatable signals. If you see one without the others, don’t commit—wait until bait compression and the temp break line up.

## Primary targets and how they act

What you’ll see and when:

- Sailfish and blackfin/small yellowfin: spring and fall transitional windows when the filament touches the shelf—short, aggressive bites.
- Mahi: summer sargassum passes through the pocket—fast, explosive schools that move on quickly.
- Wahoo: shows up on strong uphill troll days when a warm finger accelerates bait along a shallow ridge.
- Occasional larger yellowfin: when the stream edge is closer and the pocket links to a warmer filament farther offshore.

Pattern note: most bites are short and localized. Treat the area like a stopwatch—get on the fish quickly and be ready for it to shut down.

## How the fleet searches and compresses

Locals run this pocket the same way most mid‑shelf windows get worked: wide sweeps to find birds or a color break, then tight compression once bait or birds are found.

- Search pattern: long uphill troll sweeps across the pocket, then short sprints to a visible color change or bird concentration.
- Pressure behavior: expect 4–8 boats to compress on a working patch. Good captains hold a staggered approach and don’t pile in until the bait is clearly stacked.
- When it’s quiet: boats will drift the larger shell pockets, jig vertical over marks, or tighten the spread and run toward the stream edge.

Observation: early restraint pays. Too many boats on a thin weedline blows the window.

## Tactics that actually work

Start with the bait signal and match presentation to water clarity and how the bait sits.

Trolling
- Run uphill across the seam with a staggered spread—short leaders inboard, longer outside.
- In clean water slow the lead lures; in stained or rough water speed up and run lures deeper.
- When sargassum appears, use flatlines and ballyhoo on the outside.

Live‑bait suspension
- Anchor or controlled‑drift on the down‑current side when sonar shows bait stacked over a ledge.
- Suspend live pogies or threadfins just above the marks. Keep presentations subtle—high enough to avoid snags but in the strike zone.

Vertical jigs and light bait
- If bait is tight on the ledge, vertical jigs will out-produce slow‑trolled plugs. Work the face, then drop back to a light bait if the initial push stops.

Sargassum / flatline
- When weedlines push through, put out flatlines and small, high‑action lures. Mahi and short wahoo runs are sudden—be ready to throttle down and fly out a bait quickly.

Practical note: adjust quickly. This pocket changes by the hour; what worked on the previous pass may be useless ten minutes later.

## Common mistakes that kill a window

- Committing to a color break without confirming bait compression.
- Crowding a small weedline—fish often sit down‑current of the pile; give it space.
- Trying to run the bar home into an opposing tide and wind—don’t sacrifice a safe return for one more pass.
- Chasing yesterday’s GPS waypoint—this pocket depends on the filament’s position today, not yesterday.

## Safety, fuel and crew tasks

Treat the trip like a real offshore run: clear bar window, fuel margin, daylight to get back.

- One person monitors the plotter and bar reports; another handles lines during compression.
- Carry extra fuel and a plan for the stream edge if you have to push farther when the pocket goes dead.
- Remember: the safe window can close in an hour if wind and tide stack against the bar.

## When to stay inside

If the inlet is messy or the bar report is marginal, the pocket is irrelevant. In those conditions work the inlet ledges and structure—those bites are often tighter and more reliable than a risky mid‑shelf run.

## Final observation

This mid‑shelf pocket is a timing and connection play. Find the warm pocket, confirm bait stacks on the ledges, and match your presentation. When the three signals line up the bite is explosive and short; when they don’t, the marks are ghostly and the spread is wasted. Fish the signs, not the waypoint.
