The Neck is a short, sharp piece of water you check first when the local tide is running. It isn’t deep mystery water—it's a predictable interaction of a narrow throat, fast flow, outside sand tongues and small lee pockets. The inlet accelerates the current, peels bait off the flats, and drops it across the outside tongues and jetty eddies where tarpon, snook and cobia time the conveyor.

Why the Neck compresses bait
The observable part is simple: fast water through a narrow mouth forces a seam. Mullet, small crabs and glass minnows get stripped off the flats and pushed into cleaner, faster water. Where that fast strip meets the slower pockets—outside sand tongues, jetty lees, ferry lane edges—you get concentrated bait and a short downhill seam into deeper water. Fish don’t sit on the open shelf here unless something is actively moving bait through the neck. The tide does the work; predators position themselves and wait.

Reading the tidal window
This inlet gives you windows, not all-day fishing.

- Ebb: the best packet is mid to late ebb. That’s when the flow has enough bite to peel bait off the flats and the outside tongue will hold a returning seam. Expect a 45–90 minute window on a steady ebb—the bite often arrives as a tight packet rather than a drawn-out bite.
- Flood: cleaner inshore water pushes bait toward the throat. Snook and tarpon will stage tight inside the neck on the push; cobia are less predictable until the slack hits.
- Slack / transition: short but decisive. Fish compress along seam edges and the outside drop during the minute before the tide flips.
- Wind/swell: northerly chop tends to break the delicate seam; a steady southeast breeze helps keep the throat defined and presents baits cleaner.

Signs the window is building
Watch the bait and the shear. When it’s coming together you’ll see mullet or small bait pushing along the jetty lee instead of milling, birds loafing on the edge of the throat rather than scattering, a visible shear where cleaner channel water peels off the flats, and sonar marks sweeping out the channel as you troll or idle.

Where the fish stage
Tarpon, snook and cobia use the same flow differently.

- Tarpon: time the conveyor. They show up on the outgoing tide and the first hour of the ebb, tracking big mullet pushed from the bay.
- Snook: push with the tide into the inside neck and along jetty lee seams—low light and clean post-front pushes are classic.
- Cobia: appear on slack and early ebb along the outside deeper edge and around channel markers or moored debris. They hold the current break and ambush large baits.

How I work the throat
Make a reconnaissance pass on the first push—temperature doesn’t mean much here, bait movement does. I run the throat early, looking for the peel line and cleaner water seams. Fish the lee pockets and outside tongue instead of trying to cover open beach; the bite most often happens where fast water peels into calmer pockets.

For tarpon and snook I present live mullet or free-dragged baits into the seam on the down-tide side of the neck, timing the drift so the bait rides the cleaner edge into the pocket. For cobia I slow the boat, put out a live or big cut bait on the outside edge and watch the channel markers—cobia will hug the current break close to obvious structure. Keep wakes and engine noise down; this is shallow transit water and repeated pressure makes fish wary.

Tactics I trust
Set up in the lee outside the ferry lane when possible and hold position with minimal drift rather than steaming down the channel. Work the edge where fast meets slow—that seam migrates with the tide, so the exact GPS mark rarely matters as much as where the bait is moving. If nothing shows at the throat, pull across to the Key Biscayne ledges or run a few miles offshore to the first shelf edge.

Fleet behavior and pressure
Government Cut is a working shipping lane. Large wakes, shifting commercial positions and patrols will change where you can safely fish. Locals check the throat first and stage along the jetties; charters and private boats concentrate on tidy tides, squeezing bites into smaller windows and angles. Quiet boats and less chum tend to convert more often than noisy, repeated passes.

Common pressure and safety realities
Expect heavy vessel traffic and keep clear of commercial traffic. After heavy rains or dredging the channel can stain and the bite will compress or push offshore. Northerly blow, muddy runoff, or dead calm will often kill the tidy peel that fuels the neck. After a strong cold front inshore bait compresses and bluewater fish can move the other way—don’t commit unless you find a clear seam.

Micro-observations worth bookmarking
The bite often shows in a tight envelope: you’ll notice mullet moving and birds that stop and watch instead of circling. When the window is on the tarpon will stage and strip out; snook tuck into lee seams; cobia often hug channel markers at slack or early ebb. If you miss the initial packet, the activity usually dies back quickly.

When the window is fading
You’ll see the seam collapse with a northerly chop, heavy stain, or when the bait stops streaming. Birds go from focused to scattered, sonar marks disappear, and the cleaner edge becomes indistinct. At that point you either reposition for the shelf or pull off until the next tide pulse.

Pre-run checklist (what I confirm before committing)
1. Tide stage and predicted ebb window.
2. Visible bait movement at the jetties or on sonar.
3. Wind direction and shipping traffic.
4. Run plan: jetty lee first, outside tongue second, shelf probe third.

The Neck isn’t dramatic. It’s a short stretch that works when tide and bait align. Read the flow, watch bait and birds, and fish where fast peels into slow. When those pieces line up the next run out of Miami gets a lot easier.
