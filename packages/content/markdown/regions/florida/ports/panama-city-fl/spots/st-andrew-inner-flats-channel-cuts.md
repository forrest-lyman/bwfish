The water here is useful because geometry and tide do the work. The inner flats of St. Andrew Bay are broad, shallow sand interrupted by sinuous scour cuts and a few potholes. Those cuts fall away quickly from a few feet at low to 6–12 ft in the channel—natural funnels for shrimp, finger mullet and crustaceans when the tide moves. You don't mark this on a single GPS point; you learn the pattern: wide flats with narrow, deeper seams and a handful of marked cuts that hold at low tide.

## How to spot the cuts and read the flat from the wheelhouse
The pattern is visual and tactile. From the helm look for broad, clean sand interrupted by a darker, linear groove or a rounded pothole. At low water the cuts stand out as the thin, deeper lines or pools; at higher water the same lines show as converging ripples and a different texture across the flat.

Signs the bite window is building:
- tightening water texture over a cut — small, converging ripples where flow is focused.
- finger mullet or glass minnows riding the edge.
- single birds working a pass or a wedge of gulls diving near a cut.
- a faint muddy tongue pushing into a cleaner flat from a marsh or creek mouth.

Those are the practical signs. When you see them, the squeeze is forming. Fish follow the squeeze, not the slack.

## Access, launch considerations, and wind effects
This is first‑mile water—short runs from Panama City ramps and small-boat launches. Shallow-draft skiffs and bay boats are the right tools; anything drawing more than about 18–20 inches limits where you can safely run at low tide.

Plan your launch and retrieve around tide exposure. Some ramps expose long mud runs at extreme low and need a longer shove or a float ramp to avoid dragging props and skegs. Wind plays a bigger role here than offshore swell. Northerlies compress bait into the cuts; steady east–southeast winds flatten the flats and make sighting easier; quick frontal wind shifts muddle the shallow water fast.

## Reading the tide squeeze — timing and behavior
The cuts are tide machines. Floods shove bait from marshes and creek mouths into the channels; ebbs squeeze shrimp and crabs out onto flats and into shallow gutters. The productive windows are short and specific — typically a couple of hours around the moving tide rather than the absolute high or low.

Practical timing rule: fish the flow. If the tide is moving and you see bait and texture change, work the mouths and lips of the cuts. If the tide is stuck or slack, the bite will tighten into the deeper seams.

## Where the trout and reds actually sit
Spotted seatrout and red drum use the cuts for different reasons but the same basic logic: intercept prey being moved by the current.

- Spotted seatrout: they key on shrimp and small bait pushed into shallow mouths and sand gutters. You’ll find them hugging holes and the edges next to shallow grass or shell, often right where the flow slows.
- Red drum: they stage on deeper channel lips and pothole edges, then run onto the flats to intercept fleeing bait during a push.

When light is low or the tide softens they compress into the channels; when the flood gains strength they run onto the flats and gutters. That movement—compression into cuts on slack, spread onto flats on the push—is the useful pattern.

## Presentations and tackle that convert
Keep it simple and match the squeeze and sight picture. These are light-tackle flats presentations with quick feedback.

- Live shrimp under a popping cork: the most direct conversion. Fish the cork tight to channel lips and pothole edges on an incoming tide. Let the shrimp sit a beat in the current — trout will often pick it up without chasing.
- Soft plastics on 1/8–1/4 oz jigheads: drag slow, short hops across shallow gutters on the outgoing tide or along grass/shell margins. Adjust retrieve cadence to tide speed.
- Topwater plugs and small poppers at first light and late evening: when mullet squat the edges, present uphill into the flow along channel mouths.
- Free-lined mullet or croaker for reds: sight fish shallow patches with a short leader and let the bait run with the current into the cut.
- Line setup: 10–20 lb braid with a 20–30 lb fluorocarbon leader for reds; trout often take lighter leaders when pressure and clarity allow.

Those presentations are the starting point. Don’t be afraid to change weight, cadence, or leader length quickly when the conditions tweak.

## Tactical adjustments and common mistakes
You’ll learn fast what to change when the bite goes quiet.

- If the flats are flat and baitless, fish the deeper cuts. Fish retreat into the seams when the tide softens or pressure rises.
- In stained water, shorten leader and slow retrieves — trout take more tentative presentations when visibility drops.
- When birds or foamers show, switch to visible lures — poppers and twitchy plugs read better in an active surface feed than tiny plastics.
- Mobility beats anchoring. Anchoring on a cut entrance crowds the feed and pushes fish deeper. Move angles and work quiet, unpressured edges. A half‑mile of channel will often hold fish when the crowd is on one pothole.

Common mistakes: running shallow at low without local knowledge (boats get stuck), anchoring on top of the cut entrance, and fishing only at absolute high or low tides. The bite lives on the moving tide around the cut.

## Fleet behavior, pressure, and practical responses
Local skiffs and guides treat these cuts like clockwork. On a working tide you'll see boats concentrate around proven potholes and disperse as the tide swings off. Repeated hookups and anchored rigs will move trout to deeper edges or make them more nocturnal.

Practical responses: vary your angle, stay mobile, and look for the quieter edges. Work the cut a few hundred yards up or downcurrent from the crowd — the same seam seldom behaves uniformly for a mile.

## What usually matters (and what usually doesn’t)
What usually matters most:
- tide speed and direction
- channel depth and orientation
- local bait sources (creek mouths, marsh flushes)

What usually matters less:
- calendar date alone
- the exact GPS waypoint posted in a rumor chain

Read the water and the bait; those tell you when the pieces line up.

## Seasonal patterns to expect
- Spring: consistent and productive. Shrimp and finger mullet move early and push trout and reds onto the flats on predictable flood tides.
- Summer: long daylight and steady southeast winds can push bait farther. Early morning and evening tides are best for sight work; afternoon sea breezes can clean water and create short, sharp windows.
- Fall: transitional pulses when frontal bands move bait through the bay — short, productive windows on clean, cool floods.
- Winter: fish compress into the deepest cuts and potholes; low-light and slack-tide shots fade. Work deeper channel lips and watch signs of bait.

## Final, practical note
The fish show on the squeeze, not the slack. Keep a tide chart, watch the small creek mouths and marsh edges, and treat the cuts as a system: baits come through, predators follow. When the pieces line up you can punch a short day and come home early. When they don't, move to another cut or back off to a quieter channel lip and wait for the next squeeze.
