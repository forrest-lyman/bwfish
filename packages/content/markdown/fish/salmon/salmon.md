# Salmon

Salmon fishing is movement fishing. The fish don't sit evenly across a patch of water — they ride lanes, edges, and depth bands set by bait, temperature, tide, and river influence. You can see a fleet spread across the same ground and only one depth band or current line will produce. The trick is finding where traveling fish slow down enough to eat.

## Where bait and temperature overlap

Observation: fish collect where forage and comfortable water intersect. Anchovy, herring, sand lance, krill, squid — the species change by place and season, but the pattern is the same: bait stacks at a depth, and fish stage on that stack.

Why that matters: temperature and water chemistry determine how long fish stay in a zone. A pile of bait on the sonar only matters if the water around it is acceptable to salmon. Often the bait depth is more reliable than the surface signs.

Signs worth watching
- bait stacked at a repeatable depth — a consistent layer on the sounder is where fish will hang
- birds working low over small feed — they point to concentrated life, not always surface schools
- tide pushing bait against contour — current against structure stacks forage and concentrates fish
- current breaks near river plumes — edges where fresh water meets salt create lines fish use
- strikes on one side of a temperature line — fish relate to the edge, not a flat temp
- fish marks traveling through bait, not sitting under it — migrating fish intercept forage in transit

Read the bait depth first. Then confirm the temperature and current around it.

## Trolling water: depth, speed and direction

Observation: a lure can be in the right area and still fish wrong. We find fish by controlling presentation — not just covering water, but making the gear look like food moving with purpose through the column the fish occupy.

The four controls you manage
- actual lure depth — measure it, don't guess. Fish will be in the depth band where bait and water meet.
- speed through the water — current changes do funny things to speed; GPS speed lies. Adjust till the lure swims like the local forage.
- direction relative to tide — fishing with, across, or against the tide changes how the bait presents and where it sits in the column.
- turn shape and repeats — bites on turns tell a different story than bites on the flat; note which part of the pass produced and duplicate it

Watch where bites happen: on the first pass, the second, or on the turn. If bites cluster on turns, tighten your turn or change cadence. If they come on straight runs, keep the spread and depth constant and repeat that lane until it fades.

## Pressure and timing: why a bite starts or stops

Observation: fleet pressure and tidal rhythm move fish up and down. Heavy traffic doesn't stop salmon from migrating, but it will change where they take baits. A crowded lane often pushes fish deeper or scatters the bait so they refuse.

Timing matters more than boat count. A tide change or a fresh pulse of current over the same structure will restart a bite that looked finished. We fish the water that changes, not the boats that sit on yesterday's hot spot.

A simple watch
- watch bait more than boats — bait tells you where the fish want to be
- watch tide swings — slack to flood or ebb pulses commonly reactivates feeding
- expect depth shifts under pressure — fish will move down before they move far laterally

I remember a day the fleet kept trolling north while the better bait slid south. The boats were still where the fish had been. The fish weren't.

## How coastal shape and species change the rules

Observation: the coastline and species mix tune the same patterns differently. Chinook tend to relate strongly to contour and bait and will sit deeper; coho often ride higher and are more keyed to surface activity. River-mouth fisheries, sounds, bays and open ocean all change with runoff, tide and local forage.

Fish the local rules. In a river-mouth the plume edge and current seams matter most. In open water, thermoclines and bait layers rule depth. In tight water the turn and direction relative to the current become the leverage points.

Match your approach to the shape and species:
- deeper contours and strong bait stacks → slow, deep presentations and accurate depth control
- high-surface life and small forage → faster, higher presentations and attention to surface signs
- river-influenced water → read the plume edge, watch salinity and temperature transitions

Salmon reward fishermen who read moving water, not yesterday's bite. Find the bait, confirm the depth and current, present consistently, and repeat the productive lane until the water changes.
