## Why salmon sit the plume edge and tide neck

I watch fish hold the seam because it concentrates food and cuts their work. Where fresh water hits salt you get a sharp physical edge — temperature and salinity change, current shear — that traps and slows bait. Tide necks are just a tighter version of the same thing: current accelerated through a constriction that funnels prey into a narrow, predictable lane. Salmon often ride the outer edge of that lane, facing into the flow and taking easy shots as prey spills by.

You'll pick this drift when sonar shows bait or fish hugging a depth or sharp edge; when you can point to a visible seam — foam, discolored water, kelp lines, or a thermometer break; when birds and surface pickers are working a tight band; and when tide is moving so prey is actually being delivered. Timing matters: spring and early summer pulses and some fall migrations run past mouths most years.

## What the drift is imitating — the simple physics

Watch a sand lance or anchovy get shoved into the plume and it slows, tumbles, sometimes stalls. That deceleration is the trigger. A drift presentation that lets a bait or lure ride that seam — both laterally and vertically — reads like a concentrated, easy meal. The trick is not to fight the seam: let the bait move with local current speed through the contact zone and keep it in the same depth band as the bait layer.

## How fish encounter the seam

Salmon usually sit just outside or at the seam where shear brings prey into their strike zone. They’ll move laterally along the edge with the current and stack vertically where the density or temperature break occurs. Most strikes happen on passes where prey is compressed against that edge — either as the prey is funneled past or when a piece tumbles into calmer water.

## Reading the seam — what tells you this is the lane you want

Start with observations on the water and on the electronics. Signs the opportunity is building include:
- A narrow band of discolored water or foam parallel to shore.
- A sharp thermometer change across a few boat lengths.
- Current lines, weed, or debris converging at the mouth or a headland.
- Birds or sea lions working a discrete line.
- Sonar showing tight bait layers and fish marks riding into the seam.

Signs it’s breaking down:
- Wind or swell mixes the plume and erases the edge.
- Heavy runoff makes the plume turbid and prey behavior collapses.
- Slack tide — no net flow means no delivery of prey and fish disperse.
- Marks stop tracking the seam on successive passes.

The useful window is often short. A seam can look perfect on one pass and be blown apart on the next. Treat each pass as a data point — repeat the productive lane and refine depth and angle.

## How I position and run drifts

Positioning
- Put the boat upstream or outside the seam and drift across or along the edge so your bait rides the contact zone. On a flood, approach outside and drift into the seam; on an ebb, give the fish the opportunity to see prey pulled past them.
- Use drift control — engine tick, oars, sea anchor — to make controlled, repeatable passes. Precision beats brute force here; the seam is a narrow highway and you want to run it cleanly.

## Pass structure
- Short, repeated passes across the seam give better feedback than one long sweep. Each pass tells you whether marks reappear and at what depth.
- If fish are on the outer edge of the bait band, concentrate there. If they’re stacking below, you either add weight or switch to downrigger work.

## Timing
- Fish most actively during mid‑tide movement — the strong part of the flood or ebb — and often in the first and last couple of hours of the push. After a fresh runoff there’s a short window while the plume is colored but not mud‑thick.

## Gear and presentation choices

Keep the bait in the bait layer and let it ride the seam dynamics. Specifics depend on depth and how tight the seam is:
- Deep fish: downrigger is the most reliable way to keep precise depth.
- Medium depth: light planers or inline planers track depth without adding excessive weight.
- Shallow seams or estuary necks: lighter presentations, small flashers or dodgers, and shorter leaders to keep a natural profile.
- Flashers and small dodgers will add presence without changing sink rate too much; avoid heavy rigs that pull the bait well below the bait layer.

## Presentation style
- Match speed through the water, not GPS speed. If your lure is moving too quickly relative to the seam it looks like fleeing prey.
- Let the lure track with the flow or cross at natural angles that imitate prey being swept past.
- Slightly slower action and a natural ride often outfish aggressive cranking in these seams.

## Sonar reading and conversion

Sonar is the guidebook. Look for:
- Tight bait layers and sloping marks that run into the seam.
- Fish marks on the outer edge of a bait band — highest value targets.
- Repeated marks in the same depth band across passes.

Convert as you learn from the sonar:
- If bait moves deeper, add controlled weight or go to a downrigger.
- If bait moves shallower, remove weight or run lighter planers.
- If marks are visible but fish are uninterested, try changing approach angle, slow the presentation, or present a suspended profile with a small flasher/dodger.

## Common mistakes I see and how to avoid them

- Fishing by GPS speed instead of speed through water. Your bait must read like local current speed.
- Being on the wrong side of the seam. Fish commonly sit on the outer edge; drifting into the center of the plume or up into fresh water often gets blanked.
- Overweighting the rig. Heavy set‑ups that force the lure below the bait layer break the edge illusion.
- Ignoring vertical position. Sonar shows depth — match it. Too many anglers nail lateral position but run the bait 10–30 ft below the bait band.
- Poor drift control. Wide, uncontrolled sweeps spook fish and tangle gear.
- Treating the plume as static. The seam moves with tide and wind; successful drifts adjust position on the fly.

## Associated species — what else shows the seam is alive

Anchovy, herring, sand lance, and smelt will almost always be the forage driving seam behavior. Sea lions and diving birds are blunt, useful indicators. In some estuaries steelhead and sea‑run trout will use the same necks and feed the same way during migration pulses. Watch their behavior — it confirms where prey is concentrated.

## Regional differences that change how I run it

- Pacific Northwest / BC / Alaska: big freshets and large tides can push the plume far offshore. Fish may hold deeper under the seam — expect to run heavier downrigger settings.
- Northern California / Oregon: plumes shorter but intense after rains. Tide necks at jetties and spits create very narrow lanes — you need precise positioning.
- Southern California: plumes smaller and often storm‑driven. Fish may feed shallower and you’ll favor lighter presentations and tighter lateral placement.
- Estuary/shoreline systems: shallow necks with lateral shear demand different geometry — shallower depths, sight casting and tighter, lighter rigs.

## When to stop using the technique

Walk away when:
- The seam loses definition due to wind or mixing.
- Slack tide persists and marks disperse.
- A heavy, muddy plume removes prey response.

If that happens, switch to a migration troll or deeper controlled search. The plume game is about short, repeatable windows — don’t burn the day on a seam that’s gone soft.

## Practical realism — what to expect

Expect variability. Windows are brief, behavior fluctuates, and not every good-looking mark is a salmon. Sonar helps separate forage from fish, but read behavior: marks that ride the seam, show active motion and spacing consistent with larger fish are the ones worth working. Repeat productive passes, adjust depth tools, and trust what the seam and the bait tell you.

The operational core

Match depth. Match speed through water. Keep the bait on the seam edge. Make repeatable passes and convert your planers, flashers and downriggers until the bait sits in the same band as the forage. If you do that, and you read the tide and wind, the seam will give you consistent shots — exactly the kind of predictable feeding salmon like.
