## Why convert boards, flashers and hoochies when salmon are on the move

The simplest observation: a moving salmon doesn't behave like a fish holding and feeding. It has a narrow decision window. It sees something pass and either eats it in a heartbeat or it's gone. Conversions—changing board spread, flasher size or hoochie profile and spacing—are about shrinking the package into that decision window so the fish reacts before it leaves the lane.

In practice that means turning a "search" presentation (big, far-reaching, slow) into an "intercept" presentation (compact, energetic, correctly spaced). When fish are traveling they often refuse oversized, slow-sweep attractors. They will, however, take a compact package that reads like a single fleeing bait. Conversions let you change lateral placement, presentation angle and the apparent energy of the bait without moving the boat off the lane.

Read the water first; convert second. The rest is matching what you see.

## What a conversion actually changes (and why that matters)

Start with observation: what the fish see is size, motion, timing and access. Conversions alter those four things.

- Perceived size and motion: smaller flashers and tighter hoochies read as one fish. Bigger flashers present a school or cluster. If the fish are moving through a tight lane, a compact silhouette is more believable.
- Attractor–bait relationship: leader length controls whether the flasher is the story or the hoochie is. Shorten the leader and the hoochie reads as part of the flasher's package; lengthen it and it becomes the trailing target that provokes a follow-and-commit strike.
- Depth and angle: weight, line trim and board angle nudge the package through the depth band the fish are using. If the marks sit in a narrow band, you want the package to live there while still appearing to move.
- Lateral access: planar boards allow you to place a compact intercept right across a travel lane without dragging the boat out of the productive line.

Those changes are what make the difference between a fish that tracks and ignores, and one that commits.

## Reading the water — signs you should switch to an intercept package

Watch sonar and the surface. I look for translating marks, bites on the pass, short windows and fleet behavior before I change rigs.

- Sonar: moving arches or marks that steadily translate through the column instead of tight, slow schools. If marks move through bait layers while maintaining speed, that’s a travel lane.
- Bites on the pass: strikes that only happen during straight runs or when crossing a seam, not on slow approaches or holds.
- Short windows: marks appear in a narrow depth band and keep passing through the same corridor.
- Surface cues: birds or surface action that track along a steady line instead of concentrated boils.
- Fleet behavior: other boats getting single hits on passes but not repeating on the same spread—classic sign of moving fish.

If these signs line up, convert. If you ignore them and keep spreading big-search gear, you’ll either miss the bites or spook the fish.

## How to think about planer‑board conversions for a travel lane

Observation first: is the lane narrow or wide? Are fish fast or slow?

For a narrow, fast lane you want a tight, compact intercept across the fish’s transit path. Reduce lateral spread, tighten leader geometry and present a bait that will be inside the strike window as the fish passes. Narrow the presentation so it reads like one fleeing bait and so your bait spends the brief moment in front of the fish.

If fish are moving but hesitant, expand the board offset and use a bigger flasher to draw attention from farther away—the search-to-intercept switch is a tool to find the lane, then tighten to intercept. Use board angle and line length to match the depth of the marks; boards give you lateral placement without dragging the boat out of the productive line. That placement is worth more than adding another big flasher when the lane is tight.

## Which flashers you should pick and why

Flashers control visual energy. The rule of thumb when fish are moving is: reduce the flasher's dominance.

- Moving fish: downsize the flasher or choose a lower-pitch model so the flasher flashes quickly without overwhelming the hoochie. You want the hoochie to read as the meal, not the flasher as the meal.
- Holding or sluggish fish: step up to a larger, slower-rolling flasher to create attraction and provoke follows.
- Match flasher energy to forage: when small forage dominates, use lower-energy flashers and small hoochies; where bigger forage shows, step up the profile.

That match—flash rhythm and bait silhouette—is what convinces a traveling fish to commit.

## Hoochie profile and leader spacing for intercepts

The hoochie and leader spacing set the silhouette and timing.

- For a passing fish: shorten the leader so the hoochie rides close behind the flasher. That compact package is what a fish will swipe at as it passes.
- For a tentative or following fish: lengthen the leader to let the hoochie trail and tempt a follow-then-bite.
- Profile matters: slim, low-drag hoochies hold their shape and action at speed and read like natural forage on a quick pass. Bulky skirts can look like something to avoid when the fish are moving fast.

Think of leader length as bite-window timing: too long and the bite opportunity is gone before the hoochie arrives; too short and you may ruin the natural separation the fish expects. Tweak one variable at a time.

## Depth, speed and the bite window

Fish judge speed through the water. If you increase forward speed—boat speed or how the current carries your gear—you often need to decrease flasher size and leader length so the package doesn’t outrun the bite window. Conversely, if marks sit in a tight band, convert to rigs that hold in that band while still reading as moving: change weight and board angle, not just line out.

A common pattern is this: faster transit requires a tighter, lower-energy package; slower transit or holding fish can tolerate larger, slower flashers and more trailing action. Let the marks tell you which side you’re on.

## Common mistakes I see on the pass

These are operational failures that turn a good lane into frustration.

- Over-flashing: leaving a big, slow flasher on when fish are moving. It makes the bait look like a threat or a false item; fish refuse.
- Too much leader: long leaders on a fast pass make the hoochie lag and disconnect; the bite window closes before the hoochie arrives.
- Mismatched forage energy: presenting a large skirt when the forage is small and tight signals the wrong prey size.
- Ignoring sonar: running the same conversion when marks shift deeper or become stationary. Conversions must follow the water.
- Sticking to one strategy: not switching between search and intercept quickly when windows open or close.
- Poor board placement: using boards to spread gear wide when the lane is narrow—your baits miss the transit path.

Avoid those and you’ll keep more passes honest.

## Tactical sequence while you run the pass

A simple observational loop keeps you from over-committing.

1. Observe sonar and surface signs—confirm marks are translating.
2. Choose intent: use search gear (big flasher, wider spread) to find the lane; switch to intercept (smaller flasher, tighter leader, board or inside line) once you see moving marks.
3. Control contact: place a compact presentation across the path with a board or inside line; use leader spacing to set whether the hoochie reads as part of the flasher or the target.
4. Tune speed: match boat speed and line angle to the fish’s transit speed—don’t let gear race ahead of the bite window.
5. Iterate quickly: change one variable at a time. Watch where strikes happen—if they move deeper, change weight/board angle; if they disappear, reduce flasher energy or shorten the leader.

Run that loop as the lane runs. Windows are brief; your adjustments should be deliberate and fast.

## Observational reality and fleet effects

A few practical truths to keep in mind:

- Perfect presentations still get refused. Fish may be traveling but not feeding; small changes in leader length or flasher profile will flip a reluctant fish into eating.
- Windows are short. A travel lane often fishes for a few passes then dies when current, bait or fleet pressure shifts.
- Sonar can lie. A moving mark might be a current-driven bait strand, not a salmon. Confirm with tactical passes and be ready to revert to search if marks prove false.
- Fleet pressure matters. Heavy traffic tends to push fish deeper. Under pressure I favor lower-energy, less threatening conversions that sit in the fish’s path without screaming for attention.

Remember these realities when you decide how aggressively to convert.

## When to stop using intercept conversions

Abandon this approach when the signs change.

- Fish stop translating and hold deep under bait—you’ll need deeper downrigger-focused conversions or anchoring tactics.
- Bites come only on heavily weighted, slow-trolled downrigger presentations—board/hoochie intercepts stop producing.
- The travel path widens beyond your ability to place an intercept presentation—shift to a broader search pattern or relocate.

If the window collapses, don’t force it. Move and look for the next lane.

## Associated species and regional notes that change the tuning

These are adjustments, not different games.

- Coho: often ride higher and respond to faster, brighter presentations; favor smaller flashers and shorter leaders when they’re moving.
- Chinook: larger and sometimes deeper—kings can still take compact intercepts but may ignore underpowered setups in mixed fisheries.
- Pink and chum: their presence changes the "energy" baseline. Large schools can make salmon more aggressive or more selective depending on forage density.

Regionally, river mouths and tide necks produce narrow lanes where boards and tight intercepts win. On the open shelf fish may be spread vertically and downrigger conversions matter more. Colder northern waters often push fish deeper and favor slower, more pronounced flashers when fish hold; farther south fish can ride higher and prefer smaller, faster presentations. Match hoochie profile to the dominant forage—slim for sand lance/anchovy, chunkier for herring.

## Final takeaway — the conversion is an interpretive act

Conversions are about timing: getting a believable, compact package into the fish’s decision window. Watch the sonar, judge the transit speed, and tune flasher energy, leader spacing and lateral placement so your bait arrives at the exact moment a salmon decides to eat. It’s not trickery; it’s interpretation. You observe a behavior, transform your spread to mimic the forage the fish expects, and put the bait where the fish will act. A good conversion turns a moving fish’s brief attention into a grabbed hook—nothing more, nothing less.
