## When to pick a downrigger — the opportunity you’re watching for

Salmon will concentrate in tight depth bands. Sometimes that's because of temperature structure, sometimes because bait stacks there, sometimes because current compresses everything into a seam. When surface flashers and planers stop producing but your sonar, temp probe, or repeat-strike depths show a distinct band, downrigger depth work and deliberate conversions are the right tool.

You reach for this method when you see repeated signs across passes:
- sonar marks or bait concentrated at a single depth;
- a thermocline or sharp temperature jump with bait sitting on or against it;
- planers/flashers stop getting bites while deeper passes still do;
- currents or tides compress bait into tight lanes (river mouths, tide necks, ledges);
- other boats are scattered but one depth band keeps producing.

Why bother? Salmon prefer comfortable temperatures and layers with concentrated forage. A downrigger holds a lure exactly where the fish are choosing to be. Converting a surface package to a downrigger (or vice versa) lets you match the depth the fish are actually using, not just where the birds or the fleet suggest they are.

## What the presentation must do in a thermocline

Observation first: fish take what looks like natural bait moving in the layer they’re using. The downrigger's job is to keep the lure in that thin vertical window long enough and at the correct speed and angle.

How that plays out:
- The lure needs speed through the water that matches local forage/fish, not GPS speed alone.
- Line angle must let the lure ride at the intended depth and present the correct profile.
- Spend time in the layer. Quick touches through a thermocline rarely produce; fish respond to lures that live in the band.

Why conversions matter: a flasher package can attract attention, but fish may be watching the lure as it passes through a layer. Moving that same lure to a downrigger placement preserves the action while changing where the lure swims in the water column.

## Reading the water — when depth targeting will pay off

Look for repeatability. A one-off sonar return isn’t a roadmap; repeated marks that track with current and contour are.

Clear signs the window is opening:
- the same depth mark shows on multiple passes;
- temp probe shows a sharp change where those marks occur;
- flashers or hootchies only get hits when they sink through a layer;
- marks stay at the same depth even as you change speed or direction.

Signs it’s over:
- marks scatter over a wide range or vanish between passes;
- fish only bite on turns or when your gear exits the layer;
- the temperature structure mixes because of wind, tide, or an incoming plume.

Use CHIRP/echosounder returns to separate dense bait from discrete fish marks: bait tends to be uniform, dense; salmon marks are more discrete and can “flash” through the bait. Make controlled passes. Verify with a temp probe.

## Practical sonar and depth targeting rules

Don’t trust dial settings — trust actual lure depth.
- Account for line angle. A ball set at 60 ft doesn’t guarantee your lure is at 60 ft once you factor in line length, rod angle, speed, and current.
- Stagger rods at several depths and narrow in on where strikes happen.
- When testing depth, change only depth. Don’t swap lures, leaders, or speeds until you confirm where the fish are.

Run controlled passes and mark where the returns repeat. That’s your target band.

## How to convert presentations without losing the strike window

Observation: fish may react differently to a single lure at depth than to a flasher package. Conversions have to preserve the action while changing the delivery.

If fish are below a warm layer:
- put the downrigger so the lure rides just beneath the thermocline; use a leader that lets the lure move naturally through the layer.

If fish ride the top of the thermocline:
- use lighter downrigger settings, shallower balls, or planers that keep the lure pinned near the interface.

Converting flasher → downrigger:
- keep lure and leader length and maintain the trolling speed that drew attention on the flasher.
- move the whole presentation to the depth where marks or strikes occurred.
- expect a smaller strike window; fish often hit differently on a single lure in the band versus a flasher package.

Converting downrigger → planer/surface:
- only do this if marks are shallow or the fish have shown they’ll take higher.
- remember surface gear brings more lateral spread and vibration — it can draw fish from farther off the line but may also spook or produce different reactions.

When testing, let one rod act as an “attractor” (flasher or big dodger) while you probe depths nearby with a downrigger. Once fish lock, move the productive lure to a downrigger and keep adjustments small (3–6 ft).

## Positioning, timing, and small adjustments that work

Start wide then tighten.
- Run controlled-search runs across contours and seams. When marks repeat, tighten your approach and run consistent, straight passes through the productive band.

Work angles.
- A perpendicular pass reveals depth repeatability; a downstream pass tests intercept behavior.

Tweak in small increments.
- After a strike or two, adjust depth in 3–6 ft steps. Keep one variable at a time — typically depth.

Speed matters.
- Many salmon will take at slower water speeds through a thermocline. If you convert depth and suddenly speed up, you may lose the bite.

Keep records.
- Measure actual lure depth at trolling speed. The same downrigger setting can put a lure at different depths under different conditions.

## Common mistakes I see that kill the window

Misreading returns:
- Treating a dense thermocline echo as fish marks. Verify with passes and a temp probe.
- Chasing a single noisy mark that doesn’t repeat.

Timing errors:
- Dropping the downrigger on the first pass without confirming repeatability.
- Waiting until the bait band disappears — conversions work while fish repeatedly occupy a narrow band.

Presentation mistakes:
- Ignoring line angle and assuming the lure depth equals the ball depth.
- Swapping rigs or leaders during a conversion and changing lure action at the same time as depth.
- Running too high a speed after conversion.

Gear mistakes:
- Over-trusting a single sonar impression; don’t skip controlled, repeated passes.
- Using a ball that’s too light and doesn’t let the lure penetrate the layer, or too heavy so the lure zips through and spends no time in the strike zone.

## Observational realism — what to expect on the water

- Windows are short. Even with depth, speed, and action right, strikes can be sporadic. Be ready to capitalize quickly.
- Thermoclines move and break. Wind, tide, passing vessels, and plumes scatter bait and change the layer fast.
- The productive band can be only a few feet thick and will shift with current.
- Fleet pressure can push fish deeper or off the line. Your edge is consistent depth targeting and fast conversions when marks appear.

## Associated species and how they behave in the band

- Chinook (King): often the deeper, stronger-associated species — they tolerate colder layers and will lock deeper around thermoclines and drop-offs.
- Coho: more mid-column and opportunistic; they may ride higher and sometimes need conversions if they’re showing deeper.
- Steelhead (sea-run): in estuary or tide-neck situations they can show similar depth-specific behavior and be vulnerable to thermocline targeting.
- Other predators (lingcod, seabass): can appear on deep marks but are usually operationally secondary.

Different salmon species partition depth and temperature. Expect mixed results when species share a forage layer.

## Regional differences — how the same idea shifts with place and season

- Open coast: strong summer thermoclines form and hold bait deep. Fish can be tightly bound to those layers and need narrow depth targeting.
- Bays, fjords, inside waters: thermoclines are shallower and often tied to freshwater plumes; fish may use the interface of plume and ocean water, so precise downrigger placement across tidal seams matters.
- River mouths and tide-necks: salinity and temperature gradients create layers. Salmon will stage on the clean side of a plume at a specific depth — adjust as the plume moves with the tide.
- Seasonal: spring thermoclines tend to be weaker and shallower; summer thermoclines are stronger and deeper and concentrate bait differently.

Adjust expectations, but not the principle: find the band, present in it.

## When to quit and go do something else

Drop the tactic when:
- marks stop repeating or bites come only on turns;
- temperature structure mixes and bait disperses;
- fish consistently take surface gear despite earlier deep marks.

Follow the fish. If they move shallower, convert to surface or planer work and stop forcing depth.

## Why this technique exists

Salmon don’t always feed at the surface. They pick depths by temperature and where bait concentrates. Downrigger depth targeting and thermocline conversions are ways to put a natural-looking presentation into the narrow thermal and forage bands fish choose. The work is mostly observation—detecting repeatable marks tied to a layer—and disciplined testing: controlled passes, small depth changes, and conversions that preserve lure action while moving the lure into the strike zone. When a band repeats, this is the operational answer — until the fish decide otherwise.
