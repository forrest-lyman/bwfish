## Finding the productive depth band

Salmon commonly concentrate in a narrow vertical band where temperature, current and bait line up. That’s the window you want. On the surface the water can look empty and the sonar will show an obvious story: a bait layer, a thin ribbon of arches, a thermocline edge, or a steady lane of traveling marks. The trick is to recognize the pattern that repeats on successive passes and then put an offering into that exact vertical space consistently.

Observation first: if boats are getting bit only when they hit a specific depth, or your transducer shows a distinct band of bait with denser marks riding in or under it, that’s your opportunity. If the marks move past the boat in a consistent direction—traveling arches or a clear rail—you’re looking at migrating fish feeding in transit rather than fish stacked on structure.

## Reading the marks on sonar

Start with what the display is actually telling you, then interpret.

- Bait versus predators: dense, fuzzy clouds near the surface are bait. Tighter arches or compact, brighter returns in the same band are the predators—salmon will often sit just below or inside the bait layer.
- Traveling marks: linear or consistently directed arches that pass the boat are a lane. They keep the same heading on repeat passes. Pile-ups that sit over structure behave differently.
- Thermocline/temperature breaks: a crisp layer with different backscatter strength is usually a thermal or oxygen gradient. Fish may sit above, below or straddle it—watch which side holds the tight marks.
- Size and separation: brighter, larger marks imply bigger fish or multiple fish close together; long thin lines can be ingress/egress paths rather than a dense school.

A couple of practical sonar adjustments that change what you see: cone angle and tilt affect vertical separation—narrow cones compress returns, wide cones smear them. Gain too high turns plankton and micro-bait into clutter; too low and you lose mid-column targets. Speed matters: slow, controlled passes sharpen marks; fast runs smear them into noise.

Watch for repeatability. A depth band that returns after a turn and reset is worth committing to. Random single-arch blips are usually just passing fish.

## Putting your bait in the window

Once you have a depth band, convert it into a presentation that stays there.

Depth is the decision variable. Choose gear to match the band:
- Use a downrigger ball for exact depth placement when marks are tight and fixed.
- Use planers, boards or heavier weights for mid-column fish that move up and down.
- Use long leads or light wind-ons for near-surface fish that ride under bait.

Relative placement is as important as absolute depth. If salmon are under a bait band, present your offering directly under or just below that band. If they ride above a thermocline, run flashers or flashier lures slightly over the layer. Match not just the depth but the vertical relationship the fish expect.

Speed through the water controls lure depth and action. GPS boat speed is a poor proxy—you need the speed-through-water and to watch line angles and cable sag. Change one variable at a time on test passes: speed, depth, or lure action. Validate with sonar: run the offering through the mark and watch whether it slices into the mark, rides above it, or gets ignored.

If the lure rides past without a reaction you either missed the depth, have the wrong action for the fish’s position, or your speed is wrong. Use your sonar and a quick reset to confirm.

On the water workflow — passes, positioning and validation

I run the mark like this: straight pass through the band, controlled turn, reset outside the lane, then come back. That tells you if the mark is repeatable and how it behaves on a station-keeping pass.

If you’ve identified a lateral lane, run a staggered spread across it so you cover different distances and depths. Don’t circle randomly—controlled, repeatable passes reveal whether the fish are consistent.

Use forward or side imaging to pick up traveling schools before you cross them and to determine inflow direction. Position the boat so you intercept the lane with minimum wake and wake disruption. Watch how your offering shows on live sonar; that’s your quickest feedback loop.

If a mark appears and you have to get an offering into it quickly, do it. Some windows last 15–30 minutes. If the mark keeps showing but never reacts after several validated passes, move on—spending hours on a marginal, pressured target wastes daylight.

## Signs the opportunity exists — and when it fades

You want patterns that repeat.

Signs it’s worth fishing:
- Consistent marks at the same depth on successive passes.
- A bait band with tighter fish marks below or within it.
- Directional marks that track the same heading each pass—the lane.
- A thermocline that aligns with tight fish returns.
- Shot-to-shot consistency: after a turn the same band reappears.

Signs it’s fading:
- The band shifts deeper or shallower on each pass.
- The bait layer fragments or disappears.
- Marks become diffuse and non-directional.
- Bites drop even when you run the same depth—fish likely dropped out of feeding mode.

Expect windows to open and close quickly. Tide and sun can move productive depth faster than you can re-rig.

## Common operational mistakes

These are the errors that cost time and fish aboard a working boat:

- Chasing the brightest single arch. Transient big returns are often one fish. Favor recurring, repeatable marks.
- Using GPS depth instead of actual lure depth. Misread line angles and cable sag and you’ll be in the wrong band.
- Over-tweaking sonar gain. Too much gain creates clutter; too little hides mid-column features. Learn the midline setting for your unit and water.
- Misidentifying bait vs predator. Dense clouds are usually bait; predators are tighter and directional.
- Running the wrong speed. You can hold depth but have the wrong lure action for that position.
- Leaving gear static after marking. Some marks are brief—have a plan to get an offering into the window fast.
- Over-deepening because you think fish are on the bottom. Read the mark, not the bottom.

Those mistakes are operational. Good rigging won’t fix a bad read.

## Associated species and how they change the read

Species commonly show up together and shift your interpretation:

- Chinook (King) — often larger, brighter marks and frequently deeper on migration lanes.
- Coho — tend to show higher in the column and will often react better to flashers and faster speeds.
- Herring/anchovy (bait) — dense diffuse bands. Where bait sits sets the vertical relationship for salmon.
- Steelhead (sea-run) — smaller marks in river plumes and estuary runs; they change how fish group near fresh-water interfaces.

Knowing how each species positions relative to bait and thermocline helps you choose depth and presentation without second-guessing.

## Region and fleet effects that change your read

Expect regional differences and pressure to alter behavior.

- Open ocean vs river mouth: offshore marks often track deeper migration lanes and stable thermoclines; near plumes and mouths fish compress shallower along current seams and fresh-water layers.
- Latitudinal differences: colder northern waters often have deeper, more stable thermoclines; warmer southern waters can have shallower, more changeable layers.
- Sonar choice by water: in cluttered inshore water, higher-frequency or split-frequency with a narrower beam separates bait and salmon better; in deep ocean lanes lower-frequency or chirp gives better penetration and target sizing.
- Fleet behavior: pressure pushes fish deeper and more skittish. In pressured southern fisheries fish sit deeper and react less; in less-pressured migration corridors they can be looser and more willing.

Those factors change how you interpret marks and how quickly you need to validate.

## Observational realism — marks lie, windows close

Marks are not a promise. I’ve watched perfect arches slide by with no bite and watched fish hit on turns when nothing seemed to be happening. Salmon can be on-stage for migration and ignore a lure that would work minutes later when feeding mode flips on.

A few working assumptions: expect overlap of species and size classes in the same band, expect windows to be short, and expect that validation—watching your offering in the mark and seeing any reaction—is everything.

## Three questions to decide quickly

When you find a mark, answer these fast:

1. Is this a repeatable, productive feature (traveling lane, bait-plus-predator layer, or thermocline congregation)?
2. Exactly where do the salmon sit relative to that feature (above, within, below, or straddling it)?
3. What presentation will consistently place a natural-looking offering in that vertical space (downrigger depth, planer/weight/lead, flasher conversion, and speed)?

If you can answer those three and validate with a controlled pass—watching the offering on sonar and noting any reaction—you’ve turned sonar marks into catchable depth. If any answer is fuzzy or the mark is ephemeral, change a variable or relocate. Repeatable marks and quick validation beat long guesswork every time.
