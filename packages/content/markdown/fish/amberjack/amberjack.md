# Amberjack

Amberjack are structure fish with range. They hold around wrecks, reefs, rigs, towers, springs, and ledges, but they do not always sit on the same piece of metal or rock. They move with bait, current, light, and pressure.

They are efficient, not mindless. A school may rise aggressively one drift and bury itself in structure the next.

## Vertical Structure

Amberjack use height in the water column. On a wreck or rig, they may suspend above the structure, hold down-current, or sit tight enough to make the first turn of the fight the whole problem.

Useful signs:

- bait marking above structure
- fish suspended off the top of a wreck
- current pushing cleanly across relief
- marks rising toward a jig or bait
- bites on the same drift line
- fish following hooked fish upward

The mark above the wreck may be more useful than the wreck itself.

## Current And Presentation

Amberjack feed best when current carries bait naturally past the structure. Too little movement spreads fish lazily. Too much makes presentation hard and pulls baits out of the zone.

What matters:

- drift angle
- bait depth
- where the current meets relief
- how quickly fish return after pressure
- whether marks rise or stay locked down

If the fish are high, speed and reaction can work. If they are buried, the first ten seconds matter.

## Pressure

Amberjack respond to pressure by sinking into structure or shifting down-current. Heavy jigging, repeated drops, and lost fish can make a school less willing to rise.

Pressure signs:

- follows without strikes
- fish peeling away near the boat
- marks settling lower after each drift
- bites moving from high in the column to near structure

The bite may still be there, but the margin gets smaller.

## Regional Shape

Gulf amberjack often relate to rigs, wrecks, artificial reefs, and deep relief. Atlantic and Caribbean fish may use reef drops, ledges, and current-swept structure. Local bait changes their height: cigar minnows, sardines, squid, blue runners, and small jacks all move the school differently.

Amberjack fishing is a vertical reading problem. The structure starts the search. The fish decide the level.
