This isn’t a how‑to on knots or kites. It’s an operational approach: read a raise, then pick and sequence the presentation that finishes the job. Sailfish behavior—not the gear on the transom—tells you whether to sequence multiple teasers, consolidate the spread, push a live bait on a kite, or slow‑troll a depth conversion. Below I lay out the patterns I watch for, the conversions that follow, and the simple boat work that makes a hesitant pack pick one bait and strike.

## When a raise becomes a conversion window

You get a window when fish light up but don’t commit—shows behind teasers, hanging in the wake, or passing repeatedly without taking. That’s the moment sequencing matters.

Typical contexts that make these windows: light wind with clear shows, a tight color or current edge carrying ballyhoo, or heavy fleet pressure where fish have seen everything. The common thread is mixed interest: some fish want to eat, others are cautious. A raised sail is information: height, speed, and how it interacts with your teasers tell you what the fish expects. Your job is to reduce ambiguity—present one obvious target at the right altitude and angle.

Read the raise first, then act. If you confuse the fish with too many options you lose the window.

## Turning teaser probes into a single target

Observation first: a teaser is a low‑cost probe. It lights fish without offering a full meal. When a sail repeatedly inspects a teaser it’s telling you what it needs—a visible breakaway, a suspended target, or a slower, deeper option.

The sequence imitates nature. A visible conversion mimics a bait that separates from a baitball and becomes isolated. A kite bait looks suspended and helpless. A slow‑troll bait imitates a pressured bait feeding down or trying to escape below the surface. The point of sequencing is to remove choice and perceived risk: give the fish one clean target and a clear strike path.

Three presentation relationships to watch:
- distance between teaser and bait,
- angle to wind/current,
- whether the live bait looks to be fleeing or hovering.

Match the bait movement to the fish’s inspection. Hurry up for excited, high fish; slow and clean for wary, pressured followers.

## Reading the raise: which conversion to use

Make the call on the water by watching height, speed, persistence, and follow behavior.

Aggressive raises — fast, high, persistent — want quick, visible conversions. Keep a short, visible bait in the wake or on a short outrigger, flash the teaser and present the bait in the fish’s path. Signs: stays high, returns after a miss, runs across baits, tails in the wake.

Tentative raises — slow follows, fish hanging behind teasers — call for visible‑raise conversions or a kite. Move the teaser so the fish tracks it into the wake, then stop or pull back slightly to let a single bait show cleanly. A suspended kite bait above the spread often looks irresistible to a cautious fish. Signs: follows without striking, inspects from behind, slow approach, drops back rather than committing.

Pressured/educated fish — lots of boats and repeated presentations — need simplification. Reduce competing baits, move off the crowded line 100–300 yards, and present a single clean option: a kite bait or a slow‑trolled, slightly weighted bait at depth. Signs: turns away from shallow baits, moves deeper on sonar, only shows briefly at the edge of the spread.

Deep or reluctant fish — bait below the surface or fish hugging the edge — are better with slow‑troll depth conversions. Use a heavier, slower presentation that matches a bait trying to feed or escape beneath the surface. Signs: sonar marks under bait, fish follows below without rising, current pushes bait deeper.

Decide quickly. Aggressive = short bait in wake; tentative = visible‑raise then kite; deep = slow‑troll depth conversion.

## Spread management that actually converts fish

At conversion time you want the fish to be looking at one thing. That’s the running principle.

Clear line of sight: pull in lines that clutter the fish’s field of view, stagger distances so only one bait is the natural target, or temporarily douse a teaser. A teaser too close masks the bait; a teaser too far gives the fish a choice.

Angle to current/wind: set baits so the bait’s path crosses the fish’s natural approach. Fish most often meet prey head‑on or slightly below—present baits along that vector instead of forcing the fish to chase across multiple lines.

Distance relationships: for fast converts keep a short visible bait in the same wake lane as the teaser. For kite work make the kite bait the most conspicuous item in the fish’s sightline. If you need to simplify, collapse to two clear options—kite plus one short bait, or one visible long bait plus a short.

Dynamic simplification wins: complexity finds fish; simplicity converts them.

## Chum, timing, and conversion order

Light, intermittent chum holds a pack. Heavy, oily chum makes fish blasé and masks subtle raises. Use short, controlled chum to keep fish around; stop chumming before conversion so the target bait reads as the best option.

When a fish inspects teasers repeatedly, back off the chum for a moment and bring a clean bait into view. The sudden drop in chum can cue a hungry fish to commit if the bait looks vulnerable.

Typical conversion order I run: teaser → visible bait reveal → kite/live bait placement (if the fish is high) → short bait swap (for a quick convert) → slow‑troll depth option (if fish refuses shallow). Don’t jump straight from heavy chum to a deep drop—give the fish a visible target first.

## Common mistakes that kill the window

I’ve sat through all of these more times than I like. They cost hookups.

- Over‑sequencing: flipping through conversions without sticking to one confuses fish. Pick a path and execute it.
- Too many visible targets: a crowded spread gives fish choice; choice equals refusal—especially with pressured fish.
- Ignoring approach angle: put the bait on the fish’s path, not off to the side.
- Late conversions: convert while the fish is still in a forward inspection posture, not after it’s already passed your clew.
- Chum overkill: continuous heavy chum during a visible conversion blends the bait into a food field.

Fix those and you’ll turn more shows into hookups.

## Associated species and how they change the play

Dorado will take teasers and kite baits aggressively. When dorado dominate the bait you may need purer, less oily presentations to pull sail attention.

Blackfin tuna can push bait down or scatter baitfish, shortening conversion windows with tight, fast runs.

Wahoo show up aggressive and can force sail deeper or make them more cautious. If you’ve got other predators in the mess, simplify and present a single clean option.

Be aware: these species don’t change the rules—just the timing and bait choices.

## Regional tendencies that affect sequencing

Florida: kite work is common—ballyhoo and live bait are plentiful and wind/current geometry often favors kites. Visible‑raise into a kite bait is a frequent first move.

Gulf: fish ride current seams and may sit deeper. Slow‑troll depth conversions and careful spread angles are more common. Teasers draw fish off the seam—match the downcurrent approach.

Baja / Pacific coast: fish can be explosive and educated. Sequencing must be quicker and simpler—short, decisive visible conversions and immediate kite drops. Moving a few hundred yards from the fleet often helps.

Those are tendencies, not rules. Read the raise and adapt.

## Boat work and decisions that shape a conversion

Read the raise first—don’t react to every show. Note height, speed, and whether the fish returns after a pass.

Decide sequence quickly and commit. Manage the spread: pull lines, douse teasers, collapse to a single outrigger. Use boat position and speed to shape bait behavior—steady speed for kite work, controlled slowing and subtle steering for visible‑raise work, precise reduced speed for slow‑trolls.

When the window closes—fish drop, other boats crowd in—move. A fresh patch of water with the same bait and a simpler spread gives you a new window.

## Expectations: what refusals tell you

Fish will refuse good presentations. That’s part of it. A refusal followed by a long absence usually means change water or change method rather than hammer the same setup.

Windows are short. A well‑read raise can produce a hookup in seconds; if the fish hesitates after the first visible conversion it usually will not commit.

Not all raises are equal. Large, showy packs can be indecisive; a single steady follower that doesn’t flash may be the cleaner target. Fish that have seen too many teasers will inspect differently—more passes, deeper follows, lateral passes. Treat them with cleaner, lower‑profile presentations.

## Quick decision map (operational memory aid)

- High, fast, returning → short bait in wake; fast convert.
- Slow follower, hanging behind teaser → visible‑raise into kite bait; reduce clutter.
- Deep, pressured, sonar marks under bait → slow‑troll depth conversion; simplify spread.
- Heavy fleet pressure → move off the pack, present one clean option, stop chumming.

Read the fish. Simplify the scene. Match the bait to the inspection. Get one target in the fish’s eye and let it make a clean choice. That’s the work—nothing flashy, just steady observation and decisive boat work.
