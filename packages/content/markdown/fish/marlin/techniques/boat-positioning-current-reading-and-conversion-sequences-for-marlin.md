A pass isn’t valuable because you ran five lures through the water — it’s valuable because of the decision you make after it. I treat every pass like a short experiment: pick the intercept geometry you think the marlin is watching, run it cleanly, watch what the fish tells you, then change one thing and test again. This page lays out how I choose the first pass from bait/current/structure, how I interpret different raises, how crossing angles and boat speed change lure lift, and how to build short conversion sequences that ask one question at a time.

## Why the first pass should isolate an intercept geometry

Opportunities with marlin are local. One face of a bank, the edge of a bait slick, the lee of a FAD — those are the places fish set up. The mistake I see most is trying to “cover” a bank rather than present into the specific piece of water the marlin is inspecting. The first pass should be selected to reproduce the natural motion of the bait relative to the fish, not to crisscross every contour on the map.

Observe three things before you commit to that first run: where the bait sits relative to the structure, which side of the structure current is pushing bait onto or off of, and whether small predators are actively corralling bait. Those three observations point you to one simple action: present lures through the water the fish expects prey to come from.

## Choosing the first pass from bait, current and structure

If bait is stacked on the up-current face of a bank, set up to run toward that face so the lures move into the bait — it mimics prey being shoved into structure. If bait is spilling off a ledge or canyon, cross the spill line so your skirted baits come down and across the edge where a marlin can intercept the flow. If bait sits in an eddy or lee, cut the eddy edge; present through the slow-water pocket where bait concentrates and a predator can sit in ease and ambush into current.

When the slick is broad with scattered birds, the first pass should sample the denser portion of the slick in relation to the current and wind. Don’t commit to a single hot spot until a raise or a distinct track focuses your effort.

Those are operational reads — not theory. Pick the pass that puts lures in the exact column the fish is watching.

## Using crossing angles and speed to create lift

You can change where a lure rides in the water column without swapping gear. Crossing angle and relative speed are your controls.

A more perpendicular crossing to the current increases lateral motion and tends to lift skirted baits. A shallower angle keeps presentations lower in the column. Boat speed relative to the current matters more than GPS speed: if the current is pulling your lines faster than the boat, your lure will track higher. The fix is simple — change your angle or slow slightly so the lure sinks into the target depth.

Use the angle to target the fish’s vertical plane. If your echosounder shows bait at 20–40 ft, approach on an angle that forces the trolling presentation to ride through that band at the intercept point. On edges, cut across the seam so the lure moves from fast to slow water; that transition often prompts a strike because it looks like bait spilling into easier water.

## Reading a raise and how to reposition

A raise is data. Not every show means full engagement — read what the fish actually did before you commit.

When a fish commits with a full follow or clear surface hit, it’s engaged. Repeat the pass with minimal change to timing and angle and square up to present directly into the intercept point. If it struck on the turn, a slightly wider turn arc gives a better approach angle. If you saw a long tracking follow and then a fade, the fish is curious but unsure — change one variable: slow the pass, clean the presentation (less flasher action), or move a little closer into the bait. Don’t repeat the identical pass; that’s why it tracked and left.

Light bill shows or short flashes usually mean the fish is tentative or deeper. Move to a deeper-running presentation or cross at a shallower angle to keep the lure lower. A quick flash that disappears often means the fish inspected a narrow window. Match that approach corridor but make it wide enough to catch the fish’s line of attack — tight obsession on a single mark often fails.

When you reposition, change exactly one primary variable at a time: angle, depth, speed, or spread spacing. That isolates what provoked the raise. If the fish tracked outside the pattern, widen the turn or shorten lines to bring a lure onto its path. If it came in from down-current, set up a run that meets the fish head-on. If it rose on the turn, place the next pass to give it a clearer angle on a lure on the turn — a longer, smoother arc beats a sharp cut. Small, deliberate course changes win; frantic circling spooks fish and ruins presentation geometry.

## Building short conversion sequences: one question per pass

I treat a conversion as a hypothesis test. Run a pass, record the behavior, then ask one practical question and test it on the next pass. Keep sequences tight — three to four calculated passes is usually enough.

A simple framework I use:

1) Hypothesis: the fish is on the up-current face and will intercept lures coming into that face.
   - Pass: cross from down-current into up-current.
   - Observe: did it track? what depth? which lure drew attention?

2) If the fish tracked high and short: question — is it shallow or responding to bustle?
   - Test: present a shallower-running lure with reduced flash at the same arc.
   - Observe: follow/refusal/strike.

3) If the fish tracked deep or only showed the bill: question — did speed or angle keep the lure too high?
   - Test: slow the pass, shallow the crossing angle, or switch to a deeper runner.
   - Observe: change in behavior.

4) If the fish returns after a miss: question — will it take a pitch bait only?
   - Test: put out a pitch bait or live bait in the intercept zone while keeping the lures slightly off.
   - Observe: bait vs. lure response.

Each pass should answer one clear question. If the fish never responds, either it was a different animal or the current/pressure changed — back off and reassess.

## Signs an opportunity is building or fading

Opportunity builds when bait consistently marks on sonar in the same depth band, birds or tuna keep returning to the same line, and you get repeated light shows or tracking along a corridor. Steady current that creates repeatable ambush points is a big plus.

A window is fading when bait disperses, wind-driven surface shear pushes the slick away from structure, fish follow only briefly before disappearing, or multiple boats have worked the area and fish are skittish. A change in current direction or velocity is a clear sign the ambush geometry has moved and your intercept points have shifted.

Read those signs quickly and either commit while the window is open or back off to find a new intercept geometry.

## Common operational mistakes I see

The list is short because the errors repeat:

- Repeating the exact same pass after a tentative track — you learn nothing.
- Overreacting to a single cosmetic show by changing everything — you lose the geometry that produced the show.
- Changing multiple variables at once so you can’t tell what worked.
- Ignoring subtle sonar cues — a thin, stratified bait layer needs a different angle than a dispersed school.
- Committing to the wrong side of structure because you’re tied to a map position instead of reading where the current and bait are actually working.
- Chasing a distant flash indiscriminately — that fish may be under different conditions.

Fix those and your conversion rate improves because you’re learning from each pass.

## Regional notes: Baja versus SoCal

Baja tends to have clearer water and larger temperature structure. Fish there will often sit further off structure and require longer, wider arcs. Flying fish are common and change depth expectations — fish may rise from deep to intercept fast surface flyers, so faster passes with more lift can be effective. FADs and floating debris create larger ambush zones; think about intercepts that hit the lee of debris where bait piles.

In SoCal, especially with striped marlin, bait balls and tight temperature breaks are more common. Fish can be shallower and respond to tighter turns and pitch baits. Currents are often wind-driven and less predictable; conversion windows can be shorter between sets, so you have to be quicker with hypothesis tests.

Those are tendencies, not rules. Read the water first.

## How an experienced skipper thinks about passes

Plan the first run to present lures into the movement of bait relative to structure. Watch the fish’s behavior — depth, side of approach, commitment level — then change one variable to test a clear hypothesis. Use crossing angle and relative speed to move lures into the fish’s vertical window. Control lift with angle and speed before you start swapping hardware.

Keep conversions short, focused and adaptive. Each pass should give you an answer. When in doubt, move back and reassess the bait/current structure. Successful marlin fishing is less about flawless repetition and more about reading the water, asking the right question, and making a single, measured change until you either catch the fish or the window closes.

I won’t promise every pass works. Fish refuse “perfect” presentations. Windows open briefly. Learn to read whether you have a fast, shallow window or a slower, deeper one and act accordingly. The best conversions are decisive, not long.
