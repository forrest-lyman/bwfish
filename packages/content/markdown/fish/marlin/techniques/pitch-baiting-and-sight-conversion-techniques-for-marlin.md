A visible marlin has already decided to investigate. Pitch‑baiting and sight conversion exploit that decision. You’re not flinging a gamble across the spread — you’re replacing the tease with a single, natural item inside a fish’s established search path. Below I lay out when that window opens, how to place the bait so the fish intercepts it naturally, how to read refusals, and what crews do next.

## Why Pitching Works When a Marlin Shows

Observe first: a marlin that follows a teaser, flashes beside a lure, throws its bill, or makes a clean raise has shifted from passive searching to active inspection. That shift reduces variables — the fish is shallow, its heading is short‑term predictable, and visual cues dominate the decision.

Put simply: the heavy lifting — attention and tracking — has already been done by the teaser or surface activity. A pitched bait that arrives in the fish’s line of sight on the same vector looks like easy prey. Done right, pitching removes confusion from the spread and shortens the decision chain.

## Contexts where the conversion window matters
- Clean blue water over banks or FADs with visible bait layers.
- Tight bait schools with surface activity while long trolled lures get follows but no full commits.
- A single fish locked on one side of the spread or consistently tracking a teaser.

## How the Behavior Works (what you exploit)

Start with the fish’s behavior, not your gear. A visible raise means orientation and a brief search vector. Present a bait that looks like a wounded or disoriented item in that vector — free‑fall or lightly streamed at the surface — and the marlin will often choose the easiest option. Conversion is mostly continuity: the teaser has established motion and intent; the pitched bait should read like the next logical object in that motion.

Conditions that favor conversion: clear light, low chop, a clean, uncluttered wake, steady drift or current, and a single focused fish rather than a confused aggregation. When those line up the conversion window opens and it’s short.

## Shot Geometry for Visible Fish

Read the fish’s heading before you throw. Think of the marlin as a vehicle on a line for the next few seconds. Your goal is to place the bait slightly ahead of its nose along that projected path so the fish moves into the bait rather than having to stop and reverse.

## Angle and lead
- Small forward lead beats a lateral or trailing presentation. If the fish is crossing current, place the bait slightly upstream so it drifts into the fish’s eye-line.
- Don’t try to force a perpendicular intercept; those require big turns and raise rejection odds.

## Distance, altitude and splash
- Too far: more splash, more time, more chance for the fish to re‑evaluate. Too close: you risk spooking it. Choose a pitch distance that gets the bait into view quickly with minimal commotion.
- Lower altitude and soft release reduce splash signature. The way the bait hits the water should look natural.

## Boat movement and teaser control
- Keep the boat neutral or slightly slower than the fish’s inspection speed. Sharp speed changes or violent turns break the geometry.
- When converting a teaser, remove or slack the teaser long enough to clear the visual clutter before the pitched bait arrives. One clean object in the water beats two confusing ones every time.

## Converting Teasers / Pop‑Pitch to Hooked Presentations

Read the commitment. A fish that rides a teaser line, pops up repeatedly, or follows past the transom is giving you a brief window. Convert while it’s still keyed — don’t wait for a strike on the teaser.

## Timing and continuity
- Replace the teaser’s motion with the pitched bait so the presentation looks like a continuation of the tease. Same side, similar cadence, then the pitch.
- Stop the teaser or pull it back just before the pitch so the edible presentation is the only object left in the fish’s field.

Clean the spread. If lines, skirted lures, and teasers are overlapping the shot, you’ll create a choice the fish doesn’t want to make. Clear the area; present one convincing meal.

Use the boat as a staging platform. Make small, predictable course adjustments to keep the fish tracking. Quick, abrupt maneuvers break line‑of‑sight and close the window.

## Reading the Window — When to Pitch and When to Back Off

Signs the opportunity is building
- The fish returns to the same spot or side of the spread more than once.
- A single fish locks onto a teaser or lure rather than there being a confused school.
- The marlin rises cleanly and tracks forward without excessive rolling.
- Visible bait concentrated in seams or edges where fish hunt.
- Calm light and low chop so the fish can see the pitched bait.

## Signs to back off
- Bill‑rolling or tail‑walking without focused attention — more confusion than feeding.
- Multiple lures/teasers overlapping the area.
- Glare or chop that will hide the pitched bait until it’s too late.
- Fish that follows aggressively but repeatedly turns away — often a pressure or line/leader sensitivity issue.
- Spooking behavior when the boat approaches — sudden dives or erratic moves.

## Immediate Follow‑Up When a Fish Refuses or Fades

A refusal isn’t a verdict; it’s data. Ask what changed: did the bait land wrong, did a leader flash, did current shift, or did the fish never really commit? Then adjust.

Sequence to follow after a refusal
1. Quick reassess. Watch the fish and water for a few seconds. Has its heading changed? Is it still near the seam?  
2. Re‑present quickly if the fish remains interested. Change the shot slightly — angle, altitude, or a faster drop — so the bait reads as new prey.  
3. Swap the cue if needed. If you pitched a skirted lure, try a whole bait. If you used a live‑looking lure, try a different silhouette. Change the silhouette, not the distance.  
4. If the fish backs off but still keys to the teaser, re‑prime it, then convert again with a different cadence or shot geometry.  
5. If the fish is spooked or goes deep, back off, work other structure for a few minutes, then re‑approach from a different angle.

Keep tracking marks, bait behavior, and the fish’s pattern. Marlin often return to the same seam and the next opportunity may be the one that pays.

## Common Mistakes (what I see crews do that kills chances)

- Pitching into a crowd: lures, lines, and teasers overlapping the shot create indecision.
- Waiting too long: letting the fish lose focus before the bait hits makes the presentation unfamiliar.
- Overreacting after a refusal: frantic shots or repeating the same template often spook the fish permanently.
- Keeping the teaser active too long: don’t give the fish two objects to compare.
- Ignoring current shifts: a sudden current change can put your bait out of the fish’s expected line.
- Using the same angle and corner repeatedly: marlin learn templates and begin to refuse them.

## Associated Species — what their presence tells you

Tuna (yellowfin, skipjack, bonito)
- Their activity confirms bait and makes marlin more likely to be interested. They also shape how bait schools move.

## Dorado / mahi, false albacore, bonito
- Show up in the same productive water and can steal baits or light up the surface. They complicate conversions and often force cleaner presentations.

Operational relevance: these species are signals. They don’t change the basic geometry, but they alter bait behavior and the pressure on your presentation.

## Regional Notes: SoCal vs Baja

SoCal
- Cooler, often clearer water. Pitching works best on clear days when a single fish keys on a teaser near a bait seam. Windows are usually short and precise.

## Baja
- Warmer, often surface‑oriented fish with scattered bait and floating debris. FADs and current seams can produce longer windows, but heavier boat pressure means conversions must be quicker and cleaner.

Fleet pressure matters in both regions. In Baja you’ll need more stealth and smarter angles; in SoCal you’ll need speed and precision the moment the bait lights up.

## How Experienced Crews Think

Observation first, tools second. Watch the fish and bait longer than you think you need. Plan the pitch while the fish is mid‑investigation; hesitation kills conversions. One clear presentation beats many confusing ones. Adjust angle, silhouette, or cadence based on the fish’s behavior, not habit. Control noise and motion — boat handling and teaser control are as important as the bait itself.

## Observational Realism

Expect refusals. Even a perfect shot will be rejected for reasons you can’t always see — taste, pressure, or an unseen leader. Windows open and close fast; often the decisive moment is on the second or third pass. Fish change depth and approach unpredictably; be ready to switch from a surface pitch to a short trolled presentation if the fish drops. And remember: a pitch can trigger a bystander — be prepared for sudden commits from unexpected fish.

Bottom line

Pitching and sight conversion are about timing, geometry, and control. The marlin has already done the search work — your job is to deliver one convincing, natural prey item into its window quickly and cleanly. Read the fish, clear the spread, control the teaser, and adapt the presentation immediately when the fish refuses. That pattern — observation, precise presentation, controlled follow‑up — is where most real conversions come from.
