The
 technique is specific: use floating structure (natural debris, man-made FADs, logs, buoys) as a staging point to turn deep, scattered bigeye into catchable, surface‑reachable fish. Chunking is the tool that provokes a response from fish holding under or near the structure; livebait conversions are the surgical follow-up when the fish lift but refuse heavier or noisy offerings. This page explains why that often works for bigeye, how to read the opportunity, where and when to position the boat, how to time the conversion, and what the hookups and fights look like for bigeye taken this way.

## The Opportunity

- Why floating structure matters for bigeye: FADs and floating debris aggregate bait (juvenile clupeids, squids, small epipelagic fish) and create shadowed, current‑split microenvironments where bigeye hold in the water column. Structure organizes both prey and predator in space — the fish don't have to search; they patrol edges or sit just below the bait.
- Behavioral trigger: Bigeye often hold deeper and only work the edge of bait when a reliable stimulus appears (scent, falling chunks, a live bait in the slick). Chunking creates a perceptible, localized prey trail; it lifts bits of bait and scent upward, encouraging fish to come up into a shallower depth band for short windows.
- Low‑light amplification: Dawn, dusk, night, and heavy overcast boost success. Bigeye are more willing to rise and feed under low light. At night, lights on FADs can concentrate bait under the structure and make chunking especially effective.
- Fleet context: FADs are crowded in many fisheries. One boat's chunking or conversion is useful information — multiple hookups in the same spot usually indicate a persistent aggregation rather than a one‑off passerby. Conversely, heavy pressure can condition fish and shorten windows.

## How the Technique Works

- What chunking imitates: small baitfish being torn apart by predators — it creates a moving scent/bite stimulus and visible fragments that rise through the upper column. For bigeye, that stimulus must feel like accessible prey without being so overbearing that it disperses the bait field entirely.
- How fish encounter it: bigeye holding under a FAD detect the slick and follow it up into the lighter column. Chunking effectively “pulls” the feeding layer into a depth range where surface or near‑surface livebait can be presented.
- What makes it appear natural: keep chunk sizes and delivery rate similar to what a predator would tear from bait — not a continuous carpet of blood and bits. Periodic pulses that mimic a feeding event produce more rises than a steady, heavy chum slick.
- Why livebait conversions succeed: once chunking draws fish into a shallower feeding band, selective bigeye will often refuse heavy trolled lures or big skirted baits. Converting to a single, lively bait with a subtle presentation (natural swimming action, light leader, free to dive) matches the hesitation of picky fish and hooks those that follow but don't strike noisy offerings.
- Depth and light windows: the method exploits shallower feeding windows—typically when fish can be pulled into ~10–60 m (commonly 10–40 m in tropical FAD work). Outside these windows (fish holding much deeper), chunking will either fail to raise them or only produce brief, distant marks.

## Reading Conditions — Signs the Opportunity is Building

- Sonar: a persistent bait layer under/around the FAD that tightens into a denser band or shows arches rising toward surface marks — especially when the band migrates up on successive passes. Thick, layered returns directly below structure with consistent small arches above are prime.
- Birds and night lights: birds working at dawn/dusk or lights attracting bait under a FAD at night indicate active surface/near‑surface bait and increase chance.
- Current: a mild to moderate current that crosses the structure rather than running perfectly parallel. Cross currents pull chunks into a slick that trails away from the FAD where fish can intercept it. Too strong a current disperses the slick; too little current makes the chum sit and overheat or scare bait.
- Boat pressure: if other boats are hooking up repeatedly, the structure is holding and you should match their timing and placement. If many boats are present but only sporadic hookups occur, fish may be tentative — a slower conversion and lighter livebaits are needed.
- Time of day: prime windows are low light — immediately before sunrise, during twilight, and at night. Overcast days extend the window; bright midday sun shortens it.

## Boat Positioning and Movement

- Position relative to the structure: you want to create a clean slick that travels predictably from the structure into the strike zone. Position slightly upcurrent of the FAD if your goal is to let chunks and scent sweep past an anchored drift line; position downcurrent if you want the slick to move under the transom where livebaits can be placed naturally in the trail.
- Drift vs. anchored: both work. Anchoring or drifting slowly around the FAD can maintain proximity; a controlled drift with chunking off the stern creates an ideal trail for livebait conversions following behind the boat. In crowded FADs, staying mobile helps avoid compressing the fish and spooking them.
- Spread and noise control: minimize deck noise and heavy gear movement when fish are lifting but refusing. The subtle period between a chunking pulse and conversion favors a quiet, deliberate switch to livebait.
- Sanity check: don’t chase every surface flash — look for repeatable marks, consistent rises, birds repeatedly returning to the same spot, or other boats that hook up on the same contour.

## Presentation Conversions — When and How to Switch

- Timing the switch: convert to livebait when chunking produces rises or consistent marks in the upper band but the fish fail to take heavier offerings, or when hookups are brief and fish appear to be following rather than committing. If the fish are aggressively taking skirts and plugs, stay with the heavier offerings until they become selective.
- Conversion philosophy: make the bait look like an uninjured, natural member of the slick. Reduce noise, reduce drag, and present a single livebait that can dart freely. The goal is to catch the follower that the chunking pulled up but couldn't entice.
- Depth targeting: present livebaits into the depth band where the fish are responding. That band often sits 10–40 m under the FAD in low light and may deepen as the sun rises. Use sonar to confirm where the bulk of marks are, then present bait to that zone rather than just at the surface.
- Leader philosophy: lighter, longer leaders and a freer bait action increase strikes from wary bigeye. Heavy fluorocarbon and multiple hook rigs will often cause failure on conversions; the fish are selective and inspect baits carefully.
- Livebait behavior: use baits that naturally relate to the local forage and are the right size for bigeye selectivity. Too large and fish may follow; too small and fish may not register them as worthwhile.

## Reading Sonar During the Work

- Look for: tight bait bands under the structure, arches that lift off a dense base, and any vertical movement pattern over repeated passes. Small, consistent arches in the upper band after chunking are the best cue to convert.
- Distinguish bait from fish: bait generally returns as a dense, consistent layer. Fish are more discrete arches and often scatter off the edge of the bait band. When arches start to look bigger and more defined inside the upper layer, the bite window is opening.
- Use repeat passes: make controlled loops and watch the layer evolve. A single pass is data; repeated observations show whether the bait band is lifting or spreading.

## Common Mistakes (Operational Failures)

- Over‑chunking: dumping too much chum too quickly either disperses the bait, creates a greasy slick that smothers activity, or conditions bigeye to a constant stimulus so they stop responding to pulses.
- Chunking in the wrong current: placing the boat where the slick will wash into a hole or off into open water reduces intercept probability. You need the slick to cut through the feeding lane, not be carried away from it.
- Converting too late or too early: wait too long and fish fall back; switch too early and you never test the more productive heavier baits. The right moment is when fish are close enough to the trail to inspect a single livebait.
- Standing on the FAD: too much boat concentration and repeated heavy chumming will either scare fish deeper or make them busy and less likely to take a clean livebait presentation.
- Misreading sonar: treating diffuse bait as fish and converting into the wrong depth band wastes the window. Confirm with repeat passes and by watching surface signs.
- Using heavy, noisy presentations for conversions: bigeye growing cautious will reject heavy offerings — a quiet, natural livebait is the correct conversion.

## Associated Species and Why They Appear

- Yellowfin and skipjack: these species also aggregate to structure and can respond more aggressively to chunking. When they dominate the strike pattern (fast, surface strike on lighter offerings), it may mean bigeye are deeper or subordinate at that moment.
- Mahi and wahoo: attracted to floating structure and often take chunked material or livebaits, complicating the selection process. Their presence can indicate active epipelagic bait but also increase pressure and strip bait quickly.
- Operational relevance: the mix tells you something about depth and light. If skipjack dominate, the bait field and feeding are shallower; if yellowfin and wahoo dominate but bigeye marks are deeper, stick to chunk pulses and wait for a low‑light conversion.

## Regional Differences

- Tropical FADs (equatorial): bait fields are often denser and fish are more surface‑active, especially at night around lights. Chunking at night with lighted FADs produces more predictable rises; conversions may be done shallower.
- Subtropical/offshore banks and canyons: bigeye hold deeper and are more pressure‑sensitive. Chunking is most effective at dawn/dusk when the layer is already moving upward; conversions may require longer leaders and deeper presentations.
- Current and water clarity: in clear, warm water bigeye may be more wary and prefer cleaner presentations; in stained water chunking can be heavier because visibility masks the trail and encourages bold feeding.
- Fleet behavior: where FADs are heavily fished, expect snap windows and change tactics quickly — lighter baits, stealth, and strategic movement between nearby structures.

## Tactical Understanding — Decision Tree

- Detect a structured bait band under a FAD.
- Is the layer shallow enough to be moved into 10–60 m by chunking? If yes, begin measured chunk pulses.
- Watch sonar and surface: do you see arches or rises? If yes and fish ignore heavy baits, convert to a single, natural livebait presented into the slick at the depth of the marks.
- If the fish respond aggressively to heavy offerings, continue with those until they tighten up. If no response after sustained, properly timed chunking, move to another structure — the aggregation may be deeper or absent.
- Maintain quiet, minimal deck activity during conversions; be ready to adjust leader length and bait size based on followers vs. immediate strikes.

## Hookup and Fight Notes Specific to Bigeye

- Hooking pattern: bigeye hooked on conversions often take a single measured strike and run hard and deep immediately. They are powerful, can make long straight-line runs, and will try to return to structure or make long downward busts.
- Initial fight: expect a fast, heavy take followed by head‑down pressure. Keep the rod low enough to maintain hook pull without letting the fish get full leverage into the structure’s shadow or mooring lines.
- Line management: allow controlled line on the first run to avoid breakoffs; apply steady pressure afterward to keep the fish from re‑entering tight structure. Bigeye can dart laterally and make violent head shakes; be ready to adjust drag smoothly.
- Fatigue window: once on the surface, bigeye can still surge. Use short, controlled bursts to bring them up rather than trying to stop them dead. Be prepared for multiple deep dives.
- Leader and hookup durability: thin, lighter leaders used for conversions improve strike rate but are more vulnerable to bite‑offs from smaller predators and to abrasion on mooring hardware. Balance stealth and durability based on situational cues.
- Handling after catch: bigeye are robust — handling quickly and minimizing air exposure ensures better quality and safety of crew.

## When to Abandon the Technique

- Fish drop deeper than the effective pull range (you see marks remaining 80–200 m and not moving up).
- The slick is being blown away or dissipated by high winds/current and chunking cannot form a useful trail.
- Repeated, long periods of no response despite correct chunking and conversions — move to another FAD or edge feature.
- Heavy boat congestion producing continual spooks or when aggressive species repeatedly strip all bait, preventing a selective conversion to bigeye.

## Observational Realism

- Windows are often brief and fickle. A FAD that produces two hookups on one pass may be empty the next hour.
- Bigeye can be present but unreachable: they hold under the bait but won't commit to rise because of light, pressure, or small changes in current. Chunking won't always work.
- Presentations that look perfect to you may still be inspected and rejected. The conversion is as much about timing and subtlety as about getting a perfect bait into the water.

Summary

Chunking and livebait conversions at FADs give fishermen a method to turn deep, structure‑associated bigeye into reachable targets during limited windows — especially low light and mild cross‑current conditions. Success depends on reading the bait and fish on sonar, placing the boat so the slick runs through the feeding lane, delivering measured chunk pulses, and converting quietly and quickly to a natural livebait presentation when selective fish lift into the upper band. Know when the window opens and when it closes; when it closes, move. The technique rewards patience, observation, and the willingness to adapt presentation and positioning to how the fish are actually behaving that day.
