## Opportunity

Bigeye are first and foremost a depth and light animal. The technique becomes the right answer when the fish are present but out of range or unwilling to take surface/near‑surface offerings — commonly along canyon walls, steep banks, seamount edges, temperature breaks, FAD edges, or over consistent mid‑water bait layers. Typical operational windows:

- Depth window: fish concentrated roughly 200–600 ft (60–180 m) is common; can extend deeper (800–1,200 ft / 240–360 m) in some regions. If sonar shows concentrated bait and targets in that band, vertical approaches matter.
- Light window: dawn, dusk, night, heavy overcast, or early/late low light when bigeye rise or become less cautious.
- Current window: light to moderate cross‑current or shear at an edge that holds bait in a column — not clean along‑contour flow. Little lateral push lets a vertical presentation sit in the feeding band.

Experienced captains choose deep‑drop/vertical jigging because it reaches fish that will not chase trolled offerings and because it presents a small, natural profile inside a tight bait layer where bigeye feed.

## How the Technique Works

What the presentation imitates
- Small mesopelagic prey (squid, lanternfish, small fishes) in and immediately beneath the bait layer. Slow lifts and falls mimic natural rises and falls; subtle twitches imitate fleeing prey in low light.

## How fish encounter it
- Bigeye often sit below or inside a dense bait band. A vertically presented jig dropped beneath the layer and retrieved upward passes into the fish’s strike zone repeatedly with minimal lateral displacement — a natural intercept of the prey path.

## Why this succeeds under these conditions
- In low light or when oxygen/temperature confines bait vertically, bigeye have limited strike envelopes and are more likely to take something that passes slowly through that column. Heavy trolling presentations or fast-moving teasers can bounce off or spook fish holding deep; a vertical profile is less intrusive.

## Why it can fail
- If bait is widely scattered, surface‑oriented, or the thermocline forces fish well below reachable range, vertical work is inefficient. Also, strong lateral current will drag a jig out of the strike zone, making it look unnatural.

## Reading Conditions: Signs to Use It and Signs to Stop

Use vertical/deep‑drop when you see:
- A persistent, thin bait layer with solid marks immediately above or below it on sonar.
- Targets holding tight at a consistent depth throughout drift or anchor.
- Bites at dawn/dusk/night on trolled offerings clustered to one contour or location (indicates vertical availability).
- Birds or surface pushes only during low light or short, sharp pushes at first light — suggesting fish are rising briefly.
- Repeated single‑boat hookups on turns or in a tight patch — the fish are keyed to that vertical column.

Abandon or switch when:
- Marks disappear after the light changes or current shifts.
- Sonar shows chaotic, diffuse bait with no clear depth band.
- Target depth is below safe or practical capture range for available rigs.
- Fish are actively chasing surface bait — switch to trolled/bait methods.

## Boat Positioning & Reading Sonar

- Positioning: put the boat up‑current or slightly upstream of the mark so your drop penetrates the column without dragging. Over an edge, hover the boat where the contour projects up into the feeding band — fish often sit on the stand‑off side of the current.
- Drift vs. anchor: drift or very light power is preferred to present vertically without too much swing. Anchoring can be useful on a tight patch at night but beware line entanglement and angle changes as tide shifts.
- Sonar cues: look for a thin, continuous bait layer and discrete arches or blobs in the strike band. A clean bait layer with fish marks below it is classic. Use down imaging/CHIRP to resolve the vertical relationship; if marks move with the bait or hold just below, that’s a green light for vertical work.
- Watch other boats closely — repeated hookups in a precise depth band by others often beats chasing single, transient strikes.

## Presentation Conversions and Cadence

- Entry point: drop straight through the layer and let the jig sit just under or in the bait band. A dead‑stop or very slow sink phase will often trigger a following strike.
- Cadence choices:
  - Slow pitch / lift and fall: good when fish are lethargic or biting on slow falls. Small upward lifts (short, controlled pulls) with long pauses let the jig hover in the zone.
  - Aggressive lifts and speed retrieves: use when marks are active and follow aggressively; the jig mimics a fleeing baitfish.
  - Stutter then fall: short quick lifts followed by a rapid drop can provoke a reaction strike when fish sit just under the layer.
- Converting from bait/trolled results: if trolled baits are getting nervous slashes or light picks but not hookups, downsize profile and slow the presentation — the fish want something smaller or less energetic. Conversely, if multiple followers are seen, increase motion to trigger commitment.
- Changing weight/profile: adjust jig vertical speed to match the apparent prey — if fish hit on fall, use a slower sinking profile; if they chase, increase action. Match the profile to the scale of the bait in the sonar image.

## Hookup and Fight Notes Specific to Bigeye

- Bite characteristics: strikes can be aggressive with a short, violent run or soft during slow falls. In low light, expect pickier, lighter strikes.
- Hooking: bites often occur on the fall or in the hover; maintain light, consistent tension rather than wildly yanking — bigeye can spit jigs if hooked shallow. A firm, well‑timed strip or sweep during a fall strike sets better than an excessively hard hookset.
- Initial run: expect a long, deep first run. Bigeye will head downhill quickly and try to return to the layer or structure. Prepare to feed line and recover as the fish tires.
- Fight profile: sustained deep dives, headshakes, and shaking of the hook are common. Pressure application should be steady — let the fish run when it wants to, then recover slowly. Quick, high‑stalled pressure near the end risks tearing hooks from soft tissue if the fish was struck shallow.
- Handling the endgame: once near surface, bigeye may barrel roll and make last violent attempts. Be ready for sudden near‑surface bursts and for a final deep surge if you lose pressure briefly.

## Common Mistakes (Operational Failures Experienced Crews Make)

- Fishing too broadly: dropping jigs without identifying a persistent bait/fish band wastes time. Vertical work is a focused tool.
- Wrong depth target: not trusting sonar and fishing the wrong stratum (too shallow or too deep) because of expectations rather than readings.
- Overly aggressive or inconsistent cadence: switching too often between styles before the fish show a preference.
- Holding position poorly: drifting across the mark without correcting for lateral movement so the jig never spends proper time in the strike zone.
- Ignoring short windows: continuing to grind when the light window closes instead of switching tactics or moving.
- Pulling too hard on light bites: losing fish that take during a slow fall by applying sudden, hard hooksets.
- Chasing random arches: mistaking transient noise or single fish marks for a school; vertical work rewards repeatable, persistent marks.

## Associated Species and Why They Appear

- Yellowfin tuna: may appear in the same features when bait rises into shallower bands; where yellowfin are present, they often take more active, faster offerings while bigeye stick deeper.
- Albacore: in cooler water or higher latitude fisheries, albacore can mix in when bait is mid‑column; their presence often signals colder currents and different bait composition.
- Opah (moonfish): shares mesopelagic prey and can show up on the same layers; catching opah on vertical patterns is common in some regions and indicates a healthy midwater forage field.

## Regional Differences

- Canyons & NE Pacific: steep walls concentrate bait and reward careful edge work at specific contours. Night windows and pre‑dawn are often decisive.
- Offshore seamounts & tropical Pacific: FADs and seamount edges may produce shallower bigeye in some seasons — presentations may be a bit shallower, and fish can be more tolerant of active jigging.
- Gulf/Caribbean: deep drop often intersects different deep prey assemblages; slower cadences and a focus on thermoclines matter more where oxygen layers are distinct.
- Adaptation rule: depth bands and light windows change by region; always calibrate cadence and depth to local sonar and observed behavior rather than copying a single style.

## Tactical Understanding — How Pros Think About It

- Start with sonar, not with a jig: identify the vertical relationship between bait and targets before committing.
- Treat the column like a conveyor: your job is to make the jig pass through the strike band repeatedly with believable motion and minimal lateral displacement.
- Time is a resource: low‑light and current windows are finite. If the markup is good, stay and refine cadence; if it closes, change tactics quickly (troll, set livebaits higher/lower, or leave the feature).
- Watch other boats and birds for confirmation, but trust your own returns: a single hookup off your boat that you can reproduce by returning to the same depth is worth focusing on.
- Incremental changes win: small tweaks to fall rate, pause length, and lift amplitude often turn refusals into bites.

## Observational Realism

- Expect stubbornness: bigeye will often follow, bump, or inspect a perfect presentation and still refuse. That’s normal — they’re selective.
- Windows are brief: bait rises and light changes can flip availability in minutes. Be ready to shift presentations on the fly.
- Schools move: marks disperse and re‑concentrate as currents and bait shift. Re‑establish marks with a quick sonar pass before committing to a long set.
- Pressure effects: heavy fishing pressure on a feature can make bigeye twitchier; more subtle, slower presentations usually succeed where aggressive jigs do not.

Summary (When to Use It)

Use deep‑drop and vertical jigging for bigeye when your sonar shows a consistent bait layer with fish marks in a reachable depth band, and when light or current conditions create a discrete feeding column. Vertical work is about patiently presenting a natural profile through that band — read the marks, position for the column, choose a cadence that matches the fish’s mood, and be prepared for deep initial runs and a long, grinding fight. Switch quickly if the window closes; succeed by observation and subtle adaptation, not brute force.
