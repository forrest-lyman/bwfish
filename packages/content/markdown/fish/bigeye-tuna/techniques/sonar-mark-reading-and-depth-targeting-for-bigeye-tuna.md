The
 technique is simple in concept and exacting in practice: use the echosounder to find where bigeye are holding in the water column, understand why those marks exist, then match your presentation and boat work to that depth, light window, and current. For bigeye the depth axis is the fishery—getting depth right is more important than adding speed or flash.

## The Opportunity

- Why choose this method: Bigeye frequently organize off edges, thermoclines, and deep bait layers. When fish are not visible at the surface the only reliable clue is an acoustic mark. Sonar mark reading and depth targeting turns those echoes into tactical choices—whether to slow-troll deep plugs, vertical jig, drift chunk, or hold station with live baits.
- Typical situations:
  - Dense mid‑column layer 50–300 m deep that holds through daylight because of temperature/oxygen structure.
  - A tight, persistent blob or line on the edge of a canyon or drop-off that repeats on the same contour.
  - Marks that lift or thin at dawn/dusk or under overcast—small vertical movements can open a short feeding window.
  - Night layers that begin to loosen after lights go off or as moonlight changes.
- What you’re exploiting: Bigeye follow and concentrate on stacked forage at specific depths. They are often less active at the surface in bright light; a single upward move of bait or a brief light window will make deep fish available. Sonar provides the vertical and horizontal picture to time that move.

## How the Technique Works

- What the presentation must imitate: the depth, speed, and orientation of the natural forage field shown on the screen. If bait is holding at 120 m and the mark sits along a steep contour, the presentation must be where the fish are, not where they might be seen.
- Sonar logic:
  - Low‑frequency (50/83 kHz) fills out deep aggregates—good for locating deep blobs and the base of a scattering layer.
  - High‑frequency/CHIRP sharpens individual arches and separates bait from fish—useful when marks are close to the surface or when deciding whether those returns are bait or tuna.
  - Short time‑scale changes matter: a mark that holds its depth over successive passes is valuable; a mark that drifts or collapses is transient.
- From mark to method:
  - Shallow to mid marks (e.g., 30–80 m): deep divers, heavy trolling plugs, deep lures, or fast jigging can be effective. If marks are tight and slow-moving, convert to live baits or vertical presentations.
  - Mid to deep marks (e.g., 80–200+ m): vertical tactics (drop baits, heavy jigs), chunking with heavy sinkers, or downrigger setups are generally required. Trolling may work only with heavy divers and slow speeds—and only if fish will chase.
  - Tight, dense marks directly beneath the boat: stop the spread, hold station, and present baits vertically. A free‑line or short leader vertical bait often converts where spreader bars only tick.
- Presentation realism: match sink‑rate and silhouette to how the bait layer appears on sounder. A slow, hovering profile above the mark will provoke different responses than a fast, passing plug.

## Reading Conditions

- Signs the window is forming:
  - The mark thins upward and shows individual arches rising toward the surface at dawn or dusk.
  - Bait layer begins to show a “top” that breaks into smaller clumps—predators are working up through it.
  - Birds or surface activity appear simultaneously with an upward shift on sonar.
  - Repeated strong returns on the same contour on several passes—fish are holding in place.
- Signs the opportunity is closing:
  - Marks sink and compress into a denser, lower layer as light increases.
  - Returns become diffuse and patchy, indicating fish have dispersed or moved under oxygen/temperature limits.
  - The mark disappears when you approach—either the fish moved off the feature or pressure pushed them down.
- Sonar settings and interpretation habits:
  - Sweep range to include the entire water column you expect fish to use—don’t run with range too shallow and miss deeper marks.
  - Adjust gain conservatively: over‑amplifying creates phantom returns, under‑amplifying hides weak tuna marks in deep water.
  - Watch for vertical movement across repeated passes; a single pass is rarely decisive.

## Boat Positioning and Fleet Awareness

- Position relative to current and contour:
  - If fish are holding facing current/feeding into the flow, present baits up‑current so the leader and bait move naturally past their feeding face.
  - On edges or canyon lips, position slightly upcurrent of the mark and make controlled passes; approach from an angle that keeps the mark in the transducer cone.
- Station keeping vs. running a spread:
  - If marks are scattered but persistent, keep slow trolling with the spread at the depth of the marks.
  - If marks are tight and beneath the boat, kill or drastically reduce speed and convert to vertical offerings.
- Reading fleet activity:
  - One hookup on a mark with fish that stay on the sonar often indicates a productive edge; repeated hookups in the same area are the strongest signal to commit.
  - If multiple boats push and marks vanish, consider a deeper or vertical conversion rather than racing for more surface presentations.

## Common Mistakes

- Misidentifying bait for fish: dense bait shows a fine, uniform scatter; tuna create thicker, discrete arches or blobs with a different texture. Acting on bait-only returns wastes time.
- Acting on a single pass: sonar is temporal. Marks that are present on one pass and gone on the next are often transient—waiting to see repeatability saves presentations.
- Staying shallow because you “saw birds”: birds can indicate a local, short-lived surface event. If the sonar shows fish still deep, chasing birds with shallow plugs is often futile.
- Wrong presentation for depth: continuing to troll shallow plugs over a 120 m mark or running heavy vertical offerings over a shallow, mobile mark will both fail.
- Poor transducer discipline: changing range/gain or switching cones while tracking a mark can mask vertical movement; keep settings consistent while evaluating a window.

## Presentation Conversions (operational choices)

- When marks lift into a mid column (40–90 m):
  - Move from heavy divers to slower deep plugs or a short vertical live bait. Hold speed low and keep the lures at the depth the marks occupy.
- When marks are deep and steady (80–300 m):
  - Stop the spread and deploy vertical methods: drop baits, heavy jigs, or chunk and soak at the depth revealed by the sonar. If trolling is the only option, add weight/divers and slow down—expect fewer strikes but bigger fish.
- When marks are tight beneath the boat and rising:
  - Kill the engines or idle, present a vertical bait or jig, and keep the presentation directly over the mark. This is the highest‑probability conversion.
- When marks are mobile and following bait:
  - Keep moving with the mark at the same angle and speed it is using. Avoid abrupt turns that scatter bait and break the school’s pattern.

## Fish‑Specific Hookup and Fight Notes

- Typical strike and fight behaviour:
  - Bigeye often hit hard and then make deep, head‑down runs to structure or down into the thermocline. Expect short, violent initial runs followed by long directional pulls.
  - Deep hookups are common—fish can swallow baits or take them deep, increasing cut‑offs and leader stress.
- Operational fight management:
  - Apply steady, sustained pressure. Avoid aggressive high‑cadence pumping that lets the fish surge back to depth quickly.
  - When hooked deep near structure, keep the fish moving laterally away from the edge before attempting to tire and lift—allowing them to circle or get under ledges almost always costs you the fish.
  - If you detect heavy headshakes and sudden stops, reduce aggressive hauling and let the drag work; sudden violent attempts to stop the fish risk break-offs on deep runs.
- Post‑hook considerations:
  - A fish that goes deep immediately after hookup has likely been hooked low in the jaw or deeper—expect short leader life and prepare for quick, hard fights.
  - Be ready to convert hookups from trolling to vertical control: maintain motor position and avoid panicked running of the boat over the fish.

## Associated Species

- Yellowfin tuna: appear in the same general areas but usually shallower and more light‑sensitive. On sonar they may show as higher, looser marks; distinguishing yellowfin from deep bigeye is a key decision for presentation.
- Albacore: in cooler water or on the edge of a feature you can get albacore marks that are more surface‑oriented and mobile.
- Mahimahi and other surface predators: indicate surface bait activity but do not guarantee that deep bigeye are accessible; they can be a false positive for surface tactics.
Explain why they show up: the same structure and bait attract multiple predators across depth zones; sonar helps separate which depth bracket each species is using.

## Regional Differences

- Eastern Tropical Pacific / seamounts & FADs:
  - Bigeye often use the deeper parts of the scattering layer and FAD-associated aggregations. Marks may be very deep relative to similar features elsewhere—expect deeper vertical tactics.
- Subtropical canyon fisheries:
  - Canyon walls create sharp edges and more predictable holding positions. Marks will often stitch to the contour; passing the same contour repeatedly is valuable.
- Temperate offshore banks:
  - Thermocline depth and oxygen minima shape how high bigeye will come. In areas with strong oxygen restrictions they will be constrained to narrower depth bands—sonar will show a compressed layer.

Tactical Checklist (quick decisions for the deck)

- Is the mark repeatable across passes on the same contour? Yes → commit; No → watch and wait.
- Is the mark shallow enough for trolling/divers or is vertical work required? Match presentation to depth.
- Are marks moving up at dawn/dusk or under cloud cover? Be ready to kill speed and convert quickly.
- Are birds present but sonar shows deep marks? Prioritize sonar depth over surface cues.
- Did a hookup occur? Re‑evaluate: did fish stay on the mark? If yes, stay and convert; if they left, move on.

Final thought

Sonar mark reading and depth‑targeting for bigeye is less about chasing sound and more about translating vertical information into a presentation that sits exactly where the fish are willing to take it. Time windows are often short; the experienced boat puts the transducer cone on the right depth, keeps settings consistent, and converts cleanly when the mark shows the fish are inside the catchable column.
