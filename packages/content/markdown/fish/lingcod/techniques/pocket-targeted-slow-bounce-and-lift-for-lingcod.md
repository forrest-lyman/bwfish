Lingcod live in holes. Not vague cover — real three‑dimensional pockets: ledge mouths, concave seams, crevices behind boulders. The slow bounce & lift is a patient way to deliver a small profile right into those throats and let the fish make the decision. You’re not trying to yank them out of cover; you’re offering the bait the way real prey drifts, hesitates, and drops into darkness.

## Where lingcod sit and why the slow approach works
You’ll see it on sonar and on the boat: a dark notch on the face, arches stacked just inside the relief, maybe a single arch clinging to the mouth. Lingcod don’t always hit hard and fast. Often they let a bait enter their hole, inspect it, then take on a pause or a gentle fall. That’s the behavior this presentation targets.

The slow bounce imitates a small baitfish or crab carried by current that bumps into a pocket, hesitates, then stalls or falls. The lift looks like an evasive kick; the fall and pause look like the prey sliding into the cover. The technique reduces sweep across the face, keeps the lure in the strike plane, and gives a cautious fish time to commit.

Best windows are when you can control the drift so the lure approaches from slightly above or across the mouth — not dragging parallel — and when current nudges bait across relief. Early and late in tide changes, or any moment current funnels across a notch, are the typical windows.

## How to aim the lure — reading structure and drift
Start with what the structure tells you, not your instincts to hammer the bottom.

You want to present the lure so it drops into the mouth, not drag along the face. On sonar look for a break in the return or a darker seam, a concave contour, a vertical crack, or arches sitting just inside the edge. Those are the mouths.

Ledge orientation matters. Approach so the bait moves into the mouth from above or across the face. If you present from below the lure swings into blind zones or tangles on the bottom. Use drift and engine control to let current carry the lure to the mouth with a controlled drop rather than a hard bump.

If the pocket is noticeably deeper than surrounding rock, be prepared for a longer fall — count it and let the lure pause in the throat. Shallow pockets mean shorter lifts and a focus on the pause/fall rhythm.

## Cadence and presentation — how to keep the lure in the strike plane
The slow bounce breaks into three phases: contact, lift, fall/pause.

- Contact: keep a low line angle and the rod tip pointed down. Feel is everything — small taps, a slide, the moment the jig collapses into a cavity. If you lose that tactile link, you lose the pocket.
- Lift: lift 2–4 feet, slow and deliberate (1–2 seconds). This shouldn’t be a snap; a fast sweep drags the lure out of the strike plane. Think of a startle-kick, not a sprint.
- Fall/pause: let the jig drop back into the mouth and hold it motionless for 2–6 seconds. Start with 2–3 seconds; if you see follows or tentative taps, extend to 4–6 seconds.

Repeat the cycle. Lingcod often need multiple, consistent invitations before they commit.

Gear and weight choices tie directly to the rhythm and the current:

- Light current/shallow (20–40 ft): 3–6 oz. Keeps the action subtle and preserves feel.
- Moderate current/medium depth (40–80 ft): 6–10 oz. Holds bottom contact without excessive drift.
- Strong current/deep relief (>80 ft or heavy surge): 10–16+ oz. You need instant contact and the ability to tug the jig into a pocket without losing line control.

Rod and line: a moderate‑power to moderate‑heavy rod with a soft to moderate tip (about 6'6"–7'6") gives sensitivity on the fall and absorbs headshakes. Too stiff and you kill feel; too soft and you lose control. Braid for sensitivity and low stretch; add a short shock leader or fluorocarbon butt section for abrasion resistance. Keep your drag set light and smooth so you can feel short takes but still drive the hook when required.

Line angle: keep it low and soft. A steep angle lifts the jig out of the pocket; a long, low angle lets you feel subtle entrances.

## When a fish follows but won’t commit — the conversion sequence
You’ll see a tailing fish at the mouth, a follow 3–5 ft out, or soft taps during the fall. Don’t panic-hook — a hard, immediate set will yank the bait out of the pocket and teach that fish to refuse.

Step 1 — Hold contact. Return to minimal lifts that keep the lure at or just inside the mouth. Let it fall into that throat repeatedly so the fish can make a decision.

Step 2 — Subtle coaxing. Between lifts, give a very small horizontal twitch or rock of the rod tip — enough to alter the silhouette without sweeping the bait free.

Step 3 — Extended pause. After a controlled lift, hang the lure motionless at the mouth for 5–10 seconds if the fish is especially cautious.

Step 4 — The soft set. When you feel that heavier, throaty pull or a definitive hesitation, feed any slack and execute a slow, firm sweep to drive the jig into the fish’s mouth. If you only have follows and light taps, allow another one or two drop cycles rather than jumping to a hard set.

If the fish follows and then leaves, change the presentation vector: drift slightly upcurrent or downcurrent to present from a different angle, or change weight by one increment to alter the fall profile.

## What different feels tell you
Pay attention to the tactile language of the bottom:

- Sharp little ticks on bottom contact: probably junk rock or small rockfish. Test the pocket but don’t overreact.
- Soft, sustained resistance during a pause: an in‑pocket inspection — hold and wait longer.
- Immediate hard pull on the fall: fish committed on the drop — set decisively.
- Repeated follows without pressure: cautious lingcod or pressured fish — lengthen pauses and minimize movement.

Those sensations are signals. Respond with controlled, incremental changes rather than wholesale switching.

## Mistakes that kill the window
The usual killers are loss of contact and impatience.

- Losing bottom contact: either you're using too much weight or your rod tip is too high. You need feel.
- Sweeping too fast: aggressive lifts drag the bait out of the strike plane.
- Overreacting to taps: hammering the rod on every tick encourages refusals.
- Wrong drift angle: dragging parallel to the ledge rarely puts the bait into a mouth.
- Flipping cadence constantly: fish that need several repeated invites will tire of you changing rhythms every minute.

Fix one variable at a time. If followers persist, adjust angle or weight one step; don’t reset everything.

## Associated species and regional tweaks
On the same faces you’ll often see rockfish (coppers, vermilion, bocaccio) and species like cabezon and greenling — their activity can show you where pockets are active. Halibut tend to sit on sand adjacent to rock; if you spot tailing halibut near an edge you may need to change to a more open profile.

Regional differences are predictable:

- Northern waters (BC, N. California): deeper ledges and stronger currents. Expect heavier weights (8–16+ oz), slightly stiffer rods, and longer pauses.
- Southern California: more kelp and boulder patch reefs; pockets are often shallower. Lighter weights (3–8 oz) and shorter, subtler lifts work better.
- Local bait types matter: crab‑heavy areas favor a profile that stalls and flips; baitfish areas favor subtle darting and fall.

Match your silhouette to what the local prey does.

## Tactical checklist — what to watch on a drift
- Sonar: confirm a pocket or arches inside relief before committing.
- Line angle: keep it low and soft.
- Drift control: adjust speed/engine so the lure approaches from the correct vector.
- Cadence discipline: commit to a rhythm for 3–6 full cycles before changing.
- Conversion patience: followers usually need repeated entries; don’t rush the set.
- When to move: if nervous follows persist after several adjustments, the fish is likely spooked or absent — try a nearby pocket or change cadence/weight one clear step.

Restraint and geometry win more pockets than brute force. Deliver the bait into the throat, hold it in the strike plane, and let the fish decide. When you combine a steady drift, the right silhouette, and the slow bounce cadence, a hesitant follow often becomes a decisive take.
