# Lingcod

Lingcod are structure fish and patient ambushers. You find them where rock breaks, current feeds, and a single hole or face concentrates bait. Pinnacles, ledges, boulder fields, reef edges and wreckage — anywhere the current brings food past a defensible station — that's where they sit. They don't carpet the bottom; they pick positions that let them hit passing bait without leaving cover.

## Why Broken Bottom Holds Better Fish

I see more and bigger lingcod where the bottom is irregular. Smooth reef will give you fish, but the better, hold-their-ground fish prefer holes, caves, overhangs and jagged faces. Those features create pockets and current breaks that slow and concentrate bait.

Watch for these signs on the sounder and in the drift: bait hugging rock, marks stacked tight to structure, and bites that come when your lure drops into a seam or clears a ledge. Bigger fish often live on the less-pressured corners and deep edges — the spots other boats pass over. The lingcod itself will often be below the visible life, tucked under rock, waiting for the column to sweep past.

## Drift Angle: Get Your Lure Through the Face

What makes or breaks a drift is whether your presentation actually enters the fish's station. A bad drift will run parallel to the reef and never sweep the face; a good drift drives the jig or bait into the passing pocket.

Practical points I use every trip:
- start the run above the structure so your line travels down into the face,
- keep the bait touching or sweeping the bottom rather than hanging high in the water,
- read your line angle — it tells you whether you're sweeping the structure or skimming past,
- repeat the productive edge until you stop getting bites,
- and leave before a hang or lost gear tells you more about the rock than the fish.

Most bites hit as the lure clears a ledge or drops into broken relief. That's the moment the fish can drive out and grab the offering without exposing itself.

## Pressure: Where The Better Fish Move

Lingcod populations concentrate and shift under pressure. Small, easy-to-find rocks will quickly produce only little fish when they’re hard-pressed; the larger lingcod tend to hang on edges, deeper corners, and out-of-the-way rock that takes longer to find.

You’ll notice patterns:
- high spots and obvious pinnacles often yield only small fish,
- bites that come from edges instead of the center of a reef,
- fish holding tighter after a few repeated drifts,
- and a stronger bite when current first comes up.

If a spot is hammered, change where you run the drift. Push to the uphill side, the lee of a boulder, or the shadowed corner. That’s where the better fish migrate when the easy stations are fished out.

## How Regional Shape Changes The Bite

On the Pacific Coast the bite is a product of swell, drift control, depth limits, rockfish life and how much usable hard bottom is available. Local factors — bait type, water color and current strength — decide whether lingcod sit buried in relief or come up into the column to feed.

The rule that matters: read the bottom first, then the edge. The rock sets the options; the working edge and current decide which ones hold fish. Move with that pattern rather than against it, and you’ll find the spots that consistently produce.
