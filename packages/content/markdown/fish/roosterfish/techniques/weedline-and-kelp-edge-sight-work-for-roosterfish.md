The weedline and kelp edge are simple pieces of structure that do one thing well: compress bait and make predictable feeding lanes. Along Baja beaches and pockets those lanes are where sight‑work matters — the bait is visible, the rooster is visible, and each cast is a tactical choice. This isn't a drill about lures; it's about geometry, timing, and reading the fish so the bait looks like the easiest meal in the lane.

## Why the weedline and kelp edge make windows for roosters
- The physical result is obvious: tide and rip push sardina or mullet into a narrow strip where they can’t scatter. Birds, boils and repeating strikes show you where that lane sits.
- Roosterfish use the edge as a blind and a funnel. They don’t chase through open water as often as you think; they ambush or intercept bait that’s forced into one escape route.
- When bait is pinned, a correctly angled, correctly paced presentation reads as trapped bait. Get angle, speed and depth wrong and the fish will follow and refuse instead of committing.

How the edge translates into prey motion (what you’re exploiting)
Roosters respond to a confined, predictable motion. Your job is to make the bait move through that lane in the way a trapped sardina would — not dart across it or sail out into open water.

- If bait moves parallel to the seam and stays in the escape corridor, the rooster sees a forced meal and is likelier to intercept.
- If your bait slices across the weeds or pulls wide, it looks like an escape. The fish will either follow and lose interest or overrun the bait.
- Surface presentations when bait is shallow; subsurface twitches when fish are a foot or two deeper just off the edge. Speed is the variable you must tune: too slow looks vulnerable; too fast the rooster over‑runs.

When the sight window exists — and when it closes
Read the seam, not your phone. This method works while the bait is compressed and the fish are operating in short, directional runs.

Signs to fish the edge:
- Bait tightly pinned against a visible weedline or kelp mat.
- Rooster backs, combs or short dashes within a few yards of the edge.
- Repeating boils or birds working the same strip.
- Tide or rip pushing bait into the edge (incoming on a point, outgoing into a rip).
- Water clear enough to see fish or sonar shows bait nailed to the structure.

When to walk away:
- The seam disappears and bait spreads out.
- Roosters stop making short, directional runs and begin to roam or sit deep.
- Heavy surf or murk removes visual cues.
- Angling pressure has made visible fish wary — repeated casts have trained them to refuse.
- Fish follow repeatedly but never close; swap to a deeper or live‑bait approach.

Approach geometry — how to put the presentation where it needs to be
Approach and boat position change the game more than another lure.

- Angle: come in downline and off the fish’s sight. Think 20°–45° to the feeding lane, not straight at the fish. That angle gives a natural lead and reduces boat shadow.
- Drifting: cross parallel to the seam and cast ahead so the bait moves back into the fish’s path.
- Anchoring/idling: set up with the boat behind the fish’s sightline, so casts land ahead and drift back along the lane.
- Lateral clearance: keep the hull outside the outer edge. A boat inside the lane compresses or blows the seam. If you spook fish, circle wide and reset 50–100 yards beyond where they were.
- Local examples:
  - Arroyo beach breaks — approach abreast of the rip, cast downline into the outrun of trapped bait and retrieve parallel to the seam.
  - Los Barriles sand fingers — sand tongues create narrow channels; work the channel lip from the outside and cast so the bait moves toward shore along the channel edge.
  - Loreto pockets — present along the outer lip; roosters use the pocket to push bait into calmer water and strike hard from the edge.

Presentation sequence — read the fish, then escalate
Treat each sighting as a short traffic light. One clean, correct presentation early usually outperforms a lot of sloppy casts.

## Stage 1 — Visual engagement (first pass)
- Start with a low‑profile wake or a quick surface pitch aimed to trigger a look. The intent is a short reaction: look, tail slap, a burst. If the fish commits, convert immediately.
- Conversion cue: direct, increasing speed toward the bait and head aimed at it. Keep the bait moving aggressively into the lane.

## Stage 2 — If the fish follows but doesn't eat
- Slow down and increase hang time. Switch to a slower walk or a subsurface twitch that stays in the lane longer. Fish that follow and refuse often need vulnerability — give it to them.
- Conversion cue: follow distance shortens, fish aligns with the bait, or you see the fish show a shoulder. When that happens, tighten cadence and hit the strike window.

## Stage 3 — Persistent follows with no close
- Pitch a live bait or a heavier presentation ahead in the lane. If artificial motion won’t convince, a real bait that can’t escape often forces the intercept.
- Conversion cue: the fish tracks the bait closely and hesitates only in the last yard — that’s when the live bait gets it.

## Stage 4 — Last‑resort restraint
- If follows deteriorate into circling or the fish starts breaking off, cut your attempts. Shorten the presentation window, reduce casts, and only present when you have a clean sightline.

Conversion cues — practical reads, not theory
Watch the fish, not the lure. These are the real signals that decide whether you accelerate, slow, or change tactics.

- Hot: head down, comb up, direct fast chase — immediate, aggressive presentation into the lane.
- Interested/cautious: distant follows, pecks, small blows — slow the action and increase hang time.
- Suspicious: fish stalls and studies the bait or drops below it — change depth or pitch live bait further into the corridor.
- Defensive: tail slaps, abrupt angles away — back off, pause, and re‑present from a fresh angle later.

Pressure management — keep the window open
Pressure kills windows faster than current.

- Single‑clean presentation: favor quality over quantity. One or two well‑judged casts beat constant pitching.
- Boat discipline: keep the hull low. Kill the engine and drift where possible, or slip backwards into position so you don’t sit between the fish and their escape lane.
- Angler rotation: use your best caster for the first conversion attempt. Close shots and line splashes matter.
- Quiet re‑approach: if a follow fades, move 50–75 yards away and re‑enter from a different angle. Fish will accept a second presentation if they don’t see the same boat hovering.
- Time and traffic: early and late light reduce visual pressure. In busy ports like Loreto pick isolated pockets rather than competing on crowded seams.

## Common mistakes I see
- Coming straight at the fish: forces them to back into deep water or change behavior.
- Overworking a fish: repeating identical casts trains refusal.
- Presenting outside the lane: casts that slide across the weeds look like escape routes.
- Mistiming speed changes: jerks at the wrong moment drive misses.
- Ignoring tides and swell: a seam that worked an hour ago can vanish — read the edge every pass.

## Associated species and what they tell you
- Jacks: often work the same seam and can both point to the right lane and cause sudden commotion. Use jack busts to present aggressively.
- Sierra and small gamefish: indicate current and trapped bait but can also scatter the bait before a rooster commits.
- Mullet and sardina: their behavior — fleeing into the wash or compressing against the kelp — dictates where and when the lane forms.

## Regional notes — how Baja spots change the play
- Los Barriles inner lee sand fingers: lanes are tight and shallow. Roosters get close and spooky — short, accurate casts and low profiles win.
- Arroyo and beach breaks: rips make transient lanes. Be prepared to move down the beach and present to multiple small pockets rather than sitting on one fish.
- Loreto pockets and tongues: fish sit on the outer lip and strike explosively. Present a bit further out and be ready: the strike window is very short and pressure from other boats shortens your margin for error.

## Decision rules for experienced anglers
- If you can see the fish making directional runs in a confined lane, choose sight‑work at the edge over blind casting.
- Start with one clean, aggressive surface approach. Escalate to slower or live presentations based on how the fish follows.
- Adjust boat and angle before you change lures. Geometry usually wins.
- Use follow distance as your primary cue: close follows = speed; distant follows = vulnerability.
- When unsure, give fish more time and space. A spooked rooster rarely returns to the same micro‑lane within 10–20 minutes.

## A compact example — Los Barriles sand finger
- Spot: narrow weedline outside a sand finger.
- Observation: single rooster shadowing 8–10 yards off the edge, bait compressed, birds working.
- Boat work: idle 30–40 yards beyond the lane, angle in at ~25°, cast to land 6–8 yards ahead of the fish.
- Sequence: one fast surface draw to trigger a look; when the fish follows but stops at 3 yards, slow the action and hang in the lane, then accelerate into the last yard. If two clean attempts fail, pitch a live bait ahead and let it drift naturally through the corridor.
- Aftermath: pull the boat back out of sight, wait 10–15 minutes, re‑enter from a different angle if the fish disappears.

Observational realism
Roosters will do odd things — chase a jack, blow past your bait, turn and refuse. The edge gives you an improved probability, not a guarantee. Treat each sighting like a short window: read the lane and the mood, pick the least intrusive, most natural presentation, and let the rooster decide.

Think like the fish and respect the lane. When bait is pinned and your presentation travels through that escape corridor, sight‑casting at the weedline and kelp edge becomes your best percentage play on Baja beaches and pockets.
