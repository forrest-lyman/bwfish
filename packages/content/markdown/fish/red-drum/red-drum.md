# Red Drum

Red drum live the edge. Watch marsh banks, oyster bars, grass lines, potholes, surf troughs, points, and shallow flats — wherever the tide opens cover or squeezes bait into a lane. When the edge is right the fish are usually there for a reason: a tide window, a push of bait, a soft spot on the bottom, or a place to sit out pressure.

They can be hard-charging when things line up, but they’re not random. Expect behavior tied to water level, bait movement, bottom texture, and how often boats have worked the shoreline.

## Where the tide hands them bait

Observation first: reds show up where the tide gives them access. Rising water turns grass and marsh edges into feeding flats. Falling water pulls shrimp, crabs, mullet, and small bait out of cover and down drains and cuts.

You’ll see it before you see fish:

- bait flicking along a grass edge
- shrimp popping on a drain
- wakes moving down a bank
- tails over flooded grass or oysters
- muds forming in shallow potholes
- birds working a pocket of small bait

Those signs mark a current lane or shallow ambush. The edge you fish at 30 minutes before high will not be the same 30 minutes after—move with the water. Put the boat ahead of the bait and present across the path, not behind it. If the tide is rising, fish casts that ride into the grass; if it’s falling, hit drains and the mouths of cuts where bait evacuates.

## Reading a school versus a lone fish

School behavior is different. A group on the move will usually push a bank, mud a flat, or travel a shoreline in a way you can see. Singles tend to tuck into potholes, deeper cuts, or quiet, snaggy edges and wait.

What to watch and how it changes your approach:
- Direction of travel: follow the school's vector, place your casts where they're going to be, not where they were.
- Feeding vs traveling: noisy surface work ≈ active feeding; tight, steady wakes ≈ moving fish that will hit a passing bait.
- Bait size and bottom hardness: bigger bait and hard bottoms suit bigger reds; small bait on soft mud favors smaller fish and longer, slower presentations.
- Water clarity and sun angle: bright sun and clear water make fish spook on sloppy moves; low sun or dirty water lets you get tighter.
- Pressure: a well-pressured shoreline changes the school’s timing and lane.

The wake only matters if your lure or bait ends up in the fish’s path. Cast to the forward edge of the school and let the current do the rest.

## How pressure moves reds

Reds in shallow water learn quickly. Repeatedly banging a hull on a bank, dragging a trolling motor through drains, noisy poling, or sloppy approach will push fish off the edge. Often the bank looks perfect but the fish have slid to the drain or into deeper cuts.

Dirty water will hide the boat but it also hides the bite—sometimes that’s the trade you want. If you’ve burned a pocket, back off, let it sit through a tide change, or shift to adjacent drains and cuts. Approach quiet, present down-current, and be surgical with casts rather than trying to dominate the shoreline.

## How region shapes where you look

Observation over maps: the landscape tells you the likely spots.

- Louisiana: reds key drains, ponds, oysters, and muddy shallows. The network of cuts and ponds creates predictable moving lanes on every tide.
- Texas: grass, potholes, flats, and jetties are where they show. Look for edges that concentrate bait on the tide.
- Carolinas & Florida: creeks, docks, oysters, surf, and flood tides are the common holding and feeding areas.

Same fish, different structures. Read the local shape first, then apply the edge rules above.

Red drum fishing is tide reading with a fish attached. Read the water, watch the bait, stay quiet, and present where the edge is actually directing the food.
