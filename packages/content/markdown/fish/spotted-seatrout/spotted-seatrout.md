# Spotted Seatrout

Spotted seatrout are soft-edge fish. You find them where two pieces of habitat meet — grass into sand, pothole into flat, channel edge into oyster, a drop-off that funnels bait. They don't sit in the middle of a flat for no reason; they sit where bait can be ambushed or channeled past them by current and temperature.

From a distance they look scattered. Watch long enough and the pattern tightens: one depth, one edge, one bait lane will suddenly produce most of the fish. That's the window you press when it opens.

## Where Trout Stack — Grass, Potholes, and Depth

The most consistent observation: trout position where bait can be moved from cover into open water. Potholes in grass, the uphill side of a channel edge, shallow-to-deep transitions — these create lanes where bait is predictable.

You'll see it before you catch it. Mullet or glass minnows flip over a grass seam. A slick will form along the same line every morning. Birds concentrate on the same patch. Bait stacks on the drop-off and the strikes come from one side of a pothole. After a cold front the fish often hang deeper, using darker bottom as cover.

Why it works: the trout want predictable movement. They use edges and holes to force the bait into short, repeatable runs. When you can read that lane, you can present consistently.

Useful signs to confirm the lane:
- bait flipping or feeding over grass
- repeatable slicks along the same line
- birds working a concentrated patch of shrimp or minnows
- bait stacked on a drop-off with fish holding just below
- strikes concentrated on one side of a pothole
- fish suddenly holding deeper after a cold push

The slick usually shows where feeding just happened, not necessarily where the school now sits. Use it as a cue to search the adjacent edges and depth changes.

## Reading the Tide and Temperature Window

Trout are temperature- and tide-driven. Small changes flip their behavior.

Observation first: cold water pushes trout deeper or toward darker bottom; warm, stable water pulls them out onto flats. Tide determines how bait runs across those structures — whether it crosses the grass edge slowly, funnels through a channel, or pours over a pothole.

When the bite improves you'll notice a handful of consistent signs: bait comes back onto the flat, slicks begin to form, fish move from a channel edge up into the grass edge, birds work lower, and strikes often come on pauses or current swings. When it fades the bait scatters, fish quit chasing, strikes get short, and the school slides deeper.

Read the timing. The same flat can be dead on a falling tide and on fire on the next flood if the bait starts crossing that edge differently. Temperature sets the broad behavior, tide and current choreograph the movement.

## How Pressure and Disturbance Kill a Bite

Trout in clear, shallow water are pressure-sensitive. You can see it: a boat drifts too close, you cast repeatedly into a compact school, or you make a noisy approach and the better fish stop feeding while the rest hang on.

The pattern is consistent — the fish often remain in place, but the larger, more willing ones shut down first. They'll hold and refuse before they move. That's the moment you adjust: back off, change angle, or come back later with a different presentation. Pushing on a tight school usually trades the big fish for a few short grabs.

## Regional Shape — Same Species, Different Flats

The broad drivers are constant, but local pieces change the picture. Gulf seatrout behavior bends with grass type, oyster complexity, tidal range, water clarity, winter fronts, and summer heat. A flat in Texas will read differently from one in Louisiana, Florida, or the Carolinas because the bottom, bait, and water color change how edges form and how bait moves.

Treat a flat like a small system: map its depth, note the grass seams, find the potholes and uphill sides of channels, and watch how bait and birds use the place through a tide and a temperature shift. Trout reward fishermen who think in depth, grass, bait, and temperature — not just "there's an open flat."
