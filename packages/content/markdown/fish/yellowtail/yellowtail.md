Yellowtail are current fish first and structure fish second. You’ll see them under birds, boiling on chum, or smashing surface iron, but what keeps them in a spot most of the time is bait being held by kelp, reef, bank, point, or a temperature edge. Watch the water movement — that’s usually the deciding factor.

## Current Makes The Spot

I’ll say it plainly: a high spot with no current is just bottom. Kelp without bait is just shade. Yellowtail live where the feed is being organized by the water.

What I look for and why it matters:
- Uphill current on a bank or reef — it forces bait to present and gives the school a marching lane.
- Bait pinned to kelp or contour — the fish don’t have to chase; they can work the feed efficiently.
- Birds working one edge repeatedly — they point to a concentrated lane, not a random scatter.
- Meter marks stacking above structure — those marks tell you the school is using the current rather than roaming.
- Fish moving up-current through chum — they’ll push into a feed lane if the current funnels prey.
- Bonito, barracuda, or bass in the same lane — predators usually chase what the yellowtail are keyed on.

The most consistent bites come where the bait has the fewest choices. If the current pins it to one contour, the fish can predict where the food will be and will commit.

## Surface Fish And Deep Fish

I plan for both. A school that’s boiling at dawn can be tight to structure by mid-morning. Light, current strength, pressure, and bait size decide whether they feed high or bury themselves.

When they’re high you’ll notice:
- Birds working closer to the water.
- Chum slicks getting followed.
- Boils that move into the current, not away from it.
- Fish bunched and competing near the boat.

When they’re deep:
- Echo marks hug the structure and don’t trail up.
- Yo-yo jigs or heavy jigs get bites below the surface layer.
- Fly-lined baits will be ignored while the surface layer is empty.
- Drift angle matters more than exact anchor position because you need gear passing down the fish lane.

Don’t assume the school left — assume it changed depth. Change presentation before you move on.

## Pressure

Yellowtail are pressure-sensitive. They’ll sink, slide off clean structure, or push up-current away from noise. I’ve watched perfectly good fish behave poorly simply because boats were crowded or the anchor was set in the wrong place.

Common mistakes that kill a bite:
- Anchoring below the bite lane so your boat is in their path.
- Running over birds before getting set up and scattering the feed.
- Fishing light gear when the fish are buried tight to structure.
- Leaving at the first lull in current instead of watching for the next push.

If you’re on a spot with variable current, give the school room and time. Back down and re-present rather than piling boats into one small lane.

## Regional Shape

Southern California and Baja fish the same patterns: kelp lines, islands, high spots, and coastal banks tied into current and bait. In Mexican waters you’ll see larger schools traveling with sardina, mackerel, or squid. Around islands, current direction often makes one side fish and the other look dead — it’s the same bottom, different water movement.

Treat the spot as current plus bait plus depth, not a waypoint on the chart. Boats that read the lane — where the bait is being fed and how the current funnels it — get the better shots.
