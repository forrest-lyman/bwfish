The weedline/kelp‑paddy conversion is a tool you pull out when bait and predators are being concentrated by floating structure and the fish are already keyed into a predictable lane. The idea is simple: present natural food where the school expects it to appear. The trick is reading which paddies are working, approaching so your offerings drift into the strike lane, and stepping through a tight sequence from test to chum to live conversion without destroying the window with poor boatcraft.

## Why yellowtail use paddies and weedlines

Floating kelp, marine debris and weedlines gather bait — sardina, small mackerel, squid and minnows — and currents and eddies hang those bait patches against the structure. Predators like yellowtail station on the choke points where bait must pass: the down‑current lee, the exit lane around a paddy, and the seams where two currents meet. When current is steady that same side of a paddy becomes a feeding lane you can predict and work.

## Reading the opportunity: when to commit and when to test

You want to be selective. Commit when the window is forming; change tactics when it’s closing.

## Signs the opportunity is building (commit to converting)
- Birds repeatedly returning to a single paddy or line and working tightly over it.
- Surface bait pinned up against the paddy, slicks trailing downcurrent, or visible bait slicks.
- Sonar showing bait clouds hugging the paddy and marks stacked on one edge instead of under the center.
- Tail slashes, boils, or dark shapes quartering the edge.
- Meter marks and fish arches concentrated on the down‑current side rather than scattered.

## Signs it’s fading (abandon or change)
- Current slacks and the paddy drifts with no consistent flow — the feeding lane collapses.
- The paddy breaks apart or drifts into heavy kelp or shoreline where hookups mean hangups.
- Fish marks drop off the edge and sink under structure — they’ve gone deep or moved.
- Long silence after heavy chumming — you’ve either saturated or spooked the school.

## Approach geometry and boat handling

Approach quietly and up‑current relative to the paddy’s feeding lane. Fish generally face into the current, so your offerings should come from the current side and drift naturally into the lane. Never run straight over a paddy; the propwash, noise and the visual of a boat riding the kelp kills the window.

Arc around the paddy at a distance that keeps prop and kelp apart but still projects your drift or chum line into the lane. Keep the engine ready to reposition, but neutral while you convert. The choice between drifting and anchoring depends on current and the paddy’s behavior.

## Drift vs. anchor
- Drift: preferred when current is steady and paddies are isolated. Drift gives the most natural presentation and lets you reposition quickly if fish shift. Keep the motor in neutral while converting and have someone at the engine to make short, controlled moves.
- Anchor: useful when the paddy is large, current light, or you need a steady chum slick. Anchor up‑current and present downcurrent into the lane, but watch anchor rode fouls — paddies and kelp will grab lines.

Safe conversions mean short, controlled casts or hand‑pitching in tight paddies, an engine in neutral during working, and one person attending the motor. Stay stern‑clear of kelp, keep lines tidy, and avoid sudden throttle bursts that send fish scattering.

## How the technique works

You’re exploiting predictable flow and limited escape routes. Bait trapped by a paddy must flow around it; predators station where that flow funnels prey. Chunking builds a scent and visual slick that mimics pieces of prey coming off the kelp; live conversions present a single, mobile target that behaves like a fleeing baitfish. In clear Baja water the visual component is strong — silhouette and movement matter. In stained conditions the scent trail from chunking often does the heavy lifting. Current strength controls how fast chunks and live baits pass the strike zone, so adjust sinker weight and placement to keep the offering in the lane.

## Spread and pitch sequence — test to convert to chunk/live

Sequence matters: find interest quickly, then escalate to hold and convert. Keep the chain tight — visible yellowtail windows often close in minutes.

1. Test pass — assess aggression quick
   Run one fast, visible offering (iron, surface plug, or fast‑retrieved lure) just outside the paddy lane. A surface chase or short strikes tells you they’re keyed to movement and will take an aggressive presentation. If fast offerings are ignored but you still see marks, fish may be wary or deep; don’t jump straight to live bait.

2. Chum to create and hold attention
   Use short‑burst chunking into the down‑current lane. Small, frequent pieces build a scent and visual slick without plastering the area. Early heavy chumming attracts unwanted competition and can dissipate the window. Watch the slick: if fish tighten and begin staking it, you’ve created a conversion lane.

3. Live conversion — make the single target obvious
   Pitch a live bait slightly ahead of the slick so it behaves like incoming prey. Give the live enough room to run; leader length and sinker weight must prevent it from getting pinned in the kelp. Time is critical — visible schools often take the first fresh live bait.

4. Hold and rotate
   Maintain a controlled chum trail and cycle live baits rather than burning up every live you have. Rotate sizes and presentations to keep fish hungry and targeted. If fish push through the paddy into open water, switch to faster offerings (surface iron or plugs) to follow the directional change.

## Tactical adjustments by behavior

Tailers and boilers on the edge: pitch live bait into the lane where tails break the surface. Use a short, slightly weighted presentation so the bait tracks naturally through the boil.

Slow, methodical feeding with no surface activity: build a slick with smaller chunk pieces and present live bait beneath the surface. Slow pieces and smaller chunks often trigger takes better than big slabs in this mode.

Aggressive deep charges under the skirt: fish hugging kelp need a heavier, slower drop for live baits or a weighted hooked chunk dropped into the strike rail. Be cautious of hangups; work the angles and be ready to cut a rig if the kelp grabs.

If dorado or barracuda show up and start ripping the slick, reduce chunk size and either increase live bait size or move off the paddy and troll targeted spreads. Those species change the game fast and usually shorten the yellowtail window.

## Common mistakes (operational)

- Running over the paddy to “get closer.” You usually lose the strike lane and spook the school.
- Chumming heavily too early. A sloppy slick draws birds, sharks and competition and masks the natural bait cues yellowtail use.
- Pitching live bait without establishing a lane. Without a scent trail or visible interest live baits get ignored as foreign.
- Not controlling engine noise and propwash. Sudden bursts or burying the boat in kelp pushes pressured fish off the paddy.
- Presentations mismatched to fish orientation. If fish face into current, throwing baits downstream so they come from behind cuts hookups.

## Associated species and how they affect the window

Bonito and small tunas often feed the same paddies and act as early indicators — they’ll respond to the same tests and can confirm presence. Dorado are attracted to larger chunks and commotion; their arrival usually compresses the window because they dominate the slick. Barracuda hang on debris and will cut leaders and steal baits, forcing changes in leader material or bait size. Nearshore paddies can also hold kelp bass and rockfish, which foul hooks and can mask yellowtail behavior.

## Regional notes — Baja / Bahía Tortugas

Baja paddies tend to be larger and more persistent than inshore weeds farther north, so windows can last longer — and attract more competition. Warmer months shift bait composition toward squid and small mackerel; live mackerel or larger sardina convert more reliably than tiny offerings. Expect tighter fleets in Baja: rather than fighting for the exact same spot, position up‑current of the common lanes and present into the flow the fleet is using.

## Tactical mindset — sequence your decisions

Think: Spot → Assess → Test → Convert → Hold. Each step answers a single question: Are fish there? Are they keyed? Will they chase? Are they committed? Time is the enemy — rehearsed chum and live sequences win short windows. Read flow and present where the fish expect prey to appear, not where the paddy looks prettiest.

## Observational realism

Perfect presentations get refused. Fish cycle through hunger, wariness and pressure sensitivity. Expect refusals, make small adjustments, and be prepared to move when depth or current changes. Watch birds and paddies constantly; a steady flow can flip to slack in moments.

## When to stop

Quit when current collapses, the paddy drifts into heavy kelp or shore, marks sink under structure, or the slick is being torn apart by other predators. Those are clear signs the lane is gone — look for the next seam, bank, or edge concentrating bait.

Final note

This is geometry and timing more than magic. Spot paddies that concentrate bait, approach so your offerings drift into the same lane the fish are using, and step through a controlled sequence from fast tests to measured chum to timely live conversions. Good boat handling and disciplined chum keep short windows open long enough to convert.
