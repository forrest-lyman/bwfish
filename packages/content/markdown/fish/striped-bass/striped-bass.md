# Striped Bass

Striped bass are tide fish first and foremost. You’ll find them in beaches, rips, bridges, boulder fields, inlets, channels, and open-water bait schools, but what matters is moving water delivering food into predictable lanes the fish can use. Watch the current and the bait — the fish are where those two meet.

## Why Tide Often Defines The Lane

Stripers don’t sit in fixed spots. They sit on moving water features: seams, eddies, rips, bars, bridge shadows, and points. As tide speed and direction change, the useful piece of structure moves too. The exact GPS mark is rarely the thing that keeps working; the stage of the tide is.

Observation: the same ledge or point will fish at different places on the slack, flood, and ebb. Explanation: the fish position themselves to intercept bait being pushed or held by the current. In short: find the seam, not the waypoint.

## Reading The Signs On Current Seams

When you’re looking for the lane, the visual cues are usually clearer than the depthfinder alone.

You’ll see bait flipping on the seam, birds working low and tight instead of scattered, and marks stacked at one depth as the tide runs. Often the bass are a step below the surface school — birds and small feeders call attention to the area, but the larger fish hang on the edge or beneath that activity.

Quick reference signals that a seam is worth fishing:
- bait flipping on the seam
- birds working low but not scattered
- marks stacked at one depth during tide
- bites on the down-current side of structure
- water pushing over a bar or boulder line

Those signs tell you where the lane is. How you present lure or bait to that seam is the decision after you’ve found it.

## How Bait Size Changes Where Fish Hold

Stripers follow local forage. In our waters that can mean bunker, herring, eels, sand eels, bay anchovies, squid, mullet — and in some places crabs. The size and type of bait change everything about how the fish feed.

When the school is on small bait, the surface can look busy and shallow, but the larger fish often become selective. They’ll refuse bigger offerings and sit under the working birds. In those cases, birds are the best visual clue and downsized presentations win.

When larger bait is present — bunker or big herring — the bigger bass will tend to sit deeper and use the edges of the school. The bite window often tightens to low-light or transition periods, and you can pull fish off the edge with slow, weighted presentations or large plugs.

Don’t forget: big bait can pull fish away from obvious surface feeds. If you see gorged bunker or larger herring, try deeper profiles and edge presentations rather than chasing the surface frenzy.

## How Pressure and Fleet Behavior Move Fish

Stripers are tolerant when food is abundant, but clear water and shallow structure make them sensitive to pressure. Repeated drifts through the same seam, noisy approaches, or a fleet chasing the birds will move fish or push them deeper.

The pattern I see: the first boats find the birds, the fish slide under them. After a few passes, the birds stay but the bass relocate to cleaner, less-pressured seams or go deeper along the same structure. If you’re arriving on a hot mark, slow down your approach, change your angle, or fish the adjacent seam rather than repeating the fleet’s path.

## Regional Shape: Same Rules, Different Details

From bays to surf, rocks to offshore bunker schools, the same basic idea holds — read current, bait, and structure — but the details vary by region. Chesapeake fish respond to tidal creeks and main-channel eddies; Cape Cod and Montauk fish around rips and ledges; New Jersey and the Hudson can fish more on beach and inlet seams; Maine and offshore spots depend heavily on bunker schools and ledge edges.

Keep your focus on the moving parts: tide stage, where the bait is being pushed, and where fish can ambush without being blown off the seam. That’s where you’ll put the boat and the bait at the right time.

## Practical Notes From The Boat

- Don’t worship the waypoint. Watch tide stage and relocate along structure as the current changes.
- Use birds and bait activity to find likely lanes, then look for marks stacked by depth to confirm where the bigger fish are holding.
- Match bait size. Small feed — small presentations below the surface activity. Big bait — deeper edges and slower profiles.
- If the fleet has fished a spot hard, try a different angle or adjacent seam before repeating the same drift.

Striped bass reward reading moving targets more than memorizing marks. Watch current, watch bait, and set up on the seam the fish are using that tide.
