# Jack Crevalle

Jack crevalle are pressure and bait made visible. They push hard, travel fast, and turn calm water into moving water when they find food. The obvious violence can hide the useful details: school direction, bait type, current, and whether the fish are feeding or only traveling.

They are not subtle, but they are still condition-driven.

## Moving Schools

Jacks often use beaches, inlets, passes, seawalls, bridges, tide lines, and bait schools as lanes. They may pin mullet in the surf, herd glass minnows against a wall, or push menhaden through open water.

Good signs:

- bait showering ahead of a moving school
- birds running low over one line
- mud or surface chop from fish pushing shallow
- schools moving with tide rather than randomly
- repeated feeds along the same bar or inlet edge

The school's direction matters more than where it was seen.

## Feeding Or Traveling

A feeding school has shape. It turns on bait, tightens, and rebuilds after a push. A traveling school may show backs and wakes but ignore a good presentation.

Feeding signs:

- bait spraying repeatedly
- fish circling back into the same pocket
- birds staying low
- strikes coming from multiple fish

Traveling signs:

- fish moving straight without turning
- no visible bait
- birds following but not diving
- refusals despite clean casts

The difference saves time.

## Pressure

Jacks tolerate noise better than many fish, but bad angles still matter. Running through the school scatters bait and turns catchable fish into wakes. A cast ahead of the movement usually works better than chasing the boil.

In clear shallow water, they can become surprisingly selective after repeated casts.

## Regional Shape

Gulf and Atlantic jacks use beaches, passes, bays, bridges, and bait migrations. In tropical water they may mix with other jacks, roosterfish, tarpon, sharks, and snapper around reefs and shorelines.

Jack crevalle reward speed, but only after the water has been read.
