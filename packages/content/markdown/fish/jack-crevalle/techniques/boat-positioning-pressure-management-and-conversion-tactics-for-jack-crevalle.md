This isn’t about a single plug or rig — it’s about how the boat moves through a feed, where you place casts, and how you escalate or back off offerings so a chaotic, fast-moving predator becomes catchable. Jacks are obvious fish, but that obviousness hides a tight window. Run through them or present from the wrong angle and you’ll scatter the feed. Do these things instead and a push will often convert into hooks.

## Where The Push Shows Up

Recognize the physical geometry before you start casting.

- Situation: visible push — bait spraying, birds low and working a line, repeated boils along a bar, inlet throat, or channel edge. Often in narrow places where current funnels bait.
- Why this method: jacks herd bait down lanes. The boat and anglers need to become part of that lane’s choreography — get ahead of the school, space casts so fish meet natural prey, and manage pressure so the group reforms instead of dispersing.
- Typical spots: channel throats (Magdalena central channel), lee pockets and harbor tongues (Loreto harbor), inlet edges and beaches where tide‑driven flows compress bait.

## Why This Works

Simple patterns — direction, compression, timing.

- Jacks feed by forcing bait into confined geometry: edge, throat, pocket. Their attacks are directional; they hit prey ahead of their motion.
- Surface offerings look natural when fish are shallow and aggressive. Metals cover depth when fish are chasing subsurface. Livebait wins when fish tighten and get picky.
- The presentation must match the vertical and horizontal relationship: meet the school ahead and slightly inside the push.

## Boat Positioning & Approach Angles

Position matters more than flash.

- Don’t run through the school. Coming from behind or straight over them breaks bait and creates chaos.
- Intercept angle wins. Get ahead of the school’s projected path and set a stationary or slow‑drift presentation they run into.
- Trim boat speed to the lane. If current is moving the school fast, slow the boat so casts land at natural chase speed.
- Use wind/current to mask your silhouette — approach from leeward or upstream so wake and engine noise don’t blow through the feeding lane.
- In tight pockets: hold off the mouth and fish into it rather than driving inside. That encourages fish to turn into the pocket and refeed.

## Staging Ahead & Spacing Casts

Put the cast where the school will meet it, not where it already broke.

- Staging: cast ahead of the school’s path. A cast that meets the push hooks the initial responders; chasing boils loses the bite.
- Spacing: keep anglers spread laterally — several rod lengths so hookups or misses don’t collapse the feed. If everyone clusters, rotate who fishes the intercept.
- Cast placement priority:
  - First: intercept point.
  - Second: edge of the push where fish break and re-enter bait.
  - Third: flanks when the school is wide and loose.
- Rotate anglers through the intercept and edge positions to avoid burning the same angle.

## Managing Pressure & Resetting Pressured Groups

Jacks will take noise, but they get tired of it.

- Pressure tolerance: repeated aggressive casts in a small pocket make fish selective or push them through. Signs: tightening, refusing topwater, moving offshore.
- Immediate reset: if a pocket tightens or scatters, back off 100–300 yards downwind/downcurrent and let the pocket breathe. Typical wait: 5–15 minutes; longer in clear water.
- Active reset: while away, work a low‑visibility presentation (slow metal cast away or a livebait drift) to keep fish in the area without stressing the core group.
- Quiet repositioning: return from a different angle (opposite side of tide or wind) so you don’t repeat the disturbance that spooked them.
- Anchor vs drift: in strong throats a controlled drift or moderated trolling angle beats anchoring. In shallow lee pockets with little current, drift into the mouth and present ahead of the anticipated push.

## Conversion Sequence — Topwater → Metal → Livebait

This is operational logic, not dogma. Let fish feedback decide the move.

- Start topwater when:
  - Fish are visibly breaking the surface and bait is at or near the surface.
  - Boils are aggressive and repeated. Cast ahead, let the plug pop/walk, and expect strikes on the pause.
- Switch to metal when:
  - Topwater is ignored but fish are charging without finishing on the surface.
  - The school shows depth separation; metals imitate fleeing bait and reach that strike zone.
  - Metals cover water quickly on moving schools that won’t commit to surface prey.
- Move to livebait when:
  - Repeated refusals to artificials, especially in clear, shallow water where fish go selective.
  - The school tightens and becomes wary; a livebait presented ahead often gets a single bold fish to commit and trigger a chain reaction.
  - Livebait is the finesse tool after higher‑energy offerings fail.
- Tactical escalation: test with high energy (topwater), step down to metals if surface fails, then present livebait to seduce selective fish. Introducing livebait early can teach fish to lock onto quiet offerings only — work the sequence.

## Reading The Window

Watch the bait and the birds; they tell the story.

Signs it’s building:
- Repeated bait showers in the same pocket.
- Birds running low and diving consistently along a line.
- Fish turning back into the same area after a push (they rebuild).
- Sonar showing tightly compressed marks ahead of a current lane.

Signs it’s fading:
- School runs straight with no turning or bait present (travelling).
- Refusals across all presentations from multiple anglers.
- Fish moving offshore or elongating on sonar (spreading out).
- Surface churn without consistent boils — often wakes, not feeding.

## Common Operational Mistakes

Stuff I still watch experienced anglers do.

- Running through the school to “get ahead” — breaks bait and scatters fish.
- Staying on the same angle after repeated refusals — you’ve taught the fish.
- Overfishing the pocket with everyone in the intercept zone — fish tighten and go selective.
- Switching presentations too quickly without reading response — e.g., dropping to livebait before giving metals a proper run.
- Anchoring in the wrong place — inside a throat or across the path traps fish and kills the bite.

## Associated Species

Other predators follow the same lanes and change the dynamic.

- Roosterfish and other jacks: share lanes and bait dynamics in Baja.
- Yellowtail: can show in open pushes when bait runs deeper and current’s strong.
- Sharks and large predators: concentrated bait that attracts jacks often attracts sharks — they shorten windows and alter jack behavior.

These species use the same compressed bait and current features as jacks. Their presence can concentrate the feed or spoil it.

## Regional Notes — Magdalena Channel vs Loreto Harbor

Same pattern, different tempo.

- Magdalena central channel throat:
  - Strong funneling currents concentrate bait into predictable lanes. Position upstream of the throat and stage just outside the main flow for the best intercepts.
  - Resets are faster; fish push through quickly so timing is tight.
- Loreto harbor tongue / lee pocket:
  - Slower water, smaller pockets, stronger lee effects. Fish reform tighter and become selective sooner.
  - Quiet, subtle presentations (livebait or small metals) and careful angle changes matter more here.

## What Success Looks Like

A clear sequence, executed with patience.

- Observe tide, bait, birds, sonar — predict the path.
- Position to intercept rather than chase.
- Control who fishes the intercept and rotate to avoid burning the pocket.
- Convert based on feedback: surface aggression → topwater, subsurface chase → metals, selectivity → livebait.
- If the school shifts, move the boat before dramatically changing lures.

## Reality Check — Windows, Fleets, When To Go

Expect short windows and imperfect conditions.

- Refusals are normal; a single good hookup often needs the right angle and timing more than persistence.
- Windows: a pocket can be hot 10–30 minutes, then cool or scatter.
- Clear shallow water raises selectivity; murkier water relaxes it, but angles still matter.
- Other boats ruin pockets quickly; manage pressure by changing angles or stepping off and resetting.
- Abandon the feed when:
  - The school is clearly travelling: no bait, no turning, poor sonar compression.
  - Consistent refusals across angles and presentations.
  - Predators (large sharks) intercept every hookup and scatter bait.
  - You’ve tried a calm reset and fish still refuse — usually faster to find the next pocket.

If you keep the sequence simple — identify the lane, get ahead of fish, space and rotate anglers, step off and return from new angles, and convert topwater → metal → livebait based on response — one well‑placed cast will often trigger the whole school. That’s the practical endgame: observation first, position second, and let the fish tell you when to escalate.
