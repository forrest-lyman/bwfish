Swordfish are read vertically. The surface will often tell you very little — the work you need to know about is down a long piece of water. What matters is where the bait travels, how the drift sits against contour, which bait layers are stacked, and how current and light move those layers. They aren’t structure-bound the way snapper are, but structure organizes prey and that, in turn, organizes swordfish behavior.

Think of a drift as a long cross-section through the water column. The number on the screen is part of the map; the presentation is the rest.

## Depth Is The Map

During daylight the fish live in layers. Your job is putting a bait through the correct band and keeping it there long enough for a sword to find it. Often there’s no clear mark on the sounder — the difference between a blank screen and a bite is how the bait intersects a layer.

Useful signals that the layer you want is present:
- bait marking near bottom or working along a slope
- current that moves across contour and sets up holdings
- repeat bites on the same depth band
- squid or hake in the zone
- deep scattering layers showing consistency on successive passes
- drift lines that cross productive bottom features

The number on the screen doesn’t fish. Watch how the bait moves through the band: too fast, too high, scoping out on the slack — and you’ll run the same piece of water without ever putting the offering where it needs to be.

## Drift Control

Swordfishing punishes sloppy drift. A bait that’s riding too high, too low, dragging, or scoping out will slide through good water without getting tested. The difference between a working drift and a dead one is small and often fixable if you read the indicators.

Watch these live on every pass:
- line angle — tells you where the bait actually is versus where you think it is
- bottom changes during the drift — slope and breaks change how the bait rides
- current shear between surface and depth — surface indicators can lie about what’s happening where the bait is
- bait position relative to contour — up-slope, down-slope or over a ledge matters
- whether bites come on the rise, fall, or steady portion of the drift — that tells you whether to hold depth or change it

If a drift isn’t producing, don’t assume the fish moved; assume the presentation did. Adjust depth, change the scope, slide the spread, or pick a different angle until the bait fishes the layer the same way each pass.

## Night Versus Day: Two Fisheries

The same fish will behave like two different fisheries depending on light. At night they tend to spread up with squid and scattering layers. That makes the bite broader and more visual — lights, surface bait, slicks, and dimpling are useful cues. Daytime bites narrow and drop; you rely on contour, depth control, and subtle movement through layers.

Night indicators:
- squid showing around lights
- surface bait dimpling in current
- swordfish finning or surfacing on calm nights
- bites related to moon-driven or temperature-driven movement

Day indicators:
- deep bait bands well below the surface
- bites tied to contour intersections and bottom features
- current patterns that allow the bait to hold a depth band
- subtle, low-energy attacks that often don’t announce themselves

Expect different tools and patience at each time. Night lets you work wider; day forces precision.

## Pressure And Patience

Pressure is often self-inflicted. The fleet rarely spoils a drift faster than a crew that leaves a good line too soon, fishes the wrong angle, or repeats a set after the current has flipped. A productive depth band will go quiet if you stop testing it correctly; conversely, sticking with a poor presentation wastes good water.

Good swordfishing is patient and active: you hold a drift long enough to read the behavior, then make incremental changes — depth, bait, angle — until the response changes. When nothing’s happening, change something measurable and observe the result rather than adding noise.

## Structure Still Matters

Swordfish aren’t glued to structure, but structure organizes prey and creates the holding patterns swordfish use. Canyons, ledges, humps, seamounts, deep slopes, and current breaks concentrate squid, hake, and mackerel and set up scattering layers. Fish use those features on schedules that don’t always follow the surface.

Regional tendencies:
- Florida and Gulf: fish commonly relate to deep slopes, canyons, humps and current seams
- Northeast canyons: combine night-layer behavior with daytime deep drops
- Pacific and island fisheries: seamounts, banks and steep island edges dominate the picture

Remember: the surface can look dead all day while the work is happening below. Read the contour, watch how the bait moves through the column, and let that vertical picture guide the decisions you make on drift, bait, and angle.
