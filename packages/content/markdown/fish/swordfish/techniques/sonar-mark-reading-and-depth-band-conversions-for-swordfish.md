This isn’t a lesson in "drop heavier and hope." It’s about reading the column and putting your bait into the same vertical slice the swordfish are using. Good sonar work turns a vague blob into a repeatable depth window, then moves your presentation through the bands in controlled steps so the bait actually fishes the prey parcel — not through it.

## Why the mark matters more than the number on the depth finder

On the water you’ll see three useful behaviors that create the opportunity: a persistent scattering layer that holds as a band, compact squid or hake patches that show up as tight blobs, or transient bait schools that float with the surface current. Swordfish don’t always come up from nowhere to the surface; they often work those organized layers. When a mark holds in the same depth band across pings or multiple drifts, that’s your window. You want the bait to live in that window, not below or above it.

Experienced fishermen pick this method because a single drop-to-bottom or one-depth-fits-all approach misses layered behavior. The aim is to have a natural presentation that travels with the prey parcel — matching vertical motion, soak time, and the lateral path the layer follows.

Typical, real-world cases you’ll see: a broad DSL holding off a slope during the day; night layers compressing under lights but at different depths; a dense squiddy ball that appears intermittently; or repeated marks aligned with a current seam crossing your drift.

## Reading the mark: DSL, squid, or transient bait

Start with observation. What the sonar shows tells you what your conversion should look like.

- A broad, diffuse band that persists and migrates slowly is usually a DSL — mixed micronekton like myctophids and lanternfish. It’s elongated on returns and shifts with the day/night migration.
- Compact, loud, high‑density blobs with sharp edges that break apart when pressured point to squid congregations.
- Fleeting, sparse blips that scatter with surface current are transient bait or small pelagics; they don’t hold depth.

Why it matters: swordfish tend to patrol edges or feed inside these organized layers. If your bait moves unnaturally through a layer — too fast, too high above it, or plummeting below — fish will often ignore it. Match the layer’s vertical motion and give the presentation time inside the feeding micro‑zone.

## Pick a target window, not just a number

When you lock onto a repeatable mark, don’t think "30 feet" or "150 meters." Think top edge, middle, and lower edge of a band — a volume of water. Start at the top edge so the bait intercepts fish rising into the layer. Present through the band; don’t try to wrestle the bait below it.

Confirm your choice visually on sonar: look for your bait return if you have down/up imaging, or for fish marks clustering around the bait depth on subsequent pings. If you see interest, hold for several passes. If nothing happens, only then make a measured change.

## Stepwise depth conversions — move in bands, not jumps

Conversions are tactical and progressive. Large jumps overshoot the active micro‑zone; small, timed steps work.

Make changes in increments sized to the band thickness. Thin bands demand small moves — a fraction of the band matters. Broad DSLs tolerate larger steps. After each adjustment give the bait time; deeper, diffuse marks often require longer soak.

When you move, do it deliberately: top to mid‑band, wait a drift or two, then lower to the edge if needed. If the mark drifts laterally with the current, re‑center the bait into the path of the mark instead of just dropping deeper. Your job is to keep the bait inside the same parcel of water the prey occupies.

If marks are intermittent, use short probes into neighboring bands rather than committing to a full conversion. Probes are less disruptive and give you a quick read on activity.

## Timing conversions to drift and current shear

Timing is as important as depth.

Make conversions when the drift angle is stable. Chaotic boat motion — yawing, sudden speed changes, gust‑driven eddies — will scape or pull the bait out of the band during the change. Use a slack moment in the drift to reposition cleanly, then reestablish scope.

Use shear to your advantage. Shear layers often concentrate prey; if sonar shows the mark sitting on one side of a shear, place the bait in that interface. Time the bait’s arrival so it intersects the seam as you cross it — that’s when encounters spike.

If the band is shifting with tide or wind, shift with it in steps. An incoming tide that shoals the band calls for upward conversions; a sinking band requires you to step down, not to leap to bottom. Keep checking movement between pings and adjust the cadence of your steps to the speed of the band.

## Practical sonar cues that dictate action

Watch for repeatability. Marks that return at the same depth across at least two drifts are actionable. After a move, if echoes cluster around your bait depth or you see stronger returns near the bait, hold it. If the marks split and peel away, the bait isn’t in the micro‑zone — try the next step.

Vertical motion tells you what you’re looking at: slow, sinusoidal movement suggests a DSL; rapid spikes are likely squid or active predators. Target strength and edge definition also matter: tight, strong echoes usually mean concentrated prey (squid or hake), which tends to trigger sharper swordfish responses. Diffuse bands mean mixed micronekton and typically need different bait behavior and more time.

If your unit has split‑beam or down imaging, use it — you’ll get clearer target size and track. Single‑beam works too, but you’ll need to lean heavier on repeatability and band behavior.

## Common mistakes I still see

- Skipping intermediate bands and jumping straight deep. You often miss the active layer.
- Chasing every blip that shows for a single ping. Momentary returns waste time and upset drift control.
- Making conversions during chaotic drift. Changing depths while the boat yaws or speed shifts pulls the bait out of the band.
- Over‑weighting conversions to get there fast. Too much weight increases sink rate and noise; the bait will plow through the layer instead of fishing in it.
- Treating depth only and ignoring lateral re‑centering. If the bait isn’t crossing the mark horizontally, depth changes alone won’t help.
- Misidentifying DSL for squid or the reverse and applying the wrong conversion sizes and soak times.

Read those mistakes in the mark and correct them before you change gear.

## Associated species — what they tell you about behavior

Jumbo squid: tight, dense marks. Their presence often attracts swordfish, especially at night near lights.

Hake and midwater predators: show up as tighter targets near slopes and can act like squid as an attractor.

Myctophids and lanternfish: make up DSLs. Their broad layers require patience and a different presentation cadence.

Seeing any of them narrows the likely bands swordfish will patrol. Use species clues from your sonar return to shape step size and soak time.

## Regional and time‑of‑day patterns that affect conversions

Atlantic canyon/coast: DSLs can tie strongly to canyon walls and the Gulf Stream; marks may sit deeper and current is usually stronger. You’ll spend more effort re‑centering laterally and watching shear bands near edges.

Pacific seamounts and island slopes: marks may be shallower and squat tighter against contours; squid patches can be dense and localized. Vertical ranges are often shorter but demand precise positioning relative to the contour.

Night vs day: nights usually compress layers shallower — conversions are quicker, windows broader. Daytime DSLs sit deeper and require finer depth control and patience.

Equipment matters: down/up imaging and split‑beam make identification and bait tracking easier. If you’re on single‑beam, increase your observation time and focus on repeatability across pings.

## Turning observation into action

First, observe. Spend a drift confirming mark persistence and depth before you commit to a conversion. Think in bands, not in single numbers. Control the drift; anything that changes line angle — engine trimming, anchor drag, wind gust — changes whether your bait intersects the band.

Use short probes to test adjacent bands when marks are intermittent. Probes are less disruptive than full conversions. Be patient but decisive: when a conversion shows increased marking or interaction, hold it and give the fish time. If reasonable probes fail, shift laterally or try the next band.

Remember: marks open and close. Expect the window to appear and disappear quickly. A sequence of good drifts may be followed by silence; quick adaptation and repeated sonar checks separate effective boats from the rest.

## When to abandon the conversion and reposition

Drop the plan when marks stop being repeatable across pings or drifts, when current becomes too variable to hold the bait in the band, when multiple probes fail to produce interest, or when the window moves laterally off your drift path and you can’t re‑center without repositioning the boat. Don’t keep hammering the same depth band after those signs — reposition, re‑assess, and try a new parcel of water.

This method is about reading the column and making measured vertical moves in time with lateral drift. Interpret the mark (DSL, squid, transient bait), pick the right window inside it, convert in steps, and time those conversions to the drift and shear. Do that and your bait will spend the time where swordfish are actually feeding.
