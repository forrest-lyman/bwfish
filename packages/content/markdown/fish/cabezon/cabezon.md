# Cabezon

Cabezon are bottom holders. They sit in broken rock, kelp pockets, surge-washed reef, boulder fields, and crabby bottom where food moves close enough to take without much travel.

They do not need dramatic structure. They need cover, food, and enough water movement to deliver it.

## Shallow Broken Ground

Cabezon often use shallow reef differently than lingcod or rockfish. They can sit tight in small pockets, depressions, and edges that do not look large on a chart.

Useful signs:

- mixed rock and sand
- kelp edges with surge
- crab, shrimp, or small bait life
- bites close to bottom
- fish holding in small pockets between snags
- better action on the first clean pass

The best piece may be too small to mark clearly.

## Surge And Tide

Surge helps move food but can also make presentation difficult. A bait that tumbles naturally through broken bottom may get eaten. A bait dragged too fast becomes gear loss.

What matters:

- staying near bottom without plowing
- fishing pockets, not only high rock
- watching tide height over shallow reef
- letting the bait settle between lifts
- moving before the drift becomes too rough

## Pressure

Cabezon spots are often small. Pressure can remove fish or make the remaining ones hold deeper in cover. The first drift across a pocket may be the cleanest.

The fish did not chase. It waited where the food had to pass.

## Regional Shape

Pacific Coast cabezon fishing follows rocky habitat, kelp, tide, swell, and access. Jetties, shallow reefs, and nearshore rock can all hold fish when food is present and the water is workable.

Cabezon fishing rewards careful bottom contact and attention to small structure.
