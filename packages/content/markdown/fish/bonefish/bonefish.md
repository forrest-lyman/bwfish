# Bonefish

Bonefish are flats fish built around tide and bottom. They move over sand, marl, turtle grass, mangrove edges, creek mouths, and shallow banks where small crabs, shrimp, worms, and bait are exposed.

They are easy to spook because the water that makes them visible also makes the boat visible.

## Water Level And Bottom

Bonefish use tide to reach food. Too little water keeps them off the flat. Too much water spreads them into places that are harder to read. The best window often comes when fish have just enough depth to move and feed with purpose.

Useful signs:

- nervous water moving across a flat
- tails on soft bottom or grass edges
- muds forming and drifting with tide
- fish entering through a creek or channel
- rays or small sharks moving over the same feeding bottom
- singles or small groups holding into wind and tide

The mud shows activity. The fish may already be ahead of it.

## Wind, Sun, And Angle

Bonefish fishing is presentation geometry. Wind, sun, tide, and fish direction decide whether a shot is useful.

What matters:

- leading the fish without lining it
- reading speed before casting
- keeping the boat outside the lane
- choosing bottom that lets the fly or bait show naturally
- watching body language after the presentation lands

The fish that tips down is different from the fish that only passes over.

## Pressure

Bonefish flats show pressure quickly. Fish may travel faster, stop tailing, refuse heavier presentations, or avoid common skiff lanes. Quiet water does not mean empty water.

The flat had fish. They were moving like fish that had already been shown boats.

## Regional Shape

Bahamas bonefish often use broad flats, mangroves, creek systems, and marl. Florida Keys fish can be larger and more pressured, with turtle grass, oceanside flats, and clear water changing the work. Belize and Caribbean flats add local tides, wind, and mixed-species pressure.

Bonefish reward quiet reading before the cast.
