# Cobia

Cobia are travelers that become structure fish when the right object appears. Rays, turtles, buoys, wrecks, channel markers, bait balls, weed, and even large sharks can give them a reason to hold. They move with temperature and season, but they feed through opportunity.

They can look easy when they appear beside the boat. They are not always easy to make eat.

## Moving Structure

Cobia use both fixed and living structure. A ray crossing a flat, a buoy in current, a wreck holding bait, or a turtle drifting through clean water can all gather fish.

Useful signs:

- rays moving with tide
- bait stacked on buoys or markers
- fish shadowing sharks or turtles
- current pushing past a wreck or tower
- birds working over rays or bait
- cobia cruising high but turning toward food

The object matters because it creates a station in otherwise open water.

## Mood At The Boat

Cobia often show themselves before deciding. A fish may follow, circle, sink, come back, and refuse three good baits before eating one that moves correctly.

Aggressive fish:

- turn quickly on a bait
- stay high and lit
- compete with another cobia
- follow hooked fish to the boat

Reluctant fish:

- circle below the presentation
- nose the bait without opening
- sink after seeing the boat
- follow too long and lose interest

Do not confuse visibility with commitment.

## Migration And Temperature

Cobia movement follows warming water, bait, and seasonal routes. In spring and early summer, sight-fishing can depend on sun angle, water clarity, and fish moving along beaches, shoals, and channels. Around structure, the bite can last longer if bait and current stay right.

## Regional Shape

Gulf and Atlantic cobia fisheries differ by migration timing, water clarity, tower structure, rays, buoys, and nearshore bait. Some days are hunting days. Others are structure days.

Cobia reward boats that keep looking around after the obvious target passes.
