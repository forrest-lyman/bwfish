The kit of skirted plugs, spoons and spreader bars exists because yellowfin often chase fast, flashy targets instead of inspecting slow presentations. We use speed to turn a school’s competitive mood into a split‑second decision: strike or miss. Below I’ll lay out when that window opens, how the presentation reads to tuna, how to run the spread, what to watch for, and the immediate drills that turn a violent strike into a hooked fish and another pass.

## When the reaction window opens

What I watch for first is behavior — birds, bait and surface violence. That’s the signal a school is in "chase" mode.

## Observations that mean go fast
- Birds locked on a tight patch and repeatedly crashing the same water.
- Bait visible at the surface, breaking up and then reforming after a pass.
- Fast, shallow sonar marks moving across the column or climbing quickly.
- Dolphins or porpoise repeatedly corralling bait in one direction.
- Hooked fish bringing others up instantly and triggering follow‑ons.

Why those signs matter
When bait is dense and being organized by current or predators, tuna are already in a competitive, visually driven state. A quick, noisy lure reads like fleeing bait and becomes an obvious target. The opportunity is short — you can force a reaction rather than wait for an inspection bite.

## Signals the window is closing
- Birds peel off and don’t come back.
- Bait thins out or drifts apart.
- Marks on the screen flatten, drop deeper, or vanish.
- Multiple passes produce slashes but no committed hits (pressure or wary fish).
- Fish start moving away rather than holding or circling.

Fleet behavior matters too. A single boat making clean, timely passes will keep a patch reactive longer than five boats trampling the same water.

## How the presentation triggers chases

Start with the observation, then the reason.

What the lures imitate
Fast skirted plugs and spoons mimic fleeing bait: flash, profile and a noisy wake. Spreader bars add the illusion of a small school — multiple targets, exaggerated commotion. That competition is the baitfish cue tuna exploit.

How tuna encounter the spread
A staggered, high‑speed spread cuts across the school’s path. Tuna make quick decisions: the most obvious, high‑contrast, fast‑moving item wins. If your presentation reads as natural fleeing fish at range — correct silhouette, flash and cadence — you get violent reaction strikes.

Why it fails
If bait is thin, fish are deep, or the school is in a tentative inspection mood, the fast presentations look unrealistic or threatening and get ignored. Wrong speed, wrong color for the water, or a cramped spread that doesn’t present a natural flight path also kills it.

## Speed ranges and when to push or back off

Start with the feel, then pick a number.

## Operational speed ranges (not absolute)
- Skirted plugs and spreader bars: 9–14 knots. Skirts need to work without spinning out.
- Metal spoons and thin flashers: 12–18 knots. Spoons plane and flash at higher speed.
- Aggressive setups (big yellowfin, warm open water): 13–16+ knots can pay when fish are full‑on.

How I pick a speed
Start at the low end for a fresh stop. If you get follows but no hookups, ramp speed up; tuna will chase harder at higher speeds. If strikes are violent but the hookups pull off or hooks shred, back the speed a touch and tighten hookup technique.

## Match speed to mood
- Tentative but willing: stay lower in the range.
- High, fast marks and birds actively corralling: push higher.

## Spread geometry — present targets across the fish’s path

The goal is lanes that cross the natural approach of the school, not a clump of identical plugs.

## Principles
- Create crossing lanes at different distances so fish see a range of targets as they make a quick choice.
- Stagger sizes and silhouettes: teasers/short plugs, mid‑range skirts, spreader bars, long spoons.
- Avoid identical lures clustered tightly — fish inspect that and move on.

## Practical geometry to start with
- Short teaser/short plugs: 10–30 ft — immediate commotion near the transom.
- Close lures (short plugs/short spoons): 30–80 ft — grab the really aggressive chasers.
- Mid baits (skirted plugs, small bars): 80–140 ft — primary strike zone for many chasers.
- Outrigger baits (larger skirts, topwater plugs): 140–220 ft — catch passing fish or give distance to follow.
- Long leads/spreader bars/long spoons: 200–350+ ft — first‑wave triggers and long‑range flash.
Lateral spread: run lures near centerline and wide off the beam — 45° to 90° off the heading is common, adjusted to the fish’s travel direction.

Why these ranges work
You want short, table‑charge targets for close chasers and long, flashing triggers for fish that come through on a first pass. Staggering forces a split‑second decision rather than a slow inspection.

## Lure types and color choices by water

Principle first: silhouette and contrast at distance beat subtle detail. Match the major bait and visibility, then mix flash and profile.

## Clear blue water
- Use chrome/blue‑back spoons, blue/pearl skirts, silver flash bars.
- Good combos: blue/white, purple/white, chrome.
- Rationale: clear water favors bright flash and natural blue/white profiles.

## Stained/green water
- Use chartreuse/green skirts, copper/gold spoons with darker backs, green‑accent bars.
- Colors: chartreuse, green/black, gold/copper, purple/black.
- Rationale: contrast and warmer metal tones cut through reduced visibility.

## Low light, overcast, or rough seas
- High‑contrast skirts and dark backs; black/blue or black/purple patterns and heavier spoons.
- Colors: black/white, black/green, purple/black, copper.
- Rationale: silhouette against a gray sky is easier to pick up.

## Warm tropical blue (Baja)
- Toss a few bright pink/purple or hot green offerings; in very clear, warm water odd contrasts can trigger strikes.

## Spoons and flashers
- Thin, fast‑running spoons for higher speeds; copper/gold for stained water, chrome/nickel for clear. Use larger spoons at lower speeds to keep wobble; smaller thin spoons at high speed for raw flash.

Use a mix: bars and spoons for raw flash, skirts for profile and motion.

## Reading reaction vs inspection and what to do

Reaction bites — what they look like
- Fast chases, violent flashes, birds hammering and staying locked.
- Tuna hit hard at trailing edges of plugs or bars; spoon strikes are immediate and violent.
- Sonar shows marks surging up and across the column.

## How to convert a reaction strike
- Expect violent hook engagement. Don’t panic stop the boat and yank gear out.
- Maintain controlled movement — a small throttle back, not a full stop — to keep the lure working.
- Have the leader/clip man ready to clear slack, hand a short strong leader, and secure the hooked fish fast so you can pump and pull and keep other fish in the zone.
- Quick, decisive hooksets and immediate presentation of another bait in the closest lane is critical.

## Inspection bites — what they look like
- Slow follows, shadowing without attacking, tentative pecks, birds loose or sitting on water.
- Sonar shows low, tight marks staying deep or slowly rising.
- Tuna might nip skirts or pluck at strands rather than commit.

## How to convert inspection behavior
- Slow the presentation dramatically: pull back to slow trolling or drift and introduce a natural presentation — livebait, smaller slow skirts, or a bait on a short flatline behind a bar.
- Change one item in the spread: replace a mid‑spread fast bait with a livebait/slow plug on the side where fish are showing.
- Act quickly — inspection windows often last only minutes before fish sink or move.

## Immediate follow‑up (first 20–60 seconds)
- Hooked fish with others active: keep the spread stable, clear the hit rod, and immediately put a fresh bait into the closest lane. Hooked fish generate urgency — capitalize fast.
- Follows without hits: change one variable — speed, color or distance of a single bait. A common move is to pull a skirted plug inboard 20–40 ft or drop a spreader bar to create more commotion.
- Crew roles matter: have a dedicated leader/clip person and a bait switcher who can move a fresh bait into the wake the instant a strike happens.

## Common mistakes and boatcraft

Most failures are avoidable if you manage the spread and the boat.

## Operational errors I see
- Running the same pass repeatedly without varying speed, color or distance.
- Overcrowding the spread with identical baits — fish see a fake clump and ignore it.
- Using the wrong speed for the lure: spoons need speed to flash; skirts spool out if too fast.
- Misreading inspection as reaction and pushing speed, which scatters the school.
- Not having conversion gear ready — livebaits or spare skirts must be immediate.
- Poor boat handling: charging the stop, erratic turns, or stopping in the wrong spot blows the window.

## Boat positioning and timing
- Position for the fish’s path, not the floater. Run lines so the spread crosses the approach lane the fish use.
- Time passes to the school’s rhythm: one or two hard, high‑speed passes, then back off and let the patch rebuild. Repeated aggressive passes without bait rebuilding push fish off.
- Small, deliberate adjustments beat brute force. The best boats unlock a pass with one change.

## Associated species — how they affect the pass

These species show up and change how you fish.
- Dorado (mahi): respond to flashy moving targets and overlap with yellowfin around debris and weedlines.
- Wahoo: attracted to high speeds and metal spoons; they strike by speed reaction.
- Skipjack and bonito: often the first to show and push bait — their activity can cue yellowfin arrival and keep a reactive mood.
Operationally, they change school mood and can steal strikes; that may mean changing lure size or using wire on certain baits.

## Regional notes: Baja vs Southern California

Baja
- Warmer, clearer, more surface‑oriented; fish will chase at higher speeds.
- Use brighter, varied color palettes (pink, purple, blue) and long spreader bars. Long spreads and floating debris work well.

## Southern California
- Cooler, variable clarity, mixed schools; yellowfin can be deeper and more pressured.
- Start slightly slower in the high‑speed range (9–12 knots), favor darker/chrome patterns, and watch for mixed species. Be ready to swap to livebait or slower plugs when fish hesitate.

## Mental checklist before you run a high‑speed pass

Run this through before you burn the patch:
- Fish mood: birds locked and bait reforming? Go fast.
- Stagger the spread: teaser/short, mid skirt, outrigger, long bar.
- Pick initial speed: 9–12 knots if cautious, 12–16 knots if aggressive.
- Watch the first pass: reaction = maintain speed, strong hookset and immediate follow‑on bait; inspection = slow down and swap to livebait/slow plug.
- Rotate colors/sizes after two passes if no conversion.
- Protect the window: avoid over‑noising the patch and manage throttle and heading to keep fish from scattering.

High‑speed trolling isn’t brute force; it’s controlled disruption. You force a split‑second decision by presenting the right mix of speed, flash and profile across sensible lanes. Read the patch, make one clear change at a time, and have conversion gear ready — that’s how you turn visual aggression into hooks.
