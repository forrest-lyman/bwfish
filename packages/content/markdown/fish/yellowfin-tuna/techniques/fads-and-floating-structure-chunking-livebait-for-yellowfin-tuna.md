The floater does the work for you if you read it right. Shade and drag hold bait; current converging on an object piles that bait on the lee side and creates predictable approach lanes for predators. Chunking here isn't about covering ground — it's about forcing the school to show you where the fish will attack and then putting a livebait or kite into that lane at the exact second they compress and start competing.

## Why the floater gives a window
- Observation: bait and small life collect on one side of a floater, birds and small tunas keep returning to the same patch, and sonar shows grape-like blobs rather than long bands.
- Explanation: the floater creates a sheltered lee where bait accumulates. Current and the object set up approach vectors the predators use. When the bait is tight but the tuna aren't surface-chasing freebaits, a short, dense chunk slick concentrates fish locally and turns a hesitant school into a competitive one.

Reading the push — current, birds, and sonar
Watch the water first. Floating weeds, debris, and the orientation of birds will tell you which way the current is pushing into the floater. The productive side is usually the down‑current/lee side where bait piles and small predators mill. Sonar that shows tight, dense marks concentrated on one side and shoaling marks during short intervals is the practical green light. Wide, faint bands, long strings of marks, or birds that only glance and move on mean the fish are spread and not worth pressing.

How the short chunk slick works
You want a short, oily cloud that mimics a disintegrating school — short length, high density. The chunk slick imitates an intense, immediate food reward; torn bait and blood get attention. Short bursts of oily pieces keep the fish near the floater and force them to cross a predictable vector. Long, thin slicks do the opposite: they spread the fish, dilute aggression, and reduce conversion odds.

Making the slick — practical steps
Start small and watch the response for a minute. A couple of short passes, 50–200 yards of slick at most, will tell you if the school is interested. Keep the volume focused: frequent small chucks and periodic oily burps, not a continuous blanket. Place the slick into the vector the tuna are using — usually on the down‑current side or in the lane between the floater and the approaching school — so the fish have to pass through or along the chum cloud when they attack.

When to convert to livebaits or kites
Convert only when the school compresses and shows competitive behavior. The cues are tactical and visible:
- Sonar marks converge into tight blobs and rise in the water column.
- Birds lower and commit to the same patch for multiple passes.
- Chunk pieces are being actively taken and fish follow the slick instead of merely nipping the edges.
- You see tail slaps, wakes, or hooked fish immediately followed by followers.

Where to put the livebait: on the edge of the slick in the fish’s path. Often that’s the up‑current edge facing the floater or the lane between the floater and the slick so incoming tuna intercept the bait. Use a kite when you need the livebait suspended naturally on the slick edge or when sea state and bait availability favor it. Free-line or slow-troll inside the slick when fish are shallow and violent; slow troll along the edge when the school drifts past.

Boat handling that preserves the shot
Set the boat down‑current or cross‑current of the floater and slick with the stern aimed toward the likely approach lane. That keeps engine noise and wake behind the presentation. Never drive through the productive lane — a pass through the feeding area churns bait and scatters the school. Move slowly and deliberately when repositioning; if the school is committed, hold position and work the slick.

Engine and crew behavior matters as much as chum. Run low RPMs, feather the throttle during deployments, and avoid sudden revs. If drifting is required, neutral or idle is usually best while crew pitch baits or set kites. Keep crew movements quiet and purposeful — loud, visible activity spooks yellowfin, especially in clear water.

Press vs. back off — reading the mood
Press (compress and convert) when marks tighten, birds stay low, chum is being chased aggressively, and hooked fish draw immediate followers. Back off when marks spread into strings, surface life scatters, birds lift, or the fish only take the smallest scraps without follow-up. If multiple boats have circled and the school sinks or flees, you need a reset.

Resetting is simple: stop chunking, stand down visible activity, give the floater a few minutes to re-accumulate bait. A 5–15 minute pull-back often brings the school back if pressure hasn’t driven them off. Then try a fresh, short burst rather than repeating the same long slick.

## Common mistakes I've seen
- Over-chunking: a long greasy slick that stretches for hundreds of yards dilutes aggression and spreads the school.
- Converting too early: putting livebaits into uninterested fish wastes bait and spooks the school.
- Chunking on the wrong side: place the slick in the actual vector fish use, not where you think is convenient.
- Driving through the fish: repeated passes through the productive lane end the stop fast.
- Failing to reset: pounding the same approach when the school cools is wasted time.
- Misreading sonar: grape-like, concentrated marks are promising; shadowy bands often mean bait without tuna. Always cross-check sonar with birds and surface signs before converting.

Associated species — what their presence tells you
Dorado will show up on floaters and can both attract tuna and take baits themselves. Skipjack and small tunas are the first layer — they indicate productive water and often lead larger yellowfin in. Bonito behave like skipjack and serve as an early warning or a hint at the attack lane.

## Regional notes that change the math
- Baja: floaters often hold big, persistent schools. Fish are bolder and convert faster; multiple conversions per stop are common. Heavy fleet pressure on popular FAD grounds means stealth and timely resets matter.
- Southern California: fish tend to be deeper and more tentative. Chunking can work but conversions may need patience or larger livebaits; windows are typically shorter.
- Tortugas Shelf Edge / Thermal fringes: strong current breaks and thermal edges create sharp approach lanes. Current can flip the productive side quickly; place slicks on the precise convergence line for best effect.

How to think through the decision chain
Position relative to current and floater informs how many chunks you throw, which then determines whether livebaits go in. Treat it as a feedback loop: throw a small burst, watch for two minutes, convert if the fish compress, reset if they don’t. Your aim is to make the food signal clearer and more local, not louder and more widespread.

Reality check: windows are short and moods change fast
Expect quick windows and quiet minutes. Perfect presentations still get refused. This technique stacks probability — concentrate fish, force competition, and present livebaits into intercept lanes at the right second. If life stops rebuilding after a reset or the floater quits producing, move on and find another edge.

Final point
Floaters simplify where bait will be. Your job is to read the push, make the slick short and dense, convert only on compression cues, and handle the boat so you give the school a shot to compete. That timing and restraint are what turn a hesitant stop into bites.
