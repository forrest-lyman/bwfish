## Why mid‑column marks matter

Yellowfin don’t always bust on the surface. A lot of the time they sit in a band where current, structure, and bait concentrate — mid‑column predators. Sonar hands you a look into that band. If you can tell where the tuna are relative to the bait you can put a bait in the strike lane instead of guessing at surface activity. I pick this method when the screen shows repeatable, dense marks under a bait layer, especially when birds or a floater suggest food organization but the fish themselves aren’t crashing.

## Translating marks into presentations

The practical rule: match your presentation to the vertical plane and behavior the sonar shows.

- If marks show strong target strength and vertical definition — tight blobs or arches — you’re likely looking at predators. Present so your bait moves through their plane.
- If the sonar shows a diffuse, shimmering cloud, that’s bait. Tuna will often hang below or inside that layer; you want your offering to cross the gap in the angle and sink‑rate the tuna expect.
- Small adjustments matter: 5–10 ft of leader, a fraction more or less weight, or a slight speed change can move a bait from ignored to eaten. Treat those as your primary tuning knobs.

## Reading mid‑column marks: what to look for

Shape and density
- Tight, dense echoes that hold shape and move together almost always mean fish. They reflect harder and keep a vertical definition on the screen.
- Cloudy, patchy returns that smear and reform are bait. They’re less reflective and spread across more depth.

## Vertical relationship
- Fish directly below a bait layer: classic ambush position. Either present a bait that moves up into the fish or run through the bait so the tuna hits on the way through.
- Fish above the bait layer: that’s an edge situation — present at or just above the top edge where they’re likely attacking.

## Movement and coherence
- Marks that compress or rise when you chum or tease are active and convertible. That reaction is the window.
- Marks that sit but don’t respond to disturbance are either pressured or indifferent — don’t overcommit.

## Target behavior signals
- One large echo moving through a band = likely a single big fish. Use one robust presentation and keep it clear.
- Multiple echoes moving as a group = school behavior. Multiple lighter presentations staggered through the strike plane increase interception chances.

## Watch for sonar artifacts
- Thermoclines and plankton layers can mimic bait but won’t scatter or rise when teased. If a line stays thin and hard, or doesn’t move with current disturbances, be suspicious.

## Match presentation to depth and mark type

Near surface marks (0–15 ft)
- Keep it light and free: fly‑lined live sardines, shallow flatlines, small plugs and skirts. Minimal leader and low drag; you want a free, tempting profile.
- Use surface teasers to lift interest but keep hooked baits looking natural.

## Mid‑column marks (15–40 ft)
- This is the primary zone for depth targeting. Use sinking or weighted baits that hold in the 15–40 ft band, heavier flatlines, or down‑rigger style placements.
- Stagger baits through the strike plane and run a teaser at a slightly different depth to provoke reaction without spooking fish.

## Deep marks (40–80+ ft)
- Move to heavier sink rates or deeper rigs: deeper flatlines, heavier weights, or drop baits. Conversion chances drop as depth increases, so consider chum or pull to the edge.
- If fish are genuinely deep and inactive, don’t grind forever — either pull them up with a strategy or go look for a shallower, active edge.

## Fish embedded in bait
- When predators are inside a bait cloud, you want a bait that runs slightly below or through the bait without getting fouled. Multiple baits staggered through the cloud increase interception probability.

## Conversion sequences — how to respond as marks change

1. Initial contact: marks hold but don’t respond
   - Start with a slightly heavier presentation to stay in the band. Run a teaser to provoke without spooking.
   - Change depth in small increments (5–10 ft) rather than big jumps.

2. Marks tighten and rise when teased or chummed
   - Move fast to lighter, shallower offerings: shorten leaders, drop weight, switch to flatline or flyline. Concentrate baits in the strike lane and make quick passes.

3. Marks break up or sink
   - Either add weight and go deeper or move to a new patch. If marks thin and stay thin after a couple passes, the window closed — move.

4. Single target moving through a layer
   - Present a single, robust bait on a predictable intercept path. Too many baits can obscure the target.

5. School shows slashing, high competition
   - Lighten leaders, use livelier offerings, and increase presentation count. Make strikes frequent and obvious.

6. Marks go silent under pressure
   - Back off. Reposition downcurrent and try a softer approach: smaller teasers, quieter spread, and the first pass needs to be clean.

## Signals that the opportunity is building or fading

Signs it’s building
- Marks re‑form after a chum or teaser pass.
- Birds stay tight and keep returning.
- Marks climb toward the bait layer.
- Hooked fish immediately attract more returns.

## Signs it’s fading
- Returns flatten into a diffuse cloud and won’t reconcentrate.
- Birds leave and don’t come back.
- Marks sink or slide off with a current shift.
- Fish only take the smallest live baits and ignore larger offerings — often a pressure or low‑feed sign.

## Common operational mistakes

- Treating every dense return as fish; plankton or thermoclines will waste your time.
- Leaving the same rig in place after marks move. Don’t hope — change deliberately.
- Over‑weighting because you fear losing depth. Too much weight makes the bait unnatural and often puts it below the strike plane.
- Charging the boat through the zone. Work the current and approach angle instead.
- Over‑chumming tight patches until bait scatters and marks vanish.
- Chasing a flashy single echo while ignoring steady, cleaner marks nearby.

## Associated species and what they tell you

- Skipjack and bonito: they make a lot of small echoes and surface commotion. Their presence can signal productive current but also complicate sonar interpretation.
- Dorado: surface activity around floaters or weed can indicate a productive system that also holds yellowfin.
- Bluefin/other tuna: in mixed schools bluefin can change the depth behavior of yellowfin. Knowing who’s present changes what marks mean and what presentation you choose.

## Baja vs. Southern California — what changes

Baja
- Warmer, clearer water with more persistent floaters. Bait tends to be larger and marks can be cleaner. Fish often climb more readily — quick conversions to lighter rigs usually work.

## Southern California
- Cooler water, stronger thermoclines, and denser plankton noise. Fish can be deeper and more pressured. You’ll need heavier, deeper presentations and tighter sonar discrimination. Mixed schools make reading target strength critical.

## On‑boat tactics

- Approach and positioning: come upcurrent or at a gentle angle so baits sweep naturally through the mark; avoid motoring over tight returns.
- Fine tune sink rate: adjust in small steps and run multiple baits staggered vertically. Think in 5–10 ft increments.
- Use teasers and chum as probes: they expose the window but also change behavior. Use them to create the moment, then hit the strike lane decisively.
- Have a conversion plan: for each likely change have the next rig ready — lighter flatlines if marks rise, heavier if they sink, concentrate if they split.
- Mark the GPS and note drift vectors: repeatable passes beat random circling. Watch whether marks move with the current or sit relative to the bottom.

## Reality check: what to expect

Sonar gives you an advantage but it’s not perfect. Fish hide, bait scatters, and windows open and shut quickly. Expect rejection even when your bait is in the right plane. Two practical points: learn in the first two passes and be willing to move if you don’t convert; small, decisive adjustments win more often than big, slow ones.

A short, practical takeaway

Read the marks — shape, density, and their vertical relation to bait — then put a bait in that plane with the right profile and sink rate. Make adjustments in small steps, have a clear conversion sequence ready, and move quickly when the window changes. The boats that win aren’t the ones that tinker forever; they interpret the screen and make small, decisive moves.
