# Permit

Permit are flats fish that punish assumptions. They line up with tide, bottom texture, rays, channels and crab movement, but they don't always feed where they're easiest to see. You can watch a perfect-looking fish for ten minutes and get nothing, then watch a barely visible fish on a wind line tip and eat.

## Where tide, bottom, and rays put food in front of them

The common thread is moving water exposing prey. Hard sand edges, broken grass, coral patches, channel lips and ray muds change what a permit can work. If the current is putting crabs or shrimp into the open, you've got a shot; if it isn't, the fish will be sightseeing.

Useful signs that a fish has a real reason to be there:

- fish riding with stingrays
- tails or sickles over feeding bottom
- muds forming on the tide
- singles moving slowly into current
- fish sliding from a channel edge onto the flat
- nervous water that stops and tips down

Those signs tell you why the fish is where it is. The best fish is not always the highest fish.

## Reading body language — what shows a feeding fish

Body language is the work. Look at speed, height in the water column, angle to current and the tail profile. Those details separate a feeding fish from one that's just moving.

Better indicators of a feed:

- fish move slowly
- fish tip down
- fish hold on a piece of bottom
- fish track a presentation without speeding up
- fish stay with a ray or a mud

Things that usually mean you should pass and look for another shot:

- fish high and fast
- fish travelling straight across the flat
- fish already reacting to the boat
- fish that change direction away before the cast lands

Watch the whole fish, not just its head. A tail that pauses or the fish that stops forward progress and tips is a different animal than one that never slows.

## Pressure and the invisible shut-down

Permit remember pressure. Skiff noise, bad casts, heavy presentations and repeated shots change a flat for hours. The fish often stay visible — cruising or sitting — but they stop wanting to eat.

The permit did not leave. It stopped being a feeding fish.

Treat pressure as a resource. One bad pass can burn a patch of flat; restraint and careful approach buy more opportunities than brute force.

## Regional shape — how locality changes what you watch for

Flats fish are the same animal in principle, but the details differ by place. Florida Keys fish get shaped by clear water, heavy pressure, oceanside flats, channels and wreck-influenced crab movement. Belize, Mexico and much of the Caribbean add reef edges, deeper flats and more ray-associated fish into the mix.

You don't need a new strategy for every bay, just an eye for what the tide and structure are doing there. Permit fishing rewards restraint. The right shot starts long before the cast.
