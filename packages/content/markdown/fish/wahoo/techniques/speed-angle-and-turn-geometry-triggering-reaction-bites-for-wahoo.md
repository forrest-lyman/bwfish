## Why this is the play — short reaction windows, not long chases

Wahoo don’t give long, polite opportunities. They sit on an edge and take a single, brief shot at anything that suddenly looks wrong in their strike lane. That suddenness — a burst of speed, a quick change of angle, or the lure dropping behind the boat’s plane — is the thing that makes them commit. We treat it as a timing and geometry problem: get the turn right, make the lure fall and then surge it past the fish, and you’ve created a one‑second presentation they hate to ignore.

## When to reach for speed/angle/turn geometry

Use this when the environment sets up a narrow strike lane and normal, steady passes aren’t working.

- Clean current along a reef, bank, FAD or weedline with bait hugging the edge.
- Birds or bait flashing in a narrow band; you saw a single flash or a short hit on the first pass.
- Wind or boat motion makes long steady passes ineffective — the strike needs to be forced.
- Fish showed interest on a straight run but never committed.

## What the fish actually reacts to

Wahoo target motion contrasts. They don’t study a lure; they react to a quick mismatch in speed and angle.

- Sudden acceleration: a short burst makes the lure surge and flash, prompting a chase‑burst.
- Turn geometry: a tight inside turn then a quick exit changes the lure’s angle and depth relative to the fish, mimicking a fleeing baitfish crossing an edge.
- Lure fall on the turn: when the leader angle shifts the lure can drop 0.5–2 m (1.5–6 ft) relative to the boat’s plane. That vertical change plus the sideways motion is the trigger.
- Combination strikes: most hits happen as the lure accelerates out of the turn or during the immediate fall — they’re keyed to contrast, not constant motion.

## Radius, burst timing and the lure’s fall

There’s a mechanical relationship between turn tightness, how the leader loads, and how the lure presents. Learn those links and you’ll stop guessing.

## Turn radius vs. lure behavior
- Tight (inside): ~1–3 boat lengths. Produces abrupt angle change and a quick fall. Use this when fish are actively following or a first pass produced a short hit.
- Medium: ~3–6 boat lengths. Softer angle, longer exposure — better for wary fish.
- Wide: >6 boat lengths. More sweep than snap; rarely triggers reaction bites unless paired with a big acceleration.

## Acceleration timing
- Add a controlled burst of +2–4 knots for 1–3 seconds as you exit the turn. Start the burst as you hit the outside of the turn so the lure surges past the fish. That timing converts the visual fall into forward energy behind the lure.

## Relative depth shift
- Expect the lure to back down 0.5–2 m during a tight turn. It’s that short vertical collapse combined with the surge that provokes slashes and short snaps.

## Typical speeds and what to feel for
- Baseline flat‑run: 12–18 knots (lure dependent). Keep that baseline and manipulate speed inside the turn. Watch how the lure behaves — the way it swings and the delay before it re‑establishes plane tells you if your radius and throttle are in tune.

## Reading the first pass to set the second

The first pass is intelligence-gathering. Change one variable and see how the fish answer.

- Hard short strike, no hookup
  - Read: fish committed late or hit the tail.
  - Adjust: tighten the turn and accelerate sooner so the lure meets the fish head‑on instead of offering the tail.

- Fish followed but refused
  - Read: interested but wary of depth or presentation.
  - Adjust: increase the fall (tighter inside turn) and use a stronger burst so the lure looks like fleeing bait.

- Light tap or single flash
  - Read: tiny window, possibly a single fish passing.
  - Adjust: one aggressive pass — tight turn plus fast surge. Don’t waste time with soft repeats.

- Strike well outside the structure
  - Read: fish prefer intercepting fleeing bait downstream.
  - Adjust: move your passes slightly farther off the edge and keep the burst timing consistent.

## Signs the window is closing

Watch the behavior, not the clock.

- Follows stop or fish turn deeper: the school slid off the edge or shut down.
- Bites get shallower or farther away: the school is moving; don’t stay fixed.
- Multiple boats over the same line with no success: pressure likely pushed fish away. Relocate.

## Drills crews can rehearse (short, repeatable, on/off water)

Drills build reflexes. Practice them until the calls and throttle feel automatic.

1) Turn‑Radius Drill
- Set two marks ~50–100 m apart. Run inside turns at tight (1 BL), medium (3 BL) and wide (6 BL) radii.
- Watch how long the lure takes to re‑establish plane and where it hangs during the turn. Aim for repeatable lines.

2) Acceleration Burst Drill
- On exit from a practiced turn, execute a +2–4 knot burst for 1–3 seconds.
- Crew watches where the lure sits relative to the boat when the burst starts. Repeat until bursts are on cue with no overshoot.

3) Lure‑Angle/Drop Drill
- With an observer or stern camera, practice turns that produce a 1–2 m drop during the turn.
- Change throttle and radius to dial the fall. The crew should learn the visual cues of a correct silhouette and profile.

4) First‑Pass Read → Second‑Pass Response
- Have an observer call simulated reactions (“tap,” “follow,” “hard hit”).
- Crew calls and executes a single, prebriefed adjustment (tighten, widen, accelerate sooner). Rotate roles so everyone knows the right response.

5) Pressure Management Drill
- Practice one aggressive pass, then move off the line for 60–90 seconds before returning or relocating.
- Train to avoid reflexively running the same line after a reaction.

## Common mistakes that kill windows

These are the operational errors I see on other boats.

- Running the exact same pass after a reaction — fish slide or learn; change one thing.
- Turns too tight for the leader/lure setup, causing porpoising and an unnatural presentation.
- Overdoing acceleration — you’ll snap leaders, tangle spread, or make the lure unreadable.
- Too many boats or too many soft passes on a narrow edge — pressure ends short windows fast.
- Misreading the first pass: not every follow calls for tighter turns; sometimes fish want a wider sweep.

## Associated species and what they tell you

- Mahi‑mahi: common around debris and edges; their commotion can draw wahoo in and they’ll hit flashy, fast work.
- Yellowfin/skipjack: bait and small tunas create the intercept opportunities on current lines and FADs.
- Spanish mackerel/kingfish: their reaction‑bite behavior is similar — their presence often means a high‑speed, visual feed.

## Regional tweaks that actually matter

- Caribbean/Bahamas: steeper edges and clearer water — tighter turns and shorter bursts usually win. Fish are more visual; use high‑contrast flash.
- Pacific/Baja/islands: banks and FADs dictate angles; open water can need longer passes and slightly higher baseline speeds. Debris creates multiple intercept angles.
- Gulf/weedline: wake and weed change lure behavior — adjust radius so the lure doesn’t smother or foul.

## What to prioritize in the moment

Make it simple and repeatable.

- Observe first. One good read beats ten blind passes.
- Change only one variable — radius OR speed OR spread position. Keep the rest constant so you know what worked.
- Communicate fast. Prebrief short calls (“Tight/Blast,” “Wide/Slow,” “Outside pass”) and execute without debate.
- Limit passes. Two purposeful runs beat five aimless ones. If nothing after two adjusted attempts, move.

A realistic finishing note

These are brittle windows. Even perfect geometry can fail when the school slips or the bait vanishes. The play is forcing a reaction in that one instant: practice the turns and bursts until they’re second nature, read your first pass and be willing to move when the line goes cold. Keep the calls tight and the execution cleaner than the rest of the fleet — that’s how you turn fleeting chances into hookups.
