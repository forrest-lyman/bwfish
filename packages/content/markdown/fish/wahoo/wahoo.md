# Wahoo

Wahoo live on edges and in short windows. They don’t sit in slack water and they don’t wait for us to get perfect. They slide the current, zip the face of a reef or a floating log, and take what’s exposed. That habit creates few, sharp chances — a flash, a cut-off, a single surface blow — then the angle changes and the school is gone or out of reach.

Watch the water first. The fish follow what makes bait vulnerable; then they use speed to finish it. Your job is finding where the bait is made vulnerable and delivering a lure through that strip of water before the window collapses.

## Where current makes bait vulnerable

The most reliable bites come where moving water forces bait into an edge. Points, bank corners, clean reef faces, FADs, buoys, ledges, color breaks — any place current is pushing bait across or holding it tight to structure.

What to watch for (these are the patterns that matter):

- clean water pushing into a reef face or drop-off
- bait holding tight on the down-current edge
- small tuna, bonito, or flying fish stacked near the structure
- birds working fast along an edge — not circling, but running
- strikes repeatedly coming from the same current side of a feature
- fish showing once and then disappearing below the surface

That strike zone is often narrow. A boat length or a couple of turns can move you from inside the zone to useless water. Treat those edges like a seam — cross them at the moment the bait is exposed.

## Timing, speed and angle

Speed matters because wahoo use it efficiently, not because they want noise. The lure has to pass through the slice of water the fish are using. On a bank or reef that usually means working the up-current side, then sliding the lure off the edge where the bait tears free.

Practical patterns I've seen work:

- bites on turns where the lure accelerates or drops into current
- strikes just outside obvious bait marks — they strike the seam, not the middle
- repeated hits over the same contour rather than random casts
- fish cutting short baits behind the active lure — they’ll steal off the tail of action
- bites moving deeper as the sun climbs and the surface window tightens

Treat the first pass as reconnaissance: what direction are they coming from? The second pass is the confirmation — if it was a fish, you’ll usually get a committed strike or a solid follow. If not, change angle or speed.

## Pressure, short windows and boat work

Wahoo shut down fast under pressure. Too many passes over the same small edge, sloppy turns inside the bite zone, or several boats clustered on a buoy will push them deeper or freeze them up.

Signs a window is closing:

- short, tentative strikes with no follow-through
- fish following without committing
- bites sliding deeper off the structure
- strikes shifting farther from the feature
- a clear first bite with nothing on the second pass

When you see those signs, don’t grind dead water because it produced one bite earlier. Either change angle, move to the next edge, or give the zone time and look for a fresh approach — different speed, different lure, or a new entry lane through the current.

## Regional habits, not rules

Where the edges run changes with water and geography, but the pattern stays the same: moving water + vulnerable bait = opportunity.

- Bahamas/Caribbean: steep edges, tides and moon-driven flows, clean current along reef faces.
- Pacific islands/Baja: high spots, ridges, FADs and tight bait schools on structure.
- Gulf: rigs, weedlines, debris and offshore structure where bait concentrates.

Don’t treat those as strict rules — treat them as likely places to find the seam. The common thread is current that makes bait vulnerable. Fast fish demand slow, patient reading: locate the seam, time the pass, and set the spread so the lure crosses the fish’s lane before the window vanishes.
