Whe
n albacore are keyed to a band of bait and willing to chase a fast, obvious target, cedar plugs become the practical choice. They read as a fleeing or schooling bait from a distance, keep their action at speed, and often trigger the first aggressive reaction that turns a passing bite into a working stop.

This is not the finesse option. It is a visible, speed‑dependent presentation that exploits albacore’s chase response when the fish are high‑energy and the water lines up.

When you reach for cedars

- the fleet is seeing moving fish or intermittent jig strikes from the spread
- birds are low and traveling, not milling
- surface bait patches or streaming baitfish are present
- the troll bite pattern shows repeatable hits on the corners
- you want a plug that reads at distance and keeps fish interested while you finish the setup

## How the technique works

Cedar plugs read like a small fleeing bait—flash from the paint/foil, a rolling or diving action, and a steady wake. At typical albacore speeds they present as an obvious target that fish can pick up from some distance and commit to.

Key operational points:

- speed matters: cedars need a speed that balances action and control. Too slow and they roll dead; too fast and they flutter oddly. For much of the West Coast that sweet spot sits between 7 and 10 knots depending on plug size and trim.
- distance and presentation: longer baits read as larger, closer targets. Shorten if fish are close and tight; lengthen if fish show from far.
- the plug is a trigger, not the whole plan: the first strike often starts the stop. Be ready to feed bait or bring the spread in when a plug draws attention.

## Reading the spread

Adjust the spread based on how fish show and the direction of travel.

- corner plugs (longest leads) for showing baits off the stern on clearer days
- middle‑line plugs ahead for fish rolling on the surface
- slow plugs or lighter cedar nearer the boat when fish refuse the outer rigger
- staggered sizes and colors—don’t run the same plug on every line

A plugged spread should give you options: a visible ringer out wide, a smaller presentation that fishes cleaner close, and a back‑up that can be shortened fast into the stop.

## Signs the window is building

- a plug gets taken, then another rod folds when the boat turns
- birds hold direction and ride with the boat instead of scattering
- the meter shows marks building under the stern after a short chum/bait toss
- fish show on sonar rising from a distance toward the plugs
- multiple boats keep stopping on the same temperature band or line

Micro-observation:
The first plug bite usually tells you whether to commit. One bite and no higher signs: leave. One bite and birds stay low: get bait in fast.

## Common mistakes

- running cedars too slowly or too fast for their trim—action inconsistent, fish confused
- over‑complicating the spread—too many similar presentations make the stop ambiguous
- staying on a dead troll bite without getting bait in after the initial strike
- using only big, flashy plugs when the fish want something smaller and cleaner
- ignoring wind/current: a perfect plug in flat water may look wrong in a breeze

What usually matters more:
- speed control and the ability to shorten/lengthen quickly
- having at least one clean, short presentation for close‑up fish
- getting chum or live bait out immediately after the first plug bite

## When it stops working

- fish sink and refuse surface presentations—even lively cedars
- birds vanish or start milling with no direction
- the spread gets sliced up by current and plugs ride erratically
- the bite is only on slow vertical baits or on jigs deep under the boat

If the plug bite dies but the area still holds fish on the meter, switch presentations—smaller plugs, feather rigs, or livebait—before you lose the school.

## Regional notes

Pacific Northwest / Oregon, Washington:
- albacore runs are long and the water is cooler. Cedars work when warm pockets push in and fish are willing to chase. Speeds may be a touch slower; long leads and larger plugs help fish pick them out at distance.

Northern California:
- temperature seams and canyon edges produce compact bands. Cedars are useful when birds and surface bait line up along a break—shorten quickly into a tight stop when the first plug draws interest.

Southern California / Baja:
- fish can be faster and more surface-oriented. Smaller, flashier cedars at slightly higher speeds often produce. Watch for pressure from multiple boats; cedars can draw attention from fish that will otherwise ignore heavier or slower offerings.

## Associated species

- yellowfin and bluefin when warm patches show
- bonito and other surface predators around bait lanes
- mahi on cleaner warm water where plugs mimic fleeing bait near paddies

## Tactical callouts

- If a plug gets hit and the bird line stays low: feed live or chunk fast and hold the boat. The plug bite is often the ticket to a multi‑fish stop.
- If plugs are being slashed but nothing follows: shorten to cleaner, less flashy presentations or try feather rigs—the fish may be picky on profile.
- Keep one rod rigged to shorten instantly. The difference between a working school and a lost one is often how fast you can change range and present bait.

The plug is a tool. Read the bite, read the birds, and be willing to change to a quieter presentation when the school narrows its interests.
