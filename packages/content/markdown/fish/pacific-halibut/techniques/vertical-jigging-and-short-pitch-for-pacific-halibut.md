Halibut sit tight to bottom and wait for food to move through their lane. Vertical jigging and short‑pitch bottom bounces are ways to keep a lure in that narrow lane long enough for a halibut to notice, follow, and decide to eat. The trick isn’t flashy action — it’s staying in the fish’s vertical window and presenting a low‑risk, natural motion that triggers a decisive strike or lets you switch to bait when fish are touchy.

## When the opportunity shows: the sonar and drift signs that matter

First thing we look for is evidence the fish are holding and watching the drift. On the sounder that looks like tight, low arches or blobs hugging the bottom, or repeated returns along the same contour as you pass. If fish appear and disappear along the same line on successive drifts, you’ve got a lane worth working.

Operational signs from the boat are simple: followers that come up behind your jig or repeated light taps on the line. If the fleet is pinging bottom‑tight fish that refuse raised baits, or your jigs are getting trailed but not eaten, that’s the window for short‑pitch or vertical work.

## What the presentation is selling to a halibut

We’re not trying to imitate a fleeing tuna. The presentation needs to read as small, easy prey — a crippled bait or drifting morsel that doesn’t threaten the fish. Short, low‑amplitude hops and slow vertical movements do that: they stay near the bottom contour, pause, and fall in a way that looks effortless to the halibut.

Practically, the fall and the hover matter as much as the lift. Halibut commonly strike on a slow drop or during a pause after a lift. If your motion pulls the jig out of the strike window or is too lively, the fish will either ignore it or follow and then quit.

## Keeping the jig in the strike window: weight, line angle, and control

Match your weight to depth, current, and drift angle so the jig contacts or hovers just above bottom. If you don’t get that right, nothing else will save you.

General operational ranges (not rules): lighter setups for shallow, calm water (20–60 ft), medium weights for the common stuff (60–150 ft), and heavier lead for deep water or fast tidal channels (150+ ft). The practical test is simple: err on the side of enough weight to hold contact. A jig that rides high won’t get eaten.

Line angle and rod position are your live controls. A steep line angle means the jig will lift off; a shallow angle keeps it pinned to the contour. In heavier current shorten the lift height and cadence so the jig stays in the window while the current moves it laterally. Use short, deliberate rod motions: small upward pulses then controlled lets of line so the jig falls slowly into the strike zone.

## How to move the jig — short‑pitch vs. vertical jigging cadence

Short‑pitch (bottom bounce): think “bump, pause, drop.” A brief lift — often 6–18 inches — hold for a second or two, then a controlled drop and a 1–3 second hover. Repeat with a steady tempo so the jig patrols the bottom without leaping away. If followers trail but won’t eat, lengthen the pause and slow the fall; many halibut commit on a slow drop or long hover.

Vertical jigging: slightly larger sweeps (12–24 inches) with longer pauses (2–4 seconds) before a controlled fall. Use this when fish are a little off the bottom or need a touch more action to locate the lure. In cold or lethargic conditions slow everything down; when fish are active tighten the tempo but keep motions small.

Adapt cadence to what the fish tell you. If marks chase and nip, increase pulse aggression. If they follow and peel off, soften the lifts and extend the hover.

## Reading the sounder: what halibut look like and how to time your moves

Halibut rarely show as big mid‑water arches. On the screen they’re tight returns inches to a few feet off the bottom. Multiple small returns hugging the contour or repeated marks on repeat drifts point to holding fish. When followers appear behind your jig they often show as a train of short marks.

Use live feedback: drop the jig to the bottom and watch the relationship between your lure and the marks. If followers light up when the jig passes but never bite, change pause length and fall speed. If you get instant arches on a lift, shorten the lift and let the drop create the strike.

Interpretation is simple: a single dot moving slowly along the bottom is an individual fish; a cluster of dots hugging bottom usually means several fish or dense bait. A follower that wakes up and tracks the jig is your ticket to refine motion or convert to bait.

## Converting followers to live or cut bait — when to switch and how to do it

We switch when followers repeatedly track the jig but won’t commit — the jig is getting attention but not hookups. Other signals are light taps, short follows on the screen, or fish that only take on very slow falls.

Conversion needs to be quick and precise. Steps that work in sequence:

1. Drop the bait into the exact bite zone the jig was working — follow the same path the jig took.
2. Reduce leader length so the bait sits in the same vertical window as the jig.
3. Present the bait with restrained motion: occasional micro‑pitches or a very gentle drag to mimic the jig’s last behavior.
4. If needed, use a scented cut strip or a quietly kicking live bait — sometimes scent or subtle life is the final trigger.

You can use the jig as a short teaser near the bait after the switch, but be cautious — excessive action will spook fish around a baited hook. If followers vanish after the switch or conversions repeatedly fail, the fish may be pressured or the window shut; move to a different line.

## When this method fades and what forces it

This technique runs until you can’t keep the jig in the window. Heavy current that requires overwhelming weight or constant reeling will pull the jig out of the strike zone. If marks lift off the bottom and form mid‑water arches, the fish have changed feeding position and a different tactic is needed. If followers vanish after several passes, pressure or bait behavior shifts have likely closed the window. Also watch for fish that only take very quiet bait presentations — when metal action stops working, it’s a signal to change.

## Common operational mistakes to avoid

We see the same failures over and over because they’re easy to make.

- Over‑jigging: big lifts pull the jig out of the halibut’s feeding window and mimic a fleeing target.
- Wrong weight: too light and the jig rides high; too heavy and you lose the delicate motion.
- Poor drift control: cross‑cutting contours or inconsistent speed destroys the repeatable presentation halibut need.
- Bad timing on the bait switch: convert too early and you lose fish that would take the jig; too late and fish spook or move off.
- Not watching the sounder: followers and new marks are immediate cues—ignore them and you miss easy adjustments.

## Regional adjustments: how Alaska, PNW, and NorCal differ

Alaska: deeper water and strong tides. Expect heavier weights and a slower cadence. Fish often have longer pauses between moves in very cold water, so slow the fall and extend hovers. Long drift lanes demand careful attention to weight and line angle.

PNW: a mix of steep contours and tidal channels. Be ready to change weight quickly as you move from flats to channel shoulders. Current windows can be narrow; small adjustments matter.

NorCal: shallower flats are common. Use lighter gear and slightly more aggressive short‑pitches when halibut are actively feeding on small baitfish, but keep the motion compact.

## How to think about a session: position, tweak, and sequence

Positioning is primary. If you can’t get the jig tenure in the strike window, no cadence will help. Start with the contour and drift line, look for repeated marks, and then work small adjustments. A typical successful sequence: detect followers → refine cadence until you get bites → convert quickly to bait if followers persist without eating → move when conversions fail. Make small changes, not wholesale shifts.

Expect several passes. Halibut will often follow before committing; windows close fast from current, pressure, or fleet noise. Learn the small signals — a new follower, a subtle twitch on the sounder, a light tap — and respond immediately.

## Final practical rules

- Work the vertical window you see on the sounder. If fish are a foot off bottom, that’s your target.
- Keep motion small: micro‑lifts, controlled falls, and purposeful pauses.
- Match weight to current so you can micro‑move without losing contact.
- Convert to bait when followers track repeatedly but don’t eat, and place the bait exactly where the jig earned attention.

This is a patient, observational technique. When it’s right you’ll see followers, get slow strikes on the drop, and convert more than you scare off. When it’s wrong the sounder tells you — move, adjust, and look for the next window.
