# Pacific Halibut

Pacific halibut live on the bottom, but they don't treat the bottom like a blank slate. They use contour and current the way a fisherman uses wind and tide — to bring food to them. A flat that looks empty on one tide will light up when bait begins sliding across it; the fish rarely move far if the water will move food across their lie.

They feed by position. The key is not convincing a fish to chase you, it's putting your bait where the water will hand it to them.

## Edges, Saddles, and Troughs — Where the Food Collects

The halibut spots that produce usually aren't uniform sand. They're places where the current gathers, funnels, or pauses—sand against rock, a gravel edge, the base of a slope, saddles between highs, troughs and channel shoulders. Those features change how bait and scent travel across the bottom.

Signs that the bottom is working for you are practical and repeatable: bait staying near bottom, the tide cutting across contour rather than washing straight over flat ground, soft-bottom edges beside structure, repeated hookups on the same drift line, fish showing at the base of a slope, or the current softening just behind a hump. Those are the moments a flat becomes a fishery.

## Reading the Tide Window

The bite usually lives in a tide window. Too little movement and bait and scent go dead; too much and gear lifts, drifts fast, or fish refuse to move for it. The useful windows are the ones where current pushes food across the laying fish at a rate they can feed on.

Watch the sequence:

- bites often start as a tide begins to move,
- the first manageable push is frequently the best,
- windows can be short before current builds heavy,
- fish will reposition after a turn,
- and you’ll see bait marks move from scattered to tight on bottom as the window comes.

It’s not the bottom changing — it’s the water over it. Time your drifts to the window, not just to the waypoint.

## Drift Is the Spot

Halibut fishing is boat control. You can mark a perfect piece of bottom, but if your drift passes through it wrong you see nothing. A good drift keeps the bait in the feeding lane; a bad one crosses the same ground and collects nothing.

Pay attention to the feedback that tells you what to keep or change:

- line angle and how the line tracks off the rod tip,
- whether your bait is contacting or hovering off bottom,
- drift speed through the contour,
- where the bait sits relative to the slope or edge,
- and whether bites repeat at a particular depth or part of the drift.

Those observations tell you when to run the drift again or when that lane is done for the tide.

## How Region Shapes Where Fish Hold

Halibut behavior is shaped by local bottom and local tides. Alaska, British Columbia, and the Pacific Northwest all produce halibut, but the holding styles differ. In some places fish will sit on broad flats and feed as bait slides by; elsewhere they prefer steep drops, channels, or offshore banks where current concentrates food.

The successful fisherman reads bottom as moving water, not a static map. If you watch how bait and marks react to the tide and hold your drift through the lane, you'll find the fish that other boats pass by.
